Pointing, Tracking
& Handover Dynamics

MODULE 6
USER TERMINALS & D2D
CORE

Everything in this module so far assumed the beam is on the satellite. This lesson earns that assumption. A terminal must acquire a satellite it has never seen, track it across the sky at up to ~0.8° per second — possibly from a platform that is itself rolling through tens of degrees — and switch to the next satellite every few minutes without the session noticing. Each task has an error budget, the error budget has a price in decibels, and the price law is quadratic: small sloppiness is nearly free, moderate sloppiness is ruinous.

The lesson also pays two debts. m6-4's tilt optimiser ran on a placeholder uniform elevation distribution; this lesson computes the real one from constellation geometry — and the real one is worse than uniform (median serving elevation of a single tracked satellite above a 25° mask: just 35°). But the same computation delivers the module's most system-level result: with a dense constellation, the terminal serves the best of many visible satellites, and max-of-K selection moves the median elevation from 35° to 61° (K = 8) — worth more G/T to a flat panel than the mechanical tilt itself. Satellite selection is a G/T strategy, constellation density is a terminal-performance parameter, and the m2 geometry and the m6 hardware turn out to be one budget. The pass-weighted G/T that the Module 4 engine should consume finally exists at the end of this lesson, honestly derived.

1. The price of mispointing

For errors small against the beamwidth, gain loss follows the main lobe's parabolic top:

L ≈ 12 · (θ_err / θ₃dB)² dB

Verified anchor: an error of half the beamwidth costs exactly 3.0 dB (it is the half-power point). The law's quadratic shape sets the engineering tone: at one-tenth of a beamwidth the loss is 0.12 dB — negligible; at a third, 1.3 dB — visible; at a half, a MODCOD rung (m5-2). The m4-1 budget's innocuous "pointing loss: 0.3 dB" line is therefore a hardware commitment: it licenses a total pointing error of √(0.3/12) ≈ 0.16 beamwidths, and everything in this lesson is the machinery that delivers it.

The beamwidth denominator makes the commitment terminal-specific. The 65 cm dish (1.6° beamwidth, m6-1) must hold ~0.25° total error for its 0.3 dB; a gimbal, its resolvers, and its control loop must deliver that against §2's dynamics. The 1,280-element panel (≈3.2° beamwidth) enjoys twice the angular tolerance — and steers electronically with essentially perfect knowledge of its own commanded angle, so its pointing error is dominated not by actuation but by knowledge: where the satellite is, where the terminal is, and — decisively on vehicles — which way the terminal is facing (§6).

2. The dynamics of a pass

How fast the target moves, from the m2 geometry at 550 km (orbital velocity 7.59 km/s):

Culmination elevation Peak angular rate
90° (overhead)0.79 °/s
60°0.69 °/s
30°0.44 °/s
25°0.39 °/s

The rate peaks at culmination (closest approach), and angular acceleration peaks there too — the classic sizing case for a mechanical positioner is the near-overhead pass, where azimuth rate diverges as the trajectory passes close to the gimbal's zenith singularity (the "keyhole": an elevation-over-azimuth mount must spin its azimuth axis violently to follow a pass near straight up). Electronic steering has no keyhole and no rate limit; a gimballed dish needs either rate margin, a mount geometry chosen for the traffic (tilted-axis mounts), or a scheduler that avoids handing it near-zenith passes. One more line item on the m6-3 ledger between motors and silicon.

3. Where the satellites actually are

m6-4 optimised the tilt against a uniform elevation distribution, flagged as crude. The honest distribution, computed from geometry — satellites uniformly distributed on their shell, terminal serving whatever is above a 25° mask:

Statistic (single satellite, ergodic) Value
Median elevation35°
Mean elevation39°
Fraction of time above 60°9%

The sky is bottom-heavy: the solid-angle geometry that made low elevations dominant for coverage (m2) makes them dominant for service too. Consequence for the flat panel: the ergodic pass-weighted G/T, through the m6-4 scan model, is 7.7 dB/K — 3.3 dB below the broadside datasheet figure. Flat-mounted and served by a lone satellite, the panel spends its life at wide scan. Mechanical tilt claws back 2.5 dB (to 10.2 at 25° tilt). But the stronger lever is the next section's.

Selection at K = 8 is worth +2.3 dB to the flat panel — more than the tilt — and it compresses the tilt's marginal value from 2.5 dB to 0.9 dB (tilted, max-of-8: 10.9 dB/K). Three system-level readings. First, constellation density is a terminal-performance parameter: every satellite added to the shell buys decibels at every terminal on the ground, through geometry alone. Second, the serving-elevation distribution is a choice the scheduler makes (m5-3), coupled to load balancing and the m6-4 arc-avoidance exclusions — the same selection logic serves three masters. Third, the tilt's residual value concentrates exactly where selection is weakest: at high latitude, where visible satellites cluster toward one azimuth band and K-effective is small — which is why the tilt survives in real products as a latitude-dependent installation parameter rather than dying of redundancy.

5. Acquisition: finding a satellite from a cold start

Open-loop pointing, the NGSO norm (and the terminal-side mirror of m5-3 §7 / m5-5's ephemeris machinery): commanded angles = f(satellite ephemeris, own position, own attitude, time). The error budget that must sum below §1's tolerance:

Term Fixed terminal Vehicle-mounted
Ephemeris (broadcast, fresh)small (millidegree class)same
Own position (GNSS)negligible at these rangesnegligible
Timenegligible with GNSSsame
Attitude knowledgeone-time calibration (sky search)dominant — continuous, dynamic

For a fixed terminal the open-loop budget closes trivially once installed orientation is calibrated — which the terminal does itself at first light: a slow electronic sky search correlating found satellites against ephemeris solves the mounting orientation, no installer skill required (the consumer product's quiet dependence on this is total). For anything that moves, attitude is the budget, and §6 takes it.

Closed-loop refinement then holds the peak: a dish uses conical scan or step-track (dither the mechanical pointing, climb the signal gradient) or, in high-end form, monopulse (simultaneous sum/difference patterns give the error angle in one measurement, no dithering). An array dithers electronically for free — microsecond beam offsets measuring the gradient without moving anything — one more small structural advantage of steering in phase.

6. Platforms that move

The attitude term, by segment. A maritime platform rolls with periods of ~8–12 s and amplitudes from a few degrees to 25–30° in heavy seas — rates of several degrees per second, rhythmic and INS-predictable. An aircraft adds bank angles to 25–30° in normal manoeuvres with roll rates that dwarf the pass dynamics of §2, plus fuselage flexure and vibration; a land vehicle is the worst per degree — sharp, unpredictable attitude transients on rough ground. In every case the terminal must counter-rotate its beam at the platform rate plus the pass rate, using inertial attitude (dedicated IMU or the platform's own INS feed on aircraft — m6-6's installation interface), with GNSS-aided alignment.

The architecture consequence is the m6-3 ledger again, sharpened: a mechanical tracker needs rate and acceleration margin above the platform's worst dynamics (motors sized for the sea state, not the average), while an ESA needs only attitude data fast and fresh enough — its correction is computation. Latency is the ESA's exposure: 20 ms of stale attitude at 10°/s of roll is 0.2° of transient error — fine inside a 3° beamwidth, marginal inside 1.6°. Beamwidth tolerance, attitude latency, and platform dynamics form one inequality, and it, not preference, decides which segments went electronic first.

7. Handover: the switch, choreographed

The serving satellite changes on a cadence set by geometry and scheduling — operators reassign on intervals from tens of seconds to a few minutes (class figure; the constellation's scheduler owns the exact number). The choreography per switch:

1. Candidate selection — next satellite chosen by the §4 logic (elevation/load/arc-legality), from ephemeris, ahead of need. 2. Prepare — the second beam (m6-3's second analog layer, or a digital beam) acquires the candidate: pointing, then the m5-5 machinery — timing advance and Doppler pre-compensation from the same ephemeris. 3. Make — traffic authorised on the new path while the old still carries; the m5-3 scheduler grants on both. 4. Break — the old beam releases; the layer frees for the next candidate.

Executed cleanly, the user-visible interruption is bounded by the network-side switching, not the RF — target figures in the low tens of milliseconds, small against m5-5's RTT and invisible to TCP. Executed without a second beam (single-layer analog, or any mechanical tracker), the same choreography forces a break-before-make gap of at least the repointing time — which for a gimbal slewing tens of degrees is seconds, the difference between a handover and an outage. This is m6-3 §6's silicon justified by m5-3's session-continuity requirement, now with the timeline that connects them.

8. The deliverable: pass-weighted G/T, honestly

Assembling §3–§4 into the number the Module 4 engine should consume for the 1,280-element panel (broadside 11.0 dB/K):

Flat, single-satellite ergodic7.7 dB/K
Flat + 25° tilt, ergodic10.2 dB/K
Flat, max-of-8 selection10.0 dB/K

The honest chain from datasheet to service: 11.0 broadside → 10.9 served, only because selection and tilt both do their work — and 7.7 if neither does. The 3.2 dB spread across this table is pure operations and geometry: no hardware differs between its rows. m6-7's evaluator will carry the served figure, not the broadside one, into the M4 budget — and the same logic prices the dish's alternative: a tracked dish holds 19.9 dB/K across every row, its gimbal buying with motors what the panel buys with satellites.

10. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

Stage 5 of the spine: a PassModel (the ergodic and max-of-K elevation samplers, the pass-weighted integrator) and a PointingBudget (error terms RSS'd into the §1 loss law, with a gimbal-rate check against §2's dynamics). m6-4's tilt optimiser now runs on the real distribution; the M4 engine receives a served G/T with its provenance visible; and the module's central comparison gains its final term — the dish holds its 19.9 dB/K with motors and a keyhole, the panel reaches 10.9 with satellites, silicon, and a bracket.

Two threads hand forward. The platform-dynamics inequality of §6 and the INS/installation interfaces are m6-6's territory, where segment requirements and aviation certification make them contractual. And the handover choreography's network side — what the constellation's scheduler does with the selection freedom §4 exposed — is Module 7's, where this lesson's exports (serving-elevation statistics, switch cadence, interruption budget) become inputs.

Exercise 1 — The loss law and the dynamics

Price pointing error and size the tracker.

import math

def pointing_loss_db(err_deg, bw_deg):
    return 12.0 * (err_deg/bw_deg)**2

print("loss vs error (fraction of beamwidth):")
for f in [0.05, 0.1, 0.16, 0.25, 0.33, 0.5]:
    print(f"  {f:>4.2f} bw: {pointing_loss_db(f,1.0):5.2f} dB")

Re, h = 6371.0, 550.0
v = math.sqrt(398600.4/(Re+h))
def culm_rate_deg_s(e_deg):
    e = math.radians(e_deg)
    d = Re*(math.sqrt(((Re+h)/Re)**2 - math.cos(e)**2) - math.sin(e))
    return math.degrees(v/d)

print("\npeak LOS rate by culmination elevation:")
for e in [90, 60, 40, 30, 25]:
    print(f"  {e:>2}°: {culm_rate_deg_s(e):.2f} °/s")

Exercise 2 — Where the satellites are

Build the elevation distributions and measure what selection buys.

import math, random
Re, h, EMIN = 6371.0, 550.0, 25.0

def elev_to_psi(e_deg):
    e = math.radians(e_deg)
    return math.acos(Re/(Re+h)*math.cos(e)) - e

def sample_elev():
    """One satellite, uniform on the shell within the visibility cap."""
    psi_max = elev_to_psi(EMIN)
    cospsi = 1 - random.random()*(1 - math.cos(psi_max))
    psi = math.acos(cospsi)
    lo, hi = EMIN, 90.0
    for _ in range(40):
        m = (lo+hi)/2
        if elev_to_psi(m) > psi: lo = m
        else: hi = m
    return m

random.seed(7)
def stats(samples):
    s = sorted(samples)
    return s[len(s)//2], sum(s)/len(s)

single = [sample_elev() for _ in range(6000)]
med, mean = stats(single)
print(f"single satellite: median {med:.0f}°, mean {mean:.0f}°, "
      f"P(>60°) {sum(1 for e in single if e>60)/len(single):.1%}")

for K in [2, 4, 8, 16]:
    served = [max(sample_elev() for _ in range(K)) for _ in range(4000)]
    med, mean = stats(served)
    print(f"max-of-{K:>2}:      median {med:.0f}°, mean {mean:.0f}°")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 5: PassModel and PointingBudget

The served G/T, honestly derived, joins the terminal model.

import math, random
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x) if x > 0 else -999.0

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M6 SPINE PROJECT — TERMINAL MODEL
# Stage 5: pass geometry + pointing budget.
#   Stage 4 gave G/T(elevation, tilt) + the mask.
#   Stage 5 supplies the elevation DISTRIBUTION (ergodic and
#   max-of-K serving), the pass-weighted G/T the M4 engine
#   should consume, and the pointing-error budget -> loss.
# Next: m6-6 segment requirements table; m6-7 evaluator.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Re, h = 6371.0, 550.0

class PassModel:
    def __init__(self, emin_deg=25.0, seed=7):
        self.emin = emin_deg
        random.seed(seed)
    def _psi(self, e_deg):
        e = math.radians(e_deg)
        return math.acos(Re/(Re+h)*math.cos(e)) - e
    def sample(self):
        psi_max = self._psi(self.emin)
        cospsi = 1 - random.random()*(1 - math.cos(psi_max))
        psi = math.acos(cospsi)
        lo, hi = self.emin, 90.0
        for _ in range(40):
            m = (lo+hi)/2
            if self._psi(m) > psi: lo = m
            else: hi = m
        return m
    def serving_elevations(self, K=8, n=4000):
        return [max(self.sample() for _ in range(K)) for _ in range(n)]

class PointingBudget:
    def __init__(self, bw_deg, terms_deg):
        """terms_deg: dict of independent error terms, RSS'd."""
        self.bw = bw_deg
        self.terms = terms_deg
    @property
    def total_deg(self):
        return math.sqrt(sum(v*v for v in self.terms.values()))
    @property
    def loss_db(self):
        return 12.0*(self.total_deg/self.bw)**2
    def report(self, name):
        print(f"\n  POINTING BUDGET — {name} (bw {self.bw}°)")
        for k, v in self.terms.items():
            print(f"    {k:<26} {v:>6.3f}°")
        print(f"    {'TOTAL (RSS)':<26} {self.total_deg:>6.3f}°"
              f"  -> loss {self.loss_db:.2f} dB")

def scan_gt(elev, tilt, gt0=11.0, q=1.3, pol_wide=0.6):   # Stage-4 model
    scan = abs(90 - elev - tilt)
    pol = min(pol_wide, pol_wide*max(0, (scan-30)/30))
    return gt0 + 10*q*math.log10(math.cos(math.radians(min(scan, 89.9)))) - pol

pm = PassModel()
print("PASS-WEIGHTED G/T (1,280-el panel, broadside 11.0 dB/K):")
for K, tilt in [(1, 0), (1, 25), (8, 0), (8, 25)]:
    elevs = pm.serving_elevations(K=K)
    gt = sum(scan_gt(e, tilt) for e in elevs)/len(elevs)
    print(f"  K={K:>2}, tilt {tilt:>2}°: served G/T {gt:5.1f} dB/K")

dish = PointingBudget(1.6, {"install calibration": 0.05,
                            "ephemeris+geometry": 0.02,
                            "tracking lag":       0.12,
                            "wind/structure":     0.10})
dish.report("65 cm gimballed dish")

panel = PointingBudget(3.2, {"attitude (IMU, 20 ms)": 0.20,
                             "ephemeris+geometry":    0.02,
                             "phase quantisation":    0.05})
panel.report("ESA on vehicle")

PROMPT 01

Invert the ownership: the constellation operator pays for density; the terminal vendor pockets the decibels (smaller panel for the same service). PROMPT 02

Section 6's inequality — beamwidth tolerance vs attitude latency vs platform dynamics — decided which segments electrified first. Run it forward: gateway-class apertures (very high gain, sub-0.2° beams) on ships are appearing for feeder-diversity concepts. Work the numbers: what attitude latency does a 0.15° beam on a 10°/s-roll platform demand, is that achievable with current INS classes, and if not, which of the three variables gives — beamwidth (smaller aperture), platform (stabilised pedestal returns), or a hybrid? What did your answer just reinvent?

PROMPT 03

Section 7 bounded make-before-break interruption at network timescales but assumed the second beam is free to prepare.
