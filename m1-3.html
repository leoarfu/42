The Physics of Latency

MODULE 1
FOUNDATIONS

Latency is the property that made LEO worth building. The earlier lessons asserted the numbers and pointed here for the reason. This lesson supplies it: where those numbers come from, how to compute them for any orbit, what else besides the orbit adds to them, and why the difference reshapes which applications a satellite system can serve. Latency is also where physics sets a hard floor that no engineering removes — you can add bandwidth, you cannot add speed of light — which is exactly why it is decisive. The routing, handover, and inter-satellite-link mechanisms that change the path are the networking module's subject; here we establish the physics those mechanisms operate within.

1. What Latency Is, and the One Number That Sets the Floor

Latency is the time it takes a bit to travel from where it is created to where it is needed. In a satellite link the largest and most fundamental contributor is propagation delay — the time the signal spends in transit — and that is governed by a single constant: the speed of light, about 299,792 kilometres per second, near enough to 300,000 for working estimates.

That constant is the floor. A signal cannot reach a satellite and return faster than the distance allows, so the moment you choose an altitude you have chosen a minimum latency that no better hardware, no faster modem, and no extra bandwidth can beat. Everything else in the latency budget sits on top of that floor; the orbit sets the floor itself.

THE LINE TO REMEMBER You can add bandwidth. You cannot add speed of light. Latency is the one performance axis fixed by physics rather than engineering.

2. Propagation Delay From First Principles

Propagation delay over a single path is simply distance divided by speed:

t = d / c

For a signal travelling straight up to a satellite at altitude h, the one-hop delay is h divided by c. That single calculation, applied to each regime's altitude, produces the whole latency hierarchy. Straight up to a 550 km satellite takes about 1.8 ms; to an 8,000 km MEO satellite about 27 ms; to GEO at 35,786 km about 119 ms. The hundredfold spread in altitude becomes a hundredfold spread in delay, because the relationship is linear in distance.

~1.8 msOne hop, LEO 550 km
~4.0 msOne hop, LEO 1,200 km
~27 msOne hop, MEO 8,000 km
~119 msOne hop, GEO 35,786 km

3. One-Way Versus Round-Trip, and Counting Hops

Most confusion about satellite latency comes from sloppiness about what is being measured, so be precise. A single hop is ground to satellite, or satellite to ground. A useful link is built from several hops, and the figure that matters to a user is the round trip — the time for a request to reach its destination and a response to come back.

In a simple bent-pipe link, data goes up to the satellite and down to a gateway: two hops one way. The reply retraces the path: two hops back. So a round trip is four hops. That is why GEO's roughly 119 ms one-hop delay becomes roughly 477 ms of round-trip propagation, and why it is quoted at around 600 ms once the rest of the budget is added. The same four-hop counting applied to a 550 km LEO link gives only about 7 ms of round-trip propagation.

~477 msGEO round-trip propagation
~107 msMEO 8,000 km RTT prop
~7 msLEO 550 km RTT prop
~16 msLEO 1,200 km RTT prop
STATE YOUR BASIS Always say whether a latency figure is one-way or round-trip, propagation-only or end-to-end. Without that, the number means nothing.

4. Latency by Regime, Derived

Putting the hop counting together with the altitudes gives the regime hierarchy that the landscape lessons asserted. GEO sits near half a second round trip on propagation alone, which is why it is felt in any back-and-forth use. MEO at 8,000 km lands near 100 ms, low enough for many interactive uses and the reason O3b chose it. LEO at 550 km collapses propagation to single-digit milliseconds, comparable to terrestrial broadband once the rest of the budget is included — and that equivalence is the entire commercial argument for LEO. VLEO trims a little more but the marginal gain over LEO is small, which is why VLEO is chosen for imaging and signal strength rather than latency.

The lesson to internalise is that these are not vendor numbers that might improve with a better product. They are consequences of distance, and they rank exactly as altitude ranks.

5. Slant Range and the Elevation Angle

Altitude is the floor, but the real path is rarely straight up. A satellite low on the horizon is farther away than one overhead, because the line of sight cuts diagonally through a longer slant range. The lower the elevation angle at which a terminal still works, the longer that worst-case path, and the more latency it adds.

For a 550 km satellite, the distance grows from 550 km straight overhead to roughly 1,100–1,500 km near the minimum usable elevation — so the latency to a satellite just above the horizon is roughly double that of one overhead. The effect is real but bounded, and it matters most when you are quoting worst-case rather than best-case latency. It also interacts with coverage: a lower minimum elevation angle widens each satellite's footprint, reducing the satellite count, but lengthens the worst-case path and so the worst-case latency. That is one more face of the coverage-versus-everything-else tradeoff from the regimes lesson.

550 kmSlant, 550 km sat, overhead
~1,100+ kmSlant, 550 km sat, ~25° elevation

6. The Full Latency Budget — Propagation Is Only One Term

The orbit sets the floor, but the latency a user actually experiences is a sum of several terms, and for LEO the non-propagation terms can dominate. The end-to-end budget has five parts:

  • Propagation delay — time in transit, set by path length. Dominant for GEO, small for LEO.
  • Transmission (serialisation) delay — the time to clock a packet onto the link, equal to packet size divided by link rate. Usually small on a high-rate link.
  • Processing delay — time spent in the satellite's payload, the modems, and the gateway equipment demodulating, decoding, and forwarding.
  • Queuing delay — time a packet waits in buffers when a link is congested. Highly variable, and the main cause of latency spikes under load.
  • Terrestrial backhaul — once the gateway recovers the packet, it still has to travel over fibre to wherever the content lives.

The consequence is sharp for LEO. A constellation can win the propagation race in orbit, getting the bits to the ground in single-digit milliseconds, and then give the advantage back if the gateway hauls traffic hundreds of kilometres to a distant peering point, or if the content sits several networks away. The latency you can sell is the end-to-end figure, not the space-segment figure. Where gateways and peering points are placed — the ground-segment decision that owns much of this budget — is covered in the ground-segment module; here the point is that propagation is necessary to get right and far from sufficient.

THE END-TO-END POINT LEO can win in orbit and lose on the ground. A single-digit-millisecond space hop means nothing if the gateway backhauls traffic across a continent before it reaches the content.

7. Why Fibre Is Not Automatically Faster

A natural objection is that terrestrial fibre is the latency benchmark, so why would anyone route through space at all. The answer is that light travels more slowly in glass than in vacuum. The refractive index of fibre is about 1.47, so a signal in fibre moves at roughly 200,000 km/s rather than 300,000 — about a third slower per kilometre. On top of that, fibre routes are not straight: cables follow coastlines, rights of way, and existing infrastructure, adding circuity that can lengthen the real path well beyond the great-circle distance.

A LEO constellation with optical inter-satellite links sends light through vacuum at full speed, along a path much closer to the great circle. Over short distances the up-and-down overhead of reaching orbit makes fibre faster, but as distance grows the fibre penalties — slower medium plus circuity — accumulate until the space path wins. The crossover sits in the low thousands of kilometres, which is why long intercontinental routes are where LEO-with-ISLs can genuinely beat fibre on latency. The routing that realises this is the networking module's subject; the physics that makes it possible is here, and it rests entirely on light being faster in vacuum than in glass.

~200,000 km/sSpeed of light in fibre
~1,000s kmFibre-vs-ISL crossover

8. What Latency Does to Applications

Latency matters because human perception and network protocols both have thresholds, and crossing them changes what is possible rather than merely making things slower.

For real-time human interaction, the guideline figures are well established. One-way voice latency below about 150 ms is comfortable, up to about 400 ms is tolerable, and beyond that conversation breaks down with people talking over each other. Video conferencing is similar. Interactive applications like cloud gaming want round trips below about 50–100 ms before control feels disconnected from action. A GEO round trip near 600 ms sits outside all of these, which is the concrete reason it cannot serve interactive use, while a LEO round trip near 20–50 ms sits comfortably inside them.

The subtler effect is on throughput, through the way TCP works. A connection cannot have more data in flight than its window allows, so its throughput is bounded by the window size divided by the round-trip time — the bandwidth-delay product. On a high-latency link, a single connection is throttled regardless of how much raw bandwidth the link has, which is the technical reason a GEO link can have ample capacity and still feel slow. Worse, opening a connection costs several round trips for the handshake and encryption setup before any data flows, and TCP's slow-start ramps cautiously, so every page load and every new connection pays the latency penalty several times over. This is why latency, not bandwidth, is what users perceive as "responsiveness," and why halving the round trip does more for the felt experience than doubling the capacity. The transport-protocol details are developed in the networking module; the point here is that latency sets a ceiling on throughput that bandwidth cannot lift.

<150 msComfortable one-way voice
~400 msTolerable one-way voice
<50–100 msDesirable gaming RTT

9. Jitter — the Variability That Real-Time Traffic Feels

Latency is the average delay; jitter is its variation, and for real-time traffic jitter can matter as much as the mean. A voice or video stream buffers a little to smooth out variation, but if the delay swings unpredictably the buffer either underruns, causing dropouts, or grows, adding latency.

LEO introduces jitter that GEO does not. Because the satellites move, the path length changes continuously, and every few minutes the terminal hands over to a new satellite, which can change the route through the network and so the delay. Congestion adds queuing jitter on top. A well-designed system keeps jitter low through buffering and careful handover, but it is a property the architect must manage rather than ignore. The handover machinery that causes and controls this is the networking module's subject; the point here is that low average latency is not the whole story — its stability matters too.

10. The Takeaway

Latency is the one performance axis fixed by physics rather than engineering. It is distance divided by the speed of light, counted over the hops of a round trip, and it ranks the orbits exactly as altitude ranks them. That floor is unbeatable, which is precisely why LEO's low orbit is its defining advantage and why no amount of GEO bandwidth can substitute for it.

But two qualifications keep the picture honest. The orbit sets only the floor: processing, queuing, and especially terrestrial backhaul can add latency that gives back LEO's propagation advantage, so the figure that matters is end-to-end, not space-segment. And latency's stability — jitter — matters alongside its average. Carry forward the habit of decomposing any latency claim into its parts and asking which term dominates; for GEO it is propagation, and for LEO it is usually everything else.

PYTHON EXERCISES

Three exercises computing the latency physics directly — propagation by regime, slant range versus elevation, and the fibre-versus-ISL crossover. Python 3.8+ standard library only.

Exercise 1 — Propagation delay calculator

Compute one-hop, one-way (bent-pipe), and round-trip propagation delay for each regime from altitude. This is the core latency calculation.

C = 299_792.0  # km/s

def one_hop_ms(alt):        return alt / C * 1000
def bentpipe_oneway_ms(alt): return 2 * one_hop_ms(alt)   # up + down
def rtt_ms(alt):            return 4 * one_hop_ms(alt)    # request + reply

regimes = [("VLEO 350",350),("LEO 550",550),("LEO 1200",1200),
           ("MEO 8000",8000),("MEO 20200",20200),("GEO",35786)]

print(f"{'Regime':<12}{'1 hop':>10}{'1-way':>10}{'RTT':>10}")
print("-" * 42)
for name, alt in regimes:
    print(f"{name:<12}{one_hop_ms(alt):>8.1f}ms{bentpipe_oneway_ms(alt):>8.1f}ms{rtt_ms(alt):>8.1f}ms")

# queuing, and backhaul (see the budget section).

Exercise 2 — Slant range and latency versus elevation

Show how latency rises as the satellite drops toward the horizon.

import math
C, R_E = 299_792.0, 6371.0

def slant_range_km(alt, elev_deg):
    el = math.radians(elev_deg)
    return (-R_E*math.sin(el)
            + math.sqrt((R_E*math.sin(el))**2 + 2*R_E*alt + alt**2))

def rtt_ms(slant): return 4 * (slant / C) * 1000

alt = 550
print(f"{'Elev':>6}{'Slant':>12}{'RTT (prop)':>14}")
print("-" * 32)
for elev in [90, 60, 40, 25, 10]:
    s = slant_range_km(alt, elev)
    print(f"{elev:>5}deg{s:>10.0f}km{rtt_ms(s):>12.1f}ms")

# 1. How much does RTT grow from overhead (90 deg) to 25 deg?
# 2. Lower minimum elevation widens coverage but raises worst-case
#    latency. Which way would you push it for a gaming service?

Exercise 3 — Fibre versus LEO-ISL crossover

Find the distance beyond which a near-straight vacuum space path beats terrestrial fibre on latency.

C = 299_792.0
C_FIBRE = C / 1.47          # light is slower in glass
CIRCUITY = 1.4             # fibre routes ~40% longer than great circle
LEO_OVERHEAD_KM = 2 * 550  # rough up+down to reach the ISL layer

def fibre_oneway_ms(gc):    return (gc * CIRCUITY) / C_FIBRE * 1000
def leo_isl_oneway_ms(gc):  return (gc + LEO_OVERHEAD_KM) / C * 1000

print(f"{'Distance':>10}{'Fibre':>12}{'LEO-ISL':>12}{'Winner':>10}")
print("-" * 44)
for d in [500, 1500, 3000, 5500, 10000]:
    f, l = fibre_oneway_ms(d), leo_isl_oneway_ms(d)
    print(f"{d:>8}km{f:>10.1f}ms{l:>10.1f}ms{('LEO' if l < f else 'fibre'):>10}")

# 1. Roughly where is the crossover with these assumptions?
# 2. Which assumption is the model most sensitive to? Change one, re-run.
