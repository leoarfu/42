Networking, ISL
& Routing

Module 6 delivered a solved terminal that earns bits from whatever satellite serves it. This module turns the constellation from a set of links into a network. It opens with the architecture decision — bent-pipe relay versus ISL-meshed router — then builds the optical inter-satellite links that make a mesh possible (a 270 dB link budget and a beam that must aim three beamwidths into empty space), the +grid topology and its churn, routing on a graph that reconfigures every few seconds, the SDN control plane that exploits the deterministic ephemeris, and the end-to-end latency the transport layer must survive. The spine is a Python constellation router that traces a message terminal-to-terminal across the scheduled mesh. The finding: the mesh beats fibre on every long-haul route and is the only architecture that can serve an ocean — and those are the same routes.

Part I — The Topology
01

Bent-Pipe vs Mesh: The Architecture Decision

The decision that determines whether a constellation is a network at all. Amplify-and-forward relay versus ISL-meshed router, the ~2,605 km bent-pipe reach that leaves oceans dark, the LEO-vs-fibre latency crossover, and the cost inversion between ground segment and orbital hardware.

✓
02

Optical Inter-Satellite Links: Physics & Pointing

The laser link budget as the Module 4 budget at 270 dB of loss and 106 dBi of gain, closing 10 Gbps at 1 dB margin. Then the pointing problem with no analogue in the program: a 15 µrad beam that must aim ~50 µrad — three beamwidths — into apparent empty space where the receiver will be.

✓
03

The ISL Topology & Its Dynamics

The +grid assembled: two permanent intra-plane links, two inter-plane links that breathe with latitude as the planes converge. The seam where counter-rotating planes tear the mesh (12 km/s, untrackable), the polar thinning, and the churn — deterministic, ephemeris-computable, and about to break every terrestrial protocol.

✓
Part II — Routing the Network
04

Routing Under Churn

Computing a path is trivial; the graph has already changed by the time BGP would converge. Why reactive protocols fail by a category mismatch against continuous deterministic change — and the escape: stop reacting, start scheduling. Snapshot routing, and the reactive floor beneath it for the unscheduled failures.

✓
05

SDN & Segment Routing in Space

The control plane that delivers routes to 1,584 moving satellites. Why the deterministic topology deletes SDN's one weakness, why the control plane is small (3.4 MB, sub-kbps deltas), and segment routing that carries the path in the packet — the make-before-break table switch at network scale.

✓
Part III — The End-to-End Path
06

End-to-End Latency & the Transport Layer

The full latency budget: 54 ms round-trip, nine times better than GEO, faster than fibre long-haul. Then the transport pathology — TCP mistakes handover loss for congestion and mis-sizes its window on every RTT step. QUIC, proxies, and constellation-aware congestion control. Latency is a distribution, not a number.

✓
07

Synthesis: The Routing & Latency Model

The full constellation router assembled: a message traced terminal-to-terminal with its latency distribution, and the bent-pipe/mesh/fibre comparison for representative city-pairs. The capstone table where the coverage column and the latency column turn out to be literally the same column.
