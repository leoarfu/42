Ground Segment
& Operations

The half of the constellation that is not in orbit. The feeder link and the site diversity that keeps it available, the gateway placement and point-of-presence topology that connect it to the internet, and the TT&C, commissioning, and automated fleet operations that fly 1,584 satellites with a small team. The ground segment is roughly half the constellation's capital cost — a co-equal half to the satellites it serves.

Part I — The Ground Network
01

The Gateway & the Feeder Link

The feeder link as the user link's opposite: it aggregates 32 user beams into an 8 GHz trunk, has a capable ground end (a 7.3 m gateway at ~41 dB/K G/T, inverting the user-link intuition), and is engineered rather than deployed. Tens of gigabits of clear-sky capacity — and a concentration point whose failure drops every user on the satellite.

✓
02

Site Diversity & Feeder-Link Availability

Rain at Ka/Q-band needs 23 dB of margin for four-nines and unaffordable margin for five — so availability cannot be bought with margin. Site diversity buys it with geography instead: three independent gateways at 99% each reach 99.9999% jointly, because rain never soaks all of them at once. The ground segment's first principle.

✓
03

Gateway Placement & the PoP Topology

Placement as a four-way conflict — rain climate, backhaul, regulatory access, and mesh egress — resolved into ~50 gateway locations, ~150 physical sites, and ~10 Tbps of fibre. On the ground, a satellite operator is an ISP: it peers and buys transit at points of presence. Plus ground-station-as-a-service and cloud convergence.

✓
Part II — Operating the Constellation
04

TT&C at Scale

The control link that flies the fleet — telemetry, tracking, command — closing easily on an omni antenna for the tumbling and safe-mode cases. The hard part is scale: 1,584 satellites at four contacts a day is one every 14 seconds, needing 117 antennas the traditional way. Autonomy collapses it to ~15. The reactive-vs-scale wall again.

✓
05

Early-Orbit Operations & Commissioning

LEOP — separate, detumble, sun-acquire, phone home — then an 8-day electric orbit-raise to the shell. The scale is industrial: 27 launches over a year, ~129 satellites in commissioning at once, and ~317 replaced every year forever against a 5-year life. A mega-constellation is a river, not a lake.

✓
06

Anomalies, Playbooks & Automated Fleet Operations

52 anomalies a day and 7.9 million telemetry points a second — detection must be automated, and playbooks resolve 95% without a human. But identical satellites share every flaw: a design defect or a wrong playbook replicates across the whole fleet. The systematic failure, not the individual anomaly, is the gravest risk.

✓
Part III — Synthesis
07

Synthesis: The Ground & Operations Model

The whole ground segment assembled, and the number that reframes the program: it is roughly half the constellation's capital cost — ~$1.5 B against ~$1.6 B in orbit. The m7-1 trade closed: the mesh's ground advantage is not fewer gateways but clustering freedom and ocean coverage. The engineering machine is complete; the enterprise is next.
