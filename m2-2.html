Real Orbits: Perturbations & Orbital Elements

MODULE 2
ORBITAL MECHANICS

The previous lesson derived the two-body orbit — a perfect ellipse, frozen in space, repeating forever. No real satellite flies that orbit. The Earth is oblate, the atmosphere thins but persists, and the Moon, Sun, and sunlight all push. This lesson introduces the six Keplerian orbital elements, then works through the dominant perturbations in order of importance for a LEO constellation: J2 oblateness, atmospheric drag, third-body gravity, and solar radiation pressure. It closes with two cases in which perturbations are exploited rather than fought — the sun-synchronous and repeat-ground-track orbits. The spine project propagator gains J2 and drag capability here.

1. The Six Keplerian Orbital Elements

The two-body problem showed that a bound orbit is an ellipse with the Earth at a focus. An ellipse in three-dimensional space has six degrees of freedom: two numbers fix its shape and size, three fix its orientation in space, and one fixes where in the orbit the satellite is at a reference moment. The six Keplerian orbital elements are the standard encoding of these six degrees of freedom, and they are the language in which orbital data, TLE records, and perturbation models are expressed.

The semi-major axis a fixes the size of the ellipse and through it the orbital period and energy. The eccentricity e fixes the shape: zero for a circle, rising toward one for an increasingly elongated ellipse.

The remaining four elements orient the ellipse in space and place the satellite on it. The inclination i is the angle between the orbital plane and the equatorial plane, ranging from 0° (equatorial prograde) through 90° (polar) to 180° (retrograde equatorial). The right ascension of the ascending node Ω (RAAN) is the angle in the equatorial plane from the vernal equinox to the point where the satellite crosses northward; it fixes which way the orbital plane faces relative to inertial space. The argument of perigee ω is the angle in the orbital plane from the ascending node to perigee; it fixes the orientation of the ellipse within its plane. Together i, Ω, and ω fully orient the orbital ellipse in three dimensions.

The sixth element places the satellite on the ellipse at a reference time. The true anomaly ν is the angle from perigee to the satellite's current position measured at the focus. The mean anomaly M is more convenient for propagation: it advances linearly in time and is related to ν through Kepler's equation M = E − e·sin E, where E is the eccentric anomaly. For the near-circular orbits of LEO constellations, ν ≈ M ≈ the mean-motion angle n·(t − t₀), where n = 2π/T.

The TLE (Two-Line Element) format — the standard public encoding of satellite ephemerides published by US Space Surveillance — is a compact representation of these elements plus drag and ballistic terms, updated daily as the real orbit drifts. A constellation operator tracks the TLE set for every satellite and re-uploads updated elements regularly. The coverage and collision-avoidance calculations later in this module all consume orbital elements in this form.

THE SIX-ELEMENT LANGUAGE Every satellite position report, conjunction assessment, and ground-track prediction starts from six numbers: a, e, i, Ω, ω, M at a reference epoch. Perturbations are equations for how each of these drifts over time. The TLE is a snapshot of this state; SGP4 is the algorithm that propagates it forward.

2. J2 — Earth's Oblateness and Its Consequences

The dominant departure from the two-body model is the shape of the Earth. The Earth bulges at the equator and is flattened at the poles, with an equatorial radius about 21 km larger than the polar radius. This equatorial bulge exerts a gravitational force that is not purely central — it has a small component perpendicular to the radial direction — and that component makes the orbital plane precess.

In the gravitational harmonic expansion, the bulge is captured by the second zonal coefficient J2 = 1.08263 × 10⁻³. Higher harmonics exist but J2 is three orders of magnitude larger than J3 and dominates for all practical constellation analysis. Its effect on a circular orbit produces three distinct precession rates.

Node regression is the most important. The RAAN Ω drifts at a rate:

dΩ/dt = −(3/2) · n · J2 · (R⊕/a)² · cos i / (1 − e²)²

For prograde orbits (i < 90°) the node drifts westward (decreasing Ω); for retrograde orbits (i > 90°) it drifts eastward. At 550 km and 53° inclination the node regresses at roughly −6.6°/day. This matters enormously for a constellation: a plane that started pointing in a certain direction will have rotated significantly within weeks. All constellation phasing and coverage calculations must account for this continuous drift.

Apsidal precession is the drift of the argument of perigee ω. For near-circular LEO orbits the eccentricity is small enough that its practical impact is limited, but for elliptical orbits it can push perigee into the atmosphere over years if uncorrected.

The critical inclination of 63.43° is where the apsidal precession rate is exactly zero — the factor (5cos²i − 1) vanishes. This is the foundation of the Molniya orbit, a highly elliptical orbit at 63.4° whose apogee stays permanently over high northern latitudes. Most LEO broadband constellations do not use it, but it explains why 63.4° appears as a recurring value in high-latitude coverage design.

Sun-synchronous orbit exploits node regression rather than fighting it. At a retrograde inclination slightly above 90°, the node drifts eastward at exactly +0.9856°/day — the same rate at which the Earth orbits the Sun. The orbital plane therefore rotates with the Sun, maintaining a constant Sun-angle geometry. Every pass occurs at the same local solar time, which is why Earth-observation and weather satellites use sun-synchronous orbits. At 550 km the required inclination is approximately 97.6°. Sun-synchronous shells are a source of conjunction risk for LEO broadband constellations at nearby altitudes.

3. Atmospheric Drag

At LEO altitudes the atmosphere thins exponentially but does not end. At 550 km the density is on the order of 10⁻¹³ kg/m³ — extremely low, but a satellite moving at 7.6 km/s through even that near-vacuum experiences a persistent decelerating force. The drag force is F = (1/2) · ρ · v² · C_D · A, where ρ is atmospheric density, v orbital speed, C_D the drag coefficient (typically ~2.2 in the free-molecular-flow regime of LEO), and A the cross-sectional area facing the velocity vector.

Drag removes energy from the orbit, causing the semi-major axis to shrink — the orbit decays. The decay rate is governed by the ballistic coefficient β = m/(C_D · A) in kg/m²: a high-β satellite (heavy, compact) decays more slowly; a low-β satellite (light, large solar panels) decays faster. Station altitude is a genuine design trade between decay rate, link budget, latency, and launch cost, and the drag budget is always on the ledger.

Solar activity drives atmospheric density at LEO altitudes significantly. The thermosphere is heated and expands during solar maximum, roughly doubling or tripling density at 500 km relative to solar minimum. A satellite designed for a fifteen-year life must budget drag across a full solar cycle, which introduces an order-of-magnitude uncertainty in density. The February 2022 geomagnetic storm demonstrated this: elevated density at ~210 km caused Starlink to lose approximately 38 satellites that had been deployed into a staging orbit without raising altitude in time.

At end of life, drag becomes a feature. A satellite that exhausts its propellant below about 600 km will re-enter naturally within 25 years — the IADC guideline underpinning most national deorbit regulations. Above 1,200 km, debris persists indefinitely on human timescales. The 550–600 km band therefore sits at the intersection of compliant natural deorbit and acceptable drag budget. Deorbit and debris policy are covered in Module 9.

4. Third-Body Gravity and Solar Radiation Pressure

Two further perturbations are smaller than J2 and drag for most LEO constellations but are relevant at higher altitudes and for precision applications.

Lunar and solar gravity pull on the satellite with forces that vary as it moves relative to the Moon and Sun. For a LEO satellite at 550 km these combined effects are several orders of magnitude smaller than J2. At MEO and GEO, where the satellite is farther from the Earth and less dominated by Earth's own gravity, third-body effects become the dominant perturbation and must be modelled carefully. For LEO constellation design, lunar and solar perturbations can be treated as a slow source of inclination drift over years, with station-keeping margins budgeted accordingly.

Solar radiation pressure (SRP) arises because photons carry momentum. The SRP force is approximately F = P_☉ · A · (1 + ρ_r) / c, where P_☉ ≈ 1,361 W/m² at 1 AU, A is the solar-facing area, and ρ_r the reflectivity. For a satellite with large deployed panels — roughly 10 m² per satellite for a Starlink-generation design — SRP produces forces of tens of micronewtons and alters the semi-major axis and eccentricity slightly each orbit. SRP is more important for high-area-to-mass satellites, which is exactly the profile of mass-produced LEO broadband spacecraft with large deployable panels, and constellation operators include it in high-fidelity propagators and conjunction uncertainty budgets.

PERTURBATION HIERARCHY AT 550 km J2 node regression (~6.6°/day) >> atmospheric drag (continuous energy loss) > solar radiation pressure (small semi-major axis and eccentricity oscillations) > lunar/solar gravity (slow inclination drift). This ordering reverses at GEO.

5. Osculating Elements and the Perturbation Framework

The classical way to handle perturbations is the variation of parameters, or osculating elements. The idea: at any instant the satellite has a true position and velocity, and those map to a unique two-body ellipse — the osculating ellipse — that the satellite would follow if all perturbations were switched off. Perturbations cause the elements of that instantaneous ellipse to change slowly over time. A perturbation model is a set of equations for how fast each element changes due to each force.

The mean elements used in TLEs go a step further: short-period oscillations (one-orbit timescale) and long-period oscillations (many-orbit timescale) are averaged out, leaving a smooth trend. The SGP4 propagator — the standard algorithm for TLE propagation, used by every public satellite catalog — converts mean elements back to osculating elements for position prediction. Constellation operators use higher-fidelity propagators for conjunction screening and manoeuvre planning, but SGP4 is the universal starting point.

This framework is why the two-body foundation is not abandoned when perturbations are introduced. The ellipse remains the baseline; perturbations are overlaid as rates of change on its six parameters. The propagator scaffolded in Exercise 3 of the previous lesson is extended in Exercise 1 here to add J2 node regression, and in Exercise 2 to model drag decay.

6. Repeat-Ground-Track Orbits

A repeat-ground-track orbit traces the same path over the Earth's surface after an integer number of revolutions. The condition is that the satellite completes K revolutions in exactly D solar days, so that the ground track closes: the satellite's nodal precession rate and the Earth's rotation rate must satisfy a commensurability condition, and J2 node regression must be included because it changes the effective rotation rate seen from the precessing plane.

Earth-observation satellites exploit this to guarantee revisiting the same sites on a fixed cadence. LEO broadband constellations do not strictly require it, but a near-repeat design can simplify ground segment planning by making the pass geometry over gateway locations approximately periodic, reducing scheduling complexity for ground station contact windows. The effective Earth rotation rate seen from the precessing plane is (Ω̇_Earth − dΩ/dt), where Ω̇_Earth ≈ 0.004178°/s — which is why the repeat condition depends on inclination as well as altitude.

7. Design Implications: Perturbations as a Budget Line

Every perturbation in this lesson translates directly into a design cost for a constellation.

J2 node regression must be accepted and planned around in constellation geometry. If planes are at the same altitude and inclination, their nodes precess at the same rate and internal geometry is preserved. If planes are at different altitudes, their node rates differ and the phasing between planes evolves continuously — a coverage-uniformity problem. Station-keeping to freeze individual nodes is energetically prohibitive; the standard practice is to design the constellation to be coverage-stable despite nodal drift.

Drag consumes propellant. The mass budget for drag makeup across the satellite's lifetime is a concrete design parameter, typically tens to a few hundred metres per second of Δv depending on altitude, ballistic coefficient, and solar activity phase. This Δv, combined with the deorbit Δv at end of life, sizes the propellant tank and — for electric propulsion — the thruster operating schedule. For an electric propulsion system (Isp ≈ 1,500–3,000 s), fuel mass is small but thrust is low; for cold-gas or monopropellant (Isp ≈ 150–250 s), fuel mass is larger but manoeuvres are faster. Solar activity introduces a risk dimension: a satellite designed for average drag will re-enter early at solar maximum and late at solar minimum. Operators build margin into the propellant budget to cover a worst-case solar cycle.

ABOVE 1,200 km — A HARD DEBRIS BOUNDARY At 1,200 km and above, natural orbital decay takes more than a century. Any satellite that fails before deorbit becomes permanent debris. This is why ITU and most national regulators are tightening requirements for constellations at high LEO altitudes, and why the industry consensus operational band of 550–600 km is not arbitrary.

8. What This Lesson Fixes in the Design Chain

The two-body lesson established that altitude fixes speed, period, and energy. This lesson adds the next layer: altitude and inclination together fix the J2 precession rates, the drag lifetime, the constellation phasing strategy, and the propellant budget. Exercise 1 — J2 Node Regression Rate

Compute the node regression rate for circular orbits and confirm the sun-synchronous condition.

import math

MU = 398600.4418   # km^3/s^2
RE = 6378.137      # km
J2 = 1.08263e-3

def node_rate_deg_per_day(altitude_km, inc_deg):
    """J2 node regression rate in degrees per day (negative = westward)."""
    r = RE + altitude_km
    n = math.sqrt(MU / r**3)          # mean motion, rad/s
    i = math.radians(inc_deg)
    rate_rad_s = -(3/2) * n * J2 * (RE/r)**2 * math.cos(i)
    return math.degrees(rate_rad_s) * 86400   # deg/day

Exercise 2 — Drag Lifetime Estimator

Estimate natural re-entry time for a circular orbit using an exponential atmosphere model. Shows which altitudes comply with the IADC 25-year guideline.

import math

MU = 398600.4418
RE = 6378.137

# Exponential atmosphere fit: 400-800 km range, solar mean
RHO0    = 2.0e-10   # kg/m^3 at 400 km reference
H0      = 400.0     # km
H_SCALE = 70.0      # km scale height

def atm_density(altitude_km):
    return RHO0 * math.exp(-(altitude_km - H0) / H_SCALE)

def decay_rate_km_per_day(altitude_km, beta_kg_m2):
    """Approximate semi-major axis decay rate for a circular orbit (km/day)."""
    r_m   = (RE + altitude_km) * 1e3    # m
    rho   = atm_density(altitude_km)    # kg/m^3
    v_m_s = math.sqrt(MU * 1e9 / r_m)  # m/s
    da_dt = -2 * rho * v_m_s * r_m / beta_kg_m2  # m/s
    return da_dt * 86400 / 1e3          # km/day

def lifetime_years(start_alt, reentry_alt, beta, step_km=1.0):
    h = start_alt; days = 0.0
    while h > reentry_alt:
        rate = decay_rate_km_per_day(h, beta)
        if rate >= 0: return float('inf')
        days += -step_km / rate
        h -= step_km
    return days / 365.25

# Starlink-class: mass ~300 kg, A ~5 m^2, C_D ~2.2 => beta ~ 27 kg/m^2
beta = 300 / (2.2 * 5)
print(f"Ballistic coefficient: {beta:.1f} kg/m^2\n")
print(f"{'Start alt':>12}  {'Lifetime (yr)':>14}  IADC check")
print("-" * 45)
for alt in [400, 500, 550, 600, 700]:
    lt = lifetime_years(alt, 80, beta)
    tag = "COMPLIANT" if lt < 25 else "EXCEEDS 25 yr"
    print(f"{alt:>10} km  {lt:>12.1f}  {tag}")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 2: J2 Ground-Track Propagator

Extend the stage-1 circular propagator with J2 node regression to output Earth-fixed latitude and longitude.

import math

MU            = 398600.4418
RE            = 6378.137
J2            = 1.08263e-3
EARTH_ROT_S   = 7.2921150e-5   # rad/s, Earth sidereal rotation rate

def propagate_j2_ground_track(altitude_km, inc_deg, duration_min, step_s=60):
    """
    Circular orbit with J2 node regression.
    Returns list of (t_s, lat_deg, lon_deg).
    """
    r = RE + altitude_km
    n = math.sqrt(MU / r**3)           # mean motion rad/s
    i = math.radians(inc_deg)
    node_rate = -(3/2) * n * J2 * (RE/r)**2 * math.cos(i)  # rad/s

    track = []; t = 0.0
    while t <= duration_min * 60:
        raan  = node_rate * t           # RAAN at time t (start = 0)
        theta = n * t                   # mean anomaly (near-circular)

        # ECI position
        x_orb = r * math.cos(theta)
        y_orb = r * math.sin(theta)
        x = x_orb*math.cos(raan) - y_orb*math.cos(i)*math.sin(raan)
        y = x_orb*math.sin(raan) + y_orb*math.cos(i)*math.cos(raan)
        z = y_orb * math.sin(i)

        lat = math.degrees(math.asin(z / r))
        gst = EARTH_ROT_S * t          # Earth rotation since t=0
        lon = math.degrees(math.atan2(y, x) - gst)
        lon = (lon + 180) % 360 - 180  # normalise to [-180, 180]
        track.append((t, lat, lon))
        t += step_s
    return track

track = propagate_j2_ground_track(550, 53, duration_min=200, step_s=120)
print(f"Steps: {len(track)}")
for label, idx in [("t=0   ", 0), ("t=96min", 48), ("t=200min", -1)]:
    t, lat, lon = track[idx]
    print(f"{label}  lat={lat:+.2f}  lon={lon:+.2f}")
