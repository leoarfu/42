Kepler & Newton: The Two-Body Foundation

MODULE 2
ORBITAL MECHANICS

Module 1 made the regime choice qualitatively. This module makes it quantitative — and that begins with describing the motion of a single satellite exactly, which is the two-body problem solved by Newton and anticipated by Kepler. The two-body model is an idealisation: one satellite, one spherical Earth, nothing else. Real orbits depart from it, and the next lesson is about those departures. But it is not a toy: it fixes the relationship between altitude, speed, and period that determines revisit, ground-track repeat, Doppler, coverage time, and launch energy. This lesson derives that relationship from first principles and ends with the master equation — vis-viva — that gives a satellite's speed anywhere on any orbit.

1. Why the Two-Body Model Is the Foundation

A satellite in orbit is a projectile that keeps missing the Earth. It is pulled toward the planet by gravity and would fall straight down, but it also moves sideways fast enough that the surface curves away beneath it as quickly as it falls. The orbit is the path on which falling and curving exactly balance, and describing that path quantitatively is the whole of orbital mechanics at its base.

The two-body problem isolates the essential physics: a small satellite and a large central body, interacting only through their mutual gravity, with everything else — other bodies, the Earth's bulge, atmospheric drag, radiation pressure — set aside. Under those assumptions the motion has an exact closed-form solution, which is rare and valuable. It tells us the orbit is a conic section, that bound orbits are ellipses, and that speed, period, and size are locked together by simple laws. Because the solution is exact, it is the reference against which every real-orbit complication is measured, and because the complications are usually small, the two-body answer is most of the answer.

2. Newton's Gravitation and the Standard Gravitational Parameter

Newton's law of universal gravitation states that two masses attract along the line between them with a force proportional to each mass and inversely proportional to the square of their separation: F = G·M·m / r², where G is the universal gravitational constant, M and m the two masses, and r their separation. The inverse-square dependence is the single most important fact in the subject, because it is what makes bound orbits closed ellipses rather than drifting spirals.

For a satellite around the Earth, the satellite's own mass is negligible in setting the field, so the quantity that actually appears in every orbital equation is the product G·M. This product is given its own name and symbol, the standard gravitational parameter μ = G·M, because it is known far more precisely than G or M separately — it is measured directly from the motion of satellites. For Earth, μ ≈ 398,600 km³/s². Carrying μ rather than G and M is not a convenience only; it is how the field is actually known.

3. The Two-Body Problem and the Shape of Orbits

Applying Newton's second law to the inverse-square force and solving the resulting equations of motion yields the central result of the subject: the path of one body around another is a conic section — a circle, ellipse, parabola, or hyperbola — with the central body at a focus. Which conic results depends on the orbit's energy. Negative total energy gives a bound, closed orbit, which is an ellipse (a circle being the special case of zero eccentricity). Zero energy gives a parabola and positive energy a hyperbola, both of which are escape trajectories that never return.

Every satellite and every constellation this program considers is on a bound orbit, so the relevant conic is always the ellipse. The two parameters that fix an ellipse are its semi-major axis a, which sets its size, and its eccentricity e, which sets its shape — e = 0 is a circle, and e approaching 1 is an increasingly elongated ellipse. Most operational communications constellations fly near-circular orbits, because a circular orbit holds constant altitude and therefore constant coverage and link geometry, but the elliptical case is the general one and the equations are written for it.

4. Kepler's Three Laws

Kepler extracted three empirical laws from planetary observations decades before Newton explained them, and they remain the clearest statement of how a two-body orbit behaves.

The first law says the orbit is an ellipse with the central body at one focus. The point of closest approach is the perigee, the farthest point the apogee, and the line joining them through the focus is the major axis, whose half-length is the semi-major axis a.

The second law says the line from the central body to the satellite sweeps equal areas in equal times. This is conservation of angular momentum in disguise, and its practical meaning is that the satellite moves fastest at perigee, where it is closest, and slowest at apogee, where it is farthest. On a circular orbit the speed is therefore constant; on an eccentric one it varies continuously around the orbit.

The third law says the square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis: T² ∝ a³. Newton's mechanics make the proportionality exact, giving T = 2π·√(a³/μ). This is the equation that ties altitude to period, and through it to revisit time and ground-track repeat, and it is one of the two or three most-used results in the whole module.

5. Circular Orbits — Speed and Period From Altitude

For the near-circular orbits most constellations fly, the equations simplify usefully. On a circular orbit the inward gravitational force exactly supplies the centripetal force needed to hold the satellite on its circle, and setting μm/r² equal to m·v²/r and solving gives the circular orbital speed v = √(μ/r), where r is the orbital radius — the Earth's radius plus the altitude. The speed depends only on the radius, not on the satellite's mass, and it falls as the orbit rises: a low satellite must move fast to stay up, a high one moves slowly.

The period follows from the third law with a = r, giving T = 2π·√(r³/μ). Together these two equations produce the altitude-speed-period relationship that underlies every later coverage and revisit calculation. Note one consequence worth carrying: a geostationary satellite is simply the circular orbit whose period equals the Earth's true rotation period, the sidereal day of 86,164 seconds, which works out to a radius of 42,164 km and an altitude of 35,786 km. GEO is not a special kind of orbit; it is the one altitude where the period matches the planet's spin.

6. Orbital Energy and the Vis-Viva Equation

The deepest single result of the two-body problem is that an orbit's total specific energy — its energy per unit mass, kinetic plus potential — depends only on the semi-major axis: ε = −μ / (2a). Larger orbits have higher (less negative) energy; the energy fixes the size and the size fixes the energy. This is why raising an orbit costs energy and is the root of why higher orbits cost more to reach.

Combining the energy relation with the definition of kinetic and potential energy gives the vis-viva equation, the master equation of orbital speed:

v² = μ · ( 2/r − 1/a )

It gives the satellite's speed v at any point where its distance from the centre is r, on any orbit of semi-major axis a. Every speed result in the lesson is a special case of it. On a circular orbit r = a, and it reduces to v = √(μ/r). At perigee and apogee of an ellipse it gives the fastest and slowest speeds. Setting a to infinity — the boundary where the orbit is no longer bound — gives the escape speed v = √(2μ/r), which is exactly √2 times the circular speed at the same radius. Vis-viva is the equation to memorise; nearly everything kinematic in the module can be recovered from it.

THE TWO EQUATIONS TO CARRY Period: T = 2π√(a³/μ). Speed anywhere: v² = μ(2/r − 1/a). Between them they convert the single choice of altitude into the period, speed, and energy that drive coverage, revisit, Doppler, and launch cost.

7. Elliptical Orbits — Perigee, Apogee, and Conserved Quantities

On an elliptical orbit the second law plays out as a continuous trade between speed and distance. The perigee radius is a(1−e) and the apogee radius is a(1+e), and vis-viva gives the speed at each: fastest at perigee, slowest at apogee. The two are linked by conservation of angular momentum, which for the two extreme points takes the simple form v_p · r_p = v_a · r_a — the product of speed and distance is the same at perigee and apogee, so the closer the satellite is, the faster it moves, in exact proportion.

Although most communications constellations are near-circular, the elliptical case matters in practice for transfer orbits, for the highly elliptical orbits used at high latitudes, and for understanding what perturbations do to an orbit's shape over time. The two-body ellipse is also the exact backdrop against which the next lesson's perturbations are defined: a real orbit is described as an ellipse whose elements slowly change, so the ellipse must be understood first.

8. Why the Mechanics Fix the Design Tradeoffs

The equations of this lesson are not abstract; they are the quantitative form of the tradeoffs Module 1 described in words. Altitude sets the period through the third law, and the period sets how often a satellite passes over a given point, which drives revisit and ground-track repeat — the subject of the coverage lesson. Altitude sets the speed through vis-viva, and the speed sets how fast the satellite crosses the sky and how large its Doppler shift is — the subject of the Doppler lesson. Altitude sets the energy through ε = −μ/2a, and the energy sets how much must be spent to reach and maintain the orbit — part of the deployment lesson and, downstream, of the economics. The single choice of altitude propagates through all of these, and it does so through the equations established here.

This is why orbital mechanics is the first link the program deepens after the mental model. The regime tradeoff that Module 1 asked you to hold qualitatively is, underneath, this set of relations between a, r, v, T, and ε. Make them precise and the rest of constellation design becomes a matter of applying them.

9. What the Two-Body Model Leaves Out

Honesty about the idealisation is part of using it. The two-body model assumes a point-mass, perfectly spherical Earth and no force but mutual gravity, and the real situation violates every clause. The Earth is an oblate spheroid, bulging at the equator, and that bulge exerts a non-central force that makes orbits precess — the dominant real-world departure, and the engine behind sun-synchronous and repeat-ground-track orbits. The upper atmosphere, though thin, drags on low satellites and slowly lowers them, which is why low orbits decay and must be reboosted. The Moon and Sun pull on the orbit, and sunlight itself presses on the satellite. Each of these is a perturbation on the two-body ellipse, small over one revolution but accumulating over many.

The next lesson takes up these perturbations and the orbital-element description that makes them tractable. For now the point is that the two-body model is the correct starting point precisely because the perturbations are small: a real orbit is a two-body ellipse with slowly drifting parameters, so the ellipse and its governing equations come first, and the drift comes second.

IDEALISATION, NOT TOY The model is wrong in every clause — spherical Earth, no drag, no third bodies — yet correct as a starting point, because the perturbations are small per revolution. A real orbit is a two-body ellipse whose elements slowly drift. m2-2 takes up the drift.

10. Takeaway

The two-body problem gives the exact skeleton of all orbital motion, and its results are the working tools of the whole module. Gravity follows an inverse-square law, and the quantity that matters is the standard gravitational parameter μ ≈ 398,600 km³/s². Bound orbits are ellipses with the Earth at a focus, described by semi-major axis and eccentricity, and governed by Kepler's three laws: the elliptical shape, the equal-area sweep that makes the satellite fastest at perigee, and the period law T = 2π√(a³/μ). For circular orbits, speed is √(μ/r) and falls with altitude, and GEO is just the orbit whose period is a sidereal day. The energy of an orbit depends only on its semi-major axis, and the vis-viva equation v² = μ(2/r − 1/a) gives the speed anywhere on any orbit, with circular and escape speeds as special cases.

Carry vis-viva and the period law forward as the two equations the rest of the module leans on. They convert the single design choice of altitude into the speed, period, and energy that drive coverage, revisit, Doppler, and launch cost — turning Module 1's qualitative regime tradeoff into the quantitative relations that constellation design actually uses. The next lesson takes the two-body ellipse and lets its parameters drift, which is what real orbits do.

PYTHON EXERCISES — SPINE PROJECT BEGINS

These three exercises begin the module spine project — an orbital propagator and coverage tool that grows across Module 2 and feeds the capstone simulator. This lesson lays the propagator's foundation: speed, period, and a first position-over-time stepper. Python 3.8+ standard library only.

Exercise 1 — Altitude, speed, period table

The circular-orbit relations as a reusable function.

import math

MU = 398600.4418   # km^3/s^2
RE = 6378.137      # km

def circular(altitude_km):
    r = RE + altitude_km
    v = math.sqrt(MU / r)                    # km/s
    T = 2 * math.pi * math.sqrt(r**3 / MU)   # s
    return r, v, T

print(f"{'alt':>7}{'v km/s':>10}{'period':>12}")
for h in [550, 1200, 2000, 8000, 20200, 35786]:
    r, v, T = circular(h)
    print(f"{h:>7}{v:>10.3f}{T/60:>9.1f} min")

Exercise 2 — Vis-viva explorer

Speed anywhere on any orbit, with circular and escape as special cases.

import math
MU = 398600.4418; RE = 6378.137

def vis_viva(r, a):
    return math.sqrt(MU * (2.0/r - 1.0/a))   # km/s

# Elliptical orbit: perigee 550 km, apogee 2000 km altitude
rp = RE + 550
ra = RE + 2000
a  = (rp + ra) / 2
vp = vis_viva(rp, a)
va = vis_viva(ra, a)

print(f"semi-major axis a = {a:.1f} km")
print(f"perigee speed = {vp:.3f} km/s")
print(f"apogee  speed = {va:.3f} km/s")
print(f"ang. momentum check: vp*rp = {vp*rp:.0f}, va*ra = {va*ra:.0f}")

# 2. Set a = r (circular) and check vis_viva matches sqrt(MU/r).

Exercise 3 — Spine stage 1: a two-body propagator

Step a circular orbit's position over time. This is the seed of the coverage tool.

import math
MU = 398600.4418; RE = 6378.137

def propagate_circular(altitude_km, minutes, step_s=60):
    """Return (t, x, y) positions in the orbital plane for a circular orbit."""
    r = RE + altitude_km
    n = math.sqrt(MU / r**3)          # mean motion, rad/s
    out = []
    t = 0
    while t <= minutes * 60:
        theta = n * t                  # angle swept since start
        x = r * math.cos(theta)
        y = r * math.sin(theta)
        out.append((t, x, y))
        t += step_s
    return out

track = propagate_circular(550, minutes=96)   # ~one period
print(f"steps: {len(track)}")
print("first:", tuple(round(c,1) for c in track[0]))
print("last :", tuple(round(c,1) for c in track[-1]))
# After one full period (~95.6 min) the satellite returns near its start.
