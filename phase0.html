PHASE 0: THE BRIEFING

EARTH'S CONNECTIVITY GAPS & THE LEO GRAVEYARD

Before you bend the physics of low-Earth orbit, you must understand why we are going there in the first place. This phase establishes the terrestrial baseline, the economic constraints of the "Last Mile," and the brutal history of early space internet attempts.

0.1 The Anatomy of Terrestrial Networks

The internet is physical. It is a massive nervous system of submarine fiber-optic cables, terrestrial backbones, and Internet Exchange Points (IXPs). However, terrestrial infrastructure suffers from a fatal flaw: The Last Mile Economics.

  • Trenching Costs: Laying fiber costs tens of thousands of dollars per mile. In urban centers, the ROI is immediate. In rural areas, it is an economic impossibility without state subsidies.
  • Cellular Limits: 4G and 5G rely on line-of-sight to cellular towers. Mountains, forests, and sheer distance cause severe signal attenuation.

0.4 Legacy Space (GEO & MEO)

Before LEO, space connectivity was dominated by Geostationary (GEO) satellites. Parked 35,786 km above the equator, a single GEO satellite can cover a third of the Earth. But it comes with a physical tax that cannot be engineered away: the speed of light.

Orbit Type Altitude Typical Latency Primary Use Case
GEO (Geostationary) ~35,786 km ~600 ms Broadcast TV, Legacy Maritime, Rural Web Browsing
MEO (Medium Earth) ~8,000 km ~150 ms GPS, O3b Enterprise Backhaul
LEO (Low Earth) ~550 km ~30 ms Low-Latency Global Broadband, Tactical, Aviation

0.5 The 1990s LEO Graveyard

LEO is not a new idea. In the 1990s, companies like Iridium, Teledesic, and Globalstar attempted to build massive constellations. They almost all ended in spectacular bankruptcy. Why?

They lacked the three modern enablers: Reusable launch vehicles (which dropped launch costs from $10k/kg to <$2k/kg), mass-produced spacecraft, and cheap phased-array antennas for the consumer ground terminals.
