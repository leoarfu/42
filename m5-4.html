MODULE 5
AIR INTERFACE & ACCESS
CORE

The first three lessons of this module priced the physics: symbols per hertz (m5-1), bits per symbol at a given C/N₀ (m5-2), and the cost of sharing a carrier among users (m5-3). This lesson prices the packaging. Every real carrier wraps its payload in framing — synchronisation words, signalling headers, pilot symbols, encapsulation — that exists even for a single user on a dedicated link. Framing is what lets a receiver find frame boundaries, learn each frame's MODCOD before decoding it, and track carrier phase through an entire frame; it is bought with symbols that carry no user data.

This lesson does two things. First, it dissects the framing of the open standard — DVB-S2/S2X — precisely enough to compute its cost, including a reconciliation that matters for the spine project's bookkeeping: part of the framing bill was silently included in the m5-2 spectral efficiencies, and charging it twice would corrupt the engine. Second, it examines the most consequential air-interface decision in modern satcom: why the vertically integrated LEO mega-constellations abandoned the standard entirely, and what an architect gives up in each direction.

The boundary discipline continues: m5-3 charged sharing costs (guards, preambles, contention, grant latency); this lesson charges per-carrier structure (frame headers, pilots, encapsulation, padding). The two multiply in the engine and never overlap.

1. What framing buys

A DVB-S2 receiver tuning to a forward carrier knows nothing: not where frames begin, not what MODCOD each frame uses, not the carrier phase. Framing solves each problem with dedicated symbols:

  • A start-of-frame word — a fixed, known symbol pattern the receiver can detect by correlation, even below decoding threshold.
  • A signalling field announcing each frame's MODCOD and structure — necessarily encoded far more robustly than the payload, because it must be readable before the payload's MODCOD is known, by every terminal in the beam including the ones in deep fades.
  • Pilot symbols — known symbols inserted periodically through the frame so the demodulator can track carrier phase against oscillator phase noise. High-order constellations (m5-1) have small phase distances between points; without pilots, phase drift across a 33,000-symbol frame would destroy them.
  • Encapsulation headers and padding — the adaptation layer that maps variable-length IP packets into fixed-size FEC frames.

None of this is waste. All of it is overhead. The engineering content of this lesson is knowing the exact price and what each item purchases.

2. Anatomy of the DVB-S2 PLFRAME

The DVB-S2 forward-link transmission chain, from packets to symbols:

1. BBFRAME — a baseband frame: an 80-bit BBHEADER plus a data field of encapsulated user packets (and padding if the data field cannot be filled). Its size is chosen so that after FEC encoding the result is exactly one FECFRAME. 2. FECFRAME — the BBFRAME plus BCH and LDPC parity: 64,800 coded bits (normal frame; 16,200 for the short frame). This is the m5-2 object. 3. XFECFRAME — the FECFRAME mapped onto constellation symbols: 64,800 / log₂(M) symbols. QPSK: 32,400 symbols; 16-APSK: 16,200. 4. PLFRAME — the physical-layer frame actually transmitted: a PLHEADER of 90 symbols (26-symbol start-of-frame word + 64 symbols of π/2-BPSK-encoded signalling carrying the MODCOD identity), followed by the XFECFRAME organised in slots of 90 symbols, with an optional 36-symbol pilot block after every 16 slots.

Frames of different MODCODs stream back-to-back on the carrier — a QPSK 1/2 frame for a terminal in rain, then a 16-APSK 3/4 frame for one in clear sky. Each terminal decodes every PLHEADER (robust by design) and demodulates only the frames addressed to it at whatever MODCOD they announce. This structure is the ACM mechanism of m5-2, made physical.

3. The reconciliation: what m5-2 already paid

The spectral efficiencies the spine engine took from Table 13 in m5-2 are not raw code rates. Reconstructing the QPSK 1/2 value from first principles:

η = (K_bch − 80) / 64,800 × log₂(M) × 32,400 / (32,400 + 90)
η = (32,208 − 80) / 64,800 × 2 × 0.99723 = 0.988858 — exactly the Table 13 value.

The same reconstruction reproduces 16-APSK 3/4's 2.966728 exactly. Conclusion, verified to six decimal places: Table 13 efficiencies already include the BBHEADER and the PLHEADER (pilots off). The m5-2 information rates were therefore already net of that structure.

Stage 4's incremental bill is only what Table 13 excludes:

  • Pilot blocks, if enabled (section 4): 2.2–2.4%.
  • Encapsulation (GSE headers) and BBFRAME padding (section 5): 0.7–9% depending on packet sizes.
  • Superframe structure, where used (section 6).

This kind of reconciliation is not pedantry. Overhead accounting errors of a few percent, compounded across a business case's thousands of beams, are the difference between a constellation's advertised and delivered capacity — and the classic failure mode is exactly this one: two teams each charging "framing" against different baselines.

4. Pilots: phase tracking, priced

The optional pilot block is 36 known symbols after every 16 slots (1,440 payload symbols). The count of pilot blocks per frame is ⌊(S−1)/16⌋ for S slots, giving a computed pilot efficiency of:

Modulation Slots/frame Pilot blocks Pilot efficiency
QPSK3602297.62%
8-PSK2401497.73%
16-APSK1801197.63%
32-APSK162897.84%

What the ~2.3% buys: the demodulator re-anchors its carrier phase estimate every 1,440 symbols. Between pilots, phase drifts with the combined phase noise of the satellite converter and terminal oscillator. QPSK's 90° phase distances tolerate substantial drift; 32-APSK's fine amplitude-phase structure does not. In practice pilots are off for low-order MODCODs with good oscillators and on for high-order MODCODs and consumer-grade hardware — a per-carrier configuration choice, and one of the small levers a system engineer actually owns. Note the interlock with m5-1's roll-off discussion: the cheap capacity gains (tight roll-off, pilots off) all assume better hardware; the air interface lets you trade silicon quality against overhead.

5. Encapsulation: GSE and the packet-size economics

IP packets do not arrive in 64,800-bit units. Generic Stream Encapsulation (GSE, ETSI TS 102 606) maps variable-length packets into BBFRAME data fields: each packet gets a GSE header (typically ~10 bytes unfragmented), packets are concatenated to fill the frame, and a packet can be fragmented across frame boundaries to avoid padding. The cost is header-per-packet:

Packet size GSE efficiency
1,500 B (bulk traffic)99.3%
500 B (mixed)98.0%
100 B (VoIP, telemetry, TCP ACKs)90.9%

The pattern from m5-3's burst table repeats one layer up: fixed per-unit taxes make small units expensive. A beam whose traffic is dominated by small packets — an IoT service, a telemetry backhaul — loses ~9% at the encapsulation layer alone, before the access layer's small-burst penalty. The two penalties multiply, which is why small-packet services get purpose-built profiles (short FEC frames, tailored slot sizes) rather than the broadband defaults.

6. DVB-S2X: the standard, extended

DVB-S2X (EN 302 307-2, 2014) is the same architecture with the margins engineered out:

  • A finer MODCOD grid — roughly 112 MODCODs versus S2's 28, shrinking the rung spacing so ACM wastes less achieved C/N₀ as unusable headroom (the m5-2 ladder argument, refined).
  • Sharper roll-offs — 0.15, 0.10, 0.05 (the m5-1 capacity lever).
  • Constellations to 256-APSK and variants optimised separately for linear and nonlinear channels.
  • Very-low-SNR MODCODs — down to about −10 dB Es/N₀, using π/2-BPSK and spreading: the mobility and D2D end of the ladder (m5-6).
  • Channel bonding and wideband carriers (hundreds of Msym/s) for gateway feeders.
  • Superframing (Annex E) — a fixed-length outer frame structure with its own sync and signalling, designed for the things classic S2 never anticipated: beam hopping (a superframe is the natural dwell quantum for a beam that illuminates a cell for milliseconds at a time) and multi-format multiplexing. Superframing costs additional overhead of order one percent and is the standard's answer to the flexible-payload world of Module 3.

The direction of travel is visible: S2X progressively rebuilt, inside the standard, the flexibility that vertically integrated systems built natively.

7. Why the mega-constellations went proprietary

Starlink and Kuiper do not use DVB-S2X on their user links. Published independent signal analyses (Humphreys et al., UT Austin) found the Starlink Ku-band downlink to be an OFDM design — 240 MHz channels, 1,024 subcarriers, ~1.33 ms frames — structurally unlike anything in the DVB family. The reasons a rational architect makes that choice:

Vertical integration removes the standard's whole purpose. DVB exists so that satellites, gateways, chipsets, and terminals from different vendors interoperate. An operator that designs its own satellites, gateways, silicon, and terminals has no interoperability boundary inside its network; the standard's benefit evaporates while its constraints remain.

LEO system functions live below the framing. Beam hopping dwell structure, handover signalling every few minutes, ephemeris-driven timing advance and Doppler pre-compensation (m5-3 §7), phased-array beam scheduling — a purpose-built air interface integrates these natively into the frame structure. The standard retrofits them (superframing) a generation later.

Iteration speed. A proprietary waveform changes when the operator's software does. A standard changes when a committee does. For a constellation launching replacement satellites every five years, waveform agility is a real asset.

OFDM itself. Fine-grained time-frequency scheduling (m5-3's OFDMA logic) and cheap equalisation suit wideband LEO channels and align with the terrestrial silicon ecosystem — the same reason 3GPP NTN (m5-5) arrives at OFDM from the other direction.

The counter-case is equally structural. A standard buys: a multi-vendor equipment market (the entire VSAT industry runs on DVB — an operator serving enterprise, maritime, or government customers with existing terminal fleets cannot ignore it); customer-mandated interoperability (defence and institutional buyers often require standards compliance precisely to avoid vendor lock-in); regulator and partner familiarity; and shared R&D — the S2X MODCOD grid represents decades of accumulated, verified coding work that a proprietary team must reproduce or license. GEO-HTS operators and the ground-segment industry remain firmly DVB for these reasons.

The decision compresses to one question: who owns both ends of the link, and for how long? Own both ends forever — proprietary wins on every technical axis. Sell capacity into an ecosystem of other people's terminals — the standard is not optional.

9. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

The engine's Stage-3 access capacity now becomes net throughput: multiplied by pilot efficiency (if pilots are on) and encapsulation efficiency (a function of the traffic's packet-size mix), with the Table 13 reconciliation guaranteeing nothing is charged twice. For the demonstration beam: 674.3 Mbps information rate → 654.0 Mbps after scheduling (Stage 3) → 634.3 Mbps net with pilots on and 1500-byte traffic — a total information-to-net factor of 0.94. That final number is the honest ancestor of a marketing datasheet's "beam capacity."

Strategically, the lesson delivers the standard-versus-proprietary decision framework — the module's first question that is architectural rather than physical. The next two lessons follow the two branches: m5-5 takes the standards path to its terrestrial-convergence conclusion (3GPP NTN), and m5-6 takes the physics to its hardest case (direct-to-device).

Exercise 1 — The PLFRAME ledger and the Table 13 proof

Compute the framing arithmetic from the standard's constants and prove what Table 13 includes.

# DVB-S2 normal-frame constants (EN 302 307-1)
K_BCH = {"QPSK 1/2": 32208, "QPSK 3/4": 48408, "16APSK 3/4": 48408,
         "16APSK 2/3": 43040, "8PSK 3/4": 48408, "32APSK 3/4": 48408}
BITS_PER_SYM = {"QPSK": 2, "8PSK": 3, "16APSK": 4, "32APSK": 5}
TABLE13 = {"QPSK 1/2": 0.988858, "QPSK 3/4": 1.487473, "8PSK 3/4": 2.228124,
           "16APSK 2/3": 2.637201, "16APSK 3/4": 2.966728, "32APSK 3/4": 3.703295}

def reconstruct_eta(modcod):
    mod = modcod.split()[0]
    b = BITS_PER_SYM[mod]
    xfec = 64800 // b
    return (K_BCH[modcod] - 80) / 64800 * b * xfec / (xfec + 90)

print(f"{'MODCOD':<12}{'reconstructed':>14}{'Table 13':>10}{'match':>7}")
for mc in TABLE13:
    r = reconstruct_eta(mc)
    print(f"{mc:<12}{r:>14.6f}{TABLE13[mc]:>10.6f}{str(abs(r-TABLE13[mc])<1e-4):>7}")

Exercise 2 — Pilots and packets: the two incremental taxes

Sweep the Stage-4 costs that Table 13 does not include.

def pilot_efficiency(bits_per_sym):
    xfec = 64800 // bits_per_sym
    slots = xfec // 90
    n_pilot = (slots - 1) // 16
    return (90 + xfec) / (90 + xfec + 36 * n_pilot)

def gse_efficiency(pkt_bytes, header_bytes=10):
    return pkt_bytes / (pkt_bytes + header_bytes)

print("Pilot efficiency by modulation:")
for mod, b in [("QPSK",2),("8PSK",3),("16APSK",4),("32APSK",5)]:
    print(f"  {mod:>7}: {pilot_efficiency(b)*100:.2f}%")

print("\nGSE efficiency by packet size:")
for pkt in [1500, 1000, 500, 200, 100, 64]:
    print(f"  {pkt:>5} B: {gse_efficiency(pkt)*100:.2f}%")

print("\nCombined Stage-4 factor (pilots on), by traffic mix:")
for name, pkt in [("bulk", 1500), ("mixed", 500), ("IoT/VoIP", 100)]:
    f = pilot_efficiency(4) * gse_efficiency(pkt)
    print(f"  {name:>9} ({pkt} B): {f:.4f}")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 4: the framing model

The engine gains its last per-carrier factor. Net throughput emerges.

import math

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M5 SPINE PROJECT — THROUGHPUT ENGINE
# Stage 4: framing & encapsulation.
#   Stage 1: bandwidth, roll-off -> symbol rate
#   Stage 2: C/N0 -> MODCOD -> information rate
#            (Table 13: BBHEADER + PLHEADER already included)
#   Stage 3: sharing -> access capacity
#   Stage 4: pilots + GSE -> NET throughput
# Next: m5-5 NTN/OFDM branch, m5-6 D2D, m5-7 assembly.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

MODCOD_TABLE = [  # (name, eta bit/sym, EsN0 dB) — EN 302 307-1 Tbl 13
    ("QPSK 1/4", 0.490243, -2.35), ("QPSK 1/2", 0.988858, 1.00),
    ("QPSK 3/4", 1.487473, 4.03),  ("8PSK 3/4", 2.228124, 7.91),
    ("16APSK 2/3", 2.637201, 8.97),("16APSK 3/4", 2.966728, 10.21),
    ("32APSK 3/4", 3.703295, 12.73),("32APSK 9/10", 4.453027, 16.05),
]
BITS_PER_SYM = {"QPSK": 2, "8PSK": 3, "16APSK": 4, "32APSK": 5}

def select_modcod(esn0_db, margin_db=1.5):
    best = None
    for name, eta, thr in MODCOD_TABLE:
        if esn0_db >= thr + margin_db and (best is None or eta > best[1]):
            best = (name, eta, thr)
    return best

def pilot_efficiency(modcod_name):
    mod = [k for k in BITS_PER_SYM if modcod_name.startswith(k)][0]
    xfec = 64800 // BITS_PER_SYM[mod]
    slots = xfec // 90
    return (90 + xfec) / (90 + xfec + 36 * ((slots - 1) // 16))

def gse_efficiency(pkt_bytes, header_bytes=10):
    return pkt_bytes / (pkt_bytes + header_bytes)

class ThroughputEngine:
    """Stages 1–4 chained. Reports every intermediate quantity —
    the honest ledger from raw symbols to net user throughput."""
    def __init__(self, name, bw_hz, alpha, cn0_dbhz,
                 sched_eff=0.97, pilots=True, pkt_bytes=1500):
        self.name = name
        self.rs = bw_hz / (1 + alpha)                       # Stage 1
        self.esn0 = cn0_dbhz - 10 * math.log10(self.rs)
        self.modcod = select_modcod(self.esn0)              # Stage 2
        self.info_rate = self.modcod[1] * self.rs if self.modcod else 0.0
        self.sched_eff = sched_eff                          # Stage 3
        self.pilot_eff = pilot_efficiency(self.modcod[0]) if (pilots and self.modcod) else 1.0
        self.gse_eff = gse_efficiency(pkt_bytes)            # Stage 4

    @property
    def net_throughput(self):
        return self.info_rate * self.sched_eff * self.pilot_eff * self.gse_eff

    def ledger(self):
        raw = self.rs * BITS_PER_SYM[[k for k in BITS_PER_SYM
                                      if self.modcod[0].startswith(k)][0]]
        print(f"\n{'─'*58}")
        print(f"  THROUGHPUT LEDGER — {self.name}")
        print(f"{'─'*58}")
        print(f"  Symbol rate            {self.rs/1e6:>9.1f} Msym/s")
        print(f"  MODCOD                 {self.modcod[0]:>9}  (Es/N0 {self.esn0:.2f} dB)")
        print(f"  RAW bit rate           {raw/1e6:>9.1f} Mbps   (m5-1)")
        print(f"  Information rate       {self.info_rate/1e6:>9.1f} Mbps   (m5-2, incl. BB/PLHEADER)")
        print(f"  x scheduler {self.sched_eff:.2f}       {self.info_rate*self.sched_eff/1e6:>9.1f} Mbps   (m5-3)")
        print(f"  x pilots    {self.pilot_eff:.4f}     {self.info_rate*self.sched_eff*self.pilot_eff/1e6:>9.1f} Mbps   (m5-4)")
        print(f"  x GSE       {self.gse_eff:.4f}     {self.net_throughput/1e6:>9.1f} Mbps   (m5-4)")
        print(f"  NET THROUGHPUT         {self.net_throughput/1e6:>9.1f} Mbps")
        print(f"  raw -> net factor      {self.net_throughput/raw:>9.3f}")
        print(f"{'─'*58}")

# ── The demonstration beam, all four stages ─────────────
eng = ThroughputEngine("Ka forward, user beam", bw_hz=250e6, alpha=0.10,
                       cn0_dbhz=96.0)
eng.ledger()

# ── Traffic-mix sensitivity ─────────────────────────────
print(f"\n{'traffic':>10}{'pkt (B)':>9}{'net (Mbps)':>12}")
for name, pkt in [("bulk",1500),("mixed",500),("small-pkt",100)]:
    e = ThroughputEngine("t", 250e6, 0.10, 96.0, pkt_bytes=pkt)
    print(f"{name:>10}{pkt:>9}{e.net_throughput/1e6:>12.1f}")

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 01

Section 7 compressed the standard-versus-proprietary decision to "who owns both ends of the link, and for how long?" Stress-test the compression: construct the case of an operator that owns both ends today but expects to sell wholesale capacity to MNOs and government users within five years. Which air-interface choice is reversible, what does the migration cost in deployed terminals, and what does this say about the real decision variable (hint: it is not technical superiority)?

PROMPT 02

Pilots cost 2.4% always; phase-noise failures cost frames occasionally. Frame the pilots-on/off decision as an expected-value calculation: what failure rate at 32-APSK justifies the pilot tax, what terminal hardware parameter drives that rate, and who — the air-interface designer, the terminal vendor, or the network operator — actually holds the decision in a standards-based ecosystem versus a vertically integrated one?

PROMPT 03

The lesson showed fixed per-unit taxes at three layers: burst preambles (m5-3), GSE headers, and BBFRAME quantisation. An IoT operator proposes aggregating small packets at the terminal — buffering 100-byte reports for up to 500 ms and sending them as one 1,500-byte unit. Compute what this recovers at each layer, identify what it costs (which m5-3 §5 quantity grows), and decide for which service classes the trade is legitimate.

PROMPT 04

DVB-S2X superframing rebuilt beam-hopping support inside the standard roughly a decade after proprietary systems needed it. Generalise: identify the structural reason standards lag vertically integrated systems on architecture-coupled features, and the structural reason they eventually catch up. Then predict: which capability that today exists only in proprietary LEO air interfaces will be standardised next, and through which body — DVB or 3GPP?
