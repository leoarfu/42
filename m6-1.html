The Terminal
Design Problem

MODULE 6
USER TERMINALS & D2D
FOUNDATION

To everything built so far, a terminal is three numbers. Module 4 consumed its G/T on the downlink and its EIRP on the uplink; Module 5 assumed it could point — continuously, at a satellite crossing the sky in minutes — and, for the NGSO case, re-point to the next satellite without dropping a session. This module designs the hardware behind those numbers: the antennas, amplifiers, and tracking systems that turn a customer's roof, mast, fuselage, or vehicle into a node of the network. A fourth number joins in lesson m6-4 — regulatory off-axis compliance — and the module's spine project, a Python TerminalModel, will end by feeding all of them back into the Module 4 and 5 engines, closing the loop from silicon to service.

This first lesson frames the problem. It establishes what a terminal is as a system (five subsystems, one budget), sets the reflector antenna as the performance benchmark it has been for seventy years, explains why the NGSO era broke the reflector's monopoly and put electronically steered flat panels on a million roofs, and derives the first-order physics of both aperture families — including the two comparisons that define the whole module: a 1,280-element flat panel matches a 65 cm dish's area efficiency at broadside while conceding ~9 dB of G/T to it, and recovers ground on the transmit side through power aggregated across hundreds of small amplifiers.

The frame to hold for seven lessons: terminal engineering trades in three currencies — performance (G/T, EIRP, availability), cost (hardware, installation, certification), and form factor (size, height, conformality, power draw) — and every market segment weights them differently. The dish is the performance-per-euro champion; the flat panel exists because two segments (NGSO consumer and anything that moves) price the other currencies higher.

1. The terminal as the chain sees it

The upstream modules have already priced three terminal classes without ever opening one:

Each row is an interface contract: given these numbers and the geometry, the M4 engine produces C/(N₀+I₀) and the M5 engine produces net throughput. What the contract hides is that each number is itself a budget. G/T is an antenna gain minus a noise-temperature chain (m4-2's Friis cascade, now with hardware attached). EIRP is amplifier watts through antenna gain, minus everything lossy in between. Pointing is an acquisition-and-tracking control problem whose failure shows up in m4-1's pointing-loss line. This module opens each budget.

2. Anatomy of a terminal

Five subsystems, whatever the segment:

The aperture — the antenna proper: a parabolic reflector with a feed, or a planar array of hundreds to thousands of radiating elements. Sets the gain, the beamwidth, and most of the form factor. Lessons m6-1 through m6-4 live here.

The RF chain — between aperture and modem. In a dish terminal: a low-noise block (LNB) on receive and a block upconverter (BUC) with a power amplifier on transmit, both at the feed. In a phased array: the chain shatters into per-element or per-subarray pieces — low-noise amplifiers, phase shifters, power amplifiers — integrated into beamformer ICs behind the aperture. Where the first LNA sits, and what losses precede it, decides the noise temperature (section 6).

The modem — Module 5 in silicon: demodulation, LDPC decoding, the MODCOD and access machinery, the network interface. This module treats it as done.

Pointing and tracking — from a one-time manual peak-up on a GEO dish, to a two-axis gimbal, to fully electronic steering with no moving parts, plus the control loops and (on mobile platforms) the inertial aiding that keep the beam on a moving satellite from a moving vehicle. Lesson m6-5.

Power and environment — the DC budget (a consumer ESA draws on the order of 100 W — a real constraint indoors, a defining one on aircraft), thermal design, and the enclosure or radome that lets all of it survive weather, vibration, and lightning zones. The certification face of this subsystem is lesson m6-6.

3. The reflector benchmark

A parabolic reflector converts physical area into gain at an aperture efficiency η of 55–70% — feed illumination taper, spillover, blockage, and surface accuracy each shaving a few percent:

G = η · (πD/λ)² Beamwidth ≈ 70·λ/D degrees

At 20 GHz (λ = 15 mm), verified:

Diameter Gain (η = 0.65) Beamwidth
45 cm37.6 dBi2.3°
65 cm40.8 dBi1.6°
75 cm42.1 dBi1.4°

Note the calibration against the program's own history: the m4-1 terminal's 37.5 dBi is a 45 cm-class dish at honest efficiency, not a 65 cm one — a label this module's arithmetic corrects. The reflector's virtues are structural: one feed, one LNB, one PA, passive metal doing the gain work for the cost of stamped aluminium. Per decibel of G/T, nothing beats it, and nothing in this module will. Its vices are equally structural: the gain points where the metal points, so tracking means moving the metal — motors, gimbals, radomes sized for the swept volume, and a mechanical reliability budget.

4. Why NGSO broke the dish's monopoly

A GEO dish is installed once and pointed forever. A LEO terminal tracks a satellite that crosses the visible sky in minutes, then must acquire the next one — and m5-3's session continuity demands make-before-break: the terminal must be receiving on the new link before releasing the old. A mechanical gimbal can track one satellite beautifully; it cannot be in two places during the handover instant, and at consumer price and reliability points, a motorised two-axis positioner cycling every few minutes for years is a warranty catastrophe in waiting.

The electronically steered array (ESA) answers both problems at once: beam repointing in microseconds by rewriting phase settings, no wear mechanism, and — with digital or multi-beam architectures (m6-3) — two simultaneous beams for seamless handover. That is why the NGSO consumer era arrived on flat panels: not because the panel outperforms the dish (section 6 shows it does not), but because the dish's one weakness — mechanical pointing — sits exactly on the NGSO era's critical requirement. Mobility markets (maritime, aero, land) reached the same conclusion a step earlier for the same reason: the platform moves even when the satellite doesn't.

5. The flat panel: gain from count

A planar phased array builds gain from element count. With elements of gain G_e (≈ 5 dBi for a λ/2-spaced patch) and front-end losses L (taper, feed network, radome — ≈ 1.5 dB as a first-order allowance):

G_array ≈ 10·log₁₀(N) + G_e − L
Elements N Gain Physical aperture (λ/2 lattice, 20 GHz)
1,02433.6 dBi576 cm² (24 cm square)
1,28034.6 dBi720 cm² (27 cm square)
2,50037.5 dBi1,406 cm² (38 cm square)
5,00040.5 dBi2,812 cm² (53 cm square)

Two readings. First, the panel is area-efficient at broadside: 2,500 elements match the 45 cm dish's 37.5 dBi from a comparable footprint, because a well-fed lattice approaches unity aperture efficiency where a reflector loses 35% to feed physics. (Public teardowns of first-generation consumer LEO panels report on the order of 1,300 elements — consistent, per the table, with a mid-30s dBi broadside gain.) Second, the honest caveat that lesson m6-4 will quantify: every number in this table is a broadside number. Steer the beam 60° off boresight and the projected aperture, the element pattern, and the lattice all conspire to take several dB back. The dish never pays that tax; it moves instead. The module's central trade is exactly here.

6. G/T at the hardware level

m4-2's Friis cascade, now with named hardware. The noise temperature is decided by what sits in front of the first LNA and how good that LNA is:

Dish chain: antenna (T_ant ≈ 30 K, sky-pointing) → 0.3 dB of feed/OMT loss → LNB with 1.0 dB noise figure. T_sys ≈ 122 K. With the 65 cm dish's 40.8 dBi: G/T ≈ +19.9 dB/K.

Array chain: antenna (30 K) → ≈ 0.5 dB of pre-LNA loss (element, transition, T/R switch) → per-element LNA at ≈ 2.0 dB noise figure, set by silicon beamformer ICs running warm behind a sun-loaded panel. T_sys ≈ 228 K. With the 1,280-element panel's 34.6 dBi: G/T ≈ +11.0 dB/K.

The ~9 dB gap decomposes into ~6 dB of gain (element count versus a bigger dish) and ~3 dB of noise (integrated silicon versus a discrete GaAs LNB). One array subtlety deserves respect: because every element has its own LNA, all the combining losses downstream — the entire beamforming network — come after low-noise gain and barely touch T_sys. This is why arrays are viable at all: a passive array combining 1,280 elements before a single LNA would drown in feed-network loss. The per-element LNA is the architectural move that makes the flat panel's noise merely worse, rather than fatal — and its noise figure is the single most valuable dB in the panel's bill of materials.

7. EIRP at the hardware level

Dish: one power amplifier (the BUC) into one high-gain aperture. 2 W through the 45 cm dish's 41.1 dBi transmit gain (30 GHz): EIRP ≈ 44.1 dBW.

Array: N small PAs, one per element, combining coherently in space:

EIRP = 20·log₁₀(N) + G_e + 10·log₁₀(P_e)

The 20·log₁₀(N) is the array's transmit magic — power and gain each scale with N, so EIRP scales with N². For N = 1,280 at a modest P_e = 0.8 mW: EIRP ≈ 36.2 dBW — within 8 dB of the dish from milliwatt-class amplifiers that ship inside beamformer ICs. Raising P_e is the panel's EIRP lever, paid in DC power and thermal design (the §2 power subsystem, and a preview of m6-3's architecture economics).

Distributed transmit brings a reliability dividend the dish cannot match: element failures degrade, never kill. Ten percent of elements dead costs −0.92 dB of EIRP and −0.46 dB of receive gain — the beam broadens slightly, sidelobes rise slightly, service continues. A dish's single BUC is a single point of failure. For a consumer product with no service technician, and for an aircraft terminal dispatched on a maintenance schedule (m6-6), graceful degradation is worth real money.

10. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

The terminal is no longer a table row. Its three numbers are now open budgets — gain from aperture physics, temperature from a named chain, EIRP from an amplifier architecture — and the module's central trade is on the table with first numbers attached: the dish wins ~9 dB of G/T; the panel wins pointing, handover, reliability, and form factor, and claws back transmit performance through N² aggregation. The spine project begins as an ApertureModel that computes both families' budgets from their physical parameters and hands (G/T, EIRP) to the Module 4 engine.

What this lesson deliberately deferred: the array's gain law was quoted at broadside. m6-2 derives it honestly — element spacing, the array factor, grating lobes, taper — and m6-4 presents the scan-loss bill. The 9 dB gap measured here is the gap at the panel's best angle.

Exercise 1 — Two gain laws

Compute both aperture families from first principles and reproduce every table in the lesson.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x)

def dish_gain(D_m, f_ghz, eta=0.65):
    lam = 0.3/f_ghz
    return db(eta*(math.pi*D_m/lam)**2), 70*lam/D_m   # dBi, beamwidth deg

def array_gain(N, Ge_dbi=5.0, loss_db=1.5):
    return db(N) + Ge_dbi - loss_db

def array_area(N, f_ghz):
    lam = 0.3/f_ghz
    return N*(lam/2)**2                                # m2, lambda/2 lattice

print("Dishes @ 20 GHz:")
for D in [0.45, 0.65, 0.75]:
    g, bw = dish_gain(D, 20)
    print(f"  {D*100:.0f} cm: {g:.1f} dBi, {bw:.2f} deg")

print("Arrays @ 20 GHz:")
for N in [1024, 1280, 2500, 5000]:
    A = array_area(N, 20)
    print(f"  N={N:>4}: {array_gain(N):.1f} dBi, {A*1e4:.0f} cm2"
          f" ({math.sqrt(A)*100:.0f} cm square)")

Exercise 2 — The two noise chains

m4-2's Friis cascade with hardware names attached: reproduce the 9 dB decomposition.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x)
T0 = 290.0

def chain_temp(t_ant, pre_lna_loss_db, lna_nf_db):
    """T_sys referenced to the aperture: antenna through loss, then LNA.
    Post-LNA contributions neglected (that is the LNA's whole job)."""
    L = 10**(pre_lna_loss_db/10)
    return t_ant/L + T0*(1 - 1/L) + T0*(10**(lna_nf_db/10) - 1)

dish_T  = chain_temp(30, 0.3, 1.0)     # feed/OMT loss, discrete LNB
array_T = chain_temp(30, 0.5, 2.0)     # element/switch loss, SiGe BFIC

G_dish, _ = 40.8, None
G_arr = 34.6
print(f"dish : T_sys {dish_T:5.0f} K -> G/T {G_dish - db(dish_T):+.1f} dB/K")
print(f"array: T_sys {array_T:5.0f} K -> G/T {G_arr - db(array_T):+.1f} dB/K")
print(f"gap  : {(G_dish-db(dish_T)) - (G_arr-db(array_T)):.1f} dB "
      f"= {G_dish-G_arr:.1f} gain + {db(array_T/dish_T):.1f} noise")

Exercise 3 — Spine seed: ApertureModel

The M6 spine begins: both families as objects, handing (G/T, EIRP) to the Module 4 engine.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x)
T0 = 290.0

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M6 SPINE PROJECT — TERMINAL MODEL
# Stage 1 (here): ApertureModel — dish and array budgets:
#                 gain, beamwidth, T_sys, G/T, EIRP.
# Growth path:
#   m6-2  honest array factor (lattice, grating lobes, taper)
#   m6-3  beamforming architecture: power & cost vs N
#   m6-4  scan loss + regulatory off-axis mask
#   m6-5  pointing/tracking budget & handover
#   m6-6  terminal-class parameter table by segment
#   m6-7  terminal evaluator -> M4 LinkBudget -> M5 engine
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class Dish:
    def __init__(self, name, D_m, f_rx_ghz, f_tx_ghz, eta=0.65,
                 t_ant=30, feed_loss_db=0.3, lnb_nf_db=1.0,
                 pa_w=2.0, tx_loss_db=0.5):
        self.name = name
        lam_rx, lam_tx = 0.3/f_rx_ghz, 0.3/f_tx_ghz
        self.g_rx = db(eta*(math.pi*D_m/lam_rx)**2)
        self.g_tx = db(eta*(math.pi*D_m/lam_tx)**2)
        L = 10**(feed_loss_db/10)
        self.t_sys = t_ant/L + T0*(1-1/L) + T0*(10**(lnb_nf_db/10)-1)
        self.gt   = self.g_rx - db(self.t_sys)
        self.eirp = db(pa_w) - tx_loss_db + self.g_tx
        self.beamwidth = 70*lam_rx/D_m

class Array:
    def __init__(self, name, N, f_rx_ghz, f_tx_ghz, Ge=5.0, loss_db=1.5,
                 t_ant=30, pre_lna_db=0.5, lna_nf_db=2.0, pe_w=0.8e-3):
        self.name = name
        self.N = N
        self.g_rx = db(N) + Ge - loss_db
        self.g_tx = self.g_rx                       # same lattice; m6-2 refines
        L = 10**(pre_lna_db/10)
        self.t_sys = t_ant/L + T0*(1-1/L) + T0*(10**(lna_nf_db/10)-1)
        self.gt   = self.g_rx - db(self.t_sys)
        self.eirp = 20*db(N)/10 + Ge + db(pe_w)     # 20log10(N)+Ge+10log10(Pe)
        lam = 0.3/f_rx_ghz
        self.area_m2 = N*(lam/2)**2

    def degrade(self, fail_frac):
        self.eirp += 20*math.log10(1-fail_frac)
        self.g_rx += db(1-fail_frac)
        self.gt    = self.g_rx - db(self.t_sys)

def report(t):
    print(f"\n{'─'*54}")
    print(f"  APERTURE — {t.name}")
    print(f"{'─'*54}")
    print(f"  Rx gain        {t.g_rx:>8.1f} dBi")
    print(f"  T_sys          {t.t_sys:>8.0f} K")
    print(f"  G/T            {t.gt:>+8.1f} dB/K")
    print(f"  EIRP           {t.eirp:>8.1f} dBW")
    if hasattr(t, 'beamwidth'): print(f"  Beamwidth      {t.beamwidth:>8.2f} deg")
    if hasattr(t, 'area_m2'):   print(f"  Panel area     {t.area_m2*1e4:>8.0f} cm2")
    print(f"{'─'*54}")

dish  = Dish("65 cm Ka dish", 0.65, 20, 30)
panel = Array("1,280-element Ka panel", 1280, 20, 30)
report(dish); report(panel)

print(f"\nG/T gap dish-panel: {dish.gt - panel.gt:.1f} dB")
panel.degrade(0.10)
print(f"panel after 10% element failure: G/T {panel.gt:+.1f} dB/K, "
      f"EIRP {panel.eirp:.1f} dBW")

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 01

Section 4 argued that the array won the NGSO consumer market because the dish's mechanical weakness landed on the era's critical requirement. Test the argument's edges: GEO consumer broadband still ships dishes, and some NGSO systems have shipped gimballed dishes to enterprise sites. Identify the exact parameter — satellites in view, handover interval, price point, service SLA — whose value flips the answer, and state where that parameter would have to move for the dish to return to the NGSO consumer market.

PROMPT 02

Section 6 called the per-element LNA's noise figure "the single most valuable dB in the panel's bill of materials," and exercise 2 priced its temperature sensitivity. Take the manufacturer's seat: you can spend one incremental euro per terminal on (a) a lower-NF LNA process, (b) 10% more elements, or (c) a better radome. Rank the three for a consumer LEO panel using this lesson's scaling laws, state which ranking changes for an aero terminal, and identify what information from m6-4 you are still missing to make the call properly.

PROMPT 03

The N² EIRP law means transmit performance is the flat panel's strong suit while G/T is its weakness — yet m4-7 and m5-7 showed the return link (where the terminal transmits) is the capacity-starved direction. Reconcile: does the panel's EIRP advantage land exactly where the system needs it, or does the §6 G/T deficit on the forward link dominate the user experience anyway? Use the m5-7 asymmetry numbers (727 vs 283 Mbps) to commit to an answer.

PROMPT 04

This lesson corrected the program's own m4-1 label (37.5 dBi ↔ 45 cm, not 65 cm) — a 3 dB bookkeeping error that survived four modules because nothing downstream consumed the diameter, only the gain. Generalise the incident: in a real program, which terminal parameters are load-bearing interfaces (consumed downstream, errors propagate) and which are decorative labels (errors dormant)? Propose the one-line rule a requirements database should enforce so that dormant errors cannot later become load-bearing when someone finally consumes the label.
