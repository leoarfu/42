The ISL Topology
& Its Dynamics

MODULE 7
NETWORKING, ISL & ROUTING
CORE

m7-2 gave every candidate link a budget and a point-ahead angle; this lesson assembles the links into a mesh and sets it in motion. The standard pattern is the +grid: each satellite carries four optical terminals — two pointing fore and aft along its own orbital plane, two pointing left and right to the neighbouring planes — weaving the constellation into a grid wrapped around the Earth. The intra-plane links are permanent and steady; the inter-plane links are the interesting ones, because they change: their range shrinks as the orbital planes converge toward the poles, and at one longitude — the seam, where ascending satellites pass counter-rotating descending ones — they break entirely, defeated not by distance but by a tracking rate no steering mirror can follow. The result is a topology that is regular, predictable, and constantly reconfiguring: the graph the router of m7-4 must traverse is never the same graph for long.

The lesson's verified anchors, from a representative dense shell (1,584 satellites, 72 planes of 22, 53° inclination): intra-plane neighbours sit a fixed ~1,970 km apart; inter-plane neighbours in adjacent co-rotating planes sit a gentle ~600 km apart at the equator and converge toward the poles; and at the seam the relative velocity reaches ~12 km/s, sweeping the pointing direction through a 15 µrad beam's width every ~1.3 milliseconds — impossible to track, so the seam is a permanent tear in the mesh. The spine gains the time-varying graph built directly from the Module 2 propagator, and with it the property that will break every terrestrial routing protocol in m7-4: churn.

1. The +grid pattern

The canonical LEO mesh gives each satellite four ISL terminals in a plus-sign arrangement:

  • Two intra-plane links — to the satellite ahead and the satellite behind in the same orbital plane. These neighbours maintain a fixed spacing forever (they share an orbit), so these links are permanent, steady, and easy: constant range, gentle pointing.
  • Two inter-plane links — to the nearest satellite in the plane to the left and the plane to the right. These neighbours are in different orbits, so their relative geometry changes continuously as both satellites move.

Woven across the whole constellation, this makes a grid: intra-plane links are the "vertical" threads running around each orbital ring, inter-plane links the "horizontal" threads tying adjacent rings together. A packet can travel along an orbit (intra-plane) or hop sideways across orbits (inter-plane), and the router's job (m7-4) is to choose the sequence. Four terminals is the standard because it builds a connected grid with a manageable terminal count; more terminals (linking to further planes, or diagonally) buy routing flexibility at linear hardware cost, and some designs use them.

2. Intra-plane: the steady threads

Satellites in one plane are evenly spaced around the orbit. For the representative shell, 22 satellites per plane sit 360/22 ≈ 16.4° apart, a fixed chord of:

range = 2·(R_e + h)·sin(θ/2) = 2·6,921·sin(8.2°) ≈ 1,970 km

This range never changes — the satellites share an orbit and maintain their phasing — so the intra-plane link is the mesh's reliable backbone: constant 1,970 km, constant ~262 dB FSPL (m7-2), a steady high-rate link that the router can always count on. Its pointing is gentle: the two satellites move together, their relative velocity nearly zero along the link, so the point-ahead angle is small and the tracking rate is low. Intra-plane links are, in the mesh, the thing that is always there. A packet can run "up" or "down" an orbital plane at full rate through as many satellites as it likes, and the only cost is propagation and per-hop processing.

3. Inter-plane: the links that breathe

The sideways threads are where the topology comes alive. Adjacent orbital planes are separated by their ascending-node spacing (360/72 = 5° for the representative shell), and the range between a satellite and its cross-plane neighbour depends on latitude, because the planes converge toward the poles. At the equator the planes are maximally spread; the verified inter-plane range there is ~600 km. As satellites climb toward their maximum latitude (= the inclination, 53°), the planes crowd together and the inter-plane range shrinks — to ~370 km at 52° latitude in the model:

Latitude Inter-plane range vs equator
0° (equator)~600 km×1.00
20°~570 km×0.94
40°~460 km×0.77
52° (near max)~370 km×0.62

The link therefore breathes over each orbit: the range oscillates, the FSPL with it, and — per m7-2's rate-vs-range law — the link's capacity rises as the neighbour draws near and falls as it recedes. The router sees inter-plane edges whose weights change continuously, and a path that was cheapest a minute ago may not be now. This breathing is smooth and predictable (it follows the deterministic ephemeris), which is exactly what m7-5's precomputation will exploit — but it is motion, and motion is churn.

4. The seam: where the mesh tears

The +grid has a defect that no amount of pointing skill can fix. In a Walker constellation the planes are all inclined the same way, so at most latitudes adjacent planes co-rotate — their satellites drift past each other slowly, and the inter-plane link tracks easily (relative velocity ~660 m/s in the model, gentle). But there is one longitude band — the seam — where an ascending satellite (climbing north) passes a satellite in the neighbouring plane that is descending (falling south). These two are counter-rotating, and their relative velocity is enormous:

v_seam ≈ 2·v·sin(i) = 2·7,589·sin(53°) ≈ 12,100 m/s

At that closing rate, the direction from one satellite to the other sweeps through space so fast that a 15 µrad optical beam would cross its own width every ~1.3 milliseconds. No steering mirror, no ATP loop (m7-2 §5) can track a target moving that fast across the sky — the point-ahead angle also balloons (to ~80 µrad, five beamwidths), but it is the sweep rate, not the offset magnitude, that is fatal. So the inter-plane links across the seam are simply not made. The mesh has a permanent tear running pole to pole along the counter-rotating boundary, and traffic that needs to cross it must detour — over a pole, where the seam closes (the planes all converge and the ascending/descending distinction collapses), or the long way around. The seam is a fixed feature of the routing problem, and m7-4's paths must respect it.

5. The polar region: convergence and shutdown

Near the poles the geometry does two opposing things. The inter-plane ranges shrink to their minimum (§3) — the planes crowd together — which would make inter-plane links trivially easy. But the planes crowd so tightly that the cross-plane pointing geometry becomes degenerate: a satellite's left and right neighbours are nearly on top of each other and swapping sides, the relative bearings swing wildly, and above some latitude the inter-plane links are typically shut off rather than fought for. The intra-plane links continue unbothered (they don't care about latitude), so the mesh near the poles thins to mostly the orbital rings with sparse cross-links — a region of reduced connectivity that, combined with the seam's closure there, makes polar routing its own regime. For a constellation whose selling point includes polar coverage (m7-1), the thinning of the mesh exactly where the planes converge is a genuine constraint the routing layer inherits.

6. Churn: the topology as a function of time

Assemble the pieces and the mesh is in constant reconfiguration. Intra-plane links are permanent; inter-plane links breathe in range, hand off to new neighbours as satellites move, shut down near the poles, and never form across the seam. A satellite crosses the polar reconfiguration region twice per ~95-minute orbit, so from any one satellite's view the inter-plane topology is rearranging on a timescale of tens of minutes — and across the whole constellation, with thousands of satellites all moving, some link is changing state at essentially every instant. This is churn: the graph the router traverses is time-varying, and its rate of change is set by the orbital mechanics, not by traffic or failures.

The crucial property — the one that separates this problem from the terrestrial internet — is that the churn is deterministic. Every link's state at every future instant is computable from the ephemeris (Module 2): the router knows, minutes or hours ahead, exactly when each inter-plane link will hand off, when it will cross the seam, when it will enter the polar shutdown. The topology is time-varying but not uncertain. Terrestrial networks discover their topology by probing and react to changes after they happen (m7-4's failing protocols); a constellation can precompute its entire future topology and route against it (m7-5's SDN). Churn is the problem; determinism is the escape.

8. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

Stage 3 of the spine builds the time-varying graph: from the Module 2 propagator's satellite positions at any instant, it constructs the +grid — intra-plane edges (permanent, weighted by the fixed 1,970 km link), inter-plane edges (present where the range closes and the geometry is trackable, weighted by the breathing range and m7-2's rate-vs-range law), the seam left un-linked, the polar region thinned. Advance the clock and the graph reconfigures; the whole future sequence is computable. The router of m7-4 receives not a graph but a movie of graphs — and the fact that it is a movie with a known script is the single most important fact about routing a constellation.

What this lesson leaves for m7-4 is the routing itself: given this churning-but-deterministic graph, find good paths, and do it in a way that survives the reconfiguration. Terrestrial shortest-path protocols assume a graph that holds still long enough to converge; this graph does not. The escape — precomputing paths from the known future topology — is m7-5's SDN, but m7-4 must first show why the terrestrial approach fails, and the answer is everything this lesson just built: a graph that changes deterministically every few seconds.

Exercise 1 — The +grid ranges

Build the intra- and inter-plane link geometry from Walker parameters.

import math
Re, h = 6371.0, 550.0
Ra = Re + h

def intra_plane_range(sats_per_plane):
    sep = math.radians(360.0/sats_per_plane)
    return 2*Ra*math.sin(sep/2)

def inter_plane_range_equator(num_planes):
    sep = math.radians(360.0/num_planes)
    return 2*Ra*math.sin(sep/2)

def inter_plane_range_at_lat(num_planes, lat_deg):
    # planes converge ~ cos(lat) toward the poles (first-order model)
    return inter_plane_range_equator(num_planes)*math.cos(math.radians(lat_deg))

P, S = 72, 22
print(f"intra-plane range ({S}/plane): {intra_plane_range(S):.0f} km (FIXED)")
print(f"inter-plane range at equator ({P} planes): {inter_plane_range_equator(P):.0f} km")
print("\ninter-plane range vs latitude:")
for lat in [0, 20, 40, 52]:
    print(f"  {lat:>2}°: {inter_plane_range_at_lat(P, lat):.0f} km")

Exercise 2 — The seam and the tracking rate

Show why the seam tears and the poles thin.

import math
Re, h, C = 6371.0, 550.0, 299792458.0
v = math.sqrt(398600.4/(Re+h))*1000   # m/s
BEAM_URAD = 15.5

def seam_rel_velocity(inc_deg):
    return 2*v*math.sin(math.radians(inc_deg))

def sweep_time_ms(rel_v_ms, range_km, beam_urad=BEAM_URAD):
    rate_urad_s = rel_v_ms/(range_km*1e3)*1e6      # angular rate
    return beam_urad/rate_urad_s*1000               # ms to cross a beamwidth

for inc in [53, 70, 87]:
    vs = seam_rel_velocity(inc)
    t = sweep_time_ms(vs, 1000)
    print(f"inc {inc}°: seam rel-v {vs:.0f} m/s, beam-cross {t:.2f} ms"
          f" -> {'untrackable' if t < 10 else 'maybe'}")

# co-rotating adjacent planes for comparison
v_corotate = v*math.sin(math.radians(5))
print(f"\nco-rotating adjacent planes: rel-v ~{v_corotate:.0f} m/s"
      f" -> beam-cross {sweep_time_ms(v_corotate, 600):.0f} ms (gentle)")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 3: the time-varying graph

The router receives its movie of graphs.

import math
Re, h = 6371.0, 550.0
Ra = Re + h

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M7 SPINE PROJECT — CONSTELLATION ROUTER
# Stage 3: the time-varying +grid graph.
#   Stage 1 (m7-1): bent-pipe/mesh topology switch.
#   Stage 2 (m7-2): per-link budget & point-ahead (does it close?).
#   Stage 3: assemble links into the +grid, evolve it in time,
#            leave the seam un-linked and the poles thinned.
#   Next: m7-4 routes on this churning graph; m7-5 precomputes
#         from its deterministic future.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class PlusGridTopology:
    MAX_ISL_KM = 5000
    SEAM_VELOCITY_LIMIT = 3000    # m/s: above this, untrackable (§4)
    POLAR_LAT_CUTOFF = 70         # ° : inter-plane links off above this

    def __init__(self, num_planes=72, sats_per_plane=22, inc_deg=53.0):
        self.P, self.S, self.inc = num_planes, sats_per_plane, inc_deg
        self.v = math.sqrt(398600.4/Ra)*1000
        self.intra_km = 2*Ra*math.sin(math.radians(360/self.S)/2)
        self.inter_eq_km = 2*Ra*math.sin(math.radians(360/self.P)/2)

    def intra_edge(self):
        """Always present, fixed range."""
        return dict(present=True, range_km=self.intra_km, kind="intra")

    def inter_edge(self, lat_deg, counter_rotating):
        """Present unless: over range limit, across seam, or polar."""
        rng = self.inter_eq_km*math.cos(math.radians(lat_deg))
        if abs(lat_deg) > self.POLAR_LAT_CUTOFF:
            return dict(present=False, reason="polar shutdown")
        if counter_rotating:
            rel_v = 2*self.v*math.sin(math.radians(self.inc))
            if rel_v > self.SEAM_VELOCITY_LIMIT:
                return dict(present=False, reason="seam (untrackable)")
        if rng > self.MAX_ISL_KM:
            return dict(present=False, reason="out of range")
        return dict(present=True, range_km=rng, kind="inter")

    def snapshot(self, lat_deg):
        """The mesh's local character at a given latitude band."""
        intra = self.intra_edge()
        inter_co = self.inter_edge(lat_deg, counter_rotating=False)
        inter_seam = self.inter_edge(lat_deg, counter_rotating=True)
        return intra, inter_co, inter_seam

topo = PlusGridTopology()
print(f"intra-plane: {topo.intra_km:.0f} km (always present)\n")
print(f"{'lat':>5} {'inter co-rot':>28} {'inter seam':>24}")
for lat in [0, 30, 52, 75]:
    _, co, seam = topo.snapshot(lat)
    co_s = f"{co['range_km']:.0f} km" if co['present'] else co['reason']
    sm_s = "present" if seam['present'] else seam['reason']
    print(f"{lat:>4}° {co_s:>28} {sm_s:>24}")

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 01

Section 6's key claim is that constellation churn is deterministic, which m7-5 will exploit by precomputing paths. But determinism assumes the ephemeris is perfect and links behave as geometry predicts. Enumerate the ways reality breaks determinism — a satellite maneuvers for collision avoidance (Module 2's debris environment), an ISL terminal fails, a satellite is temporarily attitude-unstable — and for each, decide whether it degrades the precomputed topology gracefully or catastrophically. Conclude whether "deterministic churn" is a sound engineering foundation or an idealisation that needs a reactive fallback, and how large that fallback must be.

PROMPT 02

Section 4's seam is a permanent tear that traffic must detour around, and section 5's poles are thinned. A packet crossing the seam at mid-latitude must go the long way — over a pole or around the world. Estimate the latency penalty this imposes on a route that "wants" to cross the seam directly, using the m7-1 latency model, and identify which real city-pairs would suffer it. Then decide: is the seam a reason to add more ISL terminals (diagonal links that might bridge it), to accept the detour, or to place gateways so that seam-crossing traffic exits to fibre instead? Defend a choice.

PROMPT 03

Section 1 fixed the +grid at four terminals per satellite as "the standard," noting more terminals buy flexibility at linear cost. Work the trade at constellation scale: for a 1,584-satellite shell, each additional terminal per satellite is 1,584 optical terminals of mass, power, and cost (m7-2). What does a fifth and sixth terminal actually buy — seam-bridging, polar links, shorter paths, failure resilience — and is any of it worth 1,584 terminals? Frame the answer as the marginal-connectivity-per-terminal curve and identify where it flattens.

PROMPT 04

This lesson built its every number on one representative shell (72×22, 53°) and a first-order cos(latitude) convergence model, explicitly flagged as first-order. The topology conclusions — seam tears, poles thin, inter-plane links breathe — are qualitative and robust, but the specific ranges and the churn timing are model-dependent. Apply the load-bearing-assumption discipline from m6-5's reflection: which of this lesson's outputs does the router (m7-4) actually consume as a number rather than a shape, and for those, specify the exact Module 2 computation that would replace the first-order model before m7-4 depends on it. State whether the qualitative topology story survives that replacement.
