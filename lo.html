The Loft Model — Know It Deeper Than They Expect

The "Space as a Service" Architecture — Three Layers

Loft's business model is not a satellite operator in the traditional sense. The analogy they use internally is cloud computing: AWS doesn't build custom servers for each customer, it provides standardised infrastructure that any application can run on. Loft does the same for space. The architecture has three distinct layers:

  • The Hub — hardware abstraction layer. A universal payload adapter that sits between any customer payload and any satellite bus. Standardises power, data, command/control interfaces. Means Loft can fly any payload on any bus without bus-level NRE (Non-Recurring Engineering). This is the key to speed: no custom integration work means satellite buses can be pre-built on the shelf in Golden, CO, waiting for customer payloads.
  • Cockpit — software abstraction layer. Mission control software that abstracts away the spacecraft. Customers task their payload via a web UI or API — they never deal with TM/TC packets, orbital mechanics, or ground station scheduling. Advanced customers integrate Cockpit into their own tools via the API. The CDE role lives at the interface between the customer and Cockpit.
  • Ultimate Edge (UE) — the virtual missions layer. Customers deploy software apps (not hardware) to Loft's satellites via a containerised runtime environment backed by Microsoft Azure. The satellite becomes an edge compute node. Apps access onboard imagers, SDRs, CPUs, GPUs. This is YAM-6's primary feature and the direction the whole product is moving.
WHY THIS MATTERS FOR YOUR INTERVIEW A CDE who only knows the satellite ops side is only half-useful at Loft. The role requires you to help customers build on top of the API and operational infrastructure — that means understanding Cockpit deeply, being comfortable with REST APIs, and being able to debug why a customer's automated workflow isn't producing the data they expect. Frame everything through both the satellite ops lens AND the software/API lens.

Mission Types — Physical, Virtual, On-Demand

  • Physical missions: customer brings a hardware payload (sensor, instrument, antenna), Loft integrates it via the Hub onto a YAM or Longbow satellite bus, launches it, commissions it, and operates it. Customer gets Cockpit access for tasking. Examples: NASA MURI infrared radiometer on YAM-5, EarthDaily hyperspectral constellation, Honeywell/CSA quantum encryption sat.
  • Virtual missions: customer writes a software app, deploys it to the Ultimate Edge runtime already running on a Loft satellite. No hardware shipped. SDK + Docker-like container environment. Validated in a flight-representative dev environment, then deployed on-orbit. Examples: Helsing RF SIGINT AI on YAM-6, Agenium Space object detection, wildfire detection for SmartSat.
  • On-demand missions: customers task pre-existing Loft-owned sensors across the fleet — data as a service without a long-term contract. Newer product, less mature.

As a CDE you'll own all three types. The virtual mission side is increasingly central — YAM-6 was described as "the world's first satellite dedicated to running AI in space" and Loft is investing heavily in this direction.

The Satellite Platform — YAM Series and Longbow

  • YAM (Yet Another Mission): the workhorse multi-payload rideshare platform. YAM-2 and YAM-3 launched in 2021, YAM-5 carries NASA MURI, YAM-6 is the AI/virtual mission flagship (launched Transporter-10, early 2024). Fleet uses proven satellite buses — Loft quotes "600+ copies of our bus in orbit today" from their standard bus supplier.
  • Longbow: dedicated single-customer platform for larger missions. More capable, higher power, configurable for specific mission requirements. YAM-8 launched in early 2025 on SpaceX Transporter-13 as the debut of the Longbow platform (ESA GSTP co-funded). The French sovereign SAR satellite programme (DGA/CNES contract, ~€50M, Thales Alenia Space + TEKEVER as co-designers) uses Longbow, targeted launch 2029.
  • Loft Federal: the US government and defence subsidiary. First SDA (Space Development Agency) satellites launching 2025. Cleared for classified programmes. Partners include Ball Aerospace and Microsoft Azure Orbital for SDA NExT programme.

The CDE Role — What It Actually Means Day to Day

The CDE Is Not a Classic Satellite Operator

This is the single most important thing to internalise before the panel. The CDE role sits at the intersection of four functions that traditional aerospace keeps separate: satellite operations, customer success, software engineering, and systems engineering. A classic "satellite operator" at a traditional OEM focuses on TM/TC, anomaly response, and keeping the spacecraft alive. That's roughly 25% of what a CDE does at Loft.

The other 75% is: translating customer mission objectives into executable ConOps and configurations on Cockpit; building Python automation that makes the customer's mission run reliably at scale; debugging why a customer's API integration isn't working; helping a startup that has never operated a satellite understand what they need to do to achieve their science objective. The customer-facing and software dimensions are at least as important as the spacecraft operations dimension — probably more so, since Loft's competitive advantage is abstraction and simplicity, and the CDE is the person who delivers that experience.

THE ANALOGY TO USE IN THE INTERVIEW Think of a CDE as a DevOps engineer crossed with a forward-deployed customer engineer, operating in a real-time space environment. The spacecraft is the production system. The customer's mission is the application running on it. Your job is to keep the production system healthy, help the customer deploy and run their application successfully, and automate everything that doesn't need a human decision.

The Full Mission Lifecycle a CDE Owns

  • Requirements and ConOps: Before a satellite is built, the CDE works with the customer to define what success looks like — data volumes, latency, availability, interfaces. This feeds into the Mission SLA. At Loft, mission requirements are deliberately kept simple because the platform is standardised — the customer works within Loft's envelope rather than Loft building to the customer's spec.
  • Integration and test support: While hardware missions go through AIT in Golden, the CDE is involved in verifying that the Hub interfaces to the payload work correctly and that the Cockpit commands produce the expected payload responses. For virtual missions, the CDE helps the customer validate their app in the dev environment before deployment.
  • Launch campaign: Loft handles launch logistics, but the CDE ensures Cockpit is configured and the customer is ready to begin commissioning immediately after deployment.
  • Commissioning: First contact, initial health checks, payload bring-up sequence, first data collection, customer acceptance. CDE runs this with the customer present — they watch it live via Cockpit.
  • Nominal operations: Day-to-day health monitoring, scheduled pass execution, data delivery confirmation, performance trending, customer reporting. Python automation handles as much of this as possible — the CDE builds and maintains the automation.
  • Anomaly triage: When something goes wrong — payload not responding, data gap, unexpected telemetry — the CDE leads the investigation, coordinates with engineering, and communicates status to the customer. Speed and clarity under pressure is critical.
  • Continuous improvement: Feedback from operations and customers becomes roadmap input. The CDE is the voice of the customer inside the product org.

ConOps — What Loft Means by It

Concept of Operations at Loft is not a 200-page document. It's the operational design for a mission: what does the satellite do each orbit, how does the payload get commanded, how is data delivered, what are the failure modes and their responses? For a CDE, writing ConOps means:

  • Understanding the customer's data product requirement and working backward to pass schedules
  • Defining Cockpit scheduling templates — which commands execute in what order, with what timing
  • Documenting the nominal day-in-the-life, the commissioning sequence, and the anomaly response logic
  • Keeping it simple enough that the customer can execute it without Loft hand-holding every pass

REST API Basics for Cockpit Integration

Loft's Cockpit has an API. Customers integrate it into their own tools. As CDE you help them do this and debug it when it breaks. Know:

  • HTTP verbs: GET (retrieve), POST (create/submit), PUT/PATCH (update), DELETE
  • Authentication: API key in header (most common for satellite APIs), OAuth2 for more complex cases
  • JSON request/response format — parsing with Python's json module or requests library
  • Rate limiting and retries — a customer whose automation hammers the API during a pass window will get throttled
  • Webhooks — instead of polling for data, the API pushes a notification when data is ready. Important for latency-sensitive operations.

Docker and Containerisation — The Virtual Mission Context

Virtual missions deploy containerised apps to Loft's Ultimate Edge runtime. You don't need to write Dockerfiles but you need to understand the model:

  • A container packages an application and its dependencies so it runs identically everywhere — dev laptop, ground test environment, on-orbit runtime
  • Docker image = the blueprint. Container = the running instance.
  • Dockerfile defines what goes in the image: base OS, dependencies, app code, entry point
  • At Loft: customer writes app → packages as container → deploys to UE runtime → Loft manages the on-orbit execution environment
  • CDE debug role: if the app works on the ground but fails on-orbit, is it an environment difference, a resource constraint (CPU/RAM limit on the satellite), or a data interface issue?

Python Automation for Ops — What They're Looking For

The JD says "strong Python scripting skills." At Loft this means:

  • Automating pass execution: scripting a sequence of Cockpit API calls for each satellite contact window
  • Telemetry parsing: reading spacecraft TM data, flagging out-of-limit values, sending alerts
  • Data pipeline: moving payload data from Cockpit's delivery endpoint to the customer's storage (S3, GCS, Azure Blob), reformatting if needed
  • Health monitoring: scheduled scripts that query satellite status, compute uptime metrics, generate customer-facing reports
  • Anomaly detection: comparing current telemetry against baseline to flag drift before it becomes a failure

Prepare: one specific Python tool you've built that does any of the above, even if it's from LEOAF or a side project.

Multi-Mission Fleet Operations

Loft is scaling toward 30 satellites by 2027 with heterogeneous payloads. The ops challenge is different from operating a single mission:

  • Pass scheduling across a fleet requires prioritisation — whose data gets the first downlink window?
  • Ground station scheduling: Loft uses a global network; a CDE may need to request additional contact time if a customer's mission requires more than the standard allocation
  • Anomaly isolation in a fleet: if telemetry anomaly X appears on YAM-5 and YAM-6 but not YAM-3, it might be a bus-level issue, a ground system issue, or a payload-specific issue — the fleet comparison helps isolate
  • Configuration management: keeping track of which software version is running on which satellite, which customers have which payload configurations active

Mission SLA and Customer Expectations

Loft defines a Mission SLA with each customer upfront. As CDE you own delivery against it. Key dimensions:

  • Data latency: how long from collection to delivery at the customer endpoint? (GEO relay via Viasat RTSR can reduce this to near-real-time for some missions)
  • Availability: what fraction of scheduled passes actually produce usable data?
  • Data volume: bytes/day delivered vs contracted
  • Command response: how quickly does the payload respond to a customer command?

When SLA is not being met, the CDE must triage root cause (spacecraft, ground station, customer config, Cockpit), drive resolution, and communicate status clearly — without blaming the customer even if the issue is their config.

Anomaly Triage — The Loft Mental Model

Unlike a traditional ops centre with a tier-1/tier-2/tier-3 escalation structure, Loft CDEs own the triage end-to-end. When something goes wrong:

  • Is it the spacecraft? Check bus TM — power, thermal, ADCS, comms.
  • Is it the Hub interface? Did the command reach the payload? What did the Hub acknowledge?
  • Is it the payload? Is payload TM nominal? Is the payload in the expected mode?
  • Is it the ground station? Was the pass contact established? Was the uplink successful?
  • Is it Cockpit? Did the scheduling engine execute the command? Is there a log entry?
  • Is it the customer's config? Does the command sequence make sense? Is the payload in the right pre-condition state?

Work through this tree out loud in interview scenarios. It shows systematic thinking.

Technical Scenario Exercises — Prepare for Live Problem-Solving

What to Expect

A three-hour panel often includes a live or take-home technical exercise. At Loft this typically means either: a Python coding task (write a script that does X), a system design question (design the operational architecture for a new customer mission), or an anomaly scenario (here's a telemetry dump, what's wrong). Think out loud — they're evaluating your reasoning process, not just whether you get the right answer.

SCENARIO A — TELEMETRY ANALYSIS

You receive a daily telemetry summary showing that a physical payload's data volume delivered to the customer is 40% below the contracted SLA for the past three days. The payload TM shows it is in nominal mode and executing commands. What do you do?

WHAT THEY'RE TESTING: structured triage, SLA accountability, customer communication

  • Step 1 — Characterise the gap: 40% shortfall over 3 days. Is it every pass, or specific passes? Does it correlate with specific ground stations, specific times of day, or specific orbit geometry?
  • Step 2 — Ground station check: did all scheduled passes make contact? How many passes were executed vs scheduled? A pass-success rate drop would explain the volume shortfall without any spacecraft issue.
  • Step 3 — Pass-level data: for the passes that did execute, is data volume per pass at expected levels? If yes, it's a pass count problem. If no, it's a data rate problem.
  • Step 4 — If data rate per pass is down: is the downlink modulation nominal? Is the link being used efficiently (e.g., is the payload in the right data mode)? Did a recent Cockpit configuration change affect the scheduling window duration?
  • Step 5 — Customer notification: regardless of root cause, notify the customer now. "We've identified a data volume shortfall over the past 3 days and are actively investigating. We will provide a root cause update within [X hours]." Do not wait until you have the answer — the customer needs to know you're on it.
  • Step 6 — Recovery plan: if it's fixable (add pass windows, correct configuration), commit to a specific recovery date. If it's a structural issue (reduced pass availability due to ground station maintenance), renegotiate SLA for the affected period and provide a credit if applicable.

SCENARIO B — PYTHON CODING (LIKELY LIVE OR TAKE-HOME)

WHAT THEY'RE TESTING: Python fluency, data handling, clean readable code, error handling

SCENARIO C — NEW CUSTOMER ONBOARDING DESIGN
