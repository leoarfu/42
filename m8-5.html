Early-Orbit Operations & Commissioning

MODULE 8
GROUND SEGMENT & OPERATIONS
CORE

m8-4 sized the ground network on the assumption that satellites can be operated autonomously — and that assumption has a hidden prerequisite this lesson addresses: the satellites must first be brought into service, and at mega-constellation scale, that too must be industrialised. This lesson covers LEOP (the launch and early-orbit phase) and commissioning — everything between a satellite separating from its launch vehicle and entering revenue service. For a traditional satellite, this is a bespoke, weeks-long campaign run by a team of engineers watching one spacecraft. For a mega-constellation launching sixty satellites at a time, twenty-seven times over a year, with over a hundred satellites in commissioning simultaneously and three hundred more to replace every year forever, it cannot be bespoke. Commissioning must become a pipeline — an automated, standardised process that flows satellites from separation to service with minimal human attention per satellite — or the constellation simply cannot be deployed and sustained.

The lesson's verified anchors frame both the physics and the logistics. The physics is modest: satellites are injected at a low altitude (300 km, to save launch energy) and raise themselves to the operational shell (550 km) using electric propulsion — a 141 m/s maneuver costing ~8 days of low-thrust firing and about 1% of the satellite's mass in propellant. The logistics is the challenge: 1,584 satellites at 60 per launch is 27 launches, a year of deployment at a fortnightly cadence; ~129 satellites are in commissioning at any moment; and a 5-year satellite life means ~317 satellites — five launches' worth — must be replaced every year just to hold the constellation constant. LEOP for one satellite is a campaign; LEOP for a constellation is a factory that never stops. The spine gains the commissioning-pipeline model.

1. LEOP: from separation to safe

The launch and early-orbit phase begins the instant a satellite separates from its launch vehicle and ends when it is stable, healthy, and ready to begin its mission. For any satellite it is the most dangerous phase — more missions are lost in LEOP than in years of subsequent operation — because the satellite is in an unproven state, doing things for the first time. The sequence:

Separation and detumble. The satellite separates from the launcher, often tumbling from the separation dynamics, and must first stop rotating — the attitude control system (reaction wheels, magnetorquers) detumbles it to a stable orientation. Until it is detumbled, it cannot point its solar panels or its antennas, so this is done on the omni TT&C link (m8-4) and stored battery power, against the clock of the battery draining.

Sun acquisition and power-positive. Once detumbled, the satellite orients its solar panels toward the Sun to become power-positive — generating more power than it consumes — before the battery depletes. This is the first survival milestone: a satellite that reaches power-positive can sustain itself; one that does not is lost. Everything in early LEOP races the battery.

Communications and control established. With power secured, the satellite establishes reliable TT&C contact (m8-4), and the operators confirm its health from the first telemetry. The satellite is now safe — stable, powered, and in contact — and LEOP's critical survival phase is over. What remains is to make it operational, which means getting it to its working orbit and testing its payload.

For a mega-constellation, every one of these steps must happen for sixty satellites at once after each launch, and be robust enough to run with minimal human intervention. The satellites are designed to execute this sequence autonomously — separate, detumble, acquire the Sun, phone home — so that the ground's role is to monitor a fleet of newborns finding their feet, not to hand-fly each one through its first hours.

2. Orbit-raising: getting to the shell

Satellites are usually injected at a lower altitude than their operational shell — 300 km against an operational 550 km in the representative case — because a lower injection saves the launch vehicle energy and lets it carry more satellites. The satellites then raise themselves to the operational shell under their own propulsion. The maneuver is modest in energy: raising from 300 to 550 km is a delta-v of ~141 m/s, a small fraction of orbital velocity.

The propulsion is almost always electric (a Hall-effect or gridded-ion thruster), for the same reason electric propulsion dominates modern satellites: its high specific impulse (~1,500 s) means it does the maneuver on very little propellant — about 2.5 kg for a 260 kg satellite, ~1% of its mass — leaving mass for payload and station-keeping. The cost is time: electric thrusters produce tiny thrust (tens of millinewtons), so the raise takes ~8 days of continuous firing rather than the minutes a chemical burn would need. For a constellation this is a favourable trade — propellant mass is precious and launch cadence gives plenty of satellites in the raising pipeline, so the 8-day duration is absorbed by having many satellites raising at once. Orbit-raising also serves a second purpose: by raising satellites at different rates, the operator can phase them — spreading them around the orbital plane and into different planes to build the Walker constellation (Module 2) from a stack that all separated at the same point.

The low injection has a safety bonus, too. A satellite that fails during orbit-raising — before reaching the operational shell — is still at low altitude, where atmospheric drag will deorbit it naturally within months to a few years (Module 2's debris discipline). Injecting low means failed satellites clean themselves up, a deliberate design choice for responsible mega-constellation deployment.

3. Commissioning: from safe to operational

Reaching the operational shell, safe and powered, is not the same as being ready to serve customers. Commissioning is the testing-and-activation process that turns a healthy satellite into an operational one: checking out each subsystem, calibrating the payload (the antenna patterns, the beamforming, m6-2), verifying the ISL terminals can acquire their neighbours (m7-2), confirming the satellite can take its place in the mesh topology (m7-3) and the routing schedule (m7-5), and finally accepting it into revenue service. For the representative case this takes on the order of 30 days per satellite — checkout, raise, test, accept.

Thirty days per satellite, multiplied by the deployment cadence, is where the scale problem bites. If sixty satellites arrive every two weeks and each spends thirty days in commissioning, then at any moment:

~60 satellites/launch × (30 days ÷ 14 days/launch) ≈ 129 satellites in commissioning simultaneously

A hundred and twenty-nine satellites, each being checked out, raised, and tested at once. No team of engineers can hand-fly that many concurrent commissioning campaigns — the traditional model, one team per satellite, would need dozens of teams running in parallel forever. Commissioning must be a pipeline: an automated flow where satellites progress through standardised checkout stages with software running the routine tests and human engineers intervening only where a satellite deviates from the expected sequence (m8-6's exception model, again). The satellites commission themselves as much as possible, and the ground supervises the pipeline rather than flying each satellite through it.

4. The deployment campaign

Deploying a full constellation is an industrial campaign unlike anything in traditional space operations. The representative 1,584-satellite constellation, at 60 satellites per launch, requires 27 launches — and at a launch every two weeks, roughly a year of continuous deployment. That is 27 separation events, 27 batches of sixty newborns to shepherd through LEOP, and a commissioning pipeline running at full flow for the entire year. The deployment is not a single event but a sustained operation, a production line feeding satellites into orbit at a cadence that would have been unthinkable in the era of bespoke satellites.

This industrialisation is the defining feature of the mega-constellation era. Traditional space operations launched satellites one or a few at a time, years apart, each a hand-crafted campaign. A mega-constellation launches them by the dozen, fortnightly, through a process that must be as repeatable and automated as a factory line — because the alternative, treating each of 1,584 satellites as a bespoke campaign, is impossible. The whole enterprise depends on driving the per-satellite human cost of LEOP and commissioning toward zero, which means designing satellites that bring themselves into service and a ground system that supervises the flow rather than operating each spacecraft. Deployment at this scale is a systems-engineering achievement as much as a rocketry one.

5. Replenishment: the pipeline never stops

The most important consequence of scale is that the commissioning pipeline is not a one-time deployment that ends — it runs forever. LEO satellites have a limited life (atmospheric drag, radiation, component wear-out — ~5 years is representative), and a constellation that stops replacing them decays. To hold a 1,584-satellite constellation constant against a 5-year life:

1,584 satellites ÷ 5 years = ~317 satellites/year replaced, forever

Three hundred and seventeen satellites a year — more than five launches' worth — must be built, launched, commissioned, and brought into service every year just to keep the constellation at strength. The deployment campaign of §4 does not end when the constellation is complete; it transitions into a replenishment campaign at a lower but permanent cadence. The commissioning pipeline is a permanent fixture of the operation, not a temporary deployment phase.

This has a profound implication for the whole enterprise. A mega-constellation is not a thing you build; it is a thing you keep building — a continuous flow of satellites through manufacture, launch, LEOP, commissioning, service, and eventual deorbit, at a steady-state rate set by the fleet size and the satellite life. The economics (the modules ahead) must account for this permanent replenishment cost; the operations must sustain the pipeline indefinitely; and the deorbit discipline (Module 2's debris environment) must handle 317 satellites a year reaching end-of-life and clearing themselves from orbit. The constellation is a river, not a lake — its strength maintained by constant flow, not by a one-time fill.

6. Commissioning as the fleet's front door

Commissioning is where satellites enter the fleet, and at scale it must be an automated front door. The theme unifying this lesson with m8-4 is that both the routine operation and the bringing-into-service of a mega-constellation must be industrialised — the contact demand (m8-4) and the commissioning demand (m8-5) both scale with fleet size beyond what human-tended operations can handle, and both are solved the same way: by autonomy and pipelines, with humans supervising aggregates and handling exceptions. A mega-constellation is an operation defined by flow: satellites flowing in through commissioning, flowing through years of autonomous service, and flowing out through deorbit, at rates that make every per-satellite manual step a scaling wall.

The commissioning pipeline this lesson built is the front end of that flow, and it feeds everything downstream. A satellite that completes commissioning enters the TT&C contact schedule (m8-4), takes its place in the mesh topology and routing (Module 7), and begins earning revenue — until, five years later, it deorbits and its replacement flows through the same pipeline. The next lesson (m8-6) handles what happens during those five years of service when a satellite deviates from healthy operation: the anomalies, the playbooks, and the fleet automation that catches the exceptions the autonomy of m8-4 and m8-5 escalates. Commissioning gets satellites into service; anomaly handling keeps them there.

8. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

Stage 5 of the spine is the commissioning-pipeline model: it takes a fleet size, a launch cadence and batch size, a per-satellite commissioning time, and a satellite lifetime, and computes the deployment duration, the number of satellites in commissioning simultaneously, and the permanent replenishment rate. It models the orbit-raise (delta-v, duration, propellant) and the LEOP survival sequence. The ground segment now includes the pipeline that flows satellites into service — sized to reveal that ~129 concurrent commissionings and ~317 annual replacements demand an automated, pipelined process, not a bespoke campaign.

What the pipeline model assumes is that the flow runs smoothly — that satellites progress through LEOP and commissioning as designed. But satellites fail, deviate, and misbehave, both during commissioning and across their years of service, and a fleet of thousands generates a steady stream of anomalies that must be caught and handled without a human per satellite. That is the last piece of Part II: m8-6, the anomalies, playbooks, and fleet automation that handle the exceptions this lesson's pipeline and m8-4's contact schedule escalate. The pipeline flows; m8-6 is what happens when a satellite in the flow deviates.

Exercise 1 — Orbit-raising

The physics of getting to the shell.

import math
mu, Re, g0 = 398600.4, 6371.0, 9.81

def hohmann_dv(h1_km, h2_km):
    r1, r2 = Re+h1_km, Re+h2_km
    v1, v2 = math.sqrt(mu/r1), math.sqrt(mu/r2)
    a = (r1+r2)/2
    vp, va = math.sqrt(mu*(2/r1-1/a)), math.sqrt(mu*(2/r2-1/a))
    return ((vp-v1)+(v2-va))*1000   # m/s

def ep_raise(dv_ms, m_sat_kg, isp_s=1500, thrust_n=0.05):
    prop = m_sat_kg*(1-math.exp(-dv_ms/(isp_s*g0)))
    days = dv_ms*m_sat_kg/thrust_n/86400
    return prop, days

dv = hohmann_dv(300, 550)
prop, days = ep_raise(dv, 260)
print(f"raise 300->550 km: dv {dv:.0f} m/s")
print(f"  EP: {prop:.1f} kg propellant ({prop/260*100:.1f}%), {days:.0f} days")

Exercise 2 — The deployment and replenishment campaign

Size the pipeline.

import math

def deployment(n_total, per_launch, cadence_days):
    launches = math.ceil(n_total/per_launch)
    return launches, launches*cadence_days

def in_commissioning(per_launch, commission_days, cadence_days):
    return per_launch * commission_days/cadence_days

def replenishment(n_total, life_years, per_launch):
    per_year = n_total/life_years
    return per_year, per_year/per_launch

launches, days = deployment(1584, 60, 14)
pipe = in_commissioning(60, 30, 14)
repl, repl_launches = replenishment(1584, 5, 60)

print(f"in commissioning at once: {pipe:.0f} satellites")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 5: the commissioning pipeline

The fleet's front door.

import math
mu, Re, g0 = 398600.4, 6371.0, 9.81

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M8 SPINE PROJECT — GROUND SEGMENT
# Stage 5: the commissioning pipeline.
#   Stage 4 (m8-4) sized the TT&C network for a flying fleet.
#   Stage 5: the pipeline that BRINGS satellites into that
#   fleet — LEOP, orbit-raise, commissioning — and the
#   permanent replenishment that keeps it full.
#   Next: m8-6 handles exceptions during service.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class CommissioningPipeline:
    def __init__(self, n_total, per_launch=60, cadence_days=14,
                 commission_days=30, life_years=5):
        self.n = n_total
        self.per_launch = per_launch
        self.cadence = cadence_days
        self.commission = commission_days
        self.life = life_years

    def orbit_raise(self, h1=300, h2=550, m_sat=260, isp=1500, thrust=0.05):
        r1, r2 = Re+h1, Re+h2
        a = (r1+r2)/2
        vp = math.sqrt(mu*(2/r1-1/a)); va = math.sqrt(mu*(2/r2-1/a))
        dv = ((vp-math.sqrt(mu/r1))+(math.sqrt(mu/r2)-va))*1000
        prop = m_sat*(1-math.exp(-dv/(isp*g0)))
        days = dv*m_sat/thrust/86400
        return dict(dv_ms=round(dv), prop_kg=round(prop,1), days=round(days))

    def deployment(self):
        launches = math.ceil(self.n/self.per_launch)
        return dict(launches=launches, days=launches*self.cadence,
                    years=round(launches*self.cadence/365, 1))

    def concurrent_commissioning(self):
        return round(self.per_launch * self.commission/self.cadence)

    def replenishment(self):
        per_year = self.n/self.life
        return dict(sats_per_year=round(per_year),
                    launches_per_year=round(per_year/self.per_launch, 1))

    def report(self):
        print(f"  fleet: {self.n} satellites, {self.life}-yr life")
        print(f"  orbit-raise: {self.orbit_raise()}")
        print(f"  deployment: {self.deployment()}")
        print(f"  in commissioning at once: {self.concurrent_commissioning()}")
        print(f"  replenishment: {self.replenishment()}")

CommissioningPipeline(1584).report()

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 1

Section 5's "river not a lake" reframing means a mega-constellation's cost is dominated not by the initial build but by the permanent replenishment — ~317 satellites a year forever. This inverts the intuition that a constellation is a capital project with a big upfront cost and then cheap operation. Work the consequence: if replenishment dominates lifetime cost, what does that imply about where an operator should invest — cheaper satellites (lower replenishment cost) or longer-lived satellites (lower replenishment rate)? Compute the trade for a satellite that costs 20% more but lasts 40% longer, and decide whether the mega-constellation economics reward durability or disposability — connecting to Module 2's debris discipline (more replenishment = more deorbits).

PROMPT 2

Section 2 noted low injection is a safety feature: failed satellites deorbit naturally before reaching the shell. But this protects only against failures during raising — a satellite that fails after reaching the 550 km operational shell does not deorbit for years to decades (Module 2), and at ~317 replacements a year, some fraction will fail on-station. Quantify the debris consequence: if even 1% of satellites fail on-station rather than deorbiting under control, how many dead satellites accumulate at the shell over a decade of operation, and what does that do to the collision environment (Module 2)? Decide whether the low-injection safety feature is sufficient, or whether on-station reliability and controlled deorbit are the real debris levers for a mega-constellation.

PROMPT 3

Section 3 made commissioning a pipeline where software runs routine tests and humans handle deviations. But commissioning is where a satellite is tested for the first time — it is precisely the phase where deviations are most likely and least predictable, because the satellite has never done these things before. This is in tension with automation: you automate the known and routine, but commissioning is where the unknown surfaces. Interrogate the boundary: which commissioning steps are genuinely routine and automatable, and which require human judgement because they are where novel failures appear? Decide whether commissioning automation is safe, or whether it risks passing subtly-flawed satellites into service because the automation only checks for the failures it was designed to catch.

PROMPT 4

This lesson's numbers — 129 concurrent commissionings, 317 annual replacements — rest on a batch size (60/launch), cadence (14 days), commissioning time (30 days), and satellite life (5 years), all representative. The conclusions (commissioning must be pipelined, replenishment is permanent) are robust, but the specific pipeline load that sizes the operations team is assumption-dependent. Apply the load-bearing-assumption discipline: which input most strongly drives the concurrent-commissioning number that sizes the operations workload, and which drives the replenishment rate that dominates lifetime cost? Specify what an operator would measure from its actual deployment to replace each assumption, and state whether the pipeline-load figures are design targets or illustrations.
