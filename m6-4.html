Scan Loss, Skew
& the Regulatory Mask

MODULE 6
USER TERMINALS & D2D
CORE

Every array number in this module so far was a broadside number, and m6-2 fixed the convention that prices the departure: the embedded element pattern cos^q θ, with q ≈ 1.2–1.5, is the total scan-loss law — its first cosine power is the aperture projection itself, the excess is mismatch. This lesson spends that convention. It converts scan loss from a table into terminal performance versus elevation, re-measures m6-1's dish-versus-panel G/T gap where a LEO terminal actually operates, adds the polarisation term that scanning degrades, and then meets the constraint that shapes all of it from outside the physics: the regulatory off-axis mask, the envelope of EIRP density a terminal may radiate toward directions it is not pointing — above all, toward the geostationary arc that NGSO systems are legally obliged to protect.

The lesson's through-line is a single reframing. A flat panel's datasheet gain is its best number; its service is delivered mostly at scan, because orbital geometry (Module 2) puts satellites at low elevation most of the time. And its transmit behaviour is regulated not at the beam peak but across the whole pattern — sidelobes, taper, carrier bandwidth, and even when it is allowed to transmit at all are compliance instruments. The terminal design closes only when the scan-dependent physics and the angle-dependent law are solved together, and the spine's Stage 4 does exactly that: a scan-aware gain model with a mask-compliance checker attached.

1. The scan-loss law, spent

The verified total scan loss at q = 1.3, both directions of the link paying it (receive gain and transmit EIRP each carry the aperture):

Scan angle Loss
0°0.0 dB
30°−0.8 dB
45°−2.0 dB
60°−3.9 dB
70°−6.1 dB
75°−7.6 dB

The exponent is a design property: a well-matched, wide-scan element design earns q near 1.2; a cheap or narrowband element drifts toward 1.5, and at 60° the difference between them is a full decibel. This one parameter — set by the radiator design of m6-2 §7 and frozen at manufacture — is the panel's low-elevation destiny.

2. Elevation is scan: the flat-mount geometry

A panel lying flat on a roof has its broadside at zenith, so scan angle = 90° − elevation. Combine with the scan law:

Satellite elevation Flat-mount scan Loss (q = 1.3) With 25° tilt Loss
90°0°0.0 dB25°−0.6 dB
60°30°−0.8 dB5°−0.0 dB
40°50°−2.5 dB25°−0.6 dB
25°65°−4.9 dB40°−1.5 dB
15°75°−7.6 dB50°−2.5 dB

Module 2's geometry makes the left columns painful: solid-angle statistics put most of a constellation's visible satellites at low-to-middle elevations, and high-latitude users see their satellites systematically toward one horizon. Hence the mechanical tilt — the one degree of mechanical freedom the "no moving parts" terminal quietly keeps. Tilting the panel toward the mean satellite direction re-centres the scan range: the table's right columns show a 25° tilt converting the 25°-elevation case from −4.9 to −1.5 dB. This is why consumer NGSO terminals at high latitude mount visibly tilted, and why the installation app cares which way the roof faces: the tilt angle is a per-site optimisation of a physics tax, and the spine's Stage 4 includes the optimiser.

3. The G/T gap, re-measured where it matters

m6-1 measured the dish-versus-panel G/T gap at 8.9 dB — at broadside. The dish's gimbal holds its full gain to the horizon; the flat panel pays the §2 table. At 25° elevation, flat-mounted:

Gap = 8.9 dB (broadside) + 4.9 dB (scan at 65°) ≈ 13.8 dB

With the 25° tilt the scan penalty drops to 1.5 dB and the gap returns to ~10.4 dB. Three readings. First, the panel's honest datasheet is a curve — G/T versus elevation — and a single broadside figure overstates typical service by several dB, exactly the marketing pattern m5-1 §4 warned about, one module later and one layer down. Second, the M4 engine should consume a pass-weighted G/T, not the peak; the m6-5 pass geometry supplies the weights and the spine already anticipates the integral. Third, the tilt is not cosmetic: 3.4 dB recovered at the worst elevations is worth more than any component upgrade in the terminal's bill of materials — a site-configuration parameter outperforming hardware, the m5-7 procurement lesson in miniature.

4. Polarisation at scan

Satellite links run circular polarisation, and a planar element's axial ratio — excellent at boresight — degrades off boresight: the two orthogonal components that make circularity stop being equal and quadrature at wide scan. Two costs follow. The co-polar mismatch loss grows from ~0.1 dB near boresight toward 0.5–1 dB at 60°+ scan — a line the m4-1 budget carried as a constant and a flat panel must carry as a function of geometry. And the cross-polar discrimination (XPD) collapses faster: a system reusing spectrum on orthogonal polarisations (the m3/m4-6 reuse machinery) finds its polarisation isolation eroding exactly at the scan angles where gain is already scarce. Dishes with well-designed feeds hold axial ratio essentially constant across their (mechanically pointed) operation — one more entry on the gimbal's side of the ledger, and one more reason wide-scan element design (§1's q) is where panel engineering quality concentrates.

Skew, while polarisation is on the table: for linear-polarisation systems (much of GEO Ku), a terminal off the satellite's longitude sees the polarisation frame rotated — the skew angle — and mobile terminals (aero especially) must de-rotate continuously as the platform moves. NGSO consumer systems largely sidestep this by running circular; aero GEO terminals live with it, and m6-6 inherits the mechanism.

5. The off-axis mask: the law at every angle

A transmitting terminal is regulated across its entire pattern. The instrument is an off-axis EIRP density mask: a ceiling, in dBW per reference bandwidth (4 kHz in the classic FCC Part 25 formulation), as a function of the angle θ away from the intended direction — falling as −25·log₁₀ θ, the slope chosen decades ago to match well-behaved reflector sidelobe envelopes. The Ku-band routine-licensing values toward the GSO arc are the canonical example: a 15 − 25·log₁₀ θ dBW/4 kHz class of envelope in the first degrees, stepping down further beyond (exact constants are band- and regime-specific; the structure is universal, and ETSI's harmonised standards for terminal classes — including the aero VSAT standards m6-6 will meet — carry the same shape).

The mask's logic: your beam peak is your business; your sidelobes land on other operators' satellites, spaced along the GSO arc a couple of degrees apart, and the mask is the interference budget of the whole orbital neighbourhood written as a terminal requirement. For NGSO systems the obligation is sharpened by the ITU EPFD (equivalent power flux-density) limits protecting GSO networks from NGSO constellations in aggregate — a system-level budget that flows down to every terminal's off-axis behaviour and to operational rules (§7).

6. Compliance arithmetic

Compliance is one inequality: at every angle θ,

EIRP density on-axis + relative pattern level(θ) ≤ mask(θ)
where EIRP density = EIRP − 10·log₁₀(B_occupied / 4 kHz)

Worked, with the module's own terminals (mask class 15 − 25·log₁₀ θ; values at 1.5°/3°/7°: 10.6 / 3.1 / −6.1 dBW/4 kHz):

The consumer panel, wideband: 36.2 dBW spread over 250 MHz → on-axis density −11.8 dBW/4 kHz — below the mask at the beam peak. Its −13.2 dB uniform sidelobes sit at −25.0; with the m6-2 taper, −32.1. Compliance with room to spare — and the reason is the denominator: bandwidth is a compliance instrument. The m5-1 wideband carrier and the m5-6 spreading waveforms are, among everything else, mask engineering.

The 65 cm dish, narrowband: 44.1 dBW in a 5 MHz carrier → on-axis density 13.1 dBW/4 kHz. At θ = 1.5° — barely off its 1.6° beamwidth — the pattern is only a few dB down, and the density rides directly against the 10.6 dBW/4 kHz ceiling. This is why small-dish, narrow-carrier uplinks are the historically regulated species (the mask was written about them), why uplink power control exists as a compliance function and not only a rain function (m4-4), and why "just add transmit power" is never a free option on any terminal.

The general lesson: the mask couples m6-2's taper, m5-1's bandwidth, m5-3's power control, and this lesson's pattern into one constraint surface — the terminal's transmit design is the simultaneous solution.

7. The NGSO-specific geometry: the arc and the avoidance zone

An NGSO terminal's beam sweeps the sky as it tracks; sometimes the line from terminal through satellite passes close to the geostationary arc. At those instants the terminal's main beam — not a sidelobe — points near GSO satellites, and no taper can help. The operational answer, embedded in NGSO licensing worldwide: GSO-arc avoidance — the system computes each link's angular separation from the arc and, inside a protection zone of a few degrees, reduces power or refuses the link entirely, handing the user to a different satellite whose geometry clears the zone. The terminal's scheduler (m5-3) and the constellation's satellite-selection logic thus carry a regulatory geometry term; capacity planning at latitudes where the arc sits high in the sky must account for the excluded cone. Skew-adjacent trivia becomes system design: the arc's apparent position is a function of the user's latitude, and so, therefore, is the fraction of sky — and of satellites — a compliant terminal may use.

9. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

Stage 4 of the spine: the array's gain and EIRP become functions of elevation (with the tilt as an optimisable site parameter), polarisation loss becomes a scan-dependent line, and a mask checker tests any (EIRP, bandwidth, pattern envelope) triple against the −25·log₁₀ θ law — plus the arc-avoidance geometry as an operational constraint the m6-5 tracking logic and the m5-3 scheduler must respect. The panel's honest datasheet — the G/T-versus-elevation curve — now exists as code, and m6-1's headline gap has its full range: 8.9 dB at the panel's best angle, ~13.8 at its worst flat-mounted service angle, ~10.4 with the tilt doing its work.

What remains before the segments can be priced: the terminal must find and follow the satellite that all this geometry assumes — acquisition, tracking, and the handover choreography whose silicon m6-3 already priced. That is m6-5.

Exercise 1 — The elevation curve and the tilt optimiser

Turn the scan law into the panel's honest datasheet.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x) if x > 0 else -999.0

def scan_loss_db(scan_deg, q=1.3):
    c = math.cos(math.radians(min(abs(scan_deg), 89.9)))
    return 10*q*math.log10(c)

def panel_gt(elev_deg, tilt_deg=0.0, gt_broadside=11.0, q=1.3):
    scan = abs(90.0 - elev_deg - tilt_deg)
    return gt_broadside + scan_loss_db(scan, q)

print(f"{'elev':>5} {'flat G/T':>9} {'tilt 25°':>9} {'dish':>6}")
for e in [90, 60, 40, 25, 15]:
    print(f"{e:>4}° {panel_gt(e):>8.1f} {panel_gt(e, 25):>9.1f} {19.9:>6.1f}")

def best_tilt(elev_samples, q=1.3):
    """Tilt maximising the mean G/T over an elevation distribution."""
    best = max(range(0, 61), key=lambda t:
               sum(panel_gt(e, t, q=q) for e in elev_samples))
    return best

# crude elevation distribution: uniform 25..90 (m6-5 supplies the real one)
samples = list(range(25, 91, 5))
t = best_tilt(samples)
print(f"\nbest tilt for uniform 25–90° service: {t}°")
print(f"mean G/T flat {sum(panel_gt(e) for e in samples)/len(samples):.1f}"
      f" vs tilted {sum(panel_gt(e, t) for e in samples)/len(samples):.1f} dB/K")

Exercise 2 — The mask checker

One inequality, every angle: is this terminal legal?

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x)

def mask_dbw_4khz(theta_deg):
    """Ku GSO routine-licensing class: 15 − 25·log10 θ (1.5°..7°),
    stepped continuation beyond (structure per FCC Part 25 class)."""
    t = max(theta_deg, 1.5)
    if t <= 7.0:  return 15 - 25*math.log10(t)
    if t <= 9.2:  return -6.0
    if t <= 48.0: return 18 - 25*math.log10(t)
    return -24.0

def offaxis_density(eirp_dbw, bw_hz, rel_pattern_db):
    return eirp_dbw - db(bw_hz/4e3) + rel_pattern_db

def check(name, eirp, bw, pattern):
    """pattern: list of (theta_deg, relative level dB)."""
    print(f"\n{name}: on-axis density {eirp - db(bw/4e3):+.1f} dBW/4kHz")
    worst = -99
    for th, rel in pattern:
        d, m = offaxis_density(eirp, bw, rel), mask_dbw_4khz(th)
        margin = m - d
        worst = max(worst, -margin)
        flag = "  ** VIOLATION **" if margin < 0 else ""
        print(f"  θ={th:>4.1f}°  density {d:>6.1f}  mask {m:>6.1f}"
              f"  margin {margin:+5.1f} dB{flag}")
    print(f"  verdict: {'FAILS' if worst > 0 else 'complies'}"
          f" (worst margin {-worst:+.1f} dB)")

# uniform-illumination panel envelope vs tapered (m6-2 numbers)
panel_uni = [(1.5, -3), (3, -13.2), (7, -17), (20, -25)]
panel_tap = [(1.5, -4), (3, -20.3), (7, -25), (20, -32)]
dish      = [(1.5, -3), (3, -20),   (7, -30), (20, -40)]

check("Panel, 36.2 dBW / 250 MHz, uniform", 36.2, 250e6, panel_uni)
check("Panel, 36.2 dBW / 250 MHz, tapered", 36.2, 250e6, panel_tap)
check("Dish, 44.1 dBW / 5 MHz",             44.1, 5e6,  dish)
check("Dish, 44.1 dBW / 250 kHz (!)",       44.1, 250e3, dish)

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 4: scan, tilt, and the law

The terminal model becomes geometry-aware and legally aware.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x) if x > 0 else -999.0

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M6 SPINE PROJECT — TERMINAL MODEL
# Stage 4: elevation-dependent performance + mask compliance.
#   Stage 2 gave G(scan) with cos^q TOTAL (projection incl.)
#   Stage 3 gave power/beams/silicon per architecture.
#   Stage 4: elevation & tilt geometry, polarisation-at-scan,
#            off-axis mask check, arc-avoidance hook.
# Next: m6-5 pointing/tracking + the real pass geometry;
#       m6-6 segment table; m6-7 evaluator -> M4 -> M5.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class ScanMaskModel:
    def __init__(self, name, gt_broadside, eirp_broadside, q=1.3,
                 tilt_deg=0.0, polar_wide_db=0.6):
        self.name = name
        self.gt0, self.eirp0, self.q = gt_broadside, eirp_broadside, q
        self.tilt = tilt_deg
        self.polar_wide = polar_wide_db          # extra pol loss at 60°+

    def scan(self, elev_deg):
        return abs(90.0 - elev_deg - self.tilt)

    def _loss(self, scan_deg):
        c = math.cos(math.radians(min(scan_deg, 89.9)))
        pol = self.polar_wide * max(0.0, (scan_deg-30)/30)   # ramps 30→60°
        return 10*self.q*math.log10(c) - min(pol, self.polar_wide)

    def gt(self, elev):    return self.gt0   + self._loss(self.scan(elev))
    def eirp(self, elev):  return self.eirp0 + self._loss(self.scan(elev))

    def mask_ok(self, bw_hz, pattern, elev=30.0):
        e = self.eirp(elev)
        for th, rel in pattern:
            t = max(th, 1.5)
            m = (15-25*math.log10(t)) if t <= 7 else (-6 if t <= 9.2
                 else (18-25*math.log10(t) if t <= 48 else -24))
            if e - db(bw_hz/4e3) + rel > m:
                return False, th
        return True, None

    def arc_ok(self, arc_separation_deg, zone_deg=4.0):
        """GSO-arc avoidance: refuse the link inside the zone."""
        return arc_separation_deg >= zone_deg

    def curve(self):
        print(f"\n{'─'*54}")
        print(f"  SCAN/MASK MODEL — {self.name} (tilt {self.tilt}°)")
        print(f"{'─'*54}")
        print(f"  {'elev':>5} {'scan':>6} {'G/T':>7} {'EIRP':>7}")
        for e in [90, 60, 40, 25, 15]:
            print(f"  {e:>4}° {self.scan(e):>5.0f}° {self.gt(e):>7.1f}"
                  f" {self.eirp(e):>7.1f}")
        print(f"{'─'*54}")

flat   = ScanMaskModel("1,280-el panel, flat",   11.0, 36.2)
tilted = ScanMaskModel("1,280-el panel, tilted", 11.0, 36.2, tilt_deg=25)
flat.curve(); tilted.curve()

pat = [(1.5, -4), (3, -20.3), (7, -25), (20, -32)]
ok, at = tilted.mask_ok(250e6, pat)
print(f"\nmask check (250 MHz, tapered): {'complies' if ok else f'FAILS at {at}°'}")
for sep in [8, 4, 2]:
    print(f"arc separation {sep}°: {'link allowed' if tilted.arc_ok(sep) else 'AVOIDANCE — reselect satellite'}")
