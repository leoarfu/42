Ground Segment Architecture

QUARTER 1
FOUNDATIONS

You have spent two decades in ground segment. This lesson is not a primer on teleports or TT&C — it is a reframing exercise. The GEO ground segment is a small number of large antennas pointed at fixed slots, operated by people, sized by satellite count. The LEO ground segment inverts almost every one of those assumptions. The discriminators are constellation motion and constellation scale, and they turn the ground segment from a supporting system into a primary architectural battleground. By the end you should be able to state, with numbers, why a bent-pipe constellation needs dozens of gateways while an ISL constellation needs a handful — and why that single decision propagates into latency, landing rights, and capex.

1. What the Ground Segment Is — and the GEO→LEO Inversion

A satellite communications system decomposes into three segments: the space segment (the satellites), the user segment (the terminals, covered in Lesson 12), and the ground segment — everything else on Earth that makes the system function. For a communications constellation the ground segment contains five functional blocks, and it is worth naming them precisely because the rest of this lesson treats each in turn:

  • Gateways (feeder-link earth stations) — the antennas that carry user traffic between the constellation and the terrestrial network.
  • TT&C stations — telemetry, tracking, and command: the fleet's health-and-control nervous system.
  • The operations centres — the network operations centre (NOC) for the data plane and the satellite/mission operations centre (SOC/MOC) for the fleet.
  • Points of presence and backhaul — the terrestrial transport and peering that moves demodulated traffic to and from the public internet.
  • The cloud and edge tier — increasingly, compute and caching co-located with the gateway, blurring the line between ground segment and CDN.

None of these blocks is new to you. What is new is what happens to each one when the satellite stops sitting still.

The four inversions

Every instinct a GEO ground-segment architect carries inverts in LEO. Hold these four in mind; the entire lesson is an elaboration of them.

DimensionGEO ground segmentLEO ground segment
Antenna pointingPoint once, leave itContinuous tracking, handover every few minutes

Site count driverSatellite count (few)Coverage geometry + traffic + ISL topology

The GEO antenna is mechanically and operationally simple precisely because the satellite is stationary. A LEO satellite crosses the sky at 7–8 km/s and is visible from a given gateway for only a few minutes above a usable elevation angle before it sets and a successor must be acquired. A gateway therefore cannot be a single antenna — it must host several, so that one can acquire the next satellite before the current one drops below the horizon (make-before-break), while additional antennas carry simultaneous links for capacity. The single GEO dish becomes an antenna farm.

THE CONTACT IS NOW EPHEMERAL In GEO you provisioned a link and forgot about it. In LEO every link is temporary by construction. Continuity is an emergent property of scheduling and handover across an antenna farm, not a property of any single antenna. This is the conceptual hinge of the whole lesson: the ground segment's job shifts from maintaining links to orchestrating their continuous replacement.

2. Gateways: Feeder Links, Siting, and Diversity

The gateway is where user traffic enters and leaves the constellation. The link between gateway and satellite is the feeder link, and it is deliberately separated from the user link in most modern designs. Starlink, for example, runs user links in Ku-band and feeder links in Ka-band; the higher feeder frequency buys spectrum and supports the aggregate throughput of many users funneled through one gateway. The feeder link is the trunk; the user links are the branches.

What drives gateway siting

Gateway placement is a multi-constraint optimisation, and the constraints frequently conflict. The dominant drivers:

  1. RF propagation. Ka-band feeder links are rain-sensitive. Arid, high-altitude, low-precipitation sites give better link availability. The site's rain-rate climatology directly sets the link margin you must budget.
  2. Site diversity. Because rain fade is local, operators pair geographically separated gateways and switch the feeder traffic to whichever site has clear sky. Two sites 30–50 km apart rarely experience the same heavy rain cell simultaneously, so combined availability climbs toward the "four nines" that single sites cannot reach in wet climates.
  3. Backhaul proximity. A gateway is useless without fat fiber to a peering point. A perfect RF site stranded from terrestrial transport is a non-starter — this constraint frequently overrides the RF-optimal location.
  4. Regulatory and coordination. The site needs landing rights in the host country and frequency coordination against terrestrial fixed-service users and, critically, against the GSO arc — NGSO feeder links must respect EPFD limits and avoid interfering with geostationary systems. (Spectrum coordination and EPFD get their proper treatment in Lesson 14 and again in Q7.)

Why bent-pipe forces a dense gateway network

This is the architectural crux, and it connects directly to the ISL-versus-bent-pipe discussion from Lesson 1. In a bent-pipe constellation, a satellite has no way to forward traffic except straight back down to a gateway in its current field of view. The implication is unforgiving: to serve a user, the serving satellite must simultaneously see both the user and a gateway.

Over land with good fiber, that is manageable. Over an ocean, it is not — there is no place to put a gateway in the middle of the Pacific, so a bent-pipe constellation simply cannot serve mid-ocean users unless an island gateway happens to fall within the geometry. Polar coverage faces the same wall: gateways at extreme latitudes are operationally and politically expensive. OneWeb's Gen1 architecture, which deliberately omitted inter-satellite links, consequently required a global network on the order of forty gateway sites to stitch together its service area — and still inherited the ocean and polar gaps that gateway geography imposes.

The antenna farm and its sizing

Each gateway site is sized by two independent demands. First, continuity: enough antennas that handover from a setting satellite to a rising one never drops the feeder. Second, capacity: enough simultaneous satellite links to carry the aggregate user throughput funneled through that site. As a constellation densifies its user base, the limiting factor at a gateway shifts from continuity to capacity, and the antenna count grows accordingly. This is why a mature gateway is a fenced field of radomes rather than a single dish.

NOTE FOR THE GEO-TRAINED Your GEO teleport sized antennas by the number of satellites you operated. A LEO gateway sizes antennas by traffic and handover continuity — the satellite count is almost irrelevant to a single gateway, because each antenna is talking to a continuously changing satellite. The mental model "one antenna per bird" does not transfer.

3. TT&C at Constellation Scale

Telemetry, tracking, and command is the function that breaks most visibly under scale. Flying one GEO satellite, you schedule TT&C passes, a human reviews telemetry, commands are reviewed and uplinked. That workflow does not survive contact with a five-thousand-satellite fleet.

The arithmetic of the scaling wall

Consider the raw contact load. A LEO satellite at ~550 km makes roughly fifteen orbits per day, and a given ground station sees any individual satellite for only a few short passes. The number of TT&C contacts the system must schedule per day scales with the satellite count multiplied by passes per day. For a fleet in the thousands this is tens of thousands of contacts daily — orders of magnitude beyond what a human-staffed pass schedule can absorb.

What changes operationally

The response is automation, and it is not optional. Three shifts define modern constellation TT&C:

  • Autonomous orbit determination. Onboard GNSS receivers give each satellite its own ephemeris, collapsing the "tracking" workload that historically consumed ground resources. The ground no longer ranges every satellite; the satellite reports where it is.
  • Exception-based operations. Telemetry is processed by automated health monitoring; humans are engaged only when an anomaly trips a threshold. The NOC/SOC staff supervise a fleet rather than fly individual satellites — the same shift that turned data-centre operations from per-server administration into orchestration.
  • Contact scheduling as an optimisation problem. Which satellite talks to which antenna, when, becomes a continuous scheduling solver balancing TT&C needs, software uploads, and feeder capacity across the whole ground network.

TT&C antennas may be dedicated (often S-band) or folded into the gateway sites; mega-constellation operators tend to integrate TT&C into their gateway infrastructure rather than maintaining a separate physical network, since they already have antenna farms with constellation visibility.

THE OPERATIONS PARALLEL The transition from GEO to LEO fleet operations mirrors the transition from pets to cattle in IT infrastructure. A GEO satellite is a pet: named, individually monitored, hand-tended. A LEO constellation is a herd: managed by policy and automation, with individuals replaced rather than nursed. An architect who has internalised this analogy designs ground operations correctly; one who has not will try to scale a manual workflow and fail.

4. PoP Topology and Backhaul: Where the Bits Meet the Internet

A gateway demodulates the feeder link and recovers the user's IP packets. That is only half the job. Those packets must then reach the destination on the public internet — and the topology of that terrestrial leg determines a large fraction of the end-to-end latency and cost that the user actually experiences.

From gateway to PoP to peering

Behind each gateway sits terrestrial backhaul — fiber connecting the gateway to one or more points of presence where the operator interconnects with the rest of the internet, through transit providers, private peering, or an internet exchange (IXP). The PoP is where the operator's network meets everyone else's. Its placement is a deliberate choice: an operator wants its egress PoPs close to the content its users consume, which in practice means co-locating near major IXPs and the CDN caches of the large content providers.

This is why the low-latency promise of LEO is not delivered by the space segment alone. Shaving propagation delay to single-digit milliseconds in space is wasted if the gateway then backhauls traffic hundreds of kilometres to a distant PoP and the content sits three networks away. The full latency budget is space leg + feeder + backhaul + terrestrial transport to content. The ground segment owns three of those four terms.

THE LATENCY YOU SELL IS THE LATENCY YOU MEASURE END-TO-END A constellation can win the physics in orbit and still lose the user-perceived latency on the ground. Caching popular content at or near the gateway PoP — the cloud/edge tier of Section 5 — is frequently a larger latency win than any orbital optimisation, because it removes terrestrial network legs entirely.

Backhaul sizing

Backhaul capacity scales with the aggregate user throughput funneled through a gateway. A site aggregating tens or hundreds of gigabits per second of user demand needs commensurate fiber — another reason gateways gravitate to fiber-rich locations and another reason a remote RF-optimal site is often unusable. The feeder link and the backhaul must be capacity-matched; over-provisioning one without the other strands investment.

5. Ground-Station-as-a-Service and Cloud Convergence

The last decade produced a structural change in how ground segment is procured: you can now rent it. Cloud providers built antenna networks and exposed them as a service — AWS Ground Station, introduced around 2018, and Microsoft's Azure Orbital ground-station offering shortly after — letting an operator schedule antenna time by the minute and have the demodulated data delivered directly into cloud storage and compute, with no teleport to build.

Who uses it, and who does not

Ground-station-as-a-service transformed the economics for Earth-observation and small-fleet operators, who previously needed bespoke ground infrastructure disproportionate to their mission. For them the model removed a major capital barrier. Mega-constellation operators are a different case: at Starlink's scale, the feeder throughput and tight control requirements justify building proprietary gateways rather than renting. The build-versus-rent line falls roughly where aggregate feeder capacity and operational control outweigh the capital savings of outsourcing.

The convergence with terrestrial cloud

The more consequential trend for an architect is the merging of the ground segment with the cloud and CDN. Amazon's Kuiper is the explicit case discussed in Lesson 1: terminals and gateways designed to drop traffic directly onto AWS edge infrastructure, so that a latency-sensitive workload runs on a nearby AWS node rather than being hauled to a central data centre. The gateway stops being merely an RF demarcation point and becomes an edge PoP — a compute and caching site that happens to also have antennas.

For the systems architect the implication is that the ground-segment boundary is dissolving. The questions "where do I put a gateway," "where do I peer," and "where do I cache" are converging into one optimisation. A ground-segment design that treats the gateway as a dumb RF box and ignores the compute/CDN layer will be out-competed on user-perceived latency by one that does not.

6. How the Ground Segment Scales — and How ISLs Bend the Curve

Everything to this point converges on one decision with outsized leverage: inter-satellite links. The presence or absence of ISLs changes not just the space segment but the entire shape, cost, and political exposure of the ground segment.

The bent-pipe scaling curve

Without ISLs, gateway count is driven by the requirement that every serving satellite must see a gateway. To cover a global service area you need gateways distributed so that no serving satellite is ever without one in view — which forces gateways into inconvenient places (islands for ocean coverage, high latitudes for polar coverage) and multiplies the number of jurisdictions whose landing rights you must secure. The gateway network is large, geographically constrained, and politically complex.

The ISL scaling curve

With ISLs, traffic can route through space to a satellite that does have a gateway in view. The serving satellite no longer needs a local gateway; it needs only a path through the constellation to one. The consequences cascade favourably:

  • Far fewer gateways are required — a handful of well-chosen sites can serve a global constellation.
  • Those gateways can be clustered where it is convenient: fiber-rich, low-rain, friendly-jurisdiction locations.
  • Ocean and polar coverage become possible without local gateways, because traffic egresses through space to a continental gateway.
  • Landing-rights exposure shrinks — fewer host countries to negotiate with, which is a strategic and not merely an operational advantage.

THE TRADE IS CAPEX RELOCATION, NOT CAPEX ELIMINATION ISLs do not make the ground segment free — they move cost and complexity from the ground into the space segment. You trade a large, politically exposed gateway network for more expensive satellites carrying optical terminals. Whether that trade is worthwhile depends on your coverage requirements (do you need oceans and poles?), your tolerance for landing-rights negotiation, and your satellite unit economics. This is the single most consequential ground-segment decision an architect makes, and it is made when you choose the space architecture — which is exactly why ground segment cannot be designed after the constellation.

7. Implications for the Systems Architect

This lesson sits in the foundations quarter deliberately: the ground segment is not a downstream implementation detail, it is co-determined with the space architecture. Carry these four conclusions into the rest of the programme.

The ISL decision is a ground-segment decision

You will be tempted to treat inter-satellite links as a space-segment topic. Resist it. The ISL choice sets your gateway count, your siting freedom, your coverage envelope, and your landing-rights exposure. An architect who decides ISLs without modelling the ground-segment consequences has made a half-decision.

Gateways gravitate to fiber and dry sky, not to coverage

The RF-optimal gateway location and the buildable gateway location are usually different places, and backhaul usually wins. When you evaluate a constellation design, check that the gateway plan is consistent with terrestrial fiber and rain climatology — not just with satellite visibility geometry.

Operations must be autonomous from day one

The manual GEO operations workflow does not scale and cannot be retrofitted. Exception-based, automated fleet operations are a design requirement, not an optimisation. If a proposed ground-segment design assumes human-paced TT&C, it is not a constellation design.

The latency you sell is owned mostly by the ground

Three of the four terms in the end-to-end latency budget — feeder, backhaul, terrestrial transport — live in the ground segment. Edge caching and PoP placement frequently deliver larger user-perceived latency wins than orbital optimisation. Design the PoP and edge tier with the same rigour you give the orbit.

Exercise 1 — Gateway Pass Geometry

How long is a satellite usable to a gateway per pass, and how many antennas does continuity demand? Compute the maximum geometric pass duration for a circular orbit above a minimum elevation angle, and reason about antenna count from it.

import math

MU      = 398_600.4418     # Earth gravitational parameter, km^3/s^2
RE_KM   = 6_371            # mean Earth radius, km

def orbital_period_s(altitude_km):
    a = RE_KM + altitude_km
    return 2 * math.pi * math.sqrt(a**3 / MU)

def max_pass_duration_s(altitude_km, min_elev_deg):
    """
    Maximum geometric pass duration (overhead pass) above min elevation.
    Half-angle of the visibility cap as an Earth-central angle:
        rho = acos( Re*cos(el) / (Re+h) ) - el
    The ground track arc within the cap is traversed at the orbital rate.
    """
    el  = math.radians(min_elev_deg)
    rho = math.acos(RE_KM * math.cos(el) / (RE_KM + altitude_km)) - el
    T   = orbital_period_s(altitude_km)
    # Fraction of the orbit spent inside the +/- rho central-angle window:
    frac = (2 * rho) / (2 * math.pi)
    return frac * T

configs = [
    ("Starlink shell 2", 550),
    ("OneWeb",         1_200),
    ("Telesat LS",     1_100),
]

print(f"\n{'Constellation':<18} {'Period min':>11} {'Max pass @25deg':>16} {'@40deg':>9}")
print("-" * 56)
for name, alt in configs:
    T   = orbital_period_s(alt) / 60
    p25 = max_pass_duration_s(alt, 25)
    p40 = max_pass_duration_s(alt, 40)
    print(f"{name:<18} {T:>10.1f}  {p25:>14.0f}s {p40:>7.0f}s")

#    lasts ~P seconds with handover overhead H, sketch the minimum antenna

Expected: a 550 km satellite at 25° minimum elevation gives a maximum overhead pass on the order of a few minutes; higher altitude lengthens it. Real passes are shorter than the overhead maximum because most are not overhead. Continuity therefore needs at least two antennas just to hand over, before any capacity antennas are added.

Exercise 2 — Site Diversity and Rain Fade

Quantify why operators pair gateways. Model feeder-link availability for a single site and for a diversity pair under a simple independence assumption, then test how the benefit degrades as the sites become correlated.

def single_site_availability(outage_prob):
    """Availability of one gateway given its fade-outage probability."""
    return 1 - outage_prob

def diversity_availability(outage_prob, correlation=0.0):
    """
    Two paired sites. With independent fades, both must be down to lose
    the link: P(both down) = p^2. Correlation raises the joint outage
    toward the single-site value (fully correlated => no benefit).
    """
    p_indep = outage_prob ** 2
    p_joint = (1 - correlation) * p_indep + correlation * outage_prob
    return 1 - p_joint

print(f"\n{'Site outage p':>14} {'Single avail':>14} {'Pair (indep)':>14} {'Pair (rho=0.5)':>16}")
print("-" * 60)
for p in [0.05, 0.02, 0.01, 0.005, 0.001]:
    single = single_site_availability(p)
    pair0  = diversity_availability(p, 0.0)
    pair5  = diversity_availability(p, 0.5)
    print(f"{p:>14.3f} {single*100:>13.3f}% {pair0*100:>13.4f}% {pair5*100:>15.4f}%")

Exercise 3 — Bent-Pipe vs ISL Ground-Segment Economics

Force the Section 6 trade into numbers. Build a first-order cost comparison between a dense bent-pipe gateway network and a sparse ISL gateway network with an offsetting per-satellite optical-terminal premium. The point is not a precise answer — it is to find where the crossover sits and which variable dominates it.

def bentpipe_ground_cost(n_gateways, gw_capex, gw_annual_opex,
                         landing_rights_cost_per_jurisdiction,
                         n_jurisdictions, years):
    capex = n_gateways * gw_capex
    rights = n_jurisdictions * landing_rights_cost_per_jurisdiction
    opex  = n_gateways * gw_annual_opex * years
    return capex + rights + opex

def isl_total_cost(n_gateways, gw_capex, gw_annual_opex,
                   landing_rights_cost_per_jurisdiction, n_jurisdictions,
                   n_satellites, isl_premium_per_sat, years):
    ground = bentpipe_ground_cost(n_gateways, gw_capex, gw_annual_opex,
                                  landing_rights_cost_per_jurisdiction,
                                  n_jurisdictions, years)
    space_premium = n_satellites * isl_premium_per_sat
    return ground + space_premium

YEARS = 10

# Bent-pipe: many gateways, many jurisdictions, no ISL premium
bp = bentpipe_ground_cost(
    n_gateways = 45,
    gw_capex   = 8e6,
    gw_annual_opex = 1.2e6,
    landing_rights_cost_per_jurisdiction = 1.5e6,
    n_jurisdictions = 30,
    years = YEARS,
)

# ISL: few gateways, few jurisdictions, but pay optical terminals on every sat
isl = isl_total_cost(
    n_gateways = 8,
    gw_capex   = 8e6,
    gw_annual_opex = 1.2e6,
    landing_rights_cost_per_jurisdiction = 1.5e6,
    n_jurisdictions = 5,
    n_satellites = 1_500,
    isl_premium_per_sat = 120_000,
    years = YEARS,
)

print(f"\nBent-pipe 10-yr ground cost: ${bp/1e9:.2f}B")
print(f"ISL 10-yr (ground + space premium): ${isl/1e9:.2f}B")
print(f"Delta: ${(isl-bp)/1e9:.2f}B  -> {'ISL cheaper' if isl < bp else 'bent-pipe cheaper'}")

# Crossover: at what ISL premium per satellite do the two break even?
# Solve isl == bp for isl_premium_per_sat.
ground_isl = bentpipe_ground_cost(8, 8e6, 1.2e6, 1.5e6, 5, YEARS)
breakeven_premium = (bp - ground_isl) / 1_500
print(f"\nISL breakeven premium per satellite: ${breakeven_premium:,.0f}")
