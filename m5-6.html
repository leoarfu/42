Direct-to-Device
Physical Layer

MODULE 5
AIR INTERFACE & ACCESS
ADVANCED

Every link in this module so far assumed a cooperative terminal: a dish that points, an amplifier with watts to spend, an antenna with gain. Direct-to-device (D2D) removes the assumption. The terminal is an unmodified smartphone — designed for a cell tower two kilometres away, held at an arbitrary angle, next to a human head — and it will not change, because the entire commercial premise of D2D is serving the phones people already own. The handset's RF parameters are the fixed point of the design, and every decibel the link budget needs must be found on the space segment.

This lesson is Module 4 applied to its hardest case. The tools are all familiar — EIRP, G/T, FSPL, bandwidth, MODCOD floors — but the regime is extreme: the terminal is 40–47 dB worse than the VSAT of m4-1, and the system architecture that closes the gap (enormous apertures, narrowband carriers, waveforms operating below 0 dB SNR) looks nothing like broadband satcom. The lesson also resolves a thread left open in m5-3 and m5-5: how a satellite serves a phone that has GNSS but none of the Release-17 NTN protocol machinery — by making the network do the compensating, and making the beams small enough that it works.

The strategic frame to hold throughout: D2D is a coverage product, not a capacity product. The physics permits closing the link; it does not permit closing it with much spectral width or to many simultaneous users per beam. Section 8 does that arithmetic, and it disciplines every expectation upstream.

1. The fixed point: what a smartphone is, radiometrically

Typical handset RF characteristics at L/S-band, with the m4-1 VSAT alongside:

Parameter Smartphone VSAT terminal (m4-1) Gap
Transmit power23 dBm (0.2 W, power class 3)~3 dBW (2 W)10 dB

Polarisationlinear, orientation randommatched circular~3 dB budgeted

Three properties beyond the headline numbers matter. The antenna is unpointed: no gain can ever be recovered by aiming, and the −3 dBi figure already averages over orientations and body loss. The polarisation is linear and random against the satellite's circular, budgeting a ~3 dB mismatch permanently. And the platform is uncooperative in aggregate: the phone regulates its own power for battery and SAR compliance, and it implements exactly the protocol releases it shipped with.

Two terminal generations therefore exist. A Release-17 NTN handset implements m5-5's machinery — it computes its own timing advance and Doppler pre-compensation from GNSS plus broadcast ephemeris. A legacy handset has the GNSS but not the protocol: for it, the network must pre-compensate the beam-centre delay and Doppler on the satellite side, and the residuals across the beam must fit inside what legacy terrestrial tracking loops and random-access formats tolerate. Residual Doppler scales with beam size — of order a kilohertz across a tens-of-kilometres beam at 2 GHz — which makes small beams a protocol requirement, not just an antenna consequence. This is the design fork: UE-side compensation (new phones, Rel-17) versus network-side compensation (any phone, small beams, satellite does the work).

2. Where the decibels come from

The ~45 dB hole is filled from exactly four accounts:

Orbit altitude. LEO at 550 km instead of GEO: FSPL at 2 GHz is 158.5 dB at a 1,000 km design slant versus ~190 dB from GEO — roughly 32 dB recovered instantly (37 dB at high elevation), which is why D2D at scale is a LEO product. (MSS from GEO existed for decades — with phones that were not smartphones: external antennas, watts of transmit power, users who pointed them at the sky.)

Aperture. Antenna gain is proportional to area. At 2 GHz (λ = 15 cm), useful gain demands physically large structures — section 3 quantifies the scaling. This is the account the current generation of D2D operators is drawing on hardest, with deployable arrays from tens to hundreds of square metres reported publicly.

Bandwidth reduction. For a fixed C/N₀, SNR = C/N₀ − 10·log₁₀(B): every halving of carrier bandwidth buys 3 dB of SNR at proportional cost in rate. Narrowband is the poor man's antenna gain, purchased in throughput — section 4.

Waveform floor. Air-interface modes engineered for deeply negative SNR: DVB-S2X's VL-SNR MODCODs reach ≈ −10 dB Es/N₀ via spreading; NB-IoT buys margin with up to 128 repetitions; NR coverage enhancement stacks repetition similarly. All trade time for decibels — section 5.

Nothing else exists. Satellite transmit power helps the downlink but is bounded by the power subsystem and by regulatory flux limits toward the ground (section 7); frequency is fixed low by the handset's antennas and the allocations.

3. Aperture economics

Gain scales with area (G = 4πA/λ², ~70% efficiency assumed); beamwidth shrinks as diameter grows:

Array area Equiv. diameter Gain @ 2 GHz Beamwidth Nadir footprint (550 km)
8 m²3.2 m35.0 dBi3.3°~32 km
64 m²9.0 m44.0 dBi1.2°~11 km
220 m²16.7 m49.4 dBi0.6°~6 km

Every row is a spacecraft-engineering statement before it is an RF one: these are deployable structures in the Module-3 payload class, and area drives launch volume, deployment risk, attitude control, and cost. But the RF returns compound three ways. Gain serves both directions (uplink G/T and downlink EIRP). Small beams concentrate the scarce spectrum into small cells, multiplying areal capacity through reuse — the m3 frequency-reuse logic at maximum intensity. And small beams shrink the differential delay and Doppler that legacy handsets must absorb (section 1) — the aperture is doing protocol work. The single most important scaling law in D2D: capacity per unit area grows roughly with aperture area squared (once through gain, once through reuse density), which is why the operators' aperture race is rational.

4. The bandwidth lever, demonstrated

A worked downlink at 2 GHz, 1,000 km slant (FSPL 158.5 dB), 4 dB of polarisation and miscellaneous losses, to the handset's −30 dB/K:

Satellite beam EIRP C/N₀ SNR over 5 MHz SNR over 180 kHz
24 dBW60.1 dBHz−6.8 dB+7.6 dB
30 dBW66.1 dBHz−0.8 dB+13.6 dB
36 dBW72.1 dBHz+5.2 dB+19.6 dB

Read the middle row: at 30 dBW (1 kW EIRP — a serious but achievable per-beam figure for a large array), a 5 MHz NR carrier does not close, while a single 180 kHz resource block enjoys 13.6 dB. Same link, same C/N₀ — the carrier width is the dial. The system consequence: D2D carriers are narrow by design, capacity arrives as many narrow carriers across many small beams, and per-user peak rates are structurally modest. The uplink tells the same story with the satellite aperture as the variable: against a 64 m² array (G/T ≈ 17 dB/K), the handset's −10 dBW closes 180 kHz at ~20 dB SNR and even 1.4 MHz at ~12 dB — the big aperture converts a hopeless transmitter into a workable one.

5. The waveform floor

Below roughly 0 dB SNR, the m5-2 ladder runs out — QPSK 1/4 needs −2.35 dB Es/N₀ and nothing in the classic table goes deeper. Three mechanisms extend the floor:

Spreading (DVB-S2X VL-SNR). π/2-BPSK with spreading factors trades bandwidth for energy per symbol, reaching ≈ −10 dB Es/N₀. The S2X answer, built for exactly this regime.

Repetition (NB-IoT, NR coverage enhancement). Transmit the same block up to 128 times; the receiver combines coherently, gaining ~3 dB per doubling (up to channel-coherence limits). A 64-repetition NB-IoT uplink buys ~18 dB — which is how a sensor, or a text message, closes a link a voice call cannot.

Both are time-for-decibels trades. A message that costs 64 slots instead of one is fine for messaging and telemetry, marginal for voice, and disqualifying for broadband. The waveform floor thus maps directly onto the service tiers of section 8: each tier is a different point on the time-energy exchange.

6. Two link budgets, assembled

Pulling the accounts together for a mid-size (64 m²) array at 550 km, 2 GHz, 1,000 km design slant:

Downlink (satellite → phone): EIRP 30 dBW − FSPL 158.5 − losses 4 + G/T (−30) + 228.6 = C/N₀ 66.1 dBHz. Over one 180 kHz carrier: SNR 13.6 dB → comfortable for a few-hundred-kbps carrier; over 1.4 MHz: 4.7 dB → workable low-rate broadband; over 5 MHz: fails. The downlink is EIRP-constrained, and beam EIRP is the payload's scarcest export.

Uplink (phone → satellite): EIRP −10 dBW − 158.5 − 4 + G/T 17.0 + 228.6 = C/N₀ 73.2 dBHz — higher than the downlink. The giant receive aperture more than repays the handset's feeble transmitter. This reverses the broadband asymmetry of m4-7 (where the return link was the starved one): in D2D with a large array, the downlink is the binding constraint, because the handset's awful G/T has no aperture on its side of the link while the handset's awful EIRP does.

Both budgets carry the usual m4 machinery on top — elevation-dependent slant, and at L/S-band the atmospherics are mercifully mild (m4-3: rain is a Ka-band tax; 2 GHz shrugs at it), which is one quiet advantage of the D2D regime.

7. Spectrum: the narrowest resource

Two spectrum routes, with different physics attached:

MSS allocations (the m5-5 bands n255/n256 — L- and S-band mobile-satellite spectrum): designed for the job, internationally coordinated for space use, but scarce — of order tens of megahertz total, shared among systems.

Terrestrial partner spectrum — a mobile operator lends its licensed cellular band to a satellite partner (the "supplemental coverage from space" model, given a regulatory framework by the FCC in 2024 and mirrored elsewhere). The satellite transmits inside a terrestrial operator's band, which buys handset compatibility at the cost of hard coexistence constraints: power flux-density limits protecting the terrestrial network at the coverage boundary, per-market coordination, and carrier widths capped by what the partner can spare — typically 5 MHz-class blocks. The lesson's capacity arithmetic uses exactly such a block, because it is the realistic case.

Either way, D2D operates on one to two orders of magnitude less spectrum than a Ka-band broadband beam (5–30 MHz versus 250–400 MHz) — the root cause of section 8's conclusions.

8. The capacity arithmetic, and the elephant

Per-beam capacity for the realistic case — a 5 MHz partner-spectrum block, spectral efficiency ~1 bit/s/Hz at D2D SNRs (attenuated-Shannon at the section-4 operating points):

≈ 3–5 Mbps per beam, shared among every active user in a ~10–30 km cell (the spine's power-split model lands at ~3; a full-power-per-carrier idealisation at ~5).

Translate into services: messaging at ~1 kb per message → thousands of messages per second per beam — an excellent product, genuinely mass-market. Voice at ~10–20 kbps per call → a few hundred simultaneous calls per beam — viable, finite. Broadband at Mbps per user → a handful of users per beam — not a product, a demonstration. Frequency reuse across many small beams multiplies the totals (the §3 scaling), and larger apertures raise η — but the conclusion is robust across every plausible parameter set: D2D delivers coverage everywhere, not capacity anywhere. The commercial architecture follows: D2D as an MNO's dead-zone layer (messaging first, voice next, data last), priced as coverage insurance, while broadband stays with pointed terminals on Ka (m4, m5-1..4). An architect who lets a business plan assume otherwise has failed the module.

10. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

The spine gains Stage 6: a D2D link model with the handset constants frozen and the space segment as the only variable — aperture area, per-beam EIRP, carrier width — producing both directions' C/N₀, the widest carrier that closes at a target SNR, and the per-beam capacity that section 8's service tiers consume. It is the same engine as broadband, run at the far corner of its parameter space, and the corner inverts the familiar asymmetry: the downlink, not the uplink, binds.

Conceptually the lesson closes the module's terminal-side story: m5-1 through m5-4 assumed the terminal cooperates, m5-5 assumed it at least runs new software, m5-6 assumed nothing — and got, in exchange, the largest addressable device base in the industry and the smallest per-user capacity. m5-7 now assembles all six stages into the module's final artefact: throughput per beam, end to end, for any terminal class.

Exercise 2 — Aperture, beams, and the squared scaling

Quantify gain, footprint, and areal capacity together.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x)
lam, h = 0.15, 550e3

def array(A_m2, eff=0.7):
    G = db(4*math.pi*A_m2/lam**2 * eff)
    D = math.sqrt(4*A_m2/math.pi)
    bw = 70*lam/D                       # deg
    foot = 2*(h/1e3)*math.tan(math.radians(bw/2))
    return G, bw, foot

print(f"{'A (m2)':>7}{'G (dBi)':>9}{'beam':>7}{'foot (km)':>10}{'cells/1000km2':>15}")
for A in [8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 220]:
    G, bw, foot = array(A)
    cell_area = math.pi*(foot/2)**2
    print(f"{A:>7}{G:>9.1f}{bw:>6.2f}°{foot:>10.1f}{1000/cell_area:>15.2f}")

# Areal capacity ∝ area^2 check: per-cell rate grows with G (∝A),
# cells per km2 grow with 1/foot^2 (∝A) -> product ∝ A^2 (until
# the SNR->eta curve saturates).

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 6: the D2D link model

The engine visits the far corner of its parameter space.

import math

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M5 SPINE PROJECT — THROUGHPUT ENGINE
# Stage 6: direct-to-device.
#   Handset constants frozen; the space segment is the only
#   variable. Outputs: both directions' C/N0, widest closing
#   carrier, per-beam capacity for the §8 service tiers.
# Next: m5-7 assembles stages 1-6 into throughput-per-beam.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x)

HANDSET = {"eirp_dbw": -10.0, "gt_dbk": -30.0}   # the fixed point
CARRIERS_HZ = [5e6, 1.4e6, 720e3, 360e3, 180e3]  # NR-ish widths

class D2DLink:
    def __init__(self, name, array_m2, beam_eirp_dbw, f_ghz=2.0,
                 slant_km=1000.0, t_sys_sat=500.0, losses_db=4.0):
        self.name = name
        self.fspl = 92.45 + 20*math.log10(slant_km) + 20*math.log10(f_ghz)
        lam = 0.3/f_ghz
        g_sat = db(4*math.pi*array_m2/lam**2 * 0.7)
        self.sat_gt = g_sat - db(t_sys_sat)
        self.dl_cn0 = beam_eirp_dbw - self.fspl - losses_db \
                      + HANDSET["gt_dbk"] + 228.6
        self.ul_cn0 = HANDSET["eirp_dbw"] - self.fspl - losses_db \
                      + self.sat_gt + 228.6

    def widest_carrier(self, cn0, target_snr_db=4.0):
        for bw in CARRIERS_HZ:
            if cn0 - db(bw) >= target_snr_db:
                return bw
        return None

    def eta(self, snr_db, alpha=0.75, cap=7.406):   # m5-5 abstraction
        return min(cap, alpha*math.log2(1 + 10**(snr_db/10)))

    def report(self, spectrum_hz=5e6):
        print(f"\n{'─'*56}")
        print(f"  D2D LINK — {self.name}")
        print(f"{'─'*56}")
        for label, cn0 in [("DOWNLINK", self.dl_cn0), ("UPLINK  ", self.ul_cn0)]:
            bw = self.widest_carrier(cn0)
            if bw:
                snr = cn0 - db(bw)
                rate = bw*self.eta(snr)
                print(f"  {label}  C/N0 {cn0:5.1f} dBHz | widest carrier "
                      f"{bw/1e3:6.0f} kHz | SNR {snr:5.1f} dB | {rate/1e3:7.0f} kbps")
            else:
                print(f"  {label}  C/N0 {cn0:5.1f} dBHz | no carrier closes at target")
        # per-beam capacity: fill the block with the binding direction
        cn0 = min(self.dl_cn0, self.ul_cn0)
        snr_full = cn0 - db(spectrum_hz)
        cap_narrow = 0
        bw = self.widest_carrier(cn0)
        if bw:
            n = int(spectrum_hz // bw)
            # Total beam power splits across n carriers: per-carrier
            # C/N0 drops by 10*log10(n). (Exercise 1 interrogates this.)
            snr_split = cn0 - db(n) - db(bw)
            cap_narrow = n * bw * self.eta(snr_split)
        print(f"  BEAM ({spectrum_hz/1e6:.0f} MHz): binding dir C/N0 {cn0:.1f} dBHz"
              f" -> ~{cap_narrow/1e6:5.2f} Mbps/beam")
        print(f"  tiers: ~{cap_narrow/1e3:.0f} msgs/s @1kb | "
              f"~{cap_narrow/15e3:.0f} voice calls @15kbps")
        print(f"{'─'*56}")

# ── The aperture sweep ──────────────────────────────────
for A, eirp in [(8, 24), (64, 30), (220, 36)]:
    D2DLink(f"{A} m² array, {eirp} dBW/beam", A, eirp).report()

PROMPT 01

Section 1 froze the handset and section 2 enumerated exactly four decibel accounts. A colleague proposes a fifth: cooperative combining, where several satellites illuminate one handset coherently. Assess it honestly — what synchronisation accuracy does coherent combining across LEO baselines require (express in picoseconds against the 23 µs/s delay drift of m5-3), what does incoherent combining still buy, and why has no operator led with this?

PROMPT 02

Section 6 found the downlink binding for a large-array system. Invert the architecture: a small-array constellation (8 m² class) with many more satellites. Rerun the §6 logic — which direction binds now, what does the §3 squared-scaling say about areal capacity per unit of total deployed aperture, and under what launch-cost regime does the many-small architecture win? This is a genuine open trade in the industry; commit to a position and defend it with the module's numbers.

PROMPT 03

The §8 tier arithmetic prices messaging as the natural first product. Regulators in several jurisdictions are moving toward mandating satellite emergency messaging capability in handsets or networks. Analyse the mandate's effect through this lesson's lenses: what does guaranteed-availability emergency service require that best-effort messaging does not (which m4-4 statistic returns), what fraction of a 5 Mbps beam must be held in reserve, and who pays for reserved capacity that is almost never used?

PROMPT 04

This module's six lessons priced the terminal three times: the pointed VSAT (m5-1..4), the Rel-17 NTN device (m5-5), and the frozen handset (m5-6), with per-beam capacities spanning two orders of magnitude. Construct the unified statement: express beam capacity as a function of terminal G/T and available spectrum only, holding the space segment fixed, and determine how much of the industry's segmentation into "broadband," "mobility," and "D2D" markets is just this one function evaluated at three points.
