1. Interference in the Link Equation

When the receive terminal receives the desired signal plus an undesired interfering signal, the effective noise floor increases. With desired carrier power C and total interference power I, the composite noise floor is:

N_eff = N₀ · B + I

The carrier-to-noise-plus-interference ratio:

C/(N+I) = C / (N + I)

A convenient reformulation uses separate C/N and C/I terms:

1/(C/(N+I)) = 1/(C/N) + 1/(C/I)

In the link budget, C/(N₀+I₀) where I₀ is the interference spectral density (W/Hz) gives the effective noise density N₀_eff = N₀ + I₀ = k·T_sys + I₀. This is the "interference as noise" model — valid when the interference is uncorrelated with the desired signal, the case for most satellite interference scenarios.

2. Carrier-to-Interference Ratio: The Geometry

C/I for a satellite link depends on the geometry between the desired satellite, the interfering transmitter, and the receive terminal. The key quantity is the ratio of antenna gain toward the desired direction versus the gain toward the interfering source.

For downlink interference (an interfering satellite on the same frequency):

C/I_dB = (EIRP_desired + G_rx(θ_desired)) − (EIRP_interferer + G_rx(θ_interferer))

For a directional ground terminal pointing at the desired satellite, G_rx(θ_desired) = G_peak while G_rx(θ_interferer) is the sidelobe gain toward the interferer. The discrimination comes from the terminal's sidelobe pattern.

For satellite-to-satellite interference (the spatial reuse scenario from m3-5):

C/I_dB ≈ G_sat_main − G_sat_sidelobe(Δθ)

As derived in m3-5: C/I ≈ 20–25 dB for a well-designed phased array with −25 dB sidelobes and co-channel beams separated by two beam diameters. The ITU-R S.580 reference earth station pattern provides a standardised off-axis gain envelope used in coordination calculations between systems.

3. Within-Constellation Interference

Co-channel beams within the same satellite

As covered in m3-5, adjacent spot beams using the same frequency colour create co-channel interference. For a four-colour reuse pattern, co-channel beams are separated by at least two beam diameters, with C/I typically 20–25 dB for a well-designed array — the intra-satellite co-channel case.

Adjacent satellite interference within the constellation

When multiple constellation satellites are simultaneously visible from the terminal, signals from a non-serving satellite arrive as interference. The C/I depends on the interfering satellite's antenna gain toward the terminal (narrow spot beams provide substantial sidelobe isolation outside their service area) and the angular separation between desired and interfering satellites as seen from the terminal.

For a well-designed LEO constellation with tight spot beams (0.3–0.5°) and good sidelobe control, adjacent-satellite interference within the constellation is typically 25–35 dB below the desired signal — a fixed, manageable C/I term in the link budget.

THE GEOMETRY AT 550 KM Two adjacent Starlink satellites in the same orbital plane are separated by approximately 1,600 km. When a terminal sits midway between two satellites at similar elevations, the angular separation it sees may be only 10–30°. The terminal's ~0.5°-beam phased array has sidelobes of approximately −25 to −30 dB at this angle — providing 25–30 dB of C/I isolation.

4. Inter-System Interference: LEO-to-LEO

When two different LEO broadband constellations (e.g. Starlink and Kuiper) share the same Ka-band allocation, their satellites can interfere with each other's users — fundamentally the same geometry as within-constellation interference, but without guaranteed coordination.

Mitigation mechanisms: different inclinations and altitudes — Starlink (53°, 550 km) and Kuiper (51.9°, 590 km) drift relative to each other due to differing orbital periods, reducing persistent conjunction geometries; frequency coordination — ITU coordination between operators can assign different sub-bands or temporal sharing agreements; pointing exclusion zones — a serving constellation's beams may be required to avoid pointing toward a terminal already served by another operator's visible satellite.

In practice, inter-system LEO interference is managed through bilateral coordination agreements and ITU spectrum coordination — a regulatory process still evolving as the number of large LEO constellations grows.

5. LEO-to-GEO Interference: The EPFD Framework

The most heavily regulated interference category is NGSO (LEO) constellations interfering with GSO (GEO) satellite systems sharing the same band, addressed through the ITU's equivalent power flux density (EPFD) framework.

What EPFD measures

EPFD is the aggregate power flux density at a GEO Earth station from all NGSO satellites transmitting in the shared band, summed over every visible NGSO satellite, accounting for each satellite's transmit power, its antenna gain in the direction of the GEO Earth station (usually the sidelobe), the distance from each satellite to the station, and the reference bandwidth used for comparison with the ITU limit.

ITU EPFD limits

DirectionITU limit (Ka-band, typical)
EPFD_down (NGSO transmit, GEO Rx)−160 dBW/m²/Hz
EPFD_up (NGSO terminal transmit)−120.5 dBW/m²/Hz
EPFD_is (into space station)−168 dBW/m²/Hz

These limits are statistical — they must not be exceeded for more than a specified percentage of time (typically 0.001% for worst-case instantaneous limits, 0.01–0.1% for time-averaged limits).

Operational EPFD management

Beam blanking: when a LEO satellite's beam would illuminate a GEO Earth station, the beam is turned off or steered away, creating a capacity dead zone around that station. Power control: reducing transmit power in the vicinity of GEO stations, at the cost of reduced margin for nearby LEO users. Orbital geometry exploitation: where possible, constellation parameters minimise time in the interference geometry with high-usage GEO stations.

The fraction of capacity lost to EPFD management depends on the geographic distribution of GEO Earth stations relative to the LEO service area. In regions with many GEO stations (North America, Europe), beam blanking creates non-trivial capacity losses — estimated at 2–10% of total capacity in heavily coordinated bands.

6. GEO-to-LEO Interference

GEO satellites sharing the same frequency band also interfere with LEO user terminals. When the GEO and LEO satellite are at similar azimuth angles from the terminal, the GEO signal enters the LEO terminal's antenna sidelobes and adds interference. The magnitude depends on GEO satellite EIRP, the path loss difference (GEO at 35,786 km vs LEO at 550 km), and the terminal's off-axis discrimination.

At GEO-to-terminal distances of ~36,000 km vs LEO-to-terminal distances of ~600–2,000 km, the GEO signal arrives with approximately 20·log₁₀(36,000/1,000) ≈ 31 dB more path loss than the LEO signal. A high-EIRP GEO satellite (50–60 dBW EIRP) can still produce meaningful interference depending on geometry.

Managed through frequency separation between GEO and LEO allocations where the ITU plan allows, terminal antenna sidelobe pattern standards ensuring sufficient off-axis rejection, and scheduling of LEO service to avoid geometries where the GEO satellite is close to the LEO satellite in the sky.

7. Incorporating C/I Into the Link Budget

C/I enters the link budget as an addition to the effective noise floor. Given C/N₀ (thermal noise only) and C/I₀ (interference spectral density):

C/(N₀+I₀)_dBHz = −10·log₁₀(10^(−C/N₀/10) + 10^(−C/I₀/10))

where C/I₀_dBHz = C/I_dB + 10·log₁₀(B_interferer). For typical LEO broadband scenarios where C/I ≈ 25 dB over a 250 MHz carrier, C/I₀ = 25 + 84 = 109 dBHz. This is comparable to — not far below — the thermal C/N₀ of a strong clear-sky link (108–112 dBHz), and combining the two degrades C/(N₀+I₀) by 2–4.5 dB relative to C/N₀ alone. Interference becomes a second-order effect only when the thermal C/N₀ sits well below C/I₀ — which is exactly the situation under a deep rain fade, when the noise floor has risen. Worth fixing in memory: co-channel interference costs the most in clear sky, when the link is trying to run its highest MODCOD, and the least during the fades that dominate the availability analysis.

When C/I approaches or falls below C/N₀ — interference comparable to or stronger than thermal noise — the interference term dominates and must be managed through the frequency reuse and interference suppression techniques from m3-5.

Exercise 1 — C/I Calculation and the Interference Floor

Compute C/I for various interference scenarios and determine when interference becomes the limiting factor.

import math

def lin(db): return 10**(db/10)
def db(x):   return 10*math.log10(x)

def combined_cn0_i(cn0_dbhz, ci_db, b_interferer_hz):
    """
    C/I₀ = C/I + 10·log10(B_interferer)  [dBHz]
    1/(C/(N₀+I₀)) = 1/(C/N₀) + 1/(C/I₀)
    """
    ci0 = ci_db + 10*math.log10(b_interferer_hz)
    combined_inv = 1/lin(cn0_dbhz) + 1/lin(ci0)
    return db(1/combined_inv), db(1/lin(cn0_dbhz)), db(1/lin(ci0))

print("Combined C/(N₀+I₀) — Ka-band LEO forward link\n")
print(f"{'C/I (dB)':>10}{'C/I₀ (dBHz)':>14}{'C/N₀ (dBHz)':>14}"
      f"{'C/(N+I)₀':>12}{'Degradation':>13}")
print("-" * 64)
cn0 = 108.9   # from m4-1 example
B   = 250e6   # interferer bandwidth Hz

for ci in [35, 30, 25, 20, 15, 12, 10]:
    combined, _, _ = combined_cn0_i(cn0, ci, B)
    degrad = cn0 - combined
    ci0 = ci + 10*math.log10(B)
    print(f"{ci:>10}{ci0:>14.1f}{cn0:>14.1f}{combined:>12.1f}{degrad:>11.2f} dB")

print("\nNote: C/I must fall below ~C/N₀ − B_dBHz to significantly degrade the link")

Exercise 2 — EPFD Calculation

Compute the EPFD from a LEO constellation at a GEO Earth station and check against the ITU limit.

import math

EPFD_LIMIT_DB = -160.0   # dBW/m²/Hz — approximate ITU Ka-band downlink limit
C = 3e8

def fspl(d_km, f_ghz):
    lam = C / (f_ghz*1e9)
    return (4*math.pi*d_km*1e3 / lam)**2

def epfd_pfd(eirp_dbw, d_km, f_ghz, bw_hz):
    path_loss = fspl(d_km, f_ghz)
    return 10**(eirp_dbw/10) / (path_loss * bw_hz)

def total_epfd(sats, f_ghz, bw_hz):
    """sats: list of (eirp_dbw, distance_km). Returns total EPFD in dBW/m²/Hz."""
    total = sum(epfd_pfd(e,d,f_ghz,bw_hz) for e,d in sats)
    return 10*math.log10(total) if total>0 else -300

f = 20.0; bw = 250e6

scenarios = [
    ("3 sats, beam ON GEO station (eirp=50dBW)",
     [(50,700),(45,1200),(43,1800)]),
    ("3 sats, beam NOT on station (eirp=25dBW, -25dB SL)",
     [(25,700),(20,1200),(18,1800)]),
    ("10 sats, beam not on station (eirp=25dBW)",
     [(25,700+i*200) for i in range(10)]),
    ("30 sats, beam not on station (eirp=20dBW)",
     [(20,700+i*100) for i in range(30)]),
]

print(f"EPFD at GEO Earth station — Ka {f} GHz, BW={bw/1e6:.0f} MHz\n")
print(f"{'Scenario':<46}{'EPFD':>14}{'Margin':>10}")
print("-"*72)
for name, sats in scenarios:
    epfd = total_epfd(sats, f, bw)
    margin = EPFD_LIMIT_DB - epfd
    print(f"{name:<46}{epfd:>13.1f}dB{margin:>+9.1f}dB")
print(f"\nITU limit: {EPFD_LIMIT_DB} dBW/m²/Hz")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 6: C/I Into the Link Budget

Extend the LinkBudget engine with interference terms — the penultimate spine stage before the final synthesis in m4-7.

import math

K_DB = -228.6; T0 = 290.0

def fspl(d, f): return 92.4 + 20*math.log10(d) + 20*math.log10(f)
def nf_te(nf): return T0*(10**(nf/10)-1)
def loss_te(l, t=T0): return t*(10**(l/10)-1)
def dbl(x): return 10**(x/10)

RAIN_ZONES = {'E':22,'F':28,'G':30,'K':42,'N':95,'P':145}  # ITU-R P.837 R0.01

def rain_specific(R, f):
    P = {14:(.0229,1.265),20:(.0751,1.099),30:(.187,1.021),47:(.546,.911)}
    k,a = min(P.items(),key=lambda x:abs(x[0]-f))[1]
    return k*R**a

def rain_atten(zone, f, elev, avail=99.9):
    # Simplified P.618: A0.01 first, then scale the ATTENUATION (m4-4).
    R001=RAIN_ZONES.get(zone.upper(),28); p=max(100-avail, 0.01)
    e=math.radians(elev); Ls=3.9/math.sin(e)
    L0=35.0*math.exp(-0.015*R001)
    A001=rain_specific(R001,f)*Ls*(1/(1+Ls*math.cos(e)/L0))
    if p==0.01: return A001
    return A001*(p/0.01)**(-(0.655+0.033*math.log(p)-0.045*math.log(A001)))

class LinkBudget:
    """Stage 6: Full propagation + interference."""
    def __init__(self, name):
        self.name=name; self.losses=[]; self._chain=[]
        self._eirp=self._fspl=self._grx=self._tant=self._req=None
        self._freq=None; self._elev=None; self._zone=None
        self._scint=0.0; self._ci_db=None; self._ci_bw=None

    def set_transmitter(self, p, g): self._eirp=10*math.log10(p)+g
    def set_path(self, d, f, elev=30):
        self._fspl=fspl(d,f); self._freq=f; self._elev=elev
    def add_loss(self, lbl, v): self.losses.append((lbl,v))
    def set_antenna(self, g, tant): self._grx=g; self._tant=tant
    def add_stage(self, lbl, te, g=None): self._chain.append((lbl,te,g))
    def set_required(self, r): self._req=r
    def set_rain_zone(self, z): self._zone=z
    def set_scintillation(self, db): self._scint=db
    def set_interference(self, ci_db, bw_hz):
        self._ci_db=ci_db; self._ci_bw=bw_hz

    def _tsys(self, rain_db=0):
        tant=self._tant+(273*(1-10**(-rain_db/10)) if rain_db else 0)
        t=tant; cg=1.0
        for _,te,g in self._chain:
            t+=te/cg
            if g: cg*=dbl(g)
        return t

    def compute(self, avail=None):
        rain_db = rain_atten(self._zone,self._freq,self._elev,avail) if (avail and self._zone) else 0
        t = self._tsys(rain_db)
        total_path = self._fspl + sum(v for _,v in self.losses) + rain_db + self._scint
        c_dbw  = self._eirp - total_path + self._grx
        n0_dbhz = 10*math.log10(t) + K_DB
        cn0_thermal = c_dbw - n0_dbhz
        if self._ci_db is not None:
            ci0 = self._ci_db + 10*math.log10(self._ci_bw)
            cn0_eff = -10*math.log10(dbl(-cn0_thermal) + dbl(-ci0))
        else:
            cn0_eff = cn0_thermal
        margin = cn0_eff - self._req if self._req else None
        return cn0_eff, margin, t, rain_db

    def print_full(self, avail=99.9):
        cn0, m, t, r = self.compute(avail)
        ci_str = f"C/I={self._ci_db}dB" if self._ci_db else "no interference"
        print(f"\n  {self.name}")
        print(f"  Avail={avail}%  Zone={self._zone}  Rain={r:.1f}dB  "
              f"Scint={self._scint}dB  {ci_str}")
        print(f"  T_sys={t:.0f}K  C/N₀+I₀={cn0:.1f}dBHz  Margin={m:+.1f}dB  "
              f"{'✓' if m>=0 else '✗'}")

lb = LinkBudget("LEO Ka forward — all impairments stage 6")
lb.set_transmitter(20, 34); lb.set_path(1000, 20.0, 30)
lb.add_loss("Gaseous abs", 0.5); lb.add_loss("Pointing", 0.5)
lb.set_antenna(37.5, 30.0)
lb.add_stage("Feed 0.3dB",   loss_te(0.3),-0.3)
lb.add_stage("LNA NF=1 G=40",nf_te(1.0),  40.0)
lb.add_stage("Downconv NF=8",nf_te(8.0),  20.0)
lb.add_stage("IF NF=10",     nf_te(10.0), None)
lb.set_rain_zone('F'); lb.set_scintillation(0.7)
lb.set_interference(ci_db=25, bw_hz=250e6)
lb.set_required(93.4)  # 16-APSK 3/4 @ 208.3 Msym/s (Es/N0 10.21 dB + 83.2 dBHz)

for avail in [99.0, 99.5, 99.9, 99.95, 99.99]:
    lb.print_full(avail)
