1. Orbital Mechanics Fundamentals

Before comparing regimes, you need a working understanding of what determines an orbit. This section is not a full orbital mechanics course — that comes in Q2 — but it establishes the vocabulary and relationships you need to reason about altitude tradeoffs.

Kepler's first and third laws

Johannes Kepler's first law states that satellite orbits are ellipses with Earth at one focus. For commercial communications satellites, nearly all operational orbits are circular or near-circular — the two foci coincide at Earth's centre, the orbit has constant altitude, and the satellite moves at constant speed. Circular orbits simplify coverage analysis, link budget calculations, and ground antenna pointing, so they dominate in practice.

Kepler's third law establishes the fundamental relationship between orbital altitude and period: the square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis (for circular orbits, the orbital radius). In practical terms: the higher the orbit, the slower the satellite moves and the longer its orbital period. At GEO altitude, the period is exactly 24 hours — synchronised with Earth's rotation. At LEO altitudes of 500–600 km, the period is roughly 95–97 minutes. A LEO satellite therefore completes about 15 orbits per day.

The orbital velocity follows directly: a satellite must travel fast enough that the centripetal acceleration required to stay on the circular path equals Earth's gravitational acceleration at that altitude. At 550 km altitude, this works out to approximately 7.6 km/s. At GEO altitude, it is approximately 3.07 km/s. This velocity directly governs how quickly a LEO satellite transits the sky as seen from a ground station — the source of the handover challenge discussed in Lesson 1.

Inclination and coverage

Orbital inclination is the angle between the orbital plane and Earth's equatorial plane. A zero-inclination orbit is equatorial — the satellite stays above the equator. A 90° inclination is polar — the satellite passes over both poles. Inclinations between 0° and 90° are prograde (moving roughly eastward); inclinations between 90° and 180° are retrograde (moving roughly westward).

For communications constellations, inclination is one of the most consequential design choices because it determines the geographic distribution of coverage. A constellation with 53° inclination — like Starlink's primary shell — provides coverage up to approximately 53° latitude with denser revisit near that band and at the equator, and sparser coverage near 53° itself due to the geometry of inclined orbits. To serve polar regions consistently, you need high-inclination or polar orbits. This is why Starlink has a dedicated polar shell at 97.6° inclination (sun-synchronous) and OneWeb deployed all its satellites in near-polar orbits.

The two-body problem and perturbations

In the idealised two-body problem — one satellite orbiting one perfectly spherical, uniform Earth in a vacuum — orbits are perfect Keplerian ellipses that repeat indefinitely. Real orbits deviate from this ideal due to perturbations: Earth's equatorial bulge (J2 term), atmospheric drag at lower altitudes, solar radiation pressure, third-body effects from the Moon and Sun, and electromagnetic forces. For a systems architect, the most operationally important perturbations are atmospheric drag (dominant below 600 km) and J2-induced precession of the orbital plane (relevant for coverage uniformity in large constellations). These are treated quantitatively in the Python exercises below.

2. The Four Regimes Defined

There is no universally standardised definition of orbital regime boundaries — different organisations and textbooks draw lines at slightly different altitudes. The definitions below reflect common industry usage and the boundaries that matter for the tradeoffs in this lesson.

Very Low Earth Orbit (VLEO): 160–450 km

VLEO is the regime closest to Earth, beginning just above the point where atmospheric drag becomes too severe for sustained orbit without continuous propulsion (roughly 160 km) and extending up to approximately 450 km. At these altitudes, orbital period is 87–93 minutes. Satellites experience significant atmospheric drag — even at 400 km, the residual atmosphere is dense enough to require regular reboost manoeuvres or continuous low-thrust propulsion to maintain altitude. The International Space Station orbits at approximately 400–410 km and requires periodic reboosts using visiting spacecraft or its own thrusters.

The communications case for VLEO is compelling on paper: propagation delay drops to 1–3 ms one-way, free-space path loss is dramatically lower than LEO (improving link margins or reducing terminal size and power), and Earth observation resolution improves. The case against is equally stark: current satellite propulsion systems make continuous drag compensation expensive in propellant mass and electric power, limiting satellite lifetime severely unless very efficient ion propulsion is used. No commercial communications constellation currently operates at VLEO altitudes, though several research projects and startups — including Skimsat and VLEO Tech — are actively working on the propulsion problem.

Low Earth Orbit (LEO): 450–2,000 km

LEO is the primary regime for modern broadband constellations. The altitude band is wide — from 450 km where drag becomes manageable with periodic chemical or electric thruster burns, to approximately 2,000 km where the inner Van Allen belt radiation begins to cause unacceptable component degradation for most satellite designs. Within this band there is significant architectural variation. Starlink operates multiple shells: the lowest at approximately 340–370 km (requiring continuous propulsion), the main operational shell at 540–560 km, and supplementary shells at higher altitudes. OneWeb operates at 1,200 km. The choice of specific altitude within LEO reflects a tradeoff among drag, radiation, coverage footprint, and latency.

Orbital period at LEO altitudes ranges from about 93 minutes (at 450 km) to about 127 minutes (at 2,000 km). A satellite at 550 km is visible from a ground station for approximately 8–12 minutes per pass, depending on the minimum elevation angle. This is the fundamental driver of the handover problem: a terminal must hand off from one satellite to the next every 8–12 minutes, continuously, for the lifetime of the service.

Medium Earth Orbit (MEO): 2,000–35,000 km

MEO is the regime between LEO and GEO, encompassing two very different populations of satellites. The first population is navigation satellites: GPS (20,200 km), GLONASS (19,100 km), Galileo (23,222 km), and BeiDou (MEO component at 21,528 km) all orbit in MEO because this altitude gives each satellite a large ground footprint enabling global coverage with relatively few spacecraft (24–30 for most GNSS systems), while maintaining sufficient signal strength for the precision timing required. Navigation is explicitly latency-insensitive — GNSS receivers need precise timing but do not care about propagation delay in the communications sense.

The second MEO population is broadband communications, specifically SES's O3b constellation (now O3b mPOWER). O3b satellites orbit at approximately 8,062 km altitude in an equatorial inclined orbit at 0°. At this altitude, one-way propagation delay is approximately 125 ms — significantly better than GEO's 240 ms, and the coverage footprint per satellite is large enough that O3b can cover most of the populated tropics and mid-latitudes with a small constellation. The tradeoff: latency is better than GEO but still worse than LEO, and the equatorial inclination limits coverage to approximately ±50° latitude. MEO broadband has found a niche in maritime and enterprise markets where the latency advantage over GEO is valuable but LEO constellations are not yet available.

The zone between approximately 1,500 km and 6,000 km is avoided for long-lifetime missions because it corresponds to the most intense part of the inner Van Allen belt. Satellites in this zone accumulate radiation damage rapidly. A GEO satellite has a design life of 15+ years with radiation-hardened electronics; a LEO satellite at 550 km might achieve 5–7 years. A satellite parked at 3,000 km in the heart of the inner belt might degrade in 1–2 years without extraordinary shielding.

Geostationary Earth Orbit (GEO): 35,786 km

GEO is not a band but a specific altitude at which the orbital period exactly matches Earth's sidereal rotation period of 23 hours, 56 minutes, and 4 seconds. A satellite in a circular, zero-inclination orbit at this altitude appears stationary from the ground. The geostationary arc is therefore a finite shared resource — satellites must be separated by sufficient angular distance (typically 2° for co-frequency operation) to avoid interference, and slots on the arc are allocated and coordinated by the ITU.

Because GEO requires zero inclination and the orbital radius is fixed, the only degrees of freedom for a GEO operator are the longitudinal slot (east-west position on the arc) and the frequency/polarisation plan. The arc is densely populated — several hundred operational satellites — and the most valuable slots (those that provide good coverage of major population centres and broadcast markets) are fully occupied. New GEO operators must obtain slots from the ITU filing process, which can take years and requires demonstrating that the satellite will not cause unacceptable interference to existing operators.

Above GEO, beyond the outer Van Allen belt, lies a region sometimes called High Earth Orbit (HEO) or super-GEO, used by highly elliptical orbits such as Molniya orbits for Arctic coverage. These are specialised mission types not relevant to broadband constellations and not covered in detail here.

3. Coverage Geometry: Footprints, Elevation Angles, and the Horizon

Coverage geometry determines how many satellites you need, what elevation angles ground terminals experience, and how handover timing works. It is purely a function of orbital altitude and the minimum elevation angle your system requires.

The instantaneous coverage footprint

The coverage footprint of a satellite is the region of Earth's surface from which the satellite is visible above a given minimum elevation angle. For a circular orbit at altitude h, the half-angle of the coverage cone (measured at Earth's centre) is:

COVERAGE HALF-ANGLE FORMULA ρ = arccos(Rₑ · cos(ε) / (Rₑ + h)) − ε

where ρ is the Earth central half-angle, Rₑ = 6,371 km is Earth's mean radius, h is altitude in km, and ε is the minimum elevation angle in radians. The coverage area as a fraction of Earth's total surface is (1 − cos(ρ)) / 2.

Working through the numbers: a Starlink satellite at 550 km with a 25° minimum elevation angle covers approximately 1.3% of Earth's surface at any instant. Three GEO satellites at 10° minimum elevation cover roughly 93% of Earth's surface between ±75° latitude. This is why GEO achieved near-global coverage with three satellites while Starlink requires thousands — LEO satellites see a dramatically smaller slice of Earth at any moment.

The practical implication for constellation sizing goes beyond instantaneous coverage. A commercial service requires not just that a satellite be visible somewhere in the sky, but that it be visible above the minimum elevation angle at all times, everywhere in the service area. This continuous coverage requirement, combined with the need for handover margins and redundancy, means real constellations require substantially more satellites than the geometric minimum. A rule of thumb: multiply the geometric minimum by 3–5 for a continuous-coverage constellation with handover margins.

Elevation angle and its consequences

The elevation angle at which a ground terminal sees a satellite has cascading effects throughout the system. Low elevation angles mean the signal must travel through more atmosphere — more atmospheric attenuation, more rain fade margin required, and more multipath from reflections off terrain and structures. At 5° elevation, the signal passes through roughly ten times more atmosphere than at zenith (90°). At 10° elevation, roughly six times more. This is why satellite systems specify a minimum elevation angle: below some threshold, the link simply does not close reliably in most environments.

For LEO, the elevation angle seen by a terminal varies continuously as the satellite transits the sky. A satellite at 550 km altitude rises above a 25° elevation mask, reaches maximum elevation (which may be anywhere from 25° to 90° depending on the pass geometry), then drops back below the mask. The mean elevation angle during a usable pass is typically 40–55°. This is higher on average than a GEO terminal in the mid-latitudes might see (a GEO satellite at 30° west seen from Europe is at roughly 25–35° elevation), which is a genuine advantage for LEO link budgets in some geographies.

Handover timing

The time a LEO satellite is visible above a minimum elevation angle determines the maximum time between handovers. At 550 km altitude with a 25° elevation mask, a satellite is visible for approximately 4–7 minutes per pass. At 1,200 km (OneWeb), the pass duration extends to 9–14 minutes. These numbers drive the handover system design: beam management, Doppler compensation, timing, and the signalling overhead associated with handing a terminal from one satellite to the next. Aviation terminals, which are themselves moving at 250–900 km/h, must manage Doppler shift on both the satellite motion and aircraft motion axes simultaneously — a complication unique to that market.

DEEPER CONTEXT — DOPPLER SHIFT IN LEO

A LEO satellite at 550 km moves at approximately 7.6 km/s relative to the Earth's surface. A ground terminal sees the satellite approaching at up to 7.6 km/s at the horizon, dropping to zero radial velocity at closest approach (zenith), then receding at up to 7.6 km/s. The resulting Doppler shift on a Ka-band signal (26 GHz) is approximately ±660 kHz at the horizon — a shift large enough to require explicit compensation in the modem and the beam management system.

For a fixed ground terminal this is manageable: the satellite's trajectory is known precisely from orbital elements, and the modem can pre-compensate. For an aircraft terminal, the aircraft's own velocity (typically 250–900 km/h or 70–250 m/s) adds a second Doppler component that must be tracked in real time using the aircraft's inertial navigation system data. At Ka-band, an aircraft at 900 km/h contributes up to ±22 kHz of Doppler — small compared to satellite motion but not negligible for high-order modulation schemes.

The combination of satellite-induced and aircraft-induced Doppler shift, changing geometry during a pass, and the need to hand over between satellites while maintaining uninterrupted service makes aviation terminal modem design substantially more complex than residential LEO terminals.

4. Radiation Environment Across Altitudes

The radiation environment is one of the most important and least intuitive factors in orbit selection. Radiation affects satellite electronics through two mechanisms: total ionising dose (TID), which causes cumulative degradation of semiconductor devices over the satellite's lifetime, and single event effects (SEEs), which are transient or permanent upsets caused by individual high-energy particles passing through a device. The radiation environment varies dramatically across orbital regimes.

Sources of space radiation

Space radiation has three primary sources. First, galactic cosmic rays (GCRs) — high-energy protons and heavy ions originating outside the solar system — are present everywhere in the solar system and essentially impossible to shield against with reasonable satellite mass. They contribute a baseline radiation dose at all altitudes. Second, solar energetic particles (SEPs) — bursts of protons and electrons accelerated by solar flares and coronal mass ejections — cause dramatic but episodic increases in radiation flux, particularly dangerous for satellites in orbits that intersect the Van Allen belts. Third, trapped radiation in the Van Allen belts — protons and electrons trapped by Earth's magnetic field — represents the most intense chronic radiation environment for satellites in intermediate orbits.

TID by orbit

Total ionising dose, measured in kilorads (krad) or kilograys (kGy), is the accumulated radiation a satellite receives over its lifetime. Representative values for unshielded electronics:

1–5 krad/yrLEO (550 km, 53° inclination)
50–500 krad/yrMEO inner belt (3,000 km)
5–15 krad/yrMEO O3b (8,062 km, equatorial)
10–30 krad/yrGEO (35,786 km)

These numbers explain several important industry facts. LEO constellations below 600 km accumulate modest TID — low enough that commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) electronics, selected and screened for radiation tolerance rather than specifically radiation-hardened, can often be used. This dramatically reduces satellite manufacturing cost. GPS satellites in MEO at 20,200 km require heavily radiation-hardened components and have historically been expensive partly for this reason. GEO satellites experience higher TID than low LEO, which is one driver of GEO satellite cost — a 15-year design life requires components rated for 150–450 krad total dose.

The South Atlantic Anomaly

The South Atlantic Anomaly (SAA) is a region over the South Atlantic Ocean and South America where the inner Van Allen belt dips closest to Earth's surface, due to a combination of the offset between Earth's geographic and magnetic poles and the non-uniform structure of the magnetic field. LEO satellites passing through the SAA experience significantly elevated radiation — proton fluxes can be 10–100× higher than elsewhere in the orbit. Satellite operators routinely disable or safeguard sensitive electronics during SAA passes. The ISS, Hubble, and most LEO spacecraft have well-developed SAA protection protocols. For constellation operators, the SAA contributes meaningfully to the cumulative radiation dose seen by satellites in low-inclination or highly inclined orbits that regularly pass through this region.

Implications for component selection

The radiation environment directly shapes satellite design philosophy. A Starlink satellite at 550 km, designed for a 5–7 year life with an eventual replacement strategy built into the constellation model, can use COTS components with selective hardening of the most radiation-sensitive devices. A GPS satellite designed for a 15-year life at 20,200 km must use radiation-hardened processors, FPGAs, and memory rated for several hundred krad — components that are substantially more expensive, lower performance, and available from a more restricted supplier base. A GEO satellite at 35,786 km uses radiation-hardened components rated for 150–300+ krad over a 15–18 year design life. This is one reason a single GEO satellite costs $150–400M and takes 5–8 years to build and launch.

RADIATION HARDNESS ASSURANCE (RHA) The process of qualifying electronic components for use in a radiation environment is called Radiation Hardness Assurance. It involves characterising a component's response to TID, SEE, and displacement damage dose (DDD) through testing with radioactive sources, proton beams, and heavy-ion accelerators. For a LEO constellation satellite, a pragmatic approach uses commercial parts with lot testing and selective qualification rather than full space-grade RHA — acceptable when the satellite life is short and the cost target is aggressive. For a GEO satellite intended to operate for 15+ years, full RHA on all critical components is mandatory.

5. Eclipse Fractions and Power Budget Implications

Every satellite in a non-geosynchronous orbit periodically passes behind Earth and enters eclipse — a period during which it receives no solar illumination and must rely entirely on battery power. The fraction of time spent in eclipse, the duration of individual eclipse events, and the cycle rate all feed directly into the power system design: solar array sizing, battery capacity, thermal management, and bus voltage regulation. Eclipse is not a marginal consideration — for a LEO constellation satellite, it is one of the two or three most consequential constraints on the power system design.

Eclipse geometry and the beta angle

A satellite enters eclipse when it passes through the cone of Earth's umbral shadow — the region where Earth completely blocks the Sun. The geometry is determined by two parameters: the orbital altitude (which sets the satellite's distance from Earth's shadow cone) and the beta angle (β), defined as the angle between the orbital plane and the Sun-satellite vector direction. Beta angle is the single most important parameter for predicting eclipse behaviour.

At β = 0° (the orbital plane contains the Sun), the satellite passes through the widest part of Earth's shadow on every orbit, producing the maximum eclipse fraction. For a 550 km LEO orbit at β = 0°, Earth subtends a half-angle of approximately arcsin(6,371 / 6,921) ≈ 67° as seen from the satellite. The eclipse fraction of the orbit is approximately 2 × arcsin(6,371 / (6,371 + 550)) / (2π) ≈ 37% — meaning the satellite spends 37% of each 95-minute orbit in darkness, or about 35 minutes per orbit.

As β increases, the satellite's orbit tips relative to the Sun direction. At some critical beta angle — approximately arccos(6,371 / (6,371 + h)) for altitude h — the orbit no longer intersects Earth's shadow at all. For a 550 km orbit, this critical beta angle is approximately 67°. Above β = 67°, the satellite is in continuous sunlight. Below β = 67°, it experiences eclipses on every orbit, with the eclipse duration increasing as β decreases toward zero.

The beta angle is not constant — it changes throughout the year as Earth orbits the Sun and the Sun's ecliptic longitude shifts. For a given orbit inclination and RAAN (Right Ascension of the Ascending Node), the beta angle cycles through a range determined by the orbit's inclination relative to the ecliptic plane. For a 53° inclination orbit (Starlink primary shell), the beta angle cycles between approximately −(23.5° + 53°) and +(23.5° + 53°) — that is, between roughly −76° and +76°. In practice, the satellite spends part of each year above the critical beta angle (no eclipses) and part below it (eclipses on every orbit), with a smooth transition between the two states.

GEO eclipse: infrequent but long

GEO satellites behave entirely differently. Because the geostationary orbit lies in the equatorial plane and the Sun moves above and below the equatorial plane over the course of the year (due to Earth's 23.5° axial tilt), the Sun–Earth–GEO alignment required to produce an eclipse only occurs during two periods centred on the spring and autumn equinoxes. These eclipse seasons each last approximately 44–46 days, during which the satellite experiences a daily eclipse whose duration varies from a few minutes at the start and end of the season to a maximum of approximately 72 minutes at the equinox itself.

Over a full year, a GEO satellite experiences approximately 84 eclipse events totalling around 1,100 minutes of accumulated eclipse time. By contrast, a LEO satellite at 550 km at β = 0° experiences one 35-minute eclipse per 95-minute orbit — or roughly 5,500 eclipses per year with accumulated eclipse time of approximately 192,000 minutes per year. The GEO satellite experiences 175× less eclipse time annually than the worst-case LEO satellite — and even a well-managed LEO satellite (averaging across the full beta angle range) sees roughly 50× more annual eclipse time than GEO.

Solar array sizing

The solar array must supply the satellite's full power load during sunlit periods and simultaneously charge the battery to sustain operation through the upcoming eclipse. This double duty — load power plus battery charging — sets the minimum solar array size. For a LEO satellite with a 35-minute eclipse in a 95-minute orbit (37% eclipse fraction), the array must generate enough energy during the 60-minute sunlit period to power the satellite for 95 minutes total. If the satellite load is P watts, the array must generate at least P × 95/60 = 1.58P watts during the sunlit period (before accounting for array degradation, temperature effects, and charging efficiency losses). In practice, margins and degradation push this ratio closer to 2–2.5P.

Solar array performance degrades over time due to radiation damage, micrometeorite impacts, and thermal cycling. At LEO altitudes, arrays typically lose 2–4% of their beginning-of-life output per year. A satellite designed to deliver a specific power level at end of life (the critical constraint for maintaining payload performance) must have an array sized to that degraded level — meaning the beginning-of-life array is oversized by the cumulative degradation margin. For a 5-year LEO mission with 3% annual degradation, the array must be approximately 16% larger than the end-of-life requirement.

Battery technology and the cycle life problem

The battery must deliver the satellite's full load during each eclipse. For a 2,000 W satellite load (representative for a modern broadband LEO satellite) and a 35-minute eclipse, the energy required per eclipse is 2,000 × (35/60) ≈ 1,167 Wh. At a maximum depth of discharge (DoD) of 30% — to preserve battery cycle life — the battery must have a nameplate capacity of 1,167 / 0.30 ≈ 3,890 Wh. At a typical Li-ion cell energy density of 150–200 Wh/kg, this implies a battery mass of roughly 20–26 kg — a significant fraction of a 300 kg satellite's mass budget.

Why is DoD limited to 30%? Lithium-ion cells in space applications follow a well-characterised degradation curve: the number of cycles to 80% capacity retention (the typical end-of-life criterion) drops steeply as DoD increases. At 100% DoD, a quality space Li-ion cell might achieve 500–1,000 cycles. At 50% DoD, 3,000–5,000 cycles. At 30% DoD, 10,000–20,000 cycles. At 20% DoD, 30,000+ cycles. The ~27,000 eclipse cycles over a 5-year LEO mission therefore mandate either 20–30% DoD operation or a battery chemistry with exceptional cycle life — driving cell selection, battery management system design, and mass budget in ways that have no GEO analogue.

Modern LEO constellations increasingly use lithium-ion cells with nickel-rich cathode chemistries (NMC, NCA) that offer high energy density at acceptable cycle life when operated conservatively. Some newer designs are exploring lithium-sulfur or solid-state chemistries for improved cycle life, though these are not yet in widespread constellation use. GEO satellites, with their ~1,500 total eclipse cycles over 15 years, can use deeper discharge and have historically used nickel-hydrogen (NiH₂) batteries — heavier but extremely reliable — transitioning to Li-ion in more recent designs.

Thermal cycling consequences

Each eclipse transition subjects the satellite to a temperature swing. In sunlight, a satellite's outer surfaces (solar panels, radiators, structure) can reach +80°C to +120°C on sun-facing sides. In eclipse, the same surfaces drop to −80°C to −100°C within minutes. The temperature delta per cycle is therefore 160–220°C. Over 27,000 cycles, differential thermal expansion between dissimilar materials — aluminium structure, carbon fibre panels, copper interconnects, PCB substrates — causes fatigue accumulation at joints, solder connections, and mechanical interfaces.

This thermal fatigue is not always the life-limiting mechanism (radiation damage to electronics often is), but it must be analysed and designed against. Printed circuit boards use controlled-expansion laminates. Flex harnesses accommodate differential movement between structures. Solder joints are specified for thermal cycling rather than just mechanical strength. Solar panel substrate materials are chosen partly for their thermal cycling stability. None of these are significant considerations for GEO, where 1,500 thermal cycles over 15 years is manageable with standard aerospace practice.

BETA ANGLE MANAGEMENT AND RAAN PHASING Constellation operators carefully plan the RAAN (Right Ascension of the Ascending Node) distribution of satellites across orbital planes partly to manage the fleet-wide beta angle distribution. At any given time, some satellites will be at high beta (continuous sunlight, battery not cycling, array at full output) while others are at low beta (frequent deep eclipses, battery heavily exercised). A well-phased constellation distributes satellites across all beta angle phases, so the aggregate power generation and battery state-of-health across the fleet remains predictable. This also means that no single period of solar geometry simultaneously stress-tests all satellites — a fleet resilience consideration that is unique to multi-plane LEO constellations and has no GEO equivalent.

6. Atmospheric Drag and Orbital Lifetime

The atmosphere does not abruptly end — it thins exponentially with altitude, and its density at any given altitude varies significantly with solar activity. Atmospheric drag is the dominant orbital perturbation below approximately 600 km and remains meaningful up to roughly 1,000 km. Understanding drag is essential for constellation designers: it determines satellite lifetime, the frequency and propellant cost of orbit-maintenance manoeuvres, the deorbit timeline for end-of-life satellites, and — increasingly — the regulatory compliance posture of the entire constellation programme.

The physics of drag deceleration

Drag force acts opposite to the direction of satellite motion and is given by:

DRAG FORCE AND BALLISTIC COEFFICIENT F_drag = ½ · ρ · v² · C_D · A

Drag acceleration: a_drag = F_drag / m = ½ · ρ · v² / B

where ρ is atmospheric density (kg/m³), v is orbital velocity (m/s), C_D is the drag coefficient (typically 2.0–2.4 for a flat satellite body), A is the cross-sectional area (m²), m is satellite mass (kg), and B = m / (C_D · A) is the ballistic coefficient (kg/m²). Higher ballistic coefficient = less drag per unit of satellite mass.

At 550 km altitude, a representative Starlink satellite (mass ~295 kg, cross-section ~8 m², C_D ≈ 2.2) has a ballistic coefficient of approximately 295 / (2.2 × 8) ≈ 17 kg/m². With thermospheric density at 550 km of approximately 2.7 × 10⁻¹¹ kg/m³ at moderate solar activity, and orbital velocity of 7,600 m/s, the drag acceleration is:

a_drag = ½ × 2.7×10⁻¹¹ × 7,600² / 17 ≈ 4.6 × 10⁻⁷ m/s²

This seems tiny — less than a millionth of a g. But applied continuously over months and years, it produces significant orbital decay. The rate of altitude loss is related to the drag deceleration by dh/dt ≈ −2 · a_drag · r / v (where r is orbital radius), which at 550 km works out to approximately −50 to −200 metres per day depending on solar activity. Over a year, the orbit decays by 20–70 km without propulsive correction.

The exponential atmosphere and altitude sensitivity

Atmospheric density decreases approximately exponentially with altitude, characterised by the scale height H — the altitude increase over which density drops by a factor of e (≈ 2.718). In the upper thermosphere (LEO altitudes), the scale height is approximately 60–90 km depending on altitude and solar conditions. This exponential dependence means drag is extraordinarily sensitive to altitude:

  • A satellite at 400 km sees density roughly 10× higher than at 500 km (one scale height higher)
  • A satellite at 300 km sees density roughly 100× higher than at 500 km
  • A satellite at 600 km sees density roughly 10× lower than at 500 km

This sensitivity is why the choice between 340 km and 550 km for a Starlink shell is not a minor detail — it represents a roughly 15× difference in drag force. At 340 km, Starlink satellites require near-continuous propulsion just to maintain altitude; at 550 km, periodic reboost manoeuvres suffice. The tradeoff is latency (340 km gives lower propagation delay) versus propellant consumption and satellite lifetime.

Solar activity and F10.7

The Sun's output of extreme ultraviolet (EUV) radiation heats the thermosphere and ionosphere, causing the atmosphere to expand. During solar maximum (the peak of the approximately 11-year solar cycle), thermospheric density at LEO altitudes can be 5–10× higher than during solar minimum. This dramatically affects satellite drag and therefore orbital lifetime and propellant consumption.

The F10.7 index — solar radio flux at 10.7 cm wavelength, measured daily in solar flux units (sfu) — is the standard proxy for solar EUV activity used in atmospheric models. F10.7 ranges from approximately 65–70 sfu at solar minimum to 200–250+ sfu at solar maximum. Most operational satellite lifetime calculations use the NRLMSISE-00 or JB2008 atmospheric models with F10.7 as the primary input. A satellite lifetime predicted assuming F10.7 = 70 (solar minimum) may be 3–5× shorter in reality if the satellite operates during F10.7 = 200 conditions.

Solar Cycle 25, which began in late 2019, turned out to be one of the most active in recent decades — significantly more active than predictions made at solar minimum. Starlink satellites in the lower shells experienced substantially higher drag than modelled. SpaceX reported elevated satellite attrition rates and increased propellant consumption for altitude maintenance. In February 2022, a Starlink batch of 49 satellites was lost when a geomagnetic storm temporarily increased atmospheric density at their deployment altitude of ~210 km, causing enough drag to push them below a survivable orbit before their thrusters could compensate. This is not an abstract risk — it happened at scale, and it influenced subsequent decisions about deployment altitude and propulsion system sizing for newer Starlink generations.

THE FEBRUARY 2022 STARLINK LOSS On 3 February 2022, SpaceX launched 49 Starlink satellites into a 210 km deployment orbit. A geomagnetic storm the following day compressed the magnetosphere and increased thermospheric density at that altitude. Despite the satellites being commanded to safe mode (minimum drag orientation), 38 of 49 re-entered within days — a $50M+ loss. SpaceX subsequently adjusted its deployment strategy to higher initial altitudes for some launches. This event is the most visible demonstration of solar weather as an operational risk for LEO constellations.

The ballistic coefficient and constellation design

The ballistic coefficient B = m / (C_D · A) is under the designer's control to a limited extent. A heavier satellite (higher m) has less drag deceleration for a given area. A smaller cross-section (lower A) reduces drag. Some satellite designs include the ability to orient the satellite to reduce or maximise drag — presenting minimum cross-section for orbit maintenance, maximum cross-section for rapid deorbit. The Starlink v1.5 and v2 satellites feature adjustable solar panel orientation that serves partly this function.

For a constellation designer, the aggregate ballistic coefficient of the fleet matters for debris risk modelling. A constellation of 6,000 lightweight flat-panel satellites has a much lower aggregate ballistic coefficient than 6,000 traditional box-shaped satellites of the same mass — meaning faster natural deorbit after end of life, which is a debris mitigation benefit. This relationship between satellite form factor and debris risk is factored into FCC licence applications for new constellations, which must include quantitative orbital lifetime analyses.

Orbit maintenance propellant budget

For a satellite that must maintain a specific operational altitude against drag, the required ΔV (velocity change) per year equals the integrated drag deceleration over the year. From this, the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation gives the propellant mass fraction required:

TSIOLKOVSKY ROCKET EQUATION Δm/m₀ = 1 − exp(−ΔV / (g₀ · Isp))

where Δm is propellant consumed, m₀ is initial satellite mass, ΔV is required velocity change (m/s), g₀ = 9.81 m/s², and Isp is the specific impulse of the propulsion system (seconds). Higher Isp = more efficient propulsion = less propellant for the same ΔV.

For Starlink at 550 km, the annual drag ΔV at moderate solar activity is approximately 1–3 m/s/year. Over a 5-year mission, total drag makeup ΔV is approximately 5–15 m/s. Starlink uses krypton-fuelled Hall-effect ion thrusters with Isp ≈ 1,600 s, making the propellant fraction very small — on the order of 0.3–1% of satellite mass for drag makeup alone. The majority of the propulsion budget is reserved for orbit raising from deployment, collision avoidance manoeuvres, and end-of-life deorbit. This is why electric propulsion is essentially mandatory for LEO constellation satellites: chemical propulsion with Isp of 200–300 s would require impractical propellant fractions for the same total ΔV.

End-of-life deorbit requirements

The FCC now mandates that US-licensed satellites deorbit within 5 years of end of mission, replacing the previous 25-year guideline. This rule reflects growing concern about collision probability as constellation populations grow into the thousands. The transition from 25 to 5 years is not trivial — it fundamentally changes the propulsion system design for satellites at altitudes where natural decay takes longer than 5 years.

At 550 km, natural atmospheric decay deorbits a Starlink-like satellite in approximately 4–7 years at moderate solar activity — borderline compliant without active deorbit, but not reliably so at all solar conditions. At 1,200 km (OneWeb), natural decay takes 50–200 years — active propulsive deorbit is mandatory. OneWeb satellites must lower their orbit to below approximately 600 km at end of life and then rely on natural decay for the remainder, or execute a direct deorbit burn. The propellant reserved for this end-of-life manoeuvre is a significant constraint on the satellite's mass budget and must be budgeted from the beginning of the design process.

Conjunction analysis and collision avoidance

With thousands of satellites in a given orbital shell, the probability of close approaches between operational satellites, defunct satellites, and catalogued debris becomes non-trivial. Space-Track (operated by the US Space Force's 18th Space Defense Squadron) issues conjunction data messages (CDMs) when two catalogued objects are predicted to approach within a threshold distance — typically 5 km radially and 1 km cross-track. Constellation operators receive dozens to hundreds of CDMs per day across their fleet and must triage them by collision probability to determine which require avoidance manoeuvres.

SpaceX has reported that Starlink performs hundreds of collision avoidance manoeuvres per year across the constellation, most of them small burns to adjust timing or altitude. The automation of conjunction screening and manoeuvre decision-making is itself a significant software engineering challenge — no human team could manually review and action CDMs at the volume generated by a 6,000-satellite constellation operating in a crowded orbital regime. The transition from manual to largely autonomous conjunction response is one of the defining operational engineering challenges of the mega-constellation era.

KESSLER SYNDROME Proposed by NASA scientist Donald Kessler in 1978, Kessler syndrome describes a theoretical cascade: if satellite and debris density in a given orbital shell exceeds a critical threshold, collisions generate more debris, which causes more collisions, in a self-sustaining chain reaction that could render the affected orbital shell unusable for generations. The 2009 Iridium–Cosmos collision at 789 km created over 2,300 trackable fragments. ASAT tests — China (2007, ~850 km), Russia (2021, ~480 km), and India (2019, ~285 km) — added further debris. The 2021 Russian test generated a field that required ISS crew to shelter and produced fragments that will remain in orbit for years. No collision cascade has yet triggered, but the debris environment at LEO altitudes is measurably worsening. For a constellation architect, this means deorbit compliance, propulsion reliability, conjunction avoidance, and failure mode analysis are not optional features — they are existential requirements for regulatory approval and long-term operational viability.

7. Link Budget Sensitivity to Altitude

A link budget is the complete accounting of every gain and loss experienced by a signal as it travels from transmitter to receiver. It is the central engineering document that determines whether a satellite communications system will work at all, at what data rate, with what availability, and at what terminal cost. Every architectural choice — orbit altitude, frequency band, satellite antenna size, ground terminal size — shows up somewhere in the link budget. This section builds the link budget from first principles, works through a complete numerical example comparing LEO and GEO, and explains how each term changes with altitude.

The link budget equation

The fundamental link budget equation, in decibels, is:

LINK BUDGET — CARRIER TO NOISE DENSITY RATIO C/N₀ (dBHz) = EIRP (dBW) − FSPL (dB) − L_atm (dB) − L_misc (dB) + G/T (dB/K) − 10·log₁₀(k)

where:
EIRP = transmit power + transmit antenna gain (dBW)
FSPL = free-space path loss (dB)
L_atm = atmospheric attenuation including rain fade (dB)
L_misc = miscellaneous losses: pointing, polarisation, implementation (dB)
G/T = receive antenna gain minus system noise temperature (dB/K)
k = Boltzmann's constant = 1.38×10⁻²³ J/K → 10·log₁₀(k) = −228.6 dBW/Hz/K

The required C/N₀ for a given data rate R and modulation/coding scheme is:
Required C/N₀ = Eb/N₀_required + 10·log₁₀(R)

Every term on the right side of this equation is either a property of the satellite (EIRP), a property of the ground terminal (G/T), or a consequence of the geometry and environment (FSPL, L_atm). Orbital altitude directly controls FSPL — and FSPL is typically the largest single loss term in the budget, dominating everything else by 30–50 dB. Understanding FSPL is therefore understanding the dominant effect of altitude on system performance.

Free-space path loss in depth

Free-space path loss arises from the geometric spreading of an electromagnetic wave as it propagates. An isotropic transmitter radiates power equally in all directions. At distance d, that power is spread over a sphere of area 4πd². A receive antenna with effective aperture A_eff captures the fraction A_eff / (4πd²) of the transmitted power. FSPL is conventionally defined as the ratio of transmitted to received power when both antennas are isotropic:

FREE-SPACE PATH LOSS FSPL = (4πd/λ)² = (4πdf/c)²

In dB: FSPL (dB) = 20·log₁₀(4π/c) + 20·log₁₀(d) + 20·log₁₀(f)
Numerically: FSPL (dB) ≈ 92.45 + 20·log₁₀(d_km) + 20·log₁₀(f_GHz)

Key scaling relationships:
— Every doubling of distance: +6 dB
— Every doubling of frequency: +6 dB
— GEO vs LEO 550 km (65× farther): +36.3 dB

Working through the numbers at Ka-band (downlink at 20 GHz, a common choice for Starlink and ViaSat):

174 dBFSPL at 550 km, 20 GHz (LEO zenith)
210 dBFSPL at 35,786 km, 20 GHz (GEO zenith)
36 dBPath loss advantage of LEO over GEO
4,000×Power ratio equivalent to 36 dB

The 36 dB difference is decisive. It means that for equal transmit power and equal receive antenna size, LEO delivers 4,000× more signal power to the ground terminal than GEO. Alternatively: a GEO system needs either a 4,000× larger transmit power, a 4,000× larger satellite antenna area, a 4,000× larger ground terminal dish, or some combination across all three — just to break even with LEO on signal level. This is not a minor engineering detail. It is the fundamental reason the entire commercial satellite industry is restructuring around LEO.

EIRP: the satellite's transmitted power

Effective Isotropic Radiated Power (EIRP) is the product of the satellite's transmit power and its transmit antenna gain, expressed in dBW. It represents the power that a hypothetical isotropic antenna would need to transmit to produce the same signal level in the beam direction as the actual antenna.

For a Starlink v1.5 satellite, the downlink EIRP per beam is approximately 45–50 dBW — representing perhaps 10–20 W of RF power combined with a phased array antenna gain of 30–35 dBi. The phased array forms dozens of simultaneous beams, each with this EIRP, covering the footprint beneath the satellite. The total satellite RF output power might be 1–2 kW across all beams simultaneously.

For a GEO high-throughput satellite (HTS) like ViaSat-3, the downlink EIRP per spot beam is typically 60–68 dBW — 10–20 dB higher than Starlink per beam — achieved with a large deployable reflector antenna (typically 2–5 metres diameter) fed by a high-power travelling wave tube amplifier (TWTA). The GEO operator is forced to spend this EIRP just to compensate for the 36 dB higher path loss to GEO. The reflector is large because gain must be high; the TWTA is high-power because the link budget demands it.

This comparison illustrates the cascade effect of orbital altitude on satellite design complexity and cost. A LEO satellite can close a high-throughput link with a compact solid-state phased array. A GEO satellite requires a precision deployable reflector — a complex mechanical system that must unfurl reliably in orbit after surviving launch vibration — and high-power amplifiers that generate heat requiring dedicated thermal management. The cost difference is not primarily about launch mass; it is about this engineering complexity driven by the path loss penalty.

G/T: the ground terminal's receive quality

G/T (pronounced "G over T") is the figure of merit for a receive terminal: the ratio of antenna gain to system noise temperature. It captures both how well the terminal collects signal (gain) and how much thermal noise it introduces (noise temperature). Higher G/T means the terminal can detect weaker signals.

Antenna gain at a given frequency scales with aperture area: G = η · (π · D / λ)² for a circular aperture of diameter D and efficiency η. Doubling the antenna diameter quadruples the gain (+6 dB). At Ka-band (20 GHz, λ = 15 mm), a 50 cm diameter dish with 60% efficiency has a gain of approximately 37 dBi. A 90 cm dish has approximately 43 dBi — a 6 dB improvement.

System noise temperature T_sys combines the sky noise temperature (typically 20–50 K at Ka-band for a clear sky looking at moderate elevation), the feed and LNA noise contribution, and the ambient temperature contribution from the ground via spillover. For a well-designed flat-panel terminal at Ka-band with a low-noise amplifier at ~80 K noise temperature, T_sys might be 110–150 K. A poorly designed system with high feed losses might have T_sys of 300–500 K — a 3–7 dB penalty on G/T.

For a LEO terminal, the phased array must steer electronically to track the satellite across the sky. Phased array antennas have gain that varies with steering angle — typically dropping 3–5 dB near the horizon compared to broadside. Since LEO satellites spend time at all elevation angles from near the horizon up to near zenith, the link budget must account for worst-case G/T at low elevation angles, which is lower than the peak G/T at broadside. This is a fundamental disadvantage of phased arrays versus mechanically steered dishes, which maintain near-peak gain at all pointing angles.

The example reveals something important: even after accounting for GEO's higher EIRP (which partially compensates for its higher path loss), LEO still delivers 15 dB more carrier-to-noise ratio to the same terminal. That 15 dB margin can be spent in several ways. The system designer could use it to close a higher-order modulation scheme — for instance, 256APSK instead of 32APSK — and achieve roughly 3× higher spectral efficiency, translating to 3× more throughput per Hz of spectrum. Or the margin could be taken as rain fade protection, improving link availability in heavy precipitation. Or the terminal could be made smaller and cheaper by reducing antenna diameter until the LEO margin equals the GEO margin, while still closing the link at the same rate — the approach Starlink took with its consumer dish.

Rain fade and atmospheric effects

The worked example above assumed clear-sky conditions. In practice, rain and atmospheric gases attenuate the signal, and the system must maintain acceptable performance through specified rain conditions. The rain fade margin must be added to the required CNR threshold — the system must close the link not just in clear sky but in the worst rain conditions that occur with a given frequency.

Atmospheric attenuation at Ka-band (20 GHz downlink) from rain is typically 3–7 dB at moderate rain rates (25 mm/hour) at 30–45° elevation angles. The ITU-R P.618 rain attenuation model gives the excess attenuation for a given location, frequency, elevation angle, and availability target. A system targeting 99.9% availability (rain outage less than 8.76 hours per year) must add rain fade margin of approximately 4–8 dB in a moderate climate. A system targeting 99.5% availability (44 hours outage per year) needs only 2–4 dB.

Crucially, rain fade margin is added to the required CNR, not subtracted from the available CNR — it increases the threshold the system must meet. The LEO system's 15 dB CNR advantage over GEO means it can absorb substantially more rain fade while still maintaining the link, or alternatively can target higher availability for the same terminal size. In maritime applications, where terminals must close links in tropical rain events at low elevation angles simultaneously with high sea states that degrade pointing accuracy, the LEO path loss advantage translates directly into better service in the worst conditions.

Slant range and non-zenith geometry

The worked example used zenith geometry (satellite directly overhead) for simplicity. In practice, a LEO terminal sees satellites at varying elevation angles from the minimum cutoff (~25° for most Starlink terminals) to a maximum that depends on the pass geometry. The slant range at elevation angle ε is longer than the zenith range, and the path through the troposphere is longer (increasing atmospheric attenuation).

The slant range to a satellite at altitude h, seen at elevation angle ε, is:

SLANT RANGE FORMULA d = −Rₑ·sin(ε) + √(Rₑ²·sin²(ε) + h² + 2·Rₑ·h)

At ε = 90° (zenith): d = h (trivially)
At ε = 25°, h = 550 km: d ≈ 815 km (+3.4 dB path loss vs zenith)
At ε = 10°, h = 550 km: d ≈ 1,690 km (+9.8 dB path loss vs zenith)

The 3.4 dB additional path loss at 25° elevation compared to zenith is non-trivial — it represents a 40% reduction in signal power at the minimum usable elevation. Combined with the phased array's off-broadside gain reduction at low elevation angles (typically 2–4 dB), the link at minimum elevation is 5–7 dB weaker than at zenith. This worst-case geometry must be the one the link budget is designed to close — with rain fade margin on top. The minimum elevation angle specification therefore directly trades link quality (lower minimum elevation angle = worse worst-case link) against coverage continuity (lower minimum elevation angle = longer satellite passes = less frequent handovers). This tradeoff is a key constellation design parameter.

Doppler shift and frequency planning

LEO's high orbital velocity produces significant Doppler shift. At Ka-band downlink (20 GHz), the Doppler shift due to satellite radial velocity v_r is:

DOPPLER SHIFT Δf = f₀ · v_r / c

Maximum radial velocity for 550 km LEO: ±7.6 km/s (at horizon)
Maximum Doppler shift at 20 GHz: ±20,000 × 7,600 / 300,000,000 = ±507 kHz

At 26.5 GHz (Ka uplink): ±672 kHz
At 12 GHz (Ku downlink): ±304 kHz
At 1.6 GHz (L-band): ±40 kHz

These shifts must be compensated. The standard approach is Doppler pre-compensation at the transmitter: since the satellite's orbit is precisely known, the expected Doppler shift at any future time can be calculated and the transmit frequency offset accordingly so the received frequency is nominally on-target. Ground terminals do this in software on uplinks; satellites do it for downlinks. The residual Doppler after compensation — arising from ephemeris errors and latency in the compensation loop — must be within the modem's frequency acquisition and tracking range.

Doppler shift also affects frequency planning at the system level. Adjacent-channel interference analysis must account for the worst-case Doppler shifts of satellites at different positions in the sky. If two satellites are simultaneously visible to a terminal and one is approaching while the other is receding, their respective signals arrive at different frequencies — a natural form of frequency diversity that can be exploited or must at minimum be accounted for in the interference model.

DEEPER CONTEXT — AVIATION LINK BUDGETS

Aviation terminals add two complications to the standard LEO link budget. First, the aircraft's own velocity contributes a second Doppler component. At 900 km/h (250 m/s) on a heading directly toward or away from the satellite, the additional Doppler shift at Ka-band (20 GHz) is approximately 250/300,000,000 × 20,000,000,000 = ±16,700 Hz ≈ ±17 kHz. This is small compared to the satellite-induced ±507 kHz, but it is non-negligible for high-order modulation schemes and must be tracked using the aircraft's inertial navigation system (INS) data fed to the modem in real time.

Second, the aircraft terminal's antenna pointing must compensate for the aircraft's attitude (pitch, roll, yaw) in addition to tracking the satellite's position. A phased array terminal on an aircraft measures its own attitude from the INS and combines this with the satellite ephemeris to compute the required beam steering angles, updating at high rate to maintain pointing through turbulence and manoeuvres. The pointing accuracy requirement is tight — a 1° pointing error on a 50 cm phased array at Ka-band costs approximately 0.5–1 dB of gain — and the system must maintain accuracy even during aggressive manoeuvres such as missed approaches and go-arounds where pitch and bank angles change rapidly.

These two factors — aircraft Doppler and dynamic pointing — are the primary technical differentiators between an aviation LEO terminal and a maritime or fixed LEO terminal. They explain why aviation-certified LEO terminal development has lagged maritime by 12–18 months despite the commercial demand being comparable: the avionics integration, INS coupling, and certification testing add significant time and cost.

9. Regime Selection in Practice

Armed with the tradeoff table, you can now reason through orbit selection for specific applications. The following examples illustrate how the physical properties translate into architectural decisions.

Consumer broadband

The consumer broadband case almost exclusively selects LEO for new systems. The latency advantage over GEO is decisive for interactive applications (TCP, VoIP, video calling, cloud applications). The terminal cost advantage — LEO's lower path loss enables smaller, cheaper flat-panel antennas — is decisive for market scale. The coverage disadvantage (more satellites needed) is manageable at the economics enabled by reusable launch and consumer-grade satellite manufacturing. GEO HTS serves the same market but has an inherent latency ceiling that LEO does not. MEO is a niche choice for applications requiring better-than-GEO latency without the complexity of a large LEO constellation.

Aviation and maritime

Both markets are shifting strongly toward LEO. The latency improvement enables TCP performance at full throughput without acceleration, supporting streaming video at scale. The coverage improvement — including polar routes which GEO cannot serve — is valuable for transoceanic and transpolar flights and for shipping routes in high latitudes. The terminal challenge (tracking the moving satellite from a moving platform) is addressed by ESA technology. MEO (O3b mPOWER) holds a position in maritime for applications where GEO latency is unacceptable but a full LEO terminal is not yet certified or cost-justified.

Navigation

Navigation satellite systems are the definitive MEO application. The coverage footprint at 20,000 km enables global coverage with 24–30 satellites. The signal does not require low latency in the communications sense — it requires precise timing, which MEO provides well. The radiation environment at 20,000 km requires hardened electronics, accepted because navigation satellites are built to government budgets with long design lives. No LEO or GEO constellation can serve the GNSS function as efficiently — LEO requires too many satellites for global continuous coverage, and GEO provides insufficient geometric dilution of precision (GDOP) for precise positioning.

Earth observation

Remote sensing satellites use LEO almost exclusively, but for different reasons than communications. The primary driver is resolution: a camera with a given aperture achieves finer ground resolution from lower altitude. A 1-metre-resolution optical imager at 500 km altitude requires a 50 cm aperture; the same resolution from GEO would require a 37-metre telescope — physically impossible. Sun-synchronous LEO orbits (approximately 97–98° inclination) are preferred for Earth observation because they maintain a consistent local solar time for imaging, ensuring consistent lighting conditions on successive passes. Communications and Earth observation are therefore both LEO applications but for entirely different physical reasons.

The emerging multi-orbit landscape

The most sophisticated operators are now designing systems that deliberately span multiple regimes. SES operates both GEO (for broadcast and broad-area coverage) and MEO O3b (for enterprise and mobility latency-sensitive services). Viasat/Inmarsat has GEO capacity across multiple bands combined with LEO ambitions. Amazon Kuiper is pure LEO but integrates with AWS MEO edge computing infrastructure. The rationale for multi-orbit is that no single regime is optimal for all traffic types: bulk broadcast content suits GEO, latency-sensitive interactive traffic suits LEO, and some enterprise applications find MEO a useful middle ground. A systems architect who can only think in one regime is limited; the future belongs to those who can design for the full stack.

import math

RE = 6_371   # km

def eclipse_fraction(alt_km, beta_deg=0):
    """
    Eclipse fraction of orbit at given altitude and beta angle.
    beta_deg: angle between orbital plane and Sun direction (0 = max eclipse)
    Returns fraction 0–1 (0 = no eclipse, 1 = permanent shadow — impossible).
    At beta=0, approximately: fraction ≈ (1/π) * arcsin(RE / (RE + alt))
    More precisely using the penumbra half-angle:
    """
    beta = math.radians(beta_deg)
    rho  = math.asin(RE / (RE + alt_km))  # Earth subtended half-angle
    # Eclipse occurs when beta < rho
    if abs(beta) >= rho:
        return 0.0   # no eclipse at this beta angle
    # Eclipse fraction of orbit:
    frac = (1 / math.pi) * math.acos(
        math.sqrt(math.cos(rho)**2 - math.cos(beta)**2 - math.sin(rho)**2 * math.cos(beta)**2)
        / (math.sin(rho) * math.sin(math.pi/2 - beta))
    )
    # Simplified formula for beta=0:
    return (1 / math.pi) * math.acos(
        math.sqrt(1 - (RE / (RE + alt_km))**2)
    )
