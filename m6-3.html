CORE

m6-2 finished the geometry: pitch from the scan requirement, element count from the gain requirement, taper from the mask. What it left undetermined is everything that costs money: behind each of 1,280 elements sits electronics that must control phase and amplitude, amplify, and — somewhere — combine 1,280 signals into one beam, or several. Where that combination happens is the architecture decision, and it is the decision that sets the terminal's power draw, its bill of materials, its beam count, and therefore which m6-1 segments it can serve. Three pure answers exist — combine in RF hardware (analog), combine in software after per-element digitisation (digital), or combine in stages (hybrid) — plus a family of mechanical hybrids that steer without per-element phase control at all.

The lesson's verified anchors, stated up front because they overturn two common intuitions. First, the classic objection to digital beamforming — "the converters would melt" — is dead: modern ADC efficiency puts full per-element conversion of a 1,280-element, 500 MHz panel at ~65 W of converters. What actually walls off consumer DBF is the 12.8 Tb/s of raw digital samples those converters emit, and the interconnect and processing that must swallow it. Second, the flat panel does not beat the dish on cost — at any gain, at any plausible per-element price, a fixed dish is cheaper. What the panel beats is the dish plus the tracking gimbal that NGSO forces onto it. The ESA's economic case was never against the reflector; it was against the motor.

1. The question behind each element

Each element's signal path needs, at minimum: a low-noise amplifier (receive) or power amplifier (transmit) — m6-1 placed these and priced their noise and EIRP roles — a phase shifter implementing m6-2's steering table, and usually a variable-gain stage implementing the taper. The architecture question is what happens next: 1,280 controlled signals must become one (or K) beams. Combine them as RF voltages in a passive network and you have analog beamforming — cheap combination, but the beam is formed once, in copper, and exists singularly. Digitise each element and combine as numbers, and beams become software — plural, reconfigurable, simultaneous — at the price of per-element converters and a torrent of data. Everything else in this lesson is the working-out of that one sentence.

2. Analog beamforming: the consumer default

The consumer NGSO panel's architecture: beamformer ICs (BFICs), each serving 4–8 elements with per-element LNA/PA, phase shifter (5–6 bits typical), and gain control, feeding a corporate RF combining network to a single up/down-converter and one modem chain. A 1,280-element panel is ~200–320 BFICs of silicon-germanium plus a passive combiner — which, per m6-1 §6, sits after per-element low-noise gain and is therefore merely inefficient rather than noise-fatal.

The verified power budget at consumer scale: receive chains ≈ 25 mW/element (32 W), transmit chains ≈ 45 mW/element at milliwatt output powers and realistic back-off efficiency (58 W), control, clocking and conversion ~15 W — order 105 W for the panel, matching the ~100 W class that public teardowns report. The architecture's defining limit: the combining network embodies exactly one set of weights, so the aperture produces one beam per beamforming layer. Handover and multi-beam service must be bought another way (section 6).

3. Digital beamforming: beams as software

Digitise every element — ADC per element per polarisation on receive, DAC on transmit — and beamforming becomes multiplication and addition in a processor. The rewards are total: as many simultaneous beams as processing affords, each with its own adaptive weights; nulls steered onto interferers; calibration in software; patterns changed per packet.

The cost, priced honestly with the converter figure of merit (P ≈ FOM · 2^ENOB · f_s):

Item Value
One ADC, 10-bit ENOB, 500 Msps, FOM 50 fJ/step25.6 mW
1,280 elements × 2 (I/Q)65.5 W of converters
Aggregate raw sample stream12.8 Tb/s

The converters are a solved problem — 65 W is real but survivable, and better FOMs halve it. The wall is the second row: moving and multiplying twelve terabits per second of samples means serialised links, distribution ASICs, and beamforming silicon whose power and cost dwarf the ADCs. That is why full DBF lives where the economics invert: satellite payloads (Module 3's flexible beams — one spacecraft's DBF serves a million users, so its cost amortises), gateways (few sites, extreme performance), and defence (nulling and LPI justify anything). The consumer terminal, which must amortise its silicon over one user, stays analog.

4. Hybrid beamforming: K chains, N elements

The compromise: partition N elements into K subarrays of M each (N = K·M); beamform each subarray in analog; digitise only the K subarray outputs. Converter and processing cost scales with K, not N — a K = 16 partition of the 1,280 panel needs 0.8 W of converters and a thoroughly ordinary digital section — while the system gains up to K independently steerable digital degrees of freedom: multiple beams, adaptive combining across subarrays, per-subarray calibration.

The catch is geometric: the analog subarray has its own fixed-ish pattern, and steering the digital combination far from the subarrays' analog pointing raises quantisation lobes — the subarray lattice behaves like a coarse array with M-element spacing, and m6-2's grating mathematics returns at the subarray scale. Hybrid architectures therefore suit systems whose simultaneous beams cluster within a modest angular neighbourhood — which describes a satellite payload's coverage region well, and is exactly where hybrid DBF dominates (the Module 3 architecture, now with its terminal-side rationale visible).

Note what is architecture-independent: the per-element RF chains (~90 W here) exist in every column — the LNAs, PAs and phase control are the price of being a phased array at all, and m6-1's element-count decisions set them. The architecture decision prices only what happens after the elements — and its real currency at scale is the digital stream column, not the converter column.

6. Multi-beam, and m6-2's handover question answered

m6-2's reflection asked what make-before-break actually costs an analog terminal. The three ways to hold two beams, now priced:

Split the aperture — half the elements per beam: per-beam EIRP falls −6.0 dB (N² law: half the power and half the gain) and G/T falls −3.0 dB, for the duration of the overlap. Against m5-2's ladder that is one to several rungs during every handover — visible, frequent, and at exactly the moment the link is changing satellites. A real design only if the overlap is very short and the ladder has headroom.

A second analog beamforming layer — duplicate phase shifters and combiner behind the same elements: two full-gain beams, at nearly 2× the beamforming silicon. This is the honest consumer solution, and multi-layer BFICs exist precisely for it.

Go hybrid or digital — beams are cheap once the digital section exists; handover is a software event.

The architecture decision and the m5-3 session-continuity requirement are thus the same decision viewed from two modules — and the consumer panel's second beamforming layer is a line item that exists because of a protocol requirement three layers up.

7. The mechanical hybrids

One family refuses the premise that steering needs per-element electronics. VICTS apertures (variable-inclination continuous transverse stub — commercial implementations are well established in aero and maritime) steer by mechanically rotating two low-profile plates against each other: elevation from the plates' relative angle, azimuth from rotating the assembly. No phase shifters, no BFICs — the aperture is essentially passive metal with two motors, so its efficiency and noise behaviour approach a fixed aperture's, and its cost has no per-element term at all. Low-profile gimballed panels make the adjacent trade.

What returns with the motors: single beam, mechanical slew rates, wear — and the handover problem of §6 in its hardest form, since a mechanically steered aperture cannot be in two places at all. Where they win is where their weaknesses are least binding and their strengths priceless: aero and maritime, where a platform tracks one GEO (or schedules generous NGSO handovers), where height and drag are the binding currency (m6-6), and where the G/T-per-euro of quasi-passive metal beats silicon across the operating life. The mechanical hybrid is not a transitional technology; it is the correct answer to a particular weighting of m6-1's three currencies.

8. Cost scaling and the honest crossover

Parametric models — dish cost ≈ fixed + b·D^2.7 (structure and stiffness scale super-quadratically), array cost ≈ overhead + c·N — with the m6-2 gain laws linking D and N to the same dBi:

The table's verdict is unambiguous and worth stating without flinching: for a fixed, pointed terminal, the dish wins at every gain and every plausible per-element cost — stamped metal converting area to gain is unbeatable economics, which is why GEO consumer broadband never left it. The array's case appears only when the comparison is honest about NGSO: add a consumer-grade tracking gimbal (hundreds of euros, plus the §m6-1 warranty exposure) and a second one for make-before-break, and the analog panel at aggressive per-element cost crosses over — while also deleting the radome volume, the installation constraints, and the moving-part failure modes. The units of the ESA revolution were never dBi per euro; they were dBi per euro while tracking.

11. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

The spine gains Stage 3: an ArchitectureModel that, given element count, bandwidth, and required beams, prices each architecture's converter power, digital stream, RF power, silicon count, and per-beam performance penalty — and a cost model that finally makes m6-1's dish-versus-panel question answerable in euros, provided the question includes the gimbal. Two module threads resolved: m6-2's handover reflection now has numbers (−6 dB split versus a second layer versus digital-free), and the Module 3 payload's hybrid-digital architecture now has its terminal-side economic mirror.

What the architecture cannot fix: physics at scan. However the beam is formed, the embedded element pattern rolls off (aperture projection included) — and the regulator is waiting at the off-axis angles. m6-4 presents that bill.

Exercise 1 — The converter law and the real wall

Price digital beamforming honestly: converters, then the stream.

import math

def adc_power_w(fom_fj, enob, fs_hz):
    return fom_fj*1e-15 * 2**enob * fs_hz

def dbf_bill(N, fs_hz, enob=10, fom_fj=50, bits=None):
    bits = bits or enob
    conv = N * 2 * adc_power_w(fom_fj, enob, fs_hz)      # I/Q per element
    stream = N * 2 * bits * fs_hz                        # raw samples out
    return conv, stream

print(f"{'N':>6}{'fs (MHz)':>10}{'converters (W)':>16}{'stream (Tb/s)':>15}")
for N, fs in [(256, 500e6), (1280, 500e6), (1280, 100e6), (5000, 500e6)]:
    c, s = dbf_bill(N, fs)
    print(f"{N:>6}{fs/1e6:>10.0f}{c:>16.1f}{s/1e12:>15.2f}")

Exercise 2 — The crossover, with and without the gimbal

Reproduce §8 and then make it honest about NGSO.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x)

def dish_cost(D_m, fixed=150, b=900, exp=2.7):
    return fixed + b*D_m**exp

def dish_D_for_gain(G_dbi, f_ghz=20, eta=0.65):
    lam = 0.3/f_ghz
    return lam/math.pi * math.sqrt(10**(G_dbi/10)/eta)

def array_N_for_gain(G_dbi, Ge=5.0, loss=1.5):
    return round(10**((G_dbi - Ge + loss)/10))

def array_cost(N, per_el, overhead=60):
    return overhead + N*per_el

GIMBAL = 800          # consumer-grade tracking positioner, parametric
print(f"{'gain':>5}{'dish':>7}{'dish+gimbal':>12}{'N':>7}"
      f"{'array@0.5':>11}{'@1.5':>8}{'@3.0':>8}")
for G in [33, 36, 39, 42]:
    D = dish_D_for_gain(G); N = array_N_for_gain(G)
    dc = dish_cost(D)
    row = [f"{G:>5}", f"{dc:>7.0f}", f"{dc+GIMBAL:>12.0f}", f"{N:>7}"]
    row += [f"{array_cost(N, p):>8.0f}" for p in (0.5, 1.5, 3.0)]
    print("".join(row))

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 3: the architecture model

Every architecture priced from one parameter set; the panel's electronics join the spine.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x)

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M6 SPINE PROJECT — TERMINAL MODEL
# Stage 3: beamforming architecture — power, silicon,
#          beams, and per-beam penalties behind the
#          Stage-1/2 aperture.
# Next: m6-4 scan loss + regulatory mask (physics returns);
#       m6-5 pointing/tracking; m6-6 segment table;
#       m6-7 evaluator -> M4 -> M5.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class ArchitectureModel:
    RX_MW, TX_MW, OVERHEAD_W = 25.0, 45.0, 15.0
    def __init__(self, name, N, fs_hz=500e6, arch="analog",
                 layers=1, K=16, enob=10, fom_fj=50, el_per_ic=6):
        self.name, self.N, self.arch = name, N, arch
        self.layers, self.K = layers, K
        rf = N*(self.RX_MW + self.TX_MW)/1e3
        rf += (layers-1)*N*5.0/1e3      # extra beamforming layer: shifter+ctrl
        adc = lambda: fom_fj*1e-15 * 2**enob * fs_hz
        if arch == "analog":
            self.conv_w  = 0.1
            self.stream  = 0.0
            self.beams   = layers
            self.bfics   = math.ceil(N/el_per_ic)*layers
        elif arch == "hybrid":
            self.conv_w  = K*2*adc()
            self.stream  = K*2*enob*fs_hz
            self.beams   = K
            self.bfics   = math.ceil(N/el_per_ic)
        elif arch == "digital":
            self.conv_w  = N*2*adc()
            self.stream  = N*2*enob*fs_hz
            self.beams   = 10**9          # processing-limited
            self.bfics   = 0
        elif arch == "victs":
            self.conv_w, self.stream, self.beams, self.bfics = 0.05, 0.0, 1, 0
            rf = 8.0                      # motors + converter, no per-el chains
        self.power_w = rf + self.conv_w + self.OVERHEAD_W

    def beam_penalty(self, simultaneous):
        """dB (EIRP, G/T) penalty per beam for `simultaneous` beams."""
        if self.arch == "analog" and simultaneous > self.layers:
            frac = self.layers/simultaneous          # aperture split
            return 20*math.log10(frac), 10*math.log10(frac)
        if self.arch == "victs" and simultaneous > 1:
            return -999.0, -999.0                    # cannot
        return 0.0, 0.0

    def report(self):
        s = f"{self.stream/1e12:.2f} Tb/s" if self.stream >= 1e12 else \
            (f"{self.stream/1e9:.0f} Gb/s" if self.stream else "—")
        print(f"  {self.name:<28} {self.power_w:>6.0f} W | conv {self.conv_w:>6.1f} W"
              f" | stream {s:>10} | beams {min(self.beams, 99):>2}"
              f" | BFICs {self.bfics:>3}")

print(f"{'architecture':<30}{'power':>8}   converters      stream     beams   silicon")
for a in [ArchitectureModel("analog, 1 layer",   1280, arch="analog", layers=1),
          ArchitectureModel("analog, 2 layers",  1280, arch="analog", layers=2),
          ArchitectureModel("hybrid K=16",       1280, arch="hybrid"),
          ArchitectureModel("full digital",      1280, arch="digital"),
          ArchitectureModel("VICTS-class",       1280, arch="victs")]:
    a.report()

one = ArchitectureModel("analog, 1 layer", 1280, arch="analog", layers=1)
e, g = one.beam_penalty(2)
print(f"\nsplit-aperture handover on 1-layer analog: EIRP {e:+.1f} dB, G/T {g:+.1f} dB per beam")

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 01

Section 9's amortisation axis predicts every architecture in the table — but it treats the terminal fleet as static. A consumer operator with ten million analog terminals wants adaptive interference nulling (a digital capability) against a rising noise floor. Enumerate the realistic paths — fleet replacement, hybrid retrofit at the next hardware revision, network-side mitigation instead — price each qualitatively against the §5/§8 scalings, and identify which m4-6 quantity would have to degrade, and by how much, before any path clears its cost.

PROMPT 02

Section 6 priced three multi-beam routes and noted consumer panels ship a second analog layer "because of a protocol requirement three layers up" (m5-3's make-before-break). Invert the dependency: suppose the air-interface designers had instead specified break-before-make with a 50 ms gap. Trace the consequences downward (terminal silicon deleted, cost saved) and upward (m5 session behaviour, TCP, user experience), and judge whether the protocol requirement or the silicon was the cheaper place to solve handover. What does this say about who should sit in air-interface standardisation meetings?

PROMPT 03

The §8 crossover buried a time dimension: the dish+gimbal's cost includes a mechanical wear-out and truck-roll model, while the ESA's includes silicon infant mortality and graceful degradation (m6-1 §7). Rebuild the comparison as five-year total cost of ownership for (a) a suburban consumer, (b) a maritime vessel, (c) a rural site with no service access. Identify which cost term flips which comparison, and what warranty structure each answer implies the vendor must sell.

PROMPT 04

Section 3 concluded the DBF wall is the sample stream, and exercise 1's question 3 timelines consumer DBF against mobile-SoC processing curves. Take a position: name the year-range in which full-digital consumer terminals become rational, state the two exogenous curves your estimate rides on (converter FOM, TMAC/s per euro), and — the real question — identify what a consumer terminal would actually do with software-defined beams that its analog second layer cannot. If you cannot name a capability with revenue attached, what does that imply about whether the transition ever happens at the consumer tier?
