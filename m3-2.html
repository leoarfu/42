ADCS & Propulsion

MODULE 3
SPACE SEGMENT

A Ka-band multibeam antenna with 0.5° spot beams requires the satellite body to be pointed to within 0.1° three-sigma while moving at 7.6 km/s under continuous external disturbance torques. The subsystem that achieves this is the attitude determination and control system (ADCS). Tightly coupled to it is the propulsion subsystem, which executes the Δv budget computed in Module 2 — drag makeup, station-keeping, collision avoidance, and deorbit. This lesson covers both together, because in a modern LEO broadband satellite they share sensors, actuators, and operational modes.

1. Attitude Determination: Sensors

Attitude determination answers: given all sensor measurements at this moment, what is the satellite's orientation in inertial space? The answer — typically expressed as a quaternion or rotation matrix — is the input to the attitude control law.

Star trackers

The most accurate attitude sensor for LEO satellites is the star tracker, which images a patch of sky, identifies the star pattern against a stored catalog, and produces an attitude measurement with accuracy of 1–10 arcseconds (0.0003°–0.003°) at 1–10 Hz. Two trackers are flown for redundancy and to eliminate ambiguity when one is blinded by the Sun, Moon, or Earth limb. During normal nadir-pointing operation the satellite rotates at the orbital rate (~0.06°/s at 550 km), well within the typical 1–3°/s slew rate limit imposed by the tracker's exposure time.

Inertial measurement units

An IMU contains gyroscopes measuring angular rate at high bandwidth (100–1,000 Hz). The IMU provides short-term attitude propagation between star tracker updates — the star tracker provides absolute attitude; the IMU provides high-rate, low-latency angular rate for the control loop. MEMS gyroscopes dominate modern LEO satellites: smaller, cheaper, and lower-power than ring-laser or fibre-optic designs, though with higher bias drift. Over minutes to hours the IMU drifts from truth; star tracker updates correct this. The combined system is the attitude reference system (ARS).

Sun and Earth sensors

Coarser but simpler sensors serve safe mode, initial acquisition, and backup. Sun sensors measure the Sun angle to 0.1°–2° at very low power. Earth (horizon) sensors measure the direction to the Earth's IR limb to 0.1°–0.5°. Both are essential for attitude acquisition after separation from the launch vehicle, before star trackers have acquired their catalog match.

2. Attitude Control: Actuators

The control law takes the attitude error (estimated vs commanded attitude) and the angular rate from the IMU, and computes the torque to apply. The torque is produced by actuators.

Reaction wheels

The dominant actuator for LEO broadband satellites is the reaction wheel (RW) — a flywheel driven by an electric motor. Spinning faster exchanges angular momentum with the satellite body, producing a torque. Three wheels aligned with the three body axes (or a pyramid of four for redundancy) provide full three-axis control with no propellant consumed. The limitation is saturation: a wheel can only store angular momentum up to its maximum spin speed. External disturbance torques (gravity gradient, solar pressure, magnetic residuals) continuously add momentum to the wheel set, which must be periodically desaturated — the stored momentum dumped to the environment via magnetic torquers or thrusters.

Magnetic torquers

Magnetorquers generate a magnetic dipole moment that interacts with the Earth's field to produce a torque: T = M × B. The cross-product means the torque is always perpendicular to the local field direction — the component parallel to B is always zero — so full three-axis control requires time-averaging over multiple orbits as the field rotates. They are simple, cheap, and propellant-free, making them the preferred desaturation method at LEO altitudes where the Earth's field (~30 µT at 550 km) is strong enough to be useful.

Thruster-based attitude control

For large slews or fast collision avoidance manoeuvres, thrusters provide torque far beyond what reaction wheels can supply. The Hall thruster clusters used on Starlink fire asymmetrically — differential thrust between thruster pairs produces net torque — or accept coupled force and torque, with the trajectory correction computed separately. This dual use of the propulsion system for both attitude and orbit control is characteristic of modern LEO broadband constellation satellites.

THE DESATURATION BUDGET Routine magnetic desaturation is propellant-free and covers most LEO scenarios. But when the combined disturbance torque exceeds the magnetic torquer's capability — or during large manoeuvres that the torquers cannot respond to quickly enough — thrusters must desaturate. This adds to the propellant budget. At 550 km and 53° inclination, magnetic desaturation typically handles all routine operations; thruster desaturation is reserved for contingency.

3. The Pointing Error Budget

The payload places a pointing accuracy requirement on the satellite: the angular error between actual and commanded pointing must be small enough that the antenna beam covers the intended ground cell with acceptable gain. For a 0.5° Ka-band spot beam requiring no more than −3 dB of gain loss at the beam edge, the pointing error must be within roughly 0.1° three-sigma.

The total pointing error is the root-sum-square (RSS) of independent contributors:

The structural thermal flex contribution is often the binding constraint — it arises from the satellite body thermally cycling on the orbital period (every 95 min from sunlit to eclipse and back), which causes differential expansion between the antenna panel and the star tracker mounting bracket. Reducing it requires careful thermal design of the load path between these elements, not improvements to the control electronics.

For smaller beamwidths — emerging at V-band and E-band — the pointing requirement tightens proportionally, and electronic beam steering in the antenna (covered in m3-4) becomes the practical solution: the residual mechanical pointing error is corrected in the RF domain rather than requiring tighter mechanical pointing.

4. Orbital Maintenance Propulsion

The Δv budget from Module 2 (m2-5) translates into hardware here. For LEO broadband constellations, Hall-effect thrusters (HETs) dominate: krypton (Starlink) or xenon (OneWeb) at Isp ≈ 1,200–2,000 s. The propellant mass saving over chemical propulsion at fleet scale is decisive, as computed in the spine project.

Power-thrust tradeoff

A Hall thruster at 50 mN requires approximately 3.2 kW of input power (at ~50% efficiency). On a satellite with 5–8 kW total bus power, running the thruster at full payload power is not possible. Drag makeup burns therefore occur during eclipse (battery-powered) or during day passes when the payload is deliberately derated — scheduling the manoeuvre outside peak service windows.

CAM timing with Hall thrusters

A collision avoidance manoeuvre of 0.2 m/s on a 300 kg satellite with a 50 mN thruster takes:

t_CAM = m · Δv / F = 300 × 0.2 / 0.050 = 1,200 s ≈ 20 minutes

This is acceptable given the typical 72-hour conjunction warning time from the US Space Surveillance Network. It is planned and executed autonomously by the satellite's on-board system.

Thruster configuration

Two to four Hall thrusters are typically mounted on the anti-nadir (zenith-facing) panel, canted to provide along-track, cross-track, and radial force components. This canting allows a single thruster firing to simultaneously produce the Δv for drag makeup and a torque for attitude control — saving both propellant and time. The layout must satisfy three constraints: thrust vector near the centre of mass (minimise torque coupling), plume directed away from solar arrays and payload (plume impingement erodes surfaces), and sufficient symmetry for differential attitude control.

The slow response is managed operationally — pre-planned CAMs based on 72-hour warnings, scheduled burns during eclipse windows. For satellite designs where rapid response is safety-critical (crewed proximity operations, for example), chemical propulsion remains necessary.

5. GPS-Based Orbit Determination

A GPS receiver on a LEO satellite provides continuous position (5–20 m, 3σ) and velocity (2–5 cm/s) at every point in the orbit, enabling autonomous manoeuvre planning without dependence on ground station contact windows. At LEO altitudes the satellite is below the GPS constellation (MEO at ~20,200 km), so GPS signals arrive from above — the GPS antenna must have hemisphere coverage above the satellite body. The large Doppler shifts from the ~7.6 km/s orbital velocity (up to ±45 kHz on L1) are well within modern space-qualified GPS receivers' acquisition range.

The same GPS receiver provides nanosecond-level time synchronisation — essential for TDMA air interfaces where all satellites in the constellation must be time-synchronised to sub-microsecond accuracy. This dual use (navigation + timing) makes the GPS receiver one of the most mission-critical components on the satellite bus, which is why two receivers are typically flown for redundancy.

6. ADCS-Propulsion Interface in Operations

In normal operations the satellite is in nadir-pointing mode: the payload face points toward Earth, solar arrays track the Sun, and the attitude control loop holds pointing within budget using reaction wheels with magnetic desaturation. The GPS receiver provides continuous orbit determination. The on-board manoeuvre planner monitors along-track position against the nominal slot; when the error exceeds a threshold (typically 1–3 km), it schedules a drag makeup burn for the next available eclipse or reduced-payload window.

When a conjunction warning arrives from the ground, the satellite evaluates whether a CAM is required (probability of collision above threshold, typically 1 in 10,000), computes the required Δv, and executes autonomously or requests ground approval depending on the operator's policy. Starlink reportedly operates fully autonomously for conjunction avoidance at fleet scale.

At end of life: propellant remaining is verified, a deorbit burn is computed to lower perigee to ~350 km (natural re-entry within weeks), the burn is executed over one or more passes, and the satellite is passivated (propellant vented, batteries discharged) before re-entry.

AUTONOMOUS OPERATIONS AT SCALE With 1,584 satellites each executing ~5,475 eclipse cycles, ~16 drag makeup burns, and potentially hundreds of conjunction screenings per year, manual ground commanding of every event is impossible. The ADCS-propulsion system must be a largely autonomous loop, with the ground providing policy, configuration, and anomaly resolution — not per-event commands.

Exercise 1 — Pointing Error Budget (RSS)

Compute the combined pointing error from subsystem contributors and check against the payload beamwidth requirement.

import math

def pointing_budget(contributors, beamwidth_deg, fraction=0.20):
    req = beamwidth_deg * fraction
    rss = math.sqrt(sum(v**2 for _, v in contributors))
    margin = req / rss
    print(f"Beamwidth {beamwidth_deg}°  →  Req: {req:.4f}° (3σ)\n")
    print(f"{'Contributor':<30}{'3σ (°)':>10}")
    print("-" * 42)
    for name, s in contributors:
        print(f"{name:<30}{s:>10.4f}")
    print("-" * 42)
    print(f"{'RSS total':<30}{rss:>10.4f}")
    print(f"Margin: {margin:.2f}x  ({'PASS' if margin>=1.0 else 'FAIL'})\n")
    return rss, margin

contributors = [
    ("Star tracker (attitude det.)", 0.003),
    ("Control loop residual",        0.030),
    ("Structural thermal flex",      0.030),
    ("Boresight alignment error",    0.020),
]

print("=== Ka-band, 0.5° beam ===")
pointing_budget(contributors, 0.5)
print("=== Ka-band, 0.3° beam ===")
pointing_budget(contributors, 0.3)

Exercise 2 — CAM Duration vs Propulsion System

Compare the time required to execute a CAM using different propulsion systems, and check against the available warning time.

import math

G0 = 9.80665

def cam_table(dry_kg, dv_m_s, systems, min_warning_hr=24):
    print(f"CAM ΔV={dv_m_s} m/s  Dry mass={dry_kg} kg\n")
    print(f"{'System':<22}{'Isp':>6}{'F(N)':>8}{'m_prop(g)':>11}"
          f"{'t_burn':>11}{'OK?':>8}")
    print("-" * 68)
    for name, isp, F in systems:
        mp = dry_kg * (math.exp(dv_m_s/(G0*isp)) - 1) * 1000  # grams
        t_s = (dry_kg * dv_m_s) / F
        t_min = t_s / 60
        ok = "YES" if t_s < min_warning_hr * 3600 else "NO"
        print(f"{name:<22}{isp:>6}{F:>8.3f}{mp:>10.1f}g"
              f"{t_min:>9.1f}min{ok:>8}")

systems = [
    ("Monoprop hydrazine",    230,  5.000),
    ("Hall HET 50mN",        1600,  0.050),
    ("Hall HET 200mN",       1600,  0.200),
    ("Gridded ion 5mN",      3000,  0.005),
]
cam_table(300, 0.20, systems, min_warning_hr=24)

Exercise 3 — Reaction Wheel Saturation and Desaturation

Estimate wheel saturation frequency from a disturbance torque and compute the annual desaturation propellant cost.

import math

def wheel_desat(disturbance_nm, capacity_nms, desat_dv_m_s=5e-4,
                period_min=95.6, life_yr=5):
    t_sat_hr = capacity_nms / disturbance_nm / 3600
    desat_yr = (365.25 * 24) / t_sat_hr
    total_dv = desat_yr * life_yr * desat_dv_m_s
    print(f"Disturbance: {disturbance_nm*1e6:.0f} µN·m  "
          f"Capacity: {capacity_nms:.0f} N·m·s")
    print(f"  Saturation time: {t_sat_hr:.1f} hr  "
          f"Desats/yr: {desat_yr:.0f}  "
          f"Total desat ΔV ({life_yr}yr): {total_dv*1000:.2f} mm/s\n")

print("=== Typical LEO broadband (50 µN·m disturbance) ===")
wheel_desat(50e-6, 5.0)
print("=== Stronger disturbance (500 µN·m) ===")
wheel_desat(500e-6, 20.0)
