1. The Design Variable Hierarchy

Every constellation design begins with a small number of primary variables whose values propagate constraints through the rest of the design. Setting them in the wrong order — or failing to recognise their coupling — produces a design that is internally inconsistent or must be expensively revised. The hierarchy is:

TIER 1 — SERVICE REQUIREMENTS (market inputs)

Coverage region · minimum elevation angle · latency floor · data rate. Fixed before any orbital parameter is selected.

TIER 2 — ORBIT SELECTION

Altitude h · inclination i. Together these determine coverage cap size, latitude reach, J2 precession rates, orbital lifetime, latency floor, and drag makeup cost.

TIER 3 — WALKER GEOMETRY

Total satellites T · planes P · phasing F · minimum elevation ε_min. Constrained by Tier 2 and the coverage formulae. Not free choices once h, i, and ε_min are fixed.

TIER 4 — SATELLITE DESIGN

Dry mass · propulsion system (Isp) · design life. Follow from Tier 3 satellite count (cost pressure), Tier 2 altitude (drag budget), and deorbit regulations.

TIER 5 — DEPLOYMENT PLAN

Launch vehicle · staging altitude · deployment sequence · cadence. Follow from Tier 4 wet mass, Tier 3 Walker geometry, and the available launch market.

The hierarchy is not strictly sequential — there are significant feedback loops. Satellite count (Tier 3) drives satellite cost which drives satellite mass (Tier 4), which feeds back into the launch manifest (Tier 5). Altitude (Tier 2) drives the debris compliance mode, which constrains the staging strategy (Tier 5). But the hierarchy correctly identifies which variables constrain which, and it structures the design process.

THE KEY COUPLING The strongest feedback loop is between Tiers 3 and 4: satellite count sets the cost pressure that sets the satellite complexity that sets the satellite mass that sets the satellites-per-launch that sets the total launch count that closes the business case. Designers who pick T and P without considering the downstream mass implications often produce unaffordable constellations on paper.

2. How Tier 1 Drives Tier 2: Service Requirements to Orbit

The primary service requirement constraining altitude is the latency floor. At 550 km, the one-way propagation delay to a near-overhead satellite is roughly 1.8 ms; at 35° elevation it is 6–8 ms. A two-segment round trip (user → satellite → gateway → satellite → user) gives a target floor of 20–40 ms for well-engineered constellations. GEO at 35,786 km has a one-way delay of ~120 ms and a round-trip of ~480–600 ms — four to twenty times higher. This latency floor sets the altitude ceiling for LEO broadband at roughly 1,200 km, above which the latency advantage over GEO erodes enough to undermine the business case.

Within the LEO regime, altitude simultaneously drives coverage cap size, orbital lifetime, link budget, drag cost, and launch cost — all pulling in different directions. The industry convergence on 540–600 km reflects the band where all five constraints are simultaneously satisfied without unacceptable compromise on any one of them.

Inclination is driven by the coverage region requirement. Global or near-global service requires near-polar inclination (87°–98°). Mid-latitude service of the dense population and aviation corridors between roughly 55°S and 55°N is more efficiently served by 50°–55° inclination — denser coverage where the customers are, less wasted satellite time over polar oceans, and no polar seam conjunction risk.

WHY 540–600 km? — THE FIVE CONSTRAINTS Latency < 50 ms → altitude < ~1,200 km. IADC/FCC deorbit compliance → altitude ≤ ~600 km without mandatory deorbit burn. Manageable drag budget → altitude ≥ ~500 km. Acceptable Ka-band path loss at 35° elevation → altitude < ~700 km preferred. Falcon 9 payload performance → altitude ≤ ~600 km without significant mass penalty. Only 540–600 km satisfies all five simultaneously.

3. How Tier 2 Drives Tier 3: Orbit to Walker Geometry

Given altitude h = 550 km and inclination i = 53°, the coverage cap at ε_min = 35° has half-angle ρ_cap ≈ 33.4° and cap area ≈ 8.3% of the Earth's surface. The analytic minimum satellite count follows directly:

  • In-plane minimum: S_min = ⌈180°/33.4°⌉ = 6 satellites per plane
  • Inter-plane minimum: P_min = ⌈180°/(33.4°/cos 53°)⌉ = ⌈180°/55.5°⌉ = 4 planes
  • Theoretical minimum: T_min = 4 × 6 = 24 satellites

Starlink's first shell uses 72 planes × 22 satellites = 1,584 — approximately 66× the theoretical minimum. This k_geometry factor of 66 is not inefficiency. Starlink targets a higher effective minimum elevation than 35° (operationally 40°–45°), budgets for J2 phasing evolution, tolerates satellite outages without coverage gaps, and targets commercial density rather than the minimum viable coverage floor. The theoretical minimum gives a reference floor; the real design lives far above it.

OneWeb at 87.9° inclination provides broader latitude coverage from 648 satellites. Its k_geometry relative to the ε_min = 30° theoretical minimum is approximately 72 — similar to Starlink's, for similar reasons: margin, outage tolerance, and the additional density required near the polar seam to maintain coverage quality through the high-relative-velocity crossing zone.

k_geometry IS NOT WASTE The gap between theoretical minimum (24 satellites) and Starlink's first shell (1,584) reflects deliberate design decisions: higher elevation targets, service quality margin, satellite outage tolerance, J2 phasing robustness, and commercial density for peak demand. Every factor of k_geometry corresponds to a real performance or reliability requirement.

4. The Altitude–Inclination Tradeoff in Practice

The real tradeoffs are more textured than the formulas suggest, because the commercial context imposes constraints the geometric model does not capture.

Market geography: the vast majority of broadband demand — and virtually all commercial aviation demand — originates in the latitude band between approximately 60°S and 60°N. A 53° constellation concentrates coverage exactly where the customers are. A near-polar shell also covers polar oceans and ice sheets — real but much smaller markets. The question is whether incremental polar revenue justifies the incremental cost of the higher-inclination architecture, including polar seam management.

Starlink's answer was no, for the primary shell. The 53° shell serves the densest markets efficiently; supplementary shells at 70° and 97.6° fill the gap for premium high-latitude customers. OneWeb's answer was yes, because its initial customer base was explicitly global — remote enterprise, government, maritime, and aviation customers who require consistent worldwide coverage and will pay a premium for it.

Regulatory timing: ITU coordination and FCC licensing run on timescales of years, and filing date determines coordination priority. Several operators filed for high-inclination shells first to secure priority for global coverage they could not yet afford to deploy, intending to fill mid-latitude shells later from a position of regulatory strength. This explains some of the orbital shell structures that appear overbuilt relative to their initial deployments.

Launch site constraints: a 53° inclination is reachable from Cape Canaveral without a costly plane-change dogleg; a 97.6° retrograde orbit requires a polar launch site or an expensive dogleg. This couples inclination directly to launch infrastructure cost and availability, and is a real constraint in launch vehicle selection.

5. The Propulsion Selection Decision

The propulsion choice is effectively binary at the system level: chemical propulsion (hydrazine or bipropellant) for high thrust at moderate Isp, or electric propulsion (Hall thruster or gridded ion) for low thrust at very high Isp.

For a five-year LEO mission without inclination correction, the total Δv budget is roughly 70–105 m/s. At Isp = 230 s (hydrazine), this requires approximately 10–14% of dry mass in propellant. At Isp = 1,600 s (Hall thruster), the same budget requires approximately 0.5–0.7% of dry mass. For a 300 kg satellite, the difference is 30–42 kg vs 1.5–2 kg. At fleet scale — 1,584 Starlink satellites — this difference is roughly 47–67 tonnes vs 2.4–3.2 tonnes of propellant. The Hall thruster wins decisively on mass and therefore launch cost.

The tradeoff is manoeuvre duration. A hydrazine thruster at 5 N executes a 10 m/s manoeuvre in minutes; a Hall thruster at 50 mN requires approximately 10 hours of continuous thrusting. For routine drag makeup this is manageable — the thruster fires during eclipse periods. For collision avoidance manoeuvres with hours of warning, it is a real operational constraint that must be managed through adequate CAM Δv margins and pre-planned profiles rather than reactive last-minute burns. Starlink and OneWeb both chose Hall thrusters and manage this operationally.

THE FLEET-SCALE PROPELLANT CALCULATION At 1,584 satellites, the choice between hydrazine and a Hall thruster is a fleet-mass decision. At 30 kg propellant per satellite (hydrazine) vs 2 kg (HET), the difference is 47 tonnes of propellant across the fleet — equivalent to roughly 3 additional Falcon 9 launches at current payload fractions. That is a hard, concrete cost difference, not an abstraction.

6. The Deployment Sequence Decision

The deployment sequencing decision interacts with the commercial strategy in a way that is rarely made explicit in technical analyses. The choice between plane-by-plane deployment (concentrated coverage, faster to first revenue in one region) and distributed deployment via J2 drift (broad but sparse coverage early, slower to regional density) is a market strategy choice as much as a technical one.

Plane-by-plane deployment makes sense when the operator has a specific high-value geographic corridor where early revenue is available. Distributed deployment makes sense when the operator needs to demonstrate global capability early — to satisfy a regulatory milestone requiring a minimum number of satellites in service, or to serve a customer base that will not accept partial geographic coverage.

Starlink's initial deployment concentrated on the United States, beginning beta service in the Pacific Northwest and expanding outward as additional planes were populated. OneWeb targeted a globally distributed deployment to serve its enterprise and government customers, which required complete shell coverage rather than a regional subset — driving a different launch cadence and sequencing strategy.

The J2 drift technique enables the best of both approaches: a single launch cadence populates multiple planes simultaneously without inter-plane manoeuvres, accepting weeks to months of drift time in exchange for eliminating dedicated plane-change Δv. At the lower staging altitudes Starlink uses, the higher J2 rate compresses the drift time further, making the technique practical for aggressive deployment timelines.

7. Starlink, OneWeb, and Kuiper Compared

The three primary first-generation LEO broadband constellations represent three distinct answers to the same design problem, shaped by different customer bases, business cases, and technical capabilities.

Starlink is the most data-rate-focused, highest-density architecture. The 53° primary shell is a commercial broadband bet on mid-latitude consumer and enterprise markets. Mass production (>1,000 satellites/year), vertical launch integration (Falcon 9, Starship), and krypton Hall thruster propulsion are all optimised for lowest cost per bit delivered to mid-latitude users. Rapid iteration (v1.0, v1.5, v2.0 satellites with significant capability changes between generations) reflects a software-industry mindset applied to hardware: accept early performance limits in exchange for speed to market, then iterate.

OneWeb is the global-coverage-first architecture. The 87.9° near-polar shell at 600 km with 648 satellites provides genuine near-global coverage from a single shell at relatively modest count. The customer base — maritime, aviation, remote enterprise, government — pays higher per-Mbps prices but requires global coverage and longer operational lifetimes than the consumer model. OneWeb prioritises coverage universality over density; its capacity is far below Starlink's, but capacity is not the binding constraint for its customer base.

Kuiper (Amazon) occupies the middle ground: Walker delta at 51.9°, approximately 1,156 satellites at ~590 km. Similar mid-latitude market focus to Starlink but independently optimised, with higher satellite mass suggesting more payload per satellite. Amazon's retail and AWS cloud infrastructure create distribution and integration advantages that allow Kuiper to compete differently from Starlink rather than replicating its architecture at lower cost.

THREE ARCHITECTURES, ONE DESIGN SPACE All three constellations operate in the same 540–600 km altitude band for the same reasons. They differ in inclination — the market segmentation variable — and in satellite count and mass — the cost/coverage tradeoff variable. The design space is not infinitely large: orbital mechanics, physics, and regulation together constrain viable architectures to a much smaller set than the number of free parameters suggests.

8. What Module 2 Has Established

Module 2 has constructed the complete framework for describing, sizing, and comparing LEO satellite constellations from first principles. The framework rests on five pillars.

Orbital mechanics (m2-1, m2-2): the two-body orbit, the six Keplerian elements, J2 precession, and atmospheric drag define the physical environment. Every design parameter has an orbital mechanics consequence that cannot be wished away.

Ground geometry (m2-3): the elevation angle, slant range, pass duration, and Doppler shift describe what the orbit looks like from a user's location. These quantities are the inputs to the link budget (M4) and the air interface design (M5).

Coverage geometry (m2-4): the Walker constellation, the coverage cap, and the spacing conditions translate orbital parameters into satellite count. The k_geometry factor bridges the analytic minimum and the real-world design.

Propulsion and lifetime (m2-5): the rocket equation, the Δv budget by axis, and propulsion system selection determine satellite mass, design life, and deorbit strategy. The FCC 5-year rule and IADC 25-year guideline are hard constraints on the design space.

Launch and deployment (m2-6): the launch manifest, staging orbit, Hohmann raise, and deployment sequence determine time from decision to revenue and total launch cost as the dominant near-term cash outflow.
