MODULE 6
USER TERMINALS & D2D
CAPSTONE

Six lessons built a terminal from the aperture outward: gain and G/T (m6-1), the array factor (m6-2), the beamforming architecture (m6-3), scan loss and the regulatory mask (m6-4), pointing and the pass-weighted served G/T (m6-5), and the segment-and-certification wrapper (m6-6). This lesson runs the result through the machinery of the two modules before it. Every terminal class becomes a (G/T, EIRP) pair; that pair enters the Module 4 link budget; the resulting C/(N₀+I₀) enters the Module 5 throughput engine; and the answer — net throughput, at a cost, closing or not closing a target service — is the thing the whole program has been building toward from the terminal side. The spine's final stage is the evaluator that does this for any terminal, and the module's central question finally gets a quantitative answer: what does the dish's ~9 dB of G/T actually buy?

The answer is the lesson's spine, and it is not what a datasheet-reader expects. In clear sky, on a wideband forward carrier, the dish's advantage buys nothing: every broadband terminal saturates the MODCOD ladder and delivers the identical 954 Mbps, because — per m5-7 §5 — decibels above the top rung purchase no throughput. The G/T gap is invisible exactly where marketing points its cameras. It becomes decisive somewhere else entirely: availability. Under a 20 dB rain fade the dish still delivers 794 Mbps while the worst-case flat panel has collapsed to 211; at 25 dB the panel outages and the dish holds 477. The terminal choice does not buy peak throughput. It buys the right tail of the availability curve — the m5-7 lesson, now written in hardware. This reframes the entire module: the dish-versus-panel decision is a decision about rain margin, session continuity, and the worst 0.1% of hours, not about the headline megabits every terminal shares.

1. The evaluator: terminal → M4 → M5

The chain, assembled from the two prior modules' engines:

(G/T, EIRP) → Module 4 downlink/uplink budget → C/(N₀+I₀) → Es/N₀ = C/(N₀+I₀) − 10·log₁₀(R_s) → MODCOD selection → net throughput

Every arrow is code already written and verified: the link budget is m4-1's LinkBudget, the MODCOD ladder and overhead chain are m5-2 through m5-4, and the terminal side is m6-1 through m6-6. The evaluator is the wiring, and its value is that it makes the terminal's honest interface — the m6-5 served G/T, not the broadside datasheet number — the thing that flows into the service calculation. A terminal is not its best angle; it is its pass-weighted performance under its segment's operations, and the evaluator enforces that.

2. The clear-sky result: saturation hides the gap

Running the broadband terminals through a common LEO downlink (satellite EIRP ~52 dBW/beam, 1,200 km slant, 250 MHz Ka forward carrier):

Eleven decibels of G/T span this table and every broadband row delivers the same 954 Mbps. The reason is m5-7 §5 in one line: at 250 MHz even the worst panel's Es/N₀ (23.2 dB) sits far above the top rung's 16.05 dB threshold, so the ladder saturates and the surplus C/N₀ — 7 dB of it for the worst panel, 20 dB for the enterprise dish — buys nothing. A datasheet comparing these terminals on clear-sky throughput would find them identical and conclude, wrongly, that G/T does not matter. It matters enormously; it just does not matter here.

Now the 9 dB gap is the whole story. The dish holds its top MODCOD through a 15 dB fade that has already stepped the served panel down two rungs and the worst-case panel to a quarter of peak; at 25 dB the worst panel has left the air while the dish still carries 477 Mbps. The terminal's G/T is, precisely, its position on the availability curve — its margin against the m4-4 rain statistics — and that is what the enterprise SLA (m6-6 §2) is buying when it specifies a dish it does not, in clear sky, need. The consumer panel's answer to the same physics is not more G/T but satellite selection (m6-5 §4): staying at high elevation keeps the fade path short, moving the panel's whole availability curve without adding a decibel of hardware.

4. The return direction: where G/T never hid

The forward link saturated; the return link cannot. The terminal transmits into the satellite's fixed G/T over a narrow carrier (m4-7, m5-3), and there the terminal's EIRP is the binding term with no ladder ceiling to hide behind. The dish's single high-power BUC through high gain (44.1 dBW EIRP, m6-1) and the panel's N²-aggregated milliwatts (36.2 dBW) differ by ~8 dB, and on a return carrier into a realistic small-satellite receive G/T (~−2 dB/K, 20 MHz) that difference is throughput, directly: the dish closes 16-APSK 3/4 at ~50 Mbps while the panel manages only QPSK 1/2 at ~17 Mbps — a 3× gap, the full 8 dB EIRP difference converted to rungs with no ladder ceiling to absorb it. The m5-7 §3 forward/return asymmetry (727 vs 283 Mbps) is partly this terminal-EIRP story. The module's two apertures thus separate on both the availability of the forward link and the raw capacity of the return, while tying on the one metric — clear-sky forward throughput — that a naive comparison would use. A terminal trade study that measures only the tie is measuring the wrong thing.

The frontier restates m6-3's honest crossover with the served figures attached: for a fixed terminal the dish dominates at every G/T, and the array's cost climbs steeply because G/T is bought with element count (N per dB). The array wins only when the comparison is loaded with what the dish needs and the panel does not — the tracking gimbal, the second positioner for make-before-break, the radome volume, the moving-part warranty exposure (m6-3 §8) — and when the segment's currency (m6-6) prices agility, conformality, or self-installation above the raw decibels. The frontier is not a technology ranking; it is the input to a currency-weighted decision, and the weight is the segment.

6. The device side of D2D: the handset revisited

m5-6 built the direct-to-device link from the network's side, freezing the handset at −10 dBW EIRP and −30 dB/K G/T. This module reaches the same terminal from the opposite direction, and the two derivations meet: the handset is simply the terminal trade space evaluated at its degenerate corner — an aperture of one near-omni element (m6-1's array with N = 1), no pointing (m6-5's budget with the attitude term unbounded and no gain to protect), no scan management because there is no beam to scan, and a certification profile (m6-6) written by consumer-electronics regulators rather than the FAA. Every knob this module turned is turned to its minimum, and the result is the −30 dB/K that made D2D a coverage product.

Running it through the evaluator confirms m5-6 from the terminal side: at the handset's G/T the forward link closes only a narrow carrier (5 MHz, ~2 Mbps at the module's reference geometry — the coverage-not-capacity result), and no amount of the satellite's EIRP saturates it into the broadband regime, because the handset's aperture, not the link's power, is the binding constraint. The device side of D2D is therefore not a special case; it is the terminal trade space's boundary condition, the point where the aperture shrinks to a single unpointed element and the entire burden of closing the link moves to the space segment. The module's span — from the enterprise dish's 21 dB/K to the handset's −30 — is 51 dB of G/T across one design space, three orders of magnitude, evaluated through one engine.

7. The module, closed

Module 6's question was what produces the (G/T, EIRP, pointing) that Modules 4 and 5 consumed as given. The answer is a design space with a small number of axes — aperture family, element count and lattice, beamforming architecture, scan and mask compliance, tracking and pass geometry, segment and certification — and a small number of hard trades among them, every one of which this module made quantitative. The two portable conclusions:

First, the dish-versus-panel decision is an availability decision, not a throughput decision. Clear-sky broadband throughput is a tie that saturation enforces; the dish's G/T buys the right tail of the availability curve and the raw return-link capacity, and the panel buys agility, conformality, self-installation, and — through satellite selection — much of the availability back without hardware. A trade study that compares peak megabits compares the one number the physics has already equalised.

Second, operations and geometry co-produce terminal performance as much as hardware does. The m6-5 served G/T spanned 3.2 dB across identical silicon on selection and tilt alone; the availability curve is shaped by the scheduler's satellite choice; the segment wraps the same physics in wildly different cost. The terminal is not a component with a datasheet number. It is a component operated within a constellation under a segment's constraints, and its honest performance figure exists only at that intersection — which is exactly where this module's evaluator computes it, and exactly what Module 7 (networking, inter-satellite links, and the routing that the handover choreography feeds) will build upon.

9. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

The spine is complete: TerminalEvaluator takes any terminal (aperture, architecture, served-G/T, EIRP, segment) and returns its net throughput and availability curve through the actual M4 and M5 engines, plus its position on the cost frontier. The module's deliverable to the rest of the program is the honest terminal interface — a served G/T and an EIRP with their provenance intact, and an availability curve rather than a single number — for every class from enterprise dish to handset. Module 7 consumes the handover choreography (m6-5 §7) and the satellite-selection freedom (m6-5 §4) as inputs to routing and inter-satellite topology; the terminal, having been built from the aperture out and validated through the link, becomes a solved node.

The program's arc to here: Module 4 turned a link into decibels, Module 5 turned decibels into bits, and Module 6 turned hardware into the decibels — closing the loop from a customer's roof or fuselage or hand to the throughput they experience. What remains is the network above the links: how the bits, once a terminal has earned them, are routed across a constellation in motion. That is Module 7.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x) if x > 0 else -999.0

MODCOD = [("QPSK 1/4",0.490243,-2.35),("QPSK 1/2",0.988858,1.00),
          ("QPSK 3/4",1.487473,4.03),("8PSK 3/4",2.228124,7.91),
          ("16APSK 2/3",2.637201,8.97),("16APSK 3/4",2.966728,10.21),
          ("32APSK 3/4",3.703295,12.73),("32APSK 9/10",4.453027,16.05)]
PILOT = {"QPSK":0.9762,"8PSK":0.9773,"16APSK":0.9763,"32APSK":0.9784}

def select(es, margin=1.5):
    best = None
    for n,e,t in MODCOD:
        if es >= t+margin and (best is None or e > best[1]): best = (n,e,t)
    return best

def fspl(d_km, f_ghz):
    return 92.45 + 20*math.log10(d_km) + 20*math.log10(f_ghz)

def evaluate(name, gt, bw_hz, sat_eirp=52.0, slant_km=1200, f_ghz=20,
             atm_db=1.5, fade_db=0.0, alpha=0.10):
    cn0 = sat_eirp - fspl(slant_km, f_ghz) - atm_db - fade_db + gt + 228.6
    rs = bw_hz/(1+alpha)
    s = select(cn0 - db(rs))
    if not s: return name, cn0, "OUTAGE", 0.0
    p = PILOT[[k for k in PILOT if s[0].startswith(k)][0]]
    net = s[1]*rs*0.97*p*0.9934
    return name, cn0, s[0], net

TERMS = [("enterprise 75cm", 21.2, 250e6), ("Ka VSAT 65cm", 19.9, 250e6),
         ("aviation ESA", 11.1, 250e6), ("consumer ESA served", 10.9, 250e6),
         ("consumer ESA worst", 7.7, 250e6), ("handset D2D", -30.0, 5e6)]

print(f"CLEAR SKY:  {'terminal':<22}{'C/N0':>8}{'MODCOD':>13}{'net Mbps':>10}")
for n, gt, bw in TERMS:
    name, cn0, mc, net = evaluate(n, gt, bw)
    print(f"            {name:<22}{cn0:>8.1f}{mc:>13}{net/1e6:>10.1f}")

Exercise 3 — Spine final: the TerminalEvaluator

The module's capstone object: any terminal, through the whole program.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x) if x > 0 else -999.0

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M6 SPINE PROJECT — TERMINAL MODEL (final assembly)
#   Stage 1 aperture (m6-1)      Stage 4 scan+mask (m6-4)
#   Stage 2 array factor (m6-2)  Stage 5 pass+pointing (m6-5)
#   Stage 3 architecture (m6-3)  Stage 6 segment (m6-6)
#   This stage: run the terminal through M4 + M5 and return
#   net throughput, availability curve, cost-frontier position.
#   The loop closes: aperture -> decibels -> bits -> service.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

MODCOD = [("QPSK 1/4",0.490243,-2.35),("QPSK 1/2",0.988858,1.00),
          ("QPSK 3/4",1.487473,4.03),("8PSK 3/4",2.228124,7.91),
          ("16APSK 2/3",2.637201,8.97),("16APSK 3/4",2.966728,10.21),
          ("32APSK 3/4",3.703295,12.73),("32APSK 9/10",4.453027,16.05)]
PILOT = {"QPSK":0.9762,"8PSK":0.9773,"16APSK":0.9763,"32APSK":0.9784}

class Terminal:
    def __init__(self, name, gt_served, eirp, bw_fwd=250e6, bw_ret=4e6,
                 aperture="array", cost_eur=2000, segment="consumer"):
        self.name, self.gt, self.eirp = name, gt_served, eirp
        self.bw_fwd, self.bw_ret = bw_fwd, bw_ret
        self.aperture, self.cost, self.segment = aperture, cost_eur, segment

class TerminalEvaluator:
    def __init__(self, sat_eirp=52.0, sat_gt=-2.0, slant_km=1200,
                 f_fwd=20, f_ret=30, atm_db=1.5):
        # sat_gt modest: a small LEO satellite's receive G/T, so the
        # return does NOT saturate and terminal EIRP binds (§4).
        self.sat_eirp, self.sat_gt = sat_eirp, sat_gt
        self.slant, self.f_fwd, self.f_ret, self.atm = slant_km, f_fwd, f_ret, atm_db
    def _fspl(self, f):
        return 92.45 + 20*math.log10(self.slant) + 20*math.log10(f)
    def _select(self, es, margin=1.5):
        best = None
        for n,e,t in MODCOD:
            if es >= t+margin and (best is None or e > best[1]): best = (n,e,t)
        return best
    def _net(self, cn0, bw, alpha=0.10):
        rs = bw/(1+alpha); s = self._select(cn0 - db(rs))
        if not s: return "OUTAGE", 0.0
        p = PILOT[[k for k in PILOT if s[0].startswith(k)][0]]
        return s[0], s[1]*rs*0.97*p*0.9934
    def forward(self, t, fade_db=0.0):
        cn0 = self.sat_eirp - self._fspl(self.f_fwd) - self.atm - fade_db + t.gt + 228.6
        return self._net(cn0, t.bw_fwd)
    def ret(self, t, fade_db=0.0):
        cn0 = t.eirp - self._fspl(self.f_ret) - self.atm - fade_db + self.sat_gt + 228.6
        return self._net(cn0, t.bw_ret)
    def availability(self, t, fades=(0,5,10,15,20,25)):
        return [(f,) + self.forward(t, f) for f in fades]
    def report(self, t):
        mc_f, net_f = self.forward(t)
        mc_r, net_r = self.ret(t)
        print(f"\n{'─'*56}")
        print(f"  TERMINAL — {t.name} ({t.segment}, €{t.cost})")
        print(f"{'─'*56}")
        print(f"  G/T {t.gt:+.1f} dB/K   EIRP {t.eirp:.1f} dBW")
        print(f"  forward: {mc_f:<12} {net_f/1e6:7.1f} Mbps")
        print(f"  return:  {mc_r:<12} {net_r/1e6:7.1f} Mbps")
        print(f"  availability (fade → fwd Mbps):")
        for f, mc, net in self.availability(t):
            bar = f"{net/1e6:6.0f}" if net > 0 else "  OUT "
            print(f"     {f:>2} dB  {bar}  {mc}")
        print(f"{'─'*56}")

ev = TerminalEvaluator()
fleet = [
    Terminal("enterprise 75cm dish", 21.2, 44.1, bw_ret=20e6, aperture="dish",
             cost_eur=450, segment="enterprise"),
    Terminal("consumer ESA (served)", 10.9, 36.2, bw_ret=20e6, cost_eur=500,
             segment="consumer"),
    Terminal("consumer ESA (worst)",  7.7, 36.2, bw_ret=20e6, cost_eur=500,
             segment="consumer"),
    Terminal("handset D2D", -30.0, -10.0, bw_fwd=5e6, bw_ret=0.18e6,
             aperture="omni", cost_eur=0, segment="D2D"),
]
for t in fleet:
    ev.report(t)
