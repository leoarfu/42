1. Newton's law of gravitation & centripetal force balance

Why this matters

This is ground zero for understanding orbits. Every LEO challenge — handovers every 5–10 min, huge constellations, strong Doppler, short passes — comes from satellites moving very fast. This equation explains why.

The physics

Gravity pulls the satellite toward Earth's center. Its forward speed wants to fly it straight into space. Perfect balance creates a stable circular orbit.

Gravitational force = Centripetal force
G × M_earth × m / r² = m × v² / r

Satellite mass (m) cancels out, giving orbital velocity:

v = √(G × M_earth / r)

Real numbers – LEO vs GEO

OrbitAltitudeSpeedPeriodOrbits/day
Starlink LEO~550 km7.67 km/s95.6 min15.1
ISS~400 km7.66 km/s92.7 min15.5
GEO35,786 km3.07 km/s24 h1

What this means in practice

  • A LEO satellite circles Earth ~15× per day → disappears over the horizon in minutes.
  • An aircraft terminal must switch satellites constantly → phased arrays & predictive handovers are essential.
  • GEO stays fixed → no handovers, but ~600 ms latency (bad for real-time apps).
  • LEO delivers ~20–40 ms latency → huge win for aviation, but needs thousands of satellites.
Key takeaway: Lower altitude = faster satellites = many more needed for continuous coverage. That's why Starlink needs ~9,800 satellites while 3–5 GEO birds can do the same job statically.

Try it yourself

import math

G = 6.67430e-11       # gravitational constant
M_earth = 5.972e24    # Earth mass (kg)
R_earth = 6371000     # Earth radius (m)

def orbital_velocity(altitude_km):
r = R_earth + altitude_km * 1000
return math.sqrt(G * M_earth / r) / 1000  # km/s

print(f"LEO 550 km : {orbital_velocity(550):.2f} km/s")
print(f"GEO        : {orbital_velocity(35786):.2f} km/s")
print(f"MEO GPS    : {orbital_velocity(20200):.2f} km/s")

2. Kepler's third law – orbital period vs altitude

Why this matters

Newton gave us "how fast." Kepler's third law gives us "how long" — the time it takes to complete one full orbit. This number drives everything: how often satellites appear, how long passes last, how many you need.

T² ∝ a³ → T = 2π √(a³ / μ)

Where μ = G × M_earth ≈ 3.986 × 10¹⁴ m³/s² and a = Earth radius + altitude.

How period changes with altitude

OrbitAltitudePeriodOrbits/day
ISS~400 km~92.7 min~15.5
Starlink~550 km~95.6 min~15.1
GPS MEO~20,200 km~12 hours~2
GEO35,786 km24 h1
Key takeaway: Higher orbit = slower, longer orbit. GEO matches Earth's rotation and appears fixed. LEO screams past 15× a day — that's why mega-constellations are necessary for continuous coverage.

3. Satellite visibility cone & pass duration

Why this matters

Visibility cone determines the angular radius of a satellite's footprint from Earth’s center, directly impacting how long a satellite is visible from a ground station. In LEO, short pass durations (5-15 min) drive frequent handovers and require large constellations for continuous coverage. This is key for aviation terminals, where link stability is critical.

The physics

The visibility cone is the geocentric angle θ subtended by the satellite's coverage footprint. It depends on altitude h, Earth radius R_e (~6371 km), and minimum elevation angle E_min (typically 5-10° for clear line-of-sight, avoiding terrain/atmosphere).

θ = arccos((R_e / (R_e + h)) * cos(E_min)) - E_min

Pass duration D is the time the satellite is within this cone, approximated for circular orbits as D ≈ (2θ / ω_orb) where ω_orb = 2π / T is angular velocity from Kepler's law.

Real numbers – LEO examples

OrbitAltitudeE_minCone θAvg Pass Duration
Starlink550 km5°~60°~10 min
ISS400 km10°~55°~8 min
MEO GPS20,200 km5°~14°~6 hours
GEO35,786 km5°~8°Indefinite (fixed)

What this means in practice

  • In LEO, a satellite is visible 6-8 times/day per location, but each pass lasts minutes → requires predictive scheduling for terminals.
  • Higher E_min reduces θ (better signal but shorter passes); used in urban areas to avoid multipath.
  • For aviation: Fast aircraft motion compounds short passes, needing seamless handovers.
  • Optimization: Constellations design for overlapping cones to minimize gaps.
Key takeaway: LEO's small visibility cone and short passes (vs GEO's static view) make continuous coverage challenging but enable low latency—solved by thousands of sats with overlapping footprints.

Try it yourself

import math

R_e = 6371  # km
def visibility_cone(alt_km, e_min_deg):
    r = R_e + alt_km
    e_min = math.radians(e_min_deg)
    theta = math.acos((R_e / r) * math.cos(e_min)) - e_min
    return math.degrees(theta)

def pass_duration(alt_km, theta_deg):
    mu = 3.986e5  # km^3/s^2
    a = R_e + alt_km
    T = 2 * math.pi * math.sqrt(a**3 / mu) / 60  # min
    omega = 360 / T  # deg/min
    return 2 * theta_deg / omega  # min

print(f"Starlink cone: {visibility_cone(550, 5):.1f}°")
print(f"Pass duration: {pass_duration(550, visibility_cone(550, 5)):.1f} min")

4. LEO ground tracks & nodal precession (J₂ effect)

Why this matters

Ground tracks map a satellite's path over Earth's surface, revealing coverage patterns. The J₂ effect (Earth's equatorial bulge) causes nodal precession, shifting the orbital plane—harnessed for sun-synchronous orbits (SSO) in imaging sats, but must be corrected in propagators for accurate LEO predictions.

The physics

Ground track is the subsatellite point in ECEF frame: lat = arcsin(z/r), lon = arctan(y/x) - ω_e t (Earth rotation). J₂ precession: secular change in RAAN Ω due to oblateness J₂ ~1.0826e-3.

dΩ/dt = - (3/2) n J₂ (R_e / p)^2 cos i

Where n = √(μ / a³), p = a (1-e²). For SSO, set dΩ/dt = 360°/365.25 days ~0.986°/day.

Real numbers – LEO examples

InclinationAltitudedΩ/dt (°/day)Track Type
53° (Starlink)550 km~ -4.5Prograde, shifting west
98° (SSO)800 km~0.986Sun-sync, consistent lighting
90° (Polar)400 km~0Fixed relative to stars

What this means in practice

  • Without J₂, tracks repeat daily; with it, precession enables global coverage over time.
  • Repeated tracks: After R revs in D days, where R/D = rational (e.g., 15/1 for LEO).
  • For aviation: Predict tracks for optimal routing/handovers.
  • Perturbations: J₂ dominant in LEO (~7-8°/day for low inc); ignore higher J for basics.
Key takeaway: J₂ turns "fixed" orbits into precessing ones, enabling SSO for Earth obs but requiring compensation in LEO constellation design to avoid gaps.

Try it yourself

import math

mu = 3.986e5  # km^3/s^2
R_e = 6371  # km
J2 = 1.0826e-3

def nodal_precession(a_km, i_deg, e=0):
    n = math.sqrt(mu / a_km**3) * 86400 / (2*math.pi)  # rev/day
    p = a_km * (1 - e**2)
    return -1.5 * n * J2 * (R_e / p)**2 * math.cos(math.radians(i_deg))  # °/day

print(f"Starlink precession: {nodal_precession(R_e + 550, 53):.2f} °/day")
print(f"SSO precession: {nodal_precession(R_e + 800, 98):.3f} °/day")

5. Visibility, passes & handover frequency

Why this matters

Visibility passes are windows when a sat is above E_min from a location; in LEO constellations, this leads to handovers (switching sats) every few min to maintain connectivity. Understanding frequency minimizes drops, optimizes load, and is vital for real-time apps like aviation comms.

The physics

Visibility: Sat elevation > E_min; number of passes ~ orbits/day * (θ / 180°). Handover freq = 1 / avg service time per sat. Strategies: Max elevation, min Doppler, graph-based for load balance.

Service time ≈ pass duration - overlap margin

Handover triggers: Signal threshold, geometry change.

Real numbers – LEO examples

ConstellationSatsAvg Visible SatsHandover Freq
Starlink (partial)~1000~5-10Every 5-10 min
Full Starlink~12k~20-40Every 2-4 min
Iridium66~1-2Every 9 min
GEO (ref)31None

What this means in practice

  • LEO: 15 passes/day/location, but handovers frequent due to speed ~7.6 km/s.
  • Challenges: Doppler (~10 kHz shift), load (avoid hot spots), QoS (minimize latency spikes).
  • Strategies: Hysteresis to avoid ping-pong; AI/ML for prediction in mega-consts.
  • For aviation: Aircraft motion increases effective freq; needs beam steering.
Key takeaway: LEO's dynamic visibility demands smart handover strategies to achieve GEO-like reliability with 10x lower latency—key enabler for global broadband.

Try it yourself

import math

def passes_per_day(alt_km, lat_deg=43.6):  # e.g., Toulouse lat
    a = 6371 + alt_km
    T_min = 2 * math.pi * math.sqrt(a**3 / 3.986e5) / 60
    orbits_day = 1440 / T_min
    theta_deg = 60  # approx from topic 3
    return orbits_day * (theta_deg / 180) * math.cos(math.radians(lat_deg))  # adjust for lat

print(f"Starlink passes/day over Toulouse: {passes_per_day(550):.1f}")

6. Constellation fundamentals (Walker patterns, streets-of-coverage)

Why this matters

Constellations provide continuous/global coverage beyond single sats. Walker patterns for symmetric distribution, streets for efficient overlapping—essential for LEO mega-consts like Starlink to fill visibility gaps.

The physics

Walker: T/P/F notation (T=sats, P=planes even over 360°, F=phasing γ=F*360/T). Streets: Polar planes over 180°, overlapping footprints for "streets" (co/counter-rotating).

Plane spacing = 360° / P (Walker Delta); 180° / P (Walker Star/Streets)

Coverage: Min sats for N-fold = f(inc, alt, E_min); optimize for GDOP, gaps.

Real numbers – Examples

TypeExampleConfigSats for Global
Walker DeltaGPS55°:24/6/124
Walker StarStarlink53°:1580/22/?~12k
StreetsIridium-likePolar:66/6/266

What this means in practice

  • Walker: Uniform, good for nav (low GDOP); streets: Efficient for high-lat, fewer sats.
  • Hybrid: Starlink mixes for better coverage.
  • Design: Min gaps, max fold (visible sats); LEO needs 10x more than MEO.
  • For aviation: Ensure >1 visible sat always for handover redundancy.
Key takeaway: Walker for global symmetry, streets for targeted efficiency—LEO consts scale to thousands to overcome short passes, enabling ubiquitous connectivity.

Try it yourself

import math

def walker_positions(T, P, F, inc_deg=53):
    gamma = F * 360 / T
    planes = [i * 360 / P for i in range(P)]
    sats_per_plane = T // P
    print(f"Plane RAANs: {planes}")
    print(f"Phasing: {gamma}°")

7. Tools & data sources (SGP4, TLEs, Celestrak, Skyfield, Poliastro)

Why this matters

Real orbits use data like TLEs (Two-Line Elements) from Celestrak; SGP4 propagates them analytically. Tools like Skyfield/Poliastro enable quick sims—vital for hands-on LEO analysis without full numerical models.

The physics

TLE: Mean elements (inc, RAAN, ecc, etc.) + B* drag term. SGP4: Simplified model with J2, drag, lunisolar; accuracy ~1 km for 1 day in LEO.

Position = SGP4(TLE, time) → (x,y,z,vx,vy,vz) in TEME frame

Convert to ECEF for ground tracks.

Real numbers – Tool overview

ToolUseExample
SGP4Propagate TLEsOfficial USG lib
CelestrakTLE source~25k objects
SkyfieldPython propagationLoad TLE, compute pos
PoliastroAstrodynamicsManeuvers, plotting

What this means in practice

  • Fetch TLEs daily (decay); Space-Track for auth access.
  • Limitations: SGP4 for LEO/MEO; not high-precision (use GMAT for that).
  • Tutorials: Skyfield docs for basics; Poliastro for Lambert/maneuvers.
  • For aviation: Predict sat positions for beam pointing.
Key takeaway: TLE/SGP4 for fast, real-world sims; Skyfield/Poliastro bridge theory to code—start here for custom LEO tools.
