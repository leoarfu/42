End-to-End Latency
& the Transport Layer

MODULE 7
NETWORKING, ISL & ROUTING
ADVANCED

The routing machinery of m7-4 and m7-5 exists to move a packet from a terminal, across the mesh, to a destination. This lesson assembles what that packet actually experiences — the full end-to-end latency budget — and then hands it to the layer that has to live with it: the transport layer, TCP and QUIC, protocols built for the stable paths of the terrestrial internet and now asked to run over a network whose path length changes every fifteen seconds and whose handovers inject loss that looks exactly like congestion. The LEO mesh's headline number is excellent — a round-trip time of ~54 ms, nine times better than GEO's ~477 ms, and competitive with or better than fibre on long routes (m7-1). But the headline hides the problem. Transport protocols do not only care about how large the RTT is; they care about how stable it is, and a constellation delivers a low but restless RTT — one that steps as the path reconfigures and stutters as handovers drop packets. The mean is a selling point; the variance is the engineering problem.

This lesson builds the latency budget term by term (access up and down, mesh propagation, per-hop processing, queuing), quantifies the LEO advantage over GEO and its competitiveness with fibre, and then confronts the transport pathology: why TCP's congestion control mistakes a handover for congestion and craters throughput, why its window is mis-sized every time the RTT steps, and what QUIC and constellation-aware transport can do about it. The spine gains the end-to-end latency distribution — not a single number but a distribution, because the path changes — and a model of how transport behaves across it. This is the last component before the synthesis assembles the whole router.

1. The end-to-end latency budget

A packet's journey from terminal to terminal (or terminal to gateway) has four latency components, assembled here for a representative transcontinental route (London–NY, ~5,570 km):

Component Value Source
Access up (terminal → satellite, 40° elevation)2.7 msm6-5 slant range
Mesh propagation (across the ISL path)18.6 msm7-3 path length / c
Per-hop processing (~4 hops × 0.5 ms)2.0 msm7-4 regeneration
Queuing (light load)~1.2 msTraffic-dependent
Access down (satellite → terminal)2.7 msm6-5 slant range
Total one-way~27 msSum
Round-trip time~54 ms2× one-way

Three observations. First, mesh propagation dominates — the ~18.6 ms of light-travel across the ISL path is two-thirds of the one-way budget, and it is irreducible (it is the speed of light over the distance). Second, the access legs are small but fixed — ~2.7 ms each way, the up-and-down cost of reaching orbit that m7-1's latency crossover was built on. Third, queuing is the only load-dependent term — under light load it is a millisecond, but under congestion it grows without bound, which is exactly the regime where transport protocols make their worst decisions (§4). The budget is dominated by physics (propagation) with a small deterministic overhead (access, processing) and one dangerous variable (queuing).

2. The LEO advantage, quantified

Against the alternatives, the LEO mesh's ~54 ms RTT is a strong number:

Versus GEO. A geostationary bent-pipe link climbs to 35,786 km and back — twice, for a round trip — a propagation floor of ~477 ms RTT that no engineering can reduce, because it is light-speed over the GEO distance. The LEO mesh at ~54 ms is nine times better, and the difference is qualitative, not incremental: 477 ms breaks interactive applications (voice, video calls, gaming, responsive web) that 54 ms serves comfortably. This is the LEO value proposition in one ratio, and it is why LEO broadband exists as a category despite GEO's decades of incumbency.

Versus fibre. As m7-1 established, the LEO mesh beats fibre above the ~1,500–2,000 km crossover, because vacuum is 47% faster than glass and the mesh's fixed access cost amortises over distance. On the London–NY route the mesh's ~54 ms RTT undercuts fibre's ~76 ms; on transpacific routes the advantage exceeds 30 ms one-way. The mesh is not merely adequate against fibre on long-haul — it is faster, which opens the latency-arbitrage markets (finance, gaming) that pay premium for milliseconds.

The mean RTT, then, is a genuine competitive advantage across both comparisons. The problem the rest of the lesson addresses is that a good mean is not a good connection if the variance around it defeats the transport layer.

3. Why RTT is restless

A constellation's RTT is not a constant; it moves for two distinct reasons, on two timescales:

The path length changes (slow, deterministic). As satellites move and the router switches snapshots (m7-4, m7-5), the packet's path through the mesh changes — a different sequence of hops, a different total length, a different propagation delay. The RTT steps up or down at each snapshot boundary, on the fifteen-second timescale, predictably. A connection that opened when the path was short sees its RTT grow as the geometry evolves and the router selects a longer path, then shrink again — a slow, sawtooth-like variation driven by orbital mechanics.

Handovers drop packets (fast, disruptive). When the serving satellite changes (m6-5's terminal handover) or an ISL hands off (m7-3's inter-plane reconfiguration), there is a brief instant where in-flight packets can be lost — the make-before-break machinery bounds this to tens of milliseconds, but tens of milliseconds at 100 Mbps is hundreds of kilobytes of packets in flight, and any that are dropped are loss events the transport layer must interpret. These are fast (a handover is a moment) and disruptive (loss triggers transport's most drastic responses).

The combination is a low-mean, high-variance, occasionally-lossy path — precisely the profile that terrestrial transport protocols handle worst, because they were tuned for paths whose RTT is stable and whose loss means congestion.

4. Why TCP misbehaves

TCP's congestion control rests on two assumptions that a constellation violates:

Assumption one: loss means congestion. TCP interprets a dropped packet as a signal that a link is overloaded, and responds by halving its congestion window (cwnd) — cutting its sending rate to relieve the assumed congestion. On a constellation, a handover-induced loss is not congestion — the link is fine, a packet was merely dropped during a topology transition — but TCP cannot tell the difference. It halves its window, craters its throughput, and slowly rebuilds, only to be knocked down again at the next handover. A path with periodic non-congestive loss drives TCP into a permanent low-throughput state, its window never recovering before the next false signal. The mesh's excellent bandwidth goes unused because TCP mistakes its handovers for overload.

Assumption two: the RTT is stable. TCP sizes its window to the bandwidth-delay product (BDP = bandwidth × RTT) — the amount of in-flight data needed to keep the pipe full. When the RTT steps from 30 to 50 ms at a snapshot boundary, the BDP jumps from 375 KB to 625 KB (at 100 Mbps), and TCP's window — tuned for the old RTT — is suddenly too small to fill the pipe, so throughput drops until the window grows to the new BDP. And the retransmission timeout (RTO), computed from the RTT estimate, is mis-set when the RTT shifts: too tight and it fires spuriously (more false loss signals), too loose and it delays real recovery. Every RTT step is a re-tuning event TCP handles poorly, because its estimators assume the RTT they are tracking is stationary.

The result: a network with a superb mean RTT and abundant bandwidth delivers, to unmodified TCP, a fraction of its potential — throttled by false congestion signals and mis-sized windows. The physics is excellent; the transport layer squanders it.

5. What QUIC and constellation-aware transport can do

The transport layer is not helpless, and the industry's responses fall into three tiers:

QUIC's structural advantages. QUIC (the transport under HTTP/3) improves on TCP in ways that happen to help. Its connection migration lets a connection survive a change of network path without tearing down and re-establishing — directly relevant to handovers, which look like path changes. Its more sophisticated loss detection distinguishes loss patterns better than TCP's blunt "any loss is congestion," and being user-space it can be updated far faster than kernel TCP, so constellation-specific tuning ships quickly. QUIC does not solve the problem, but it is a better substrate for solving it.

Performance-enhancing proxies (PEPs). The operator can split the transport connection: a PEP at the gateway terminates the user's TCP connection locally (hiding the satellite path's RTT and loss from the user's stack) and runs a constellation-tuned protocol across the mesh, re-originating a clean connection to the destination. This is the classic satellite-transport fix (long used for GEO's huge RTT), and it works — at the cost of breaking end-to-end encryption assumptions and adding a middlebox, tensions that QUIC's encryption-by-default specifically resists. The PEP-versus-QUIC friction is a live architectural argument.

Constellation-aware congestion control. The deepest fix is a congestion controller that knows it is on a constellation — that treats handover-timed loss as non-congestive (the network can even signal an impending handover, since it is scheduled and known, m7-5), that anticipates RTT steps from the routing schedule rather than discovering them, and that sizes its window to the known upcoming path. The same determinism that made routing tractable (m7-4) can inform the transport layer: a constellation knows its future RTT, and a transport protocol that consumed that knowledge could pre-adjust rather than react. This is the frontier, and it is the constellation's determinism reaching all the way up the stack.

6. The latency distribution, not a number

The lesson's synthesis-facing deliverable is a reframing: a constellation's latency is a distribution, not a value. The RTT has a low mean (~54 ms), a slow sawtooth variation as paths reconfigure (tens of ms of swing over minutes), and occasional loss spikes at handovers. Characterising a constellation link means reporting its whole distribution — median, the jitter envelope, the tail set by worst-case paths and handover gaps — because that is what the transport layer and the application experience. A median-only spec (as m6-4 warned for terminal G/T, and m5-1 for throughput) overstates the connection's quality; the jitter and the tail are where the user's experience of a video call or a game actually lives.

This is the module's version of a theme the whole program has repeated: the honest figure is a distribution, and the mean is the marketing number. The constellation delivers a superb median latency and a restless one, and whether that restlessness is invisible or ruinous depends on the transport layer's ability to absorb it. The spine's Stage 6 therefore produces not a latency but a latency distribution across the snapshot sequence, and the transport model that predicts what TCP or QUIC makes of it.

Exercise 1 — The latency budget

Assemble the end-to-end budget and compare to GEO and fibre.

import math
Re, h, c = 6371.0, 550.0, 299792.458
c_fiber = c/1.468

def slant_km(elev_deg, alt=h):
    e = math.radians(elev_deg)
    return Re*(math.sqrt(((Re+alt)/Re)**2 - math.cos(e)**2) - math.sin(e))

def leo_budget(ground_km, n_hops=4, elev=40, proc_ms=0.5, queue_ms_per_hop=0.3):
    access = slant_km(elev)/c*1000
    mesh_prop = ground_km/c*1000
    mesh_proc = n_hops*proc_ms
    queue = n_hops*queue_ms_per_hop
    ow = 2*access + mesh_prop + mesh_proc + queue
    return dict(access=access, prop=mesh_prop, proc=mesh_proc,
                queue=queue, one_way=ow, rtt=2*ow)

b = leo_budget(5570)
for k, v in b.items():
    print(f"  {k:>8}: {v:6.2f} ms")

geo_rtt = 4*35786/c*1000
print(f"\nGEO RTT: {geo_rtt:.0f} ms  ->  LEO is {geo_rtt/b['rtt']:.1f}x better")
fiber_rtt = 2*5570*1.4/c_fiber*1000
print(f"fibre RTT (London-NY): {fiber_rtt:.0f} ms vs LEO {b['rtt']:.0f} ms")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 6: the latency distribution

Turn routed paths into what transport experiences.

import math, statistics
Re, h, c = 6371.0, 550.0, 299792.458

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M7 SPINE PROJECT — CONSTELLATION ROUTER
# Stage 6: end-to-end latency + transport behaviour.
#   Stages 1-5 routed a packet across the mesh.
#   Stage 6: turn a path into an end-to-end latency, assemble a
#            DISTRIBUTION across the snapshot sequence, and model
#            how TCP/QUIC fare against its steps and loss spikes.
#   Next: m7-7 assembles the full ConstellationRouter.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

def slant_km(elev_deg, alt=h):
    e = math.radians(elev_deg)
    return Re*(math.sqrt(((Re+alt)/Re)**2 - math.cos(e)**2) - math.sin(e))

class LatencyModel:
    def __init__(self, proc_ms=0.5, elev=40):
        self.proc, self.access = proc_ms, slant_km(elev)/c*1000

    def path_latency_ms(self, path_range_km, n_hops, queue_ms=0.0):
        prop = path_range_km/c*1000
        return 2*self.access + prop + n_hops*self.proc + queue_ms

    def distribution(self, snapshots):
        """snapshots: list of (path_range_km, n_hops, queue_ms).
        Returns the RTT distribution the transport layer sees."""
        rtts = [2*self.path_latency_ms(r, n, q) for r, n, q in snapshots]
        return dict(median=statistics.median(rtts),
                    mean=statistics.mean(rtts),
                    jitter=max(rtts)-min(rtts),
                    p95=sorted(rtts)[int(0.95*len(rtts))-1],
                    steps=[round(rtts[i+1]-rtts[i], 1)
                           for i in range(len(rtts)-1)])

class TransportModel:
    def __init__(self, bw_mbps=100):
        self.bw = bw_mbps
    def bdp_kb(self, rtt_ms):
        return self.bw*1e6*rtt_ms/1000/8/1e3
    def tcp_efficiency(self, rtt_sequence, handover_snaps=()):
        """Fraction of pipe filled per snapshot: window lags RTT steps,
        halves on handover loss (false congestion)."""
        win = self.bdp_kb(rtt_sequence[0]); out = []
        for i, rtt in enumerate(rtt_sequence):
            if i in handover_snaps: win *= 0.5      # false congestion
            need = self.bdp_kb(rtt)
            out.append(min(1.0, win/need))
            win = max(win, need)                     # grow toward BDP
        return out

# a transcontinental connection over 6 snapshots, path breathing
snaps = [(5570, 4, 0.5), (5900, 4, 0.8), (6200, 5, 1.0),
         (5800, 4, 2.0), (5570, 4, 0.5), (6400, 5, 1.2)]
lm = LatencyModel()
dist = lm.distribution(snaps)
print("RTT distribution over the snapshot sequence:")
for k, v in dist.items():
    print(f"  {k:>7}: {v}")

tm = TransportModel(100)
rtts = [2*lm.path_latency_ms(r, n, q) for r, n, q in snaps]
eff = tm.tcp_efficiency([round(x) for x in rtts], handover_snaps={2})
print(f"\nTCP pipe-fill per snapshot (handover at snap 2):")
print("  " + "  ".join(f"{e:.0%}" for e in eff))
print(f"  mean efficiency: {sum(eff)/len(eff):.0%} of available bandwidth")

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 01

Section 5 presented three transport responses — QUIC, PEPs, constellation-aware CC — and noted the PEP-versus-QUIC friction: PEPs need to see and split the connection, QUIC encrypts to prevent exactly that. Take the operator's architectural decision: a PEP delivers immediate, large throughput gains for today's TCP traffic but breaks on QUIC's encryption and is a middlebox the industry is moving away from. Decide whether a new constellation should invest in gateway PEPs (fixing today's traffic, fighting tomorrow's encryption) or bet on constellation-aware endpoints (fixing tomorrow's traffic, helpless for unmodified clients today). What does the traffic mix — how much is already QUIC — do to the answer, and how fast is that mix changing?

PROMPT 02

Section 5's deepest fix has the network signal impending handovers to the transport layer, since they are scheduled and known. This is a layering violation — the network layer telling the transport layer about its internal topology events — that the internet's design principles (the end-to-end argument) specifically discourage. Argue both sides: the pragmatic case that a constellation's determinism is too valuable to waste on layering purity, versus the principled case that cross-layer signalling creates brittle, constellation-specific transport that fragments the internet. Where should the knowledge of the schedule live, and what is the minimal cross-layer interface that captures most of the benefit with least of the coupling?

PROMPT 03

Section 6 insisted latency is a distribution, and the spine reports median, jitter, and p95. But different applications care about different moments: bulk transfer cares about mean throughput (hence mean RTT via BDP), video calls care about jitter, gaming cares about the p99 tail, and a stock-trading arbitrageur cares about the minimum achievable latency on the best path. Design the latency spec a constellation operator should publish: which moments of the distribution, for which application classes, and how honest can a single published figure be? Connect this to the program's recurring 'distribution not mean' theme (m5-1, m6-4, m5-7) and decide whether the network layer's honesty problem is the same as or different from the physical layer's.

PROMPT 04

This lesson's transport pathology rests on TCP's "loss means congestion" assumption meeting handover loss. But m6-5 and m7-5 bounded handover loss to tens of milliseconds via make-before-break, and a perfect make-before-break would drop no packets at all. Interrogate the dependency: how much of the transport problem is intrinsic (RTT steps, which happen even with lossless handover) versus an artefact of imperfect handover (the loss, which better handover could eliminate)? Determine whether the right place to fix constellation transport is at the transport layer (smarter congestion control) or at the handover layer (lossless make-before-break), and what each fix costs — connecting the m6-5 second-beamforming-layer hardware to the m7-6 transport behaviour as two ends of one problem.
