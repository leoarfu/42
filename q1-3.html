The Physics of Latency

QUARTER 1
FOUNDATIONS

Latency is the number that defines LEO's commercial case. Every marketing claim about "low latency satellite internet" ultimately rests on physics — the speed of light, the geometry of orbital altitude, and the distance signals must travel. This lesson derives those numbers from first principles, traces how latency propagates through a full end-to-end system, and examines why different applications have different tolerance thresholds. By the end you will be able to calculate propagation delay for any orbit, identify the dominant latency contributor in any architecture, and evaluate whether a given application is genuinely LEO-dependent.

1. Speed of Light and Propagation Delay — First Principles

Everything in satellite latency begins with one number: the speed of light in vacuum, c = 299,792,458 metres per second, usually rounded to 300,000 km/s for engineering calculations. This is not a technology parameter. It cannot be improved by better modulation, more powerful transponders, or smarter protocols. It is a fundamental constant of the universe, and it sets an absolute floor on how quickly information can travel between two points.

The propagation delay between two points separated by distance d is simply:

PROPAGATION DELAY — ONE WAY t_prop = d / c

where d = path length (km), c = 299,792 km/s

Example: d = 35,786 km (GEO altitude)
t_prop = 35,786 / 299,792 = 119.4 ms (one way, nadir only)

That 119.4 ms is the minimum possible delay for a signal travelling from Earth's surface straight up to GEO altitude, in vacuum. It assumes the terminal is directly beneath the satellite — the best-case geometry, which never occurs in practice for all users simultaneously. It also assumes the signal goes only to the satellite. A complete communication path — terminal to satellite, satellite to gateway, gateway to internet, internet back to gateway, gateway to satellite, satellite to terminal — covers much more distance.

The two-hop problem

Commercial satellite internet services are almost universally two-hop paths: the user terminal talks to the satellite, the satellite talks to a ground gateway, and the gateway connects to the terrestrial internet. Traffic in the opposite direction traverses the same path in reverse. The total propagation path for a single user request — from the user's browser to a web server and back — therefore includes four satellite-link traversals: up to satellite, down to gateway, up to satellite again (return path), down to user. For GEO, this means the propagation delay component of the RTT is at minimum 4 × 119 ms = 476 ms — and that is the best possible case with the user directly below the satellite and the gateway at the sub-satellite point.

In practice, GEO terminals are not directly beneath the satellite. A GEO satellite sitting at 35,786 km altitude above the equator, serving a terminal at 51° north latitude (London) at a typical elevation angle of 25–30°, has a slant range — the actual diagonal distance between terminal and satellite — of approximately 40,600 km. The one-way propagation delay becomes 40,600 / 299,792 = 135 ms. For a full two-hop internet exchange, the four-traversal RTT propagation component alone is 540 ms. Add network processing time at gateways and internet backbone delay (typically 20–80 ms for content hosted in Europe), and the real-world measured RTT is 580–680 ms.

Slant range geometry

The slant range from a ground terminal to a satellite at altitude h, at elevation angle ε, is derived from the law of cosines applied to the triangle formed by Earth's centre, the terminal, and the satellite. The exact formula is:

SLANT RANGE FORMULA d_slant = √[ (Rₑ + h)² − Rₑ² · cos²(ε) ] − Rₑ · sin(ε)

where Rₑ = 6,371 km (Earth mean radius)
h = satellite altitude (km)
ε = elevation angle (degrees → radians for computation)

At ε = 90° (zenith): d_slant = h [trivially — straight up]
At ε = 25°, h = 35,786 km: d_slant ≈ 40,590 km
At ε = 25°, h = 550 km: d_slant ≈ 935 km
At ε = 25°, h = 1,200 km: d_slant ≈ 1,930 km

These slant range numbers deserve attention. At 25° elevation angle — a reasonable minimum for a working LEO service — a Starlink satellite at 550 km is 935 km away. A OneWeb satellite at 1,200 km is 1,930 km away. The one-way propagation delays are 3.1 ms and 6.4 ms respectively. These numbers seem almost absurdly small compared to GEO, and that is exactly the point: LEO collapses the propagation delay by a factor of 40–130× compared to GEO.

IMPORTANT: SLANT RANGE VS ALTITUDE The altitude of a satellite is the vertical distance above Earth's surface. The slant range is the actual line-of-sight path length between terminal and satellite, which depends on elevation angle. For latency calculations, use slant range, not altitude. At low elevation angles, slant range can be 2–4× the satellite's altitude for LEO, and 1.1–1.3× for GEO. This distinction matters most for LEO where the factor is larger.

The refractive index correction

Light travels at c in vacuum. In the real atmosphere, the refractive index is slightly greater than 1, meaning signals slow down slightly as they pass through the troposphere and ionosphere. The tropospheric refractive index correction adds approximately 6–8 metres of effective path length — a propagation delay correction of around 20 nanoseconds — which is entirely negligible for latency budgeting. The ionosphere introduces a frequency-dependent group delay that can reach 5–50 nanoseconds at Ka-band frequencies under normal conditions, and up to several microseconds at L-band during severe ionospheric disturbances. For a latency budget measured in milliseconds, these corrections are at least three orders of magnitude below the propagation term and can be ignored at this level of analysis. For precision timing applications (GNSS, two-way time transfer), they are critical — but that is a different domain.

2. The Full Latency Budget: Every Component Counted

Propagation delay is the largest and most fundamental component of satellite link latency, but it is not the only one. A complete end-to-end latency budget for a satellite internet connection includes multiple distinct contributors. Understanding each one matters because they have different magnitudes, different dependencies on system architecture, and different amenability to optimisation. Conflating them leads to unrealistic expectations — both about what LEO can deliver and about what cannot be improved regardless of orbit.

Component 1 — Propagation delay

As derived above: the time for a signal to travel from terminal to satellite and down to gateway, multiplied by the number of satellite-link hops on the path. For a standard two-hop bent-pipe LEO service (no inter-satellite links), this is 2 × slant_range / c per direction, or 4 × slant_range / c for a full round trip. At a mean Starlink elevation angle of roughly 50–60° during a typical pass, mean slant range is approximately 700–800 km, giving a one-way propagation delay of 2.3–2.7 ms. Round-trip propagation for a two-hop path: approximately 9–11 ms.

Component 2 — On-board processing delay

A bent-pipe satellite performs no processing: it receives a signal, frequency-converts it, amplifies it, and retransmits it. The on-board delay is dominated by the electrical path through the transponder — amplifiers, filters, and switching. This is typically 0.5–2 ms depending on transponder design and frequency plan. A regenerative satellite (one that demodulates, processes, and re-modulates the signal on board) introduces additional delay: modem demodulation and re-modulation adds another 1–5 ms. Inter-satellite link nodes that include on-board routing add packet switching delay of 0.1–1 ms per hop through the constellation.

Component 3 — Ground station and gateway processing

At each gateway, the signal is demodulated, the IP packets are extracted, header inspection is performed, and routing decisions are made. A modern high-performance gateway can process packets in under 1 ms. However, gateways also perform scheduling, buffering, and quality-of-service processing. Under load, gateway queuing delay can contribute 1–5 ms. Gateway processing is typically small but not zero — budget 1–3 ms round trip for gateway delay in a well-designed system.

Component 4 — Serialisation delay

Serialisation delay is the time required to put a packet onto a link — equal to the packet size divided by the link data rate. For a 1,500-byte IP packet (maximum Ethernet frame size) on a 100 Mbps satellite link, serialisation delay is 1,500 × 8 / 100,000,000 = 0.12 ms — negligible. On a 1 Mbps link, the same packet takes 12 ms to serialise. In constrained environments — low-throughput mobility terminals, narrowband IoT links — serialisation delay can dominate over propagation. This is why you cannot directly compare latency numbers across systems without knowing the link data rate and packet size.

Component 5 — Queuing and scheduling delay

Satellite links are shared resources. Multiple terminals compete for capacity on both the forward link (gateway to terminal) and the return link (terminal to gateway). The scheduler allocates time slots or frequency segments to users. A packet arriving at the gateway for a specific terminal must wait until that terminal's next allocated slot. Under light load, this wait is negligible. Under heavy load or with burst traffic, queuing delay can reach tens of milliseconds — large enough to noticeably degrade latency-sensitive applications. This is why satellite internet latency measured during peak hours is often significantly worse than measured at off-peak times, even when the propagation component is unchanged.

Component 6 — Terrestrial network delay

Once the signal leaves the satellite gateway and enters the terrestrial internet, it travels through internet exchange points, backbone routers, and content delivery networks to the destination server. This delay depends entirely on the geographic distance between the gateway and the content server, and on the routing policy of the networks traversed. A Starlink user in Australia accessing a content server in the United States via a gateway in Australia incurs: satellite propagation (~10 ms), plus Sydney-to-Los-Angeles internet backbone (~95 ms), plus return. The terrestrial component dominates. Starlink partially addresses this with ISLs that can route traffic across the constellation rather than into the local internet, but only where Starlink has Points of Presence at both ends.

Measuring versus modelling

Latency measurements reported by users, press, and regulators often conflate these components in ways that make comparison difficult. A measurement of "20 ms ping to 8.8.8.8 (Google DNS)" captures propagation plus satellite processing plus gateway processing plus the terrestrial path from the gateway to Google's anycast DNS infrastructure. It does not isolate the satellite's contribution. A "25 ms RTT" for Starlink measured from a terminal in rural Texas to a server in Dallas might reflect excellent gateway proximity, not anything unique about Starlink's satellite design. The same terminal pinging a server in Tokyo might show 180–220 ms, with the satellite contributing only 15 ms of that total. Latency numbers are only meaningful when accompanied by the complete path description.

3. RTT Calculations Across All Orbital Regimes

With the slant range formula and the latency budget framework established, we can now compute realistic round-trip time estimates for each orbital regime. These are engineering baselines — the numbers a systems architect uses when evaluating whether a given application can be served from a given orbit.

Methodology

For each regime we compute: (a) minimum RTT — nadir geometry, no queuing, no terrestrial delay; (b) typical RTT — mean elevation angle geometry, moderate load, gateway within 20 ms terrestrial; (c) worst-case RTT — minimum elevation angle, gateway in different region, moderate queuing. The minimum RTT establishes the physical floor; the typical RTT is what a well-designed production system delivers; the worst-case establishes service level agreement boundaries.

RTT ESTIMATION FORMULA RTT = 2 × (2 × d_slant / c) + t_processing + t_queuing + t_terrestrial

The factor of 2 outside the brackets accounts for the full round trip.
The factor of 2 inside accounts for two satellite-link hops per direction
(terminal→satellite→gateway for user traffic in bent-pipe architecture).

For ISL-routed paths: replace 2×d_slant with sum of all ISL and feeder link hops.

VLEO: 250–450 km

At 350 km altitude, nadir slant range equals altitude: 350 km. One-way propagation is 350/299,792 = 1.17 ms. Four traversals (RTT): 4.7 ms. Add gateway processing and moderate terrestrial delay: typical RTT of 8–15 ms. This is remarkable — approaching the latency of a transcontinental terrestrial fibre path. VLEO has no commercial constellation today, but these numbers explain why it attracts serious attention. The constraint is propulsion: maintaining orbit against atmospheric drag at 350 km requires continuous or very frequent thrusting, severely limiting satellite lifetime and increasing propellant mass fraction.

LEO: 450–2,000 km

The Starlink operational shell at 540–560 km has become the reference point for modern LEO latency. At nadir, slant range = 550 km, one-way propagation = 1.83 ms, four-traversal RTT propagation component = 7.3 ms. At mean elevation angle 55° during a typical pass, slant range ≈ 720 km, one-way propagation = 2.4 ms, four-traversal RTT propagation = 9.6 ms. At minimum elevation angle 25°, slant range ≈ 930 km, one-way propagation = 3.1 ms, four-traversal RTT propagation = 12.5 ms.

Adding gateway processing (~2 ms), scheduling (~3 ms), and a typical terrestrial component to nearby content (~15 ms), the production RTT is in the range 30–45 ms for a typical Starlink user accessing regional content. This is consistent with independently measured Starlink performance data, which shows median RTT of 25–45 ms depending on region and time of day.

OneWeb at 1,200 km presents a different profile. Nadir slant range = 1,200 km, one-way propagation = 4.0 ms, four-traversal RTT propagation = 16 ms. At 25° elevation, slant range ≈ 1,930 km, RTT propagation = 25.7 ms. OneWeb's total RTT is typically 50–70 ms in production — worse than Starlink, but still dramatically better than GEO and well within the threshold for most interactive applications.

MEO: 2,000–35,000 km

SES's O3b mPOWER constellation operates at approximately 8,062 km altitude in an equatorial orbit. At nadir, slant range = 8,062 km, one-way propagation = 26.9 ms, four-traversal RTT propagation = 107.5 ms. With gateway processing and terrestrial delay, O3b's production RTT for enterprise services is typically 130–160 ms. This is significantly worse than LEO, but the O3b business case does not depend on matching LEO latency — it depends on being dramatically better than GEO (130 ms vs 650 ms) while providing large capacity to regions where LEO coverage or gateway infrastructure is unavailable. For maritime and enterprise markets in the tropics and mid-latitudes, O3b's latency is adequate for most applications except real-time gaming and voice in some configurations.

GPS satellites at 20,200 km altitude are the other major MEO population. They are not communications satellites in the conventional sense — they broadcast ranging signals — but the one-way propagation delay of 67 ms is actually a design feature: GNSS receivers use the known propagation delay to compute their position. Any perturbation to this delay — caused by ionospheric effects, multipath, or atmospheric moisture — is a source of positioning error to be corrected, not a service quality metric.

GEO: 35,786 km

The GEO propagation calculation is well-established but worth doing precisely. At nadir (terminal directly below satellite), slant range = 35,786 km, one-way propagation = 119.4 ms, four-traversal propagation RTT = 477.5 ms. In practice, most GEO terminals in populated regions see elevation angles of 20–45°, yielding slant ranges of 38,000–45,000 km and one-way propagation delays of 127–150 ms. The production four-traversal RTT propagation component is 508–600 ms. With gateway and terrestrial contributions, measured GEO RTT is consistently 550–700 ms.

THE 150 MS THRESHOLD The ITU G.114 recommendation specifies that one-way delay above 150 ms is "not recommended" for most conversational voice applications, and perceptible degradation begins at 150 ms one-way (300 ms RTT). GEO fails this standard at the propagation level alone — before any other delay source. All current commercial LEO constellations clear this threshold comfortably. For video conferencing (Zoom, Teams), the acceptable RTT threshold is typically 200–300 ms; both LEO and O3b MEO are well within range. For gaming, application-specific thresholds vary from 50 ms (competitive FPS) to 150 ms (casual); most LEO systems meet the 150 ms threshold consistently.

4. TCP, QUIC, and Why Protocols Amplify Propagation Delay

Raw propagation delay is the floor, but the protocols that carry internet traffic can multiply its practical impact by factors of 10 or more. Understanding TCP's behaviour under high latency is essential context for why GEO internet is so much worse in practice than its 600 ms propagation delay alone would suggest, and why LEO's 30–40 ms RTT enables applications that 100 ms RTT cannot.

TCP connection establishment

Before any data flows, TCP requires a three-way handshake: the client sends a SYN, the server responds with SYN-ACK, and the client sends ACK. This requires one full round trip before a single byte of application data can be sent. For HTTPS connections (the majority of all web traffic), TLS negotiation then adds one to two more round trips before the request can be issued. A complete HTTPS connection setup over GEO therefore requires 2–3 RTTs of setup time before the first byte of content arrives: 2 × 625 ms = 1,250 ms minimum. A user requesting a web page over GEO waits at least 1.25 seconds before the first byte of content can arrive — and that is before the server processes the request. Over LEO at 35 ms RTT, the same setup takes 2 × 35 ms = 70 ms. This 18× difference in connection setup time is a major contributor to the perceived difference in responsiveness.

The congestion window and throughput ceiling

TCP's congestion control algorithm governs how much data a sender can have "in flight" — transmitted but not yet acknowledged — at any time. The congestion window starts small (one or a few maximum segment sizes, or MSS) and grows by one MSS per acknowledged segment in slow start, doubling each round trip until a loss occurs or the slow-start threshold is reached. The maximum achievable throughput is bounded by:

TCP THROUGHPUT CEILING (SIMPLIFIED) Throughput ≤ CWND / RTT

where CWND = congestion window size (bytes), RTT = round-trip time (seconds)

Example: CWND = 65,535 bytes (default max before window scaling)
At RTT = 600 ms (GEO): Throughput ≤ 65,535 / 0.6 ≈ 109 kbps
At RTT = 35 ms (LEO): Throughput ≤ 65,535 / 0.035 ≈ 1.87 Mbps
With window scaling (CWND = 4 MB):
At RTT = 600 ms (GEO): ≤ 53 Mbps
At RTT = 35 ms (LEO): ≤ 914 Mbps

The numbers above show the fundamental problem: without TCP window scaling, a GEO link with 600 ms RTT and a 65 kB window is throttled to around 109 kbps regardless of how much physical capacity the satellite transponder provides. Modern TCP implementations use window scaling (RFC 1323) to extend the window to megabytes, substantially improving throughput. But the fundamental relationship holds: for the same congestion window, throughput scales inversely with RTT. A LEO link with 17× lower RTT can deliver 17× higher TCP throughput with identical congestion window size.

There is a more subtle problem: slow start. A new TCP connection begins with a congestion window of 1 MSS (1,460 bytes) and doubles each RTT until the slow-start threshold. Over GEO (600 ms RTT), reaching a 1 MB window requires approximately 10 doubling steps, taking 6 seconds. Over LEO (35 ms RTT), the same window is reached in 0.35 seconds. Small objects — web page assets, API responses, DNS lookups — are often transferred in a few KB and may never leave slow start. For these small transfers, the throughput is not limited by the physical link capacity or even the congestion window ceiling; it is limited by how many RTTs the slow-start algorithm requires to ramp up.

Performance Enhancing Proxies and their death

The satellite industry's response to TCP's poor performance over high-latency links was the Performance Enhancing Proxy (PEP). A PEP sits at each gateway and intercepts TCP connections in both directions. It spoofs acknowledgements locally — when a packet arrives at the gateway from the internet, the PEP immediately sends a fake ACK back to the server, telling it the packet was received at the terminal. This allows the server's congestion window to grow quickly without waiting for the terminal to actually receive and acknowledge the data. The PEP also manages a separate local TCP connection to the terminal, potentially applying satellite-specific congestion control algorithms. PEPs worked reasonably well for HTTP/1.1 traffic.

The transition to HTTPS (HTTP over TLS) destroyed transparent PEPs. TLS encrypts the TCP payload end-to-end, including the TCP header content the PEP needed to manipulate. A transparent PEP cannot intercept an encrypted connection without breaking it. By 2018, roughly 85% of web traffic was HTTPS. By 2022, it was over 95%. The satellite industry's primary latency mitigation tool for TCP had been rendered obsolete by a security improvement that had nothing to do with satellites.

QUIC and the modern alternative

QUIC (RFC 9000, 2021) is a transport protocol designed by Google and standardised by the IETF that runs over UDP rather than TCP. It incorporates TLS 1.3 natively, provides connection establishment in a single RTT (or zero RTT for resumed connections), and implements its own congestion control independent of the operating system. For satellite links, QUIC's key advantages are: fewer round trips to establish an encrypted connection (1 RTT vs 2–3 for TCP+TLS), multiplexed streams without head-of-line blocking (so a lost packet on one stream does not stall all streams as in HTTP/2 over TCP), and a pluggable congestion control framework that allows satellite-aware algorithms to be deployed without changing endpoints.

HTTP/3 — the current version of the HTTP protocol — runs over QUIC. As of 2024, HTTP/3 accounts for approximately 30% of HTTP traffic and is enabled by default in Chrome, Firefox, Safari, and major content delivery networks. For LEO operators, QUIC is a net positive: its 1-RTT connection setup adds only 35 ms (one Starlink RTT) rather than the 120+ ms added by TCP+TLS setup. For GEO operators, QUIC helps somewhat — 1 RTT × 600 ms = 600 ms vs 2–3 RTTs × 600 ms = 1,200–1,800 ms — but the propagation delay still dominates.

DEEPER CONTEXT — TCP BBR AND SATELLITE CONGESTION CONTROL

Google's BBR (Bottleneck Bandwidth and Round-trip propagation time) congestion control algorithm, available in Linux kernels since 2016, takes a fundamentally different approach to TCP congestion control. Instead of inferring congestion from packet loss (the traditional Reno/CUBIC approach), BBR models the bottleneck bandwidth and RTT directly, maintaining a congestion window sized to the bandwidth-delay product. This makes BBR much more effective on high-bandwidth, high-latency links — including satellite links — because it does not interpret delay variation as congestion. Starlink and other LEO operators have reported significant throughput improvements for users running BBR-compatible configurations. BBR is now being deployed at major CDNs. For a satellite systems architect, knowing that BBR exists and behaves well on satellite links is relevant when specifying congestion control requirements for gateway and terminal software stacks.

A related metric is the Bandwidth-Delay Product (BDP): the amount of data that must be in flight to fully utilise a link at a given throughput and RTT. BDP = throughput × RTT. For a 100 Mbps link at 35 ms RTT, BDP = 100 × 10⁶ × 0.035 = 437,500 bytes ≈ 427 KB. For a 100 Mbps link at 600 ms RTT, BDP = 7.5 MB. The TCP window must be at least as large as the BDP to fully utilise the link. GEO requires very large congestion windows to utilise high-throughput capacity; LEO requires modest windows that are well within normal TCP behaviour without window scaling.

5. Application Latency Thresholds: What Actually Matters and Why

Not all applications are latency-sensitive. A significant fraction of internet traffic — file downloads, software updates, video streaming buffers, email — is effectively latency-insensitive beyond a broad threshold. The commercial case for LEO is built on the applications that are genuinely latency-sensitive and the markets where those applications matter most. Understanding where the thresholds come from — and why — equips you to evaluate constellation design decisions and market strategies with precision.

Voice over IP

Human auditory perception of conversational delay has been extensively studied. The ITU-T G.114 standard, which has formed the basis of telecommunications delay specifications since the analogue era, establishes 150 ms one-way delay as the recommended maximum for most applications. Above 150 ms one-way (300 ms RTT), conversational rhythm begins to degrade: speakers start to "talk over" each other because the feedback loop between speech and perceived response is disrupted. Above 300 ms one-way (600 ms RTT) — exactly GEO's propagation delay alone — conversations require explicit "over-and-out" protocol to manage turn-taking, much like radio communications. This is not a matter of user preference or cultural expectation; it reflects the neurological timescales of speech planning and auditory feedback.

All current commercial LEO constellations deliver production RTTs of 25–70 ms, well within the G.114 recommendation. VoIP over LEO sounds indistinguishable from terrestrial internet VoIP to most users. VoIP over GEO was technically possible with echo cancellation and careful jitter buffering, but the conversational experience was noticeably degraded. This is why satellite phone providers operating via GEO geostationary have always occupied a niche market for users with no terrestrial alternative, never a mainstream communications medium.

Video conferencing

Video conferencing applications (Zoom, Teams, Google Meet) are more tolerant than pure VoIP because the visual channel provides feedback cues that partially compensate for audio delay. Zoom's published latency target is under 150 ms one-way; its Quality of Experience degrades measurably above 200–250 ms one-way, and most users find conferences over 400 ms one-way (800 ms RTT) uncomfortable for sustained meetings. GEO's 600–650 ms RTT is borderline: some users tolerate it, many do not. LEO's 30–50 ms RTT is comfortably within the acceptable range for all conferencing applications.

The aviation market presents a specific challenge: at 35,000 feet, passengers' tolerance for poor video conferencing quality is lower than at home, because the alternative in-flight experience is highly valued. A passenger paying $30 for in-flight connectivity and experiencing a degraded Teams meeting is more likely to complain than a home user on a slow rural GEO connection. This asymmetry of user expectation makes latency performance a more commercially sensitive metric in aviation than in residential broadband.

Cloud gaming

Cloud gaming services (Microsoft xCloud, NVIDIA GeForce Now, Amazon Luna) stream video of a remotely-rendered game to a client device, sending controller inputs back to the server. The round-trip latency from input to rendered output must be below approximately 80 ms to feel responsive for most game genres. Competitive first-person shooters require 20–40 ms for professional-level play. The latency budget is consumed by: input capture (~1 ms), network RTT, server rendering time (~5–10 ms), video encoding (~5–10 ms), network buffer/jitter management (~10–20 ms), and display latency (~8–16 ms at 60 Hz). With a total budget of 80 ms and non-network contributors consuming 30–50 ms, the available network RTT budget is roughly 30–50 ms.

LEO constellations with RTTs of 30–45 ms are within this window — barely for competitive gaming, comfortably for casual gaming. GEO at 600 ms is entirely excluded. This market is significant: global cloud gaming revenue exceeded $3 billion in 2023 and is growing rapidly. For LEO operators targeting aviation and maritime markets, cloud gaming is a premium service differentiator that requires LEO latency and simply does not work on GEO. Several airlines have cited gaming as a driver of their LEO connectivity procurement decisions.

Financial trading and market data

Electronic trading latency requirements are the most extreme of any commercial application. High-frequency trading firms in the 2010s spent tens of millions of dollars on microwave links between Chicago and New York because microwave propagation is 30–40% faster than light through fibre-optic cable (fibre's refractive index of ~1.5 means light travels at c/1.5 = ~200,000 km/s, versus ~300,000 km/s through air). The latency difference between a microwave path and a fibre path from Chicago to New York is approximately 4 milliseconds — and that 4 ms was commercially valuable enough to justify the infrastructure investment.

For this application, even LEO latency is multiple orders of magnitude too slow. The relevant question is not whether satellite can support HFT — it cannot — but whether satellite can support institutional trading where latency requirements are in the range of 10–50 ms rather than microseconds. For institutions operating in remote regions or requiring resilience backup for terrestrial connectivity, LEO can serve as a latency-adequate secondary path for non-HFT activity. Some Starlink Enterprise customers use it exactly this way.

Web browsing and interactive applications

Human perception of web page load time has been extensively studied by the web performance community. The key thresholds (based on Nielsen's and Google's research) are: under 100 ms feels instantaneous; 100–300 ms is perceptible but not disruptive; 300–1,000 ms is clearly delayed but users remain engaged; above 1 second, users lose focus and abandonment increases. For web applications that require multiple sequential request-response exchanges (each RTT adds latency), GEO's 600 ms RTT means a three-request cascade takes 1,800 ms minimum — well into the abandonment zone. LEO's 35 ms RTT means the same cascade takes 105 ms — comfortably imperceptible.

The practical impact on web experience is therefore not just "GEO feels slow" but "GEO makes modern web applications functionally broken." Single-page applications, JavaScript-heavy sites, and anything requiring authentication tokens or redirects involve multiple RTTs. HTTP/2 and HTTP/3 partially address this through multiplexing and connection reuse, but the fundamental constraint is unchanged: each sequential dependency in a page load adds one RTT of minimum delay.

6. Fibre Versus Satellite: When the Photon Wins Underground

A counterintuitive but important result of latency physics is that for sufficiently long terrestrial distances, LEO satellite can actually beat fibre-optic cable on latency. This is not marketing; it is geometry and physics, and it has concrete commercial implications.

The refractive index penalty

Light travels at approximately 299,792 km/s in vacuum. In a glass optical fibre, the refractive index is approximately 1.467 for standard single-mode fibre (ITU-T G.652), meaning light travels at c/1.467 ≈ 204,500 km/s. The effective propagation velocity is about 68% of the vacuum speed of light. This refractive index penalty means that for a given geographic distance, the propagation time through fibre is approximately 1.47× longer than through vacuum.

Additionally, fibre cables do not follow straight-line great-circle paths. They follow coastlines, continental margins, and submarine cable routes that are determined by geography, geopolitics, marine biology (cables are routed away from fishing areas), and the location of landing stations. The actual cable path length between two cities is typically 20–50% longer than the great-circle distance. The combination of the refractive index penalty and the routing inefficiency means the effective propagation latency of undersea fibre is roughly 1.47 × 1.25 ≈ 1.8× what it would be if light could travel in a straight vacuum line between the same endpoints.

FIBRE vs LEO SATELLITE LATENCY CROSSOVER Fibre one-way latency ≈ d_great_circle × routing_factor / (c / n_fibre)
= d_gc × 1.25 / (204,500 km/s)

LEO satellite one-way latency ≈ 2 × d_slant_mean / c
≈ 2 × 750 km / 299,792 km/s ≈ 5 ms (Starlink, mean path)

Crossover distance (fibre latency = LEO latency):
d_gc × 1.25 / 204,500 = 0.005 s
d_gc ≈ 0.005 × 204,500 / 1.25 ≈ 818 km

For distances greater than ~800 km, LEO satellite has lower propagation
latency than conventional fibre cable, before accounting for network hops.

This result is surprising to most people when they first encounter it. A Starlink terminal in London communicating with a server in New York via satellite has a propagation path of approximately 2 × 750 km (two satellite hops) + terrestrial at both ends ≈ 20 ms of satellite propagation. The London-New York transatlantic fibre distance is approximately 5,570 km great-circle, actual cable route approximately 6,800 km, fibre propagation time 6,800 / 204,500 = 33 ms. The satellite path is faster in propagation terms — before accounting for gateway processing, ISP network hops, and content server location.

Where fibre wins: the network effect

Despite the propagation advantage on long routes, fibre wins on end-to-end latency for most users for a simple reason: the internet's content and infrastructure is concentrated in terrestrial data centres connected by fibre. A user reaching a server in the same city via fibre experiences sub-millisecond propagation delay. The same user over satellite still incurs two satellite hops (minimum ~7 ms) plus gateway terrestrial connectivity to the content. For local and regional content, fibre always wins. Satellite's latency advantage over fibre only materialises on long international routes — typically over 1,000 km great-circle — where the satellite path's two short hops to a well-placed gateway are genuinely shorter in propagation time than the fibre route.

This is the basis for one of the genuinely compelling niche applications of LEO satellite with ISLs: replacing or supplementing undersea cable on certain international routes. Starlink's ISL network can potentially route traffic from one continent to another via the constellation at near-vacuum-speed-of-light, potentially beating the latency of undersea cables on routes where the cable is long and the Starlink gateway placement is favourable. This is not yet a commercial product in the traditional sense, but it is a technically real capability that has attracted attention from financial institutions and content delivery networks evaluating premium low-latency international routing.

THE HOLLOW-CORE FIBRE DEVELOPMENT Conventional single-mode fibre has a refractive index of about 1.47. Hollow-core photonic-bandgap fibre, in which light propagates through an air-filled core rather than a glass core, has an effective refractive index approaching 1.0 — nearly the vacuum speed of light. BT and others have demonstrated hollow-core fibre at scale; the technology is advancing toward commercial deployment. If hollow-core fibre achieves widespread submarine cable deployment, the LEO satellite latency advantage on long international routes will narrow significantly. For a satellite architect, this is a technology risk factor in any business case that depends on satellite's latency advantage over fibre on international routes. The crossover distance would shrink from ~800 km to ~1,000+ km due to routing factor alone, but the routing inefficiency penalty would remain. Hollow-core fibre does not eliminate the satellite advantage; it reduces it on the longest routes.

7. ISLs and the Speed-of-Light Routing Advantage

Inter-satellite links (ISLs) change the latency calculation in a fundamental way. Without ISLs, a satellite constellation is a collection of independent bent-pipe relays: each satellite receives traffic from a terminal and routes it to the nearest ground gateway. With ISLs, the constellation becomes a distributed network in the sky, capable of routing traffic from terminal to terminal via multiple satellite hops without touching the ground. The latency implications are profound for certain routes.

The routing geometry with ISLs

Consider a Starlink terminal in Auckland, New Zealand, communicating with a server in London. Without ISLs: Auckland terminal → Starlink satellite → New Zealand gateway → New Zealand internet → transpacific cable → US internet → transatlantic cable → UK internet → London server. Total fibre path: approximately 30,000 km of cable, giving roughly 150 ms of fibre propagation, plus terrestrial routing delays. With ISLs and appropriate gateway placement: Auckland terminal → Starlink satellite → ISL hops across the Pacific → ISL hops across the North Atlantic → Starlink satellite → UK gateway → London server. The satellite-segment path at near-vacuum speed of light covers approximately 30,000 km equivalent great-circle distance (two short feeder links plus ISL path) at c = 30,000 / 299,792 = 100 ms, compared to the fibre path's ~150 ms. The ISL-routed path is faster in propagation terms.

This is the commercial rationale behind Starlink's ISL network, which was first activated in 2021 with the v1.5 satellite generation. SpaceX has described the ISL network as enabling "global connectivity without touching the ground" — a capability that was impossible for any previous commercial satellite system and provides a genuine physical-layer latency advantage on intercontinental routes.

ISL latency contributions

Each ISL hop introduces additional latency: the propagation delay across the ISL link plus on-board processing at the intermediate satellite. ISL links between adjacent Starlink satellites in the same orbital plane are approximately 1,500–3,500 km long depending on orbital altitude and inter-satellite spacing. At 3,000 km, one-way ISL propagation is 10 ms. Processing at the satellite router adds approximately 0.5–2 ms. A path requiring 10 ISL hops across a constellation therefore adds approximately 100–120 ms of ISL-specific latency, replacing what would otherwise be much longer undersea cable propagation for the same geographic route.

The net effect depends strongly on the route. For Auckland-to-London, ISLs help significantly. For New York to Washington DC (350 km by road), ISLs offer no advantage over a direct terrestrial fibre path, and the satellite feeder links add latency relative to fibre. This is why LEO with ISLs has a nuanced competitive position: it is not universally faster than fibre; it is faster on long international routes where the cable latency is high and the constellation routing is efficient.

OneWeb's architectural choice

OneWeb's constellation, as originally deployed, has no ISLs. All traffic routes through ground gateways. This was a deliberate architectural and economic decision: ISLs add hardware complexity (laser or RF pointing mechanisms, thermal management, additional mass and power), increase satellite cost, and require sophisticated on-board routing software. For OneWeb's target markets — enterprise, government, and aviation — the absence of ISLs is a meaningful capability limitation only on very long international routes. For a maritime terminal in the North Sea communicating with a gateway in the UK, no ISLs are needed. OneWeb's investors decided this tradeoff was acceptable given the cost and schedule pressures of their post-bankruptcy constellation rebuild. Future OneWeb generations (under Eutelsat ownership) have ISLs in their roadmap.

ISL ROUTING AS COMPETITIVE MOAT The ISL capability creates a competitive asymmetry that is difficult to replicate quickly. Building a constellation with ISLs requires designing the on-board routing hardware and software from the start, deploying enough satellites for the ISL mesh to be useful, and operating a space-based routing network at scale. Starlink has done this; OneWeb has not. For enterprise customers on international routes where latency matters — financial services, global media, aviation with intercontinental flights — Starlink's ISL capability is a genuine differentiator that OneWeb cannot offer without a new satellite generation.

8. Latency Budgeting in Practice: The Architect's Approach

Latency specifications in system design are not single numbers — they are distributions. A service level agreement might specify that 95% of user sessions must achieve RTT below 100 ms, and 99% below 200 ms. Building a system to meet these specifications requires understanding not just the mean latency but the full distribution, including tail latencies driven by scheduling, congestion, handover events, and gateway load.

Building the latency budget

The structured approach to latency budgeting starts with the allocation: given a total RTT budget from the SLA (e.g., 100 ms 95th-percentile), how much can each component consume? A worked example for a LEO bent-pipe service targeting 100 ms P95 RTT:

LATENCY BUDGET ALLOCATION — WORKED EXAMPLE Total P95 RTT budget: 100 ms
Propagation (2-hop, P95 geometry): 18 ms [high elevation angle tail]
On-board processing (2× satellite): 4 ms
Gateway processing (2× gateway): 4 ms
Serialisation (1,500B @ 50 Mbps): 1 ms
Queuing / scheduling P95: 15 ms [budget for load peak]
Terrestrial (gateway to CDN PoP): 30 ms [budget for CDN placement]
──────────────────────────────────────────
Total allocated: 72 ms
Margin: 28 ms [for unmodelled effects]

Key constraint: terrestrial (30 ms) and queuing (15 ms) are the
dominant non-propagation contributors — both are engineering choices.

This budget reveals that to meet 100 ms P95, gateway placement must ensure the terrestrial path to major CDN Points of Presence is under 30 ms — which constrains where gateways can be located. It also reveals that scheduling delay is the second-largest component under peak load, making the scheduler design and traffic management policy critical to SLA compliance.

Handover events and latency spikes

LEO satellites are in constant motion relative to ground terminals. A terminal must hand over from one satellite to the next every 4–14 minutes depending on altitude and elevation mask. During handover, there is a brief period of link disruption while the new satellite link is established. Modern LEO systems use predictive handover (the new satellite link is pre-established before the old one is dropped) to minimise disruption, but even predictive handover introduces a latency spike of 20–200 ms during the handover event due to beam acquisition, timing synchronisation, and scheduling re-establishment.

These handover spikes contribute to the P99 and maximum latency percentiles. A service achieving 35 ms median RTT might experience 150–300 ms RTT during handover events, which occur approximately once every 8 minutes. For applications that are sensitive to latency spikes rather than average latency — real-time gaming, audio conferencing — these spikes are perceptible. They manifest as brief audio glitches, frame drops in video, or micro-stutters in gaming. Reducing handover spike duration is an active area of engineering development in all LEO constellation programmes.

The latency floor and how to communicate it honestly

A recurring challenge in satellite service marketing is the gap between the headline latency number (often the median or mean, under good conditions) and the latency distribution that users actually experience. A system that delivers 30 ms median RTT but 300 ms P99 RTT due to scheduling tails and handover events is not, in practice, a 30 ms system for latency-sensitive applications. Real-time applications care about percentile latency, not median latency — a VoIP call that experiences a 300 ms spike every 8 minutes is noticeable. A file download that occasionally takes 300 ms longer is not.

The architect's responsibility is to specify latency requirements in terms of the distribution that the application actually needs — typically P50, P95, and P99 — and to design the system to meet all three. Marketing latency as a single number without specifying the percentile is a standard industry practice that creates expectation mismatches; recognising this allows you to ask the right questions when evaluating vendor claims or writing specifications.

9. The Limits of What LEO Can Promise

The enthusiastic narrative around LEO latency is largely accurate — LEO constellations deliver dramatically better latency than GEO for interactive applications, and this enables services that were previously impossible via satellite. But there are real limits to what LEO can promise, and a competent architect understands them.

LEO is not fibre

For the majority of users in the majority of locations for the majority of use cases, fibre-to-the-premises (FTTP) terrestrial broadband delivers lower latency than LEO satellite. A fibre connection from a home in London to a content server also in London has a round-trip propagation delay of under 1 ms. LEO cannot match this for local content. LEO's addressable market is users and locations where fibre is not economically viable — rural and remote locations, mobility platforms (aircraft, ships, vehicles), and international routes where cable latency is high. LEO competes with GEO and low-quality fixed wireless, not with high-quality urban fibre.

Ground segment location matters enormously

The terrestrial segment of a satellite connection — from the gateway to the content server — can easily dominate total latency even on a LEO system. A Starlink user in rural Alaska connecting to a gateway in Seattle (1,500 km, ~10 ms terrestrial) accessing a server in Los Angeles (~30 ms from Seattle) experiences a total RTT of approximately 10 ms (propagation) + 10 ms (Seattle gateway terrestrial) + 30 ms (LA server) × 2 = ~80 ms. If the same user's traffic is routed to a gateway in Virginia due to load balancing, the terrestrial component alone becomes ~80 ms one way, pushing total RTT above 200 ms. Gateway placement and traffic routing policy are as important to delivered latency as satellite altitude.

Coverage gaps and latency degradation

Constellation coverage is not uniform in time and space. At any given moment, some geographic areas are served by satellites at high elevation angles (low slant range, low latency) while others are served by satellites at low elevation angles (high slant range, higher latency). During periods of lower satellite density — as constellations grow toward their target population — coverage quality varies more. Early Starlink deployments in 2020–2021 showed significant latency variability as the constellation was not yet dense enough to guarantee consistent high-elevation service. A mature, fully-deployed constellation at target population shows much lower latency variance. This temporal dimension of constellation maturity is relevant for service planning: latency commitments that are achievable at full deployment may not be consistently met during the deployment phase.

The last-mile and terminal limits

Finally, the satellite link is only part of the path. In aviation, the signal must traverse the aircraft's onboard network from the roof-mounted terminal to the passenger device — adding 1–5 ms of Wi-Fi and local network latency plus the delays of the onboard traffic management system. In maritime, the signal may traverse a ship's network with dozens of hops. In enterprise deployments, VPN overhead, firewall inspection, and corporate network routing add latency that can dominate over the satellite contribution. The satellite architect cannot control these downstream components, but must account for them in any end-to-end latency claim.

THE SINGLE MOST IMPORTANT LATENCY TRUTH LEO satellite latency is not determined by the satellite — it is determined by the full path from application to application. The satellite contributes 10–20 ms to a total budget that includes terrestrial routing, protocol overhead, and application processing. For routes where terrestrial routing is short (local content, nearby gateways), satellite propagation is the dominant term and LEO's advantage is clear. For routes where terrestrial routing is long (remote gateways, international content), the satellite contribution is a small fraction of total latency and optimising the satellite does little. Know your path, know your budget, know where to invest engineering effort.

import numpy as np

# Constants
c = 299_792  # km/s — speed of light in vacuum
Re = 6_371   # km  — Earth mean radius

def slant_range(h_km, elev_deg):
    """
    Compute slant range from ground terminal to satellite.
    h_km    : satellite altitude above surface (km)
    elev_deg: elevation angle of satellite above horizon (degrees)
    Returns : slant range in km
    """
    eps = np.radians(elev_deg)
    r_sat = Re + h_km
    # Law of cosines on triangle (Earth centre, terminal, satellite)
    d = np.sqrt(r_sat**2 - (Re * np.cos(eps))**2) - Re * np.sin(eps)
    return d

    """One-way propagation delay in milliseconds."""
    return slant_range(h_km, elev_deg) / c * 1000

    Round-trip propagation delay for n_hops satellite traversals.
    Default 4 hops: terminal→sat→gw (up+down) × 2 directions.
    """
    return one_way_prop_delay_ms(h_km, elev_deg) * n_hops

        self.name = name
        self.components = []
        self.n_sim = n_sim

    def add_component(self, label, mean_ms, std_ms, note=""):
        """Add a latency component with Gaussian distribution."""
        self.components.append({
            'label': label,
            'mean': mean_ms,
            'std': std_ms,
            'note': note
        })

    def simulate(self):
        """Monte Carlo simulation of total RTT distribution."""
        total = np.zeros(self.n_sim)
        for comp in self.components:
            samples = np.random.normal(comp['mean'], comp['std'], self.n_sim)
            samples = np.clip(samples, 0, None)  # no negative latency
            total += samples
        return total

    def report(self):
        print(f"\n{'='*65}")
        print(f"LATENCY BUDGET: {self.name}")
        print(f"{'='*65}")
        print(f"{'Component':<32} {'Mean':>8} {'StdDev':>8} {'Note'}")
        print(f"{'-'*65}")
        for c in self.components:
            print(f"{c['label']:<32} {c['mean']:>7.1f}ms {c['std']:>7.1f}ms  {c['note']}")
        total_mean = sum(c['mean'] for c in self.components)
        total_std  = np.sqrt(sum(c['std']**2 for c in self.components))
        print(f"{'-'*65}")
        print(f"{'Total (deterministic sum)':<32} {total_mean:>7.1f}ms")
        print(f"\nMonte Carlo simulation ({self.n_sim:,} runs):")
        samples = self.simulate()
        for pct in [50, 75, 90, 95, 99]:
            val = np.percentile(samples, pct)
            bar = '█' * int(val / 5)
            print(f"  P{pct:>2}: {val:6.1f} ms  {bar}")
        print(f"  Max:  {samples.max():6.1f} ms")
        print()

# ─── SCENARIO 1: LEO 550 km bent-pipe, gateway 500 km away ───

The Monte Carlo approach captures the reality that latency is a distribution, not a single number. Notice how the standard deviation of the queuing component dominates the spread of the P95–P99 tail for LEO, while the propagation component dominates the spread for GEO. This has direct implications for SLA design.
