Synthesis:
The Routing & Latency Model

MODULE 7
NETWORKING, ISL & ROUTING
CAPSTONE

Six lessons built the network layer from the architecture decision outward: bent-pipe versus mesh (m7-1), the optical links that make a mesh possible (m7-2), the +grid topology and its churn (m7-3), routing on a graph that never holds still (m7-4), the SDN control plane that delivers the routes (m7-5), and the end-to-end latency the transport layer must survive (m7-6). This lesson assembles them into one artefact — the ConstellationRouter — and runs a message through it end to end: from a terminal, up through the handover choreography, across the scheduled mesh, down to a destination, with its full latency distribution attached. Then it does what the whole module was for: it compares bent-pipe, mesh, and terrestrial fibre for representative city-pairs, and reads off which architecture wins where, and why.

The capstone finding is a single table that unifies the module's two central arguments — coverage and latency — into one grid. For every long-haul route, the mesh delivers a latency fibre cannot match (54 ms London–NY against fibre's 76, 104 ms Sydney–LA against 165) and is the only architecture that can serve the route at all, because a bent-pipe constellation has "no path" between endpoints thousands of kilometres apart. For every short route, fibre wins on latency (16 ms NYC–DC against 5) and both space architectures merely serve. And against GEO, the mesh is nine times better everywhere. The coverage argument and the latency argument, which m7-1 claimed were the same argument, turn out in the assembled model to be literally the same column: the routes where the mesh wins latency are exactly the routes where the bent-pipe has no path. This lesson proves that claim by building the thing that computes it.

1. The assembled router

The ConstellationRouter is the composition of all six stages:

terminal → [m6-5 access] → ingress satellite → [m7-4 routing on the m7-3 topology, m7-2 links, m7-1 architecture] → egress satellite → [m6-5 access] → destination, with [m7-5 control] delivering the routes and [m7-6 transport] experiencing the latency

Each stage is a component already built and verified: the topology graph (Stage 1/3) built on the Module 2 propagator, the ISL budgets (Stage 2) that weight its edges, the router core (Stage 4) that finds paths, the control plane (Stage 5) that distributes them, and the latency model (Stage 6) that turns a path into what the connection feels. The synthesis is the wiring — a single object that takes a source terminal, a destination, and a time, and returns the route, its latency distribution, and the architecture comparison. Nothing new is derived; everything is composed. That composition is the network layer's answer to the program's question: given a terminal that earns its bits (Module 6), an air interface that carries them (Module 5), and a link budget that sets the rate (Module 4), what does the user's packet actually experience crossing the world?

Every number traces to a lesson, and the whole is exactly the sum of its parts — the value of building the spine incrementally is that the final assembly holds no surprises, because each term was verified where it was introduced. The packet climbs to orbit (m6-5's access leg), crosses the mesh along a scheduled path (m7-5 delivered the tables, m7-4 computed them, m7-3 supplied the topology, m7-2 closed the links), and descends to the destination — and the 54 ms round trip is the network layer's headline, assembled from six lessons' worth of verified components.

But the single number is the median (m7-6's lesson): over a session, the packet's serving satellite hands over every few minutes (m6-5), the ISLs it traverses hand off on the churn schedule (m7-3), and each handover steps the RTT and risks a loss the transport layer must absorb (m7-6). The assembled router therefore reports not 54 ms but a distribution — a low median, a sawtooth jitter as paths reconfigure, a tail set by the worst path and the handover gaps — and a model of what TCP or QUIC makes of it. The message trace is a median; the connection is a distribution.

Three readings, each a module argument confirmed:

The mesh wins long-haul latency. Above the ~1,500–2,000 km crossover (m7-1), the mesh beats fibre on every route, and the advantage grows with distance — 22 ms on London–NY, 61 ms on Sydney–LA. Vacuum is 47% faster than glass, and the mesh's fixed access cost amortises. This is the latency-arbitrage value proposition, quantified.

The bent-pipe has no path. Every long route shows "no path" for the bent-pipe architecture, because the endpoints are thousands of kilometres apart and no single satellite sees both (m7-1's ~2,605 km reach). The bent-pipe serves only the short NYC–DC hop, where a gateway is co-visible. The mesh's ISLs are not a latency luxury; they are the only way to serve these routes at all.

These are the same column. The routes where the mesh wins latency (long-haul) are exactly the routes where the bent-pipe has no path. m7-1 claimed the coverage argument and the latency argument were one argument; the assembled table makes it literal — the "no path" column and the "mesh wins" column coincide perfectly. A mesh's ISL earns its cost twice over on the same routes: it makes them possible and makes them fast.

And underneath all of it, the GEO column is a flat 477 ms — nine times worse than the mesh everywhere, the incumbent architecture that LEO displaced for every latency-sensitive application. The table is the module in one grid: fibre for the short and local, mesh for the long and the oceanic, GEO for neither.

The map is the answer to m7-1's opening question — bent-pipe or mesh — resolved across the whole traffic space. A constellation targeting short-haul regional broadband over well-connected land could be a bent-pipe and lose nothing. A constellation targeting global coverage, oceanic and polar routes, or latency-competitive long-haul must be a mesh, and the mesh's optical ISLs (m7-2), churning topology (m7-3), snapshot routing (m7-4), SDN control (m7-5), and transport-aware endpoints (m7-6) are the machinery that makes the "must" possible. Every lesson in the module was a component of one capability: routing a packet across the world through space faster than the world can route it through glass.

5. What the module built, and what it exports

Module 7 turned a constellation from a set of links into a network. It began with a solved terminal (Module 6) that earns bits from whatever satellite serves it, and it delivered a network layer that carries those bits from any terminal to any destination — across oceans a bent-pipe cannot cross, along long-haul routes faster than fibre, with a latency distribution the transport layer can be taught to absorb. The ConstellationRouter is the artefact: a composition of six stages that routes a message and reports what it experiences.

What the module exports to the rest of the program: to the ground segment and operations of Module 8, the gateway placement constraints (where the mesh must touch down to reach the terrestrial internet) and the traffic the mesh delivers to those gateways; to the service and economics modules, the latency distribution and coverage map that determine what the constellation can sell (the latency-arbitrage markets, the oceanic and polar coverage, the interactive-application capability GEO lacked). The network layer is the bridge between the physics (Modules 1–6) and the business (the modules ahead): it converts a constellation's orbital and optical capabilities into the service a customer buys — a fast, global, always-on path — and it quantifies exactly where that path beats the alternatives and where it does not.

The program's arc through seven modules: an orbit (Module 2), a spacecraft (Module 3), a link (Module 4), an air interface (Module 5), a terminal (Module 6), and now a network (Module 7). What remains is to operate it, cost it, and sell it — the ground segment that keeps it running, the economics that justify it, and the regulation that governs it. Module 8 begins that turn, and it begins where this module ended: at the gateways where the mesh meets the Earth.

Exercise 1 — The capstone comparison

Reproduce the architecture table for any set of routes.

import math
Re, h, c = 6371.0, 550.0, 299792.458
c_fiber = c/1.468

def slant_km(elev, alt=h):
    e = math.radians(elev)
    return Re*(math.sqrt(((Re+alt)/Re)**2 - math.cos(e)**2) - math.sin(e))

def mesh_rtt(ground_km, elev=40, proc=0.5, queue=0.3):
    n_hops = max(2, round(ground_km/1500))
    ow = 2*slant_km(elev)/c*1000 + ground_km/c*1000 + n_hops*(proc+queue)
    return 2*ow

def fibre_rtt(ground_km, detour=1.4):
    return 2*ground_km*detour/c_fiber*1000

def geo_rtt():
    return 4*35786/c*1000

def bent_pipe_serves(ground_km, reach=2605):
    return ground_km <= reach

ROUTES = [("NYC-DC", 330), ("London-NY", 5570), ("Tokyo-Singapore", 5300),
          ("Singapore-London", 10850), ("Sydney-LA", 12070)]

print(f"{'route':<18}{'km':>6}{'bent-pipe':>11}{'mesh':>7}{'fibre':>7}"
      f"{'GEO':>6}{'winner':>9}")
for name, d in ROUTES:
    m, f = mesh_rtt(d), fibre_rtt(d)
    bp = "serves" if bent_pipe_serves(d) else "no path"
    win = "mesh" if m < f else "fibre"
    print(f"{name:<18}{d:>6}{bp:>11}{m:>6.0f}{f:>7.0f}{geo_rtt():>6.0f}{win:>9}")

Exercise 3 — Spine final: the ConstellationRouter

The module's capstone: every stage composed.

import math, heapq, statistics
Re, h, c = 6371.0, 550.0, 299792.458
c_fiber = c/1.468

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M7 SPINE PROJECT — CONSTELLATION ROUTER (final assembly)
#   Stage 1 topology switch (m7-1)   Stage 4 router core (m7-4)
#   Stage 2 ISL budgets (m7-2)       Stage 5 control plane (m7-5)
#   Stage 3 +grid & churn (m7-3)     Stage 6 latency dist. (m7-6)
#   This: compose all six -> route a message, report its latency
#   distribution, compare architectures. The network layer's answer.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class ConstellationRouter:
    def __init__(self, mode="mesh", bent_pipe_reach_km=2605):
        self.mode = mode
        self.reach = bent_pipe_reach_km

    def _slant(self, elev=40):
        e = math.radians(elev)
        return Re*(math.sqrt(((Re+h)/Re)**2 - math.cos(e)**2) - math.sin(e))

    def can_serve(self, ground_km):
        if self.mode == "bent_pipe":
            return ground_km <= self.reach
        return True    # mesh: ISL reaches anywhere with satellites overhead

    def route_latency(self, ground_km, n_hops=None, queue_ms=0.0):
        if not self.can_serve(ground_km):
            return None
        n_hops = n_hops or max(2, round(ground_km/1500))
        ow = (2*self._slant()/c*1000 + ground_km/c*1000
              + n_hops*0.5 + (queue_ms or n_hops*0.3))   # default light queuing
        return 2*ow

    def session_distribution(self, snapshots):
        """snapshots: [(ground_km, n_hops, queue_ms)]. Returns the RTT
        distribution the transport layer sees."""
        rtts = [self.route_latency(d, n, q) for d, n, q in snapshots]
        rtts = [r for r in rtts if r]
        return dict(median=round(statistics.median(rtts), 1),
                    jitter=round(max(rtts)-min(rtts), 1),
                    p95=round(sorted(rtts)[int(0.95*len(rtts))-1], 1))

    def compare(self, ground_km):
        mesh = ConstellationRouter("mesh").route_latency(ground_km)
        bp = ConstellationRouter("bent_pipe").route_latency(ground_km)
        fibre = 2*ground_km*1.4/c_fiber*1000
        geo = 4*35786/c*1000
        return dict(mesh=round(mesh, 0),
                    bent_pipe=round(bp, 0) if bp else "no path",
                    fibre=round(fibre, 0), geo=round(geo, 0),
                    winner="mesh" if mesh < fibre else "fibre")

router = ConstellationRouter("mesh")

print("═══ end-to-end message: London -> NY ═══")
print(f"  RTT: {router.route_latency(5570):.1f} ms\n")

print("═══ architecture comparison ═══")
for name, d in [("NYC-DC", 330), ("London-NY", 5570), ("Sydney-LA", 12070)]:
    c_ = router.compare(d)
    print(f"  {name:<12} mesh {c_['mesh']:>4} | bent-pipe {str(c_['bent_pipe']):>7}"
          f" | fibre {c_['fibre']:>4} | GEO {c_['geo']:>3} -> {c_['winner']}")

print("\n═══ session distribution (London-NY, 8 snapshots) ═══")
session = [(5570,4,0.5),(5720,4,0.8),(5900,5,1.0),(6050,5,1.5),
           (5810,4,0.9),(5570,4,0.5),(5680,4,0.7),(5950,5,1.2)]
print(f"  {router.session_distribution(session)}")

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 01

The capstone table's cleanest result — that the "bent-pipe no path" column and the "mesh wins latency" column coincide — is elegant but rests on the specific routes chosen. Stress-test it: are there routes where the two columns disagree — where the bent-pipe can serve (endpoints within reach of gateways) but the mesh still wins on latency, or where the mesh is the only server but fibre would still be faster if it existed? Construct such a route if one exists, and determine whether the coincidence is a deep truth about the geometry or an artefact of picking transcontinental examples. What would break it?

PROMPT 02

The module built the mesh's case on latency and coverage, and the capstone confirmed both. But the mesh's costs — optical ISLs on every satellite (m7-2), the churning topology (m7-3), the routing and control complexity (m7-4, m7-5), the transport pathology (m7-6) — are all real and all absent from the capstone table, which shows only latency. Reconstruct the honest comparison: if the table included a cost row and a complexity row, would the mesh still win the long-haul routes, or does its latency advantage have to clear a cost bar the table hides? Identify the route or market where the mesh's latency win is real but does not justify its cost, and what that implies about ISLs as a selective rather than universal choice.

PROMPT 03

Section 5 framed the module as the bridge from physics to business, exporting the latency and coverage maps as 'what the constellation sells.' But a latency advantage is only worth money if a customer pays for it, and most internet traffic is regional (below the crossover, where fibre wins). Quantify the addressable market for the mesh's specific advantages: what fraction of global traffic is long-haul enough to benefit from the latency edge, oceanic/polar enough to need the coverage, or latency-sensitive enough (finance, gaming) to pay premium? Decide whether the mesh's demonstrated technical superiority on long-haul routes translates into a business large enough to justify the constellation, or whether the mesh is a technical triumph serving a niche — and what that means for whether future constellations build ISLs.

PROMPT 04

This synthesis composed six verified stages into a router whose headline (54 ms) is 'exactly the sum of its parts.' But the final exercise's question 3 flags that the spine used an analytic hop-count estimate (ground_km/1500) rather than a real routed path through the m2 propagator and m7-3 topology. Apply the load-bearing-assumption discipline one last time for the module: does the 54 ms headline survive replacing the analytic estimate with a real routed path — and if the real path is a Manhattan grid route (m7-4) longer than the great-circle estimate, by how much does the headline degrade? Specify the computation that would replace the estimate, predict whether the mesh still beats fibre after it, and state honestly whether the module's central claim is proven or merely strongly indicated by the model built here.
