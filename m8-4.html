TT&C at Scale

MODULE 8
GROUND SEGMENT & OPERATIONS
CORE

Part I built the network that carries the constellation's traffic. Part II keeps the constellation itself alive, and it begins with the link that flies it: TT&C — telemetry, tracking, and command. This is the control link to every satellite, entirely separate from the user and feeder data paths: telemetry is the stream of health data coming down (voltages, temperatures, attitude, subsystem status — is the satellite alive and well?), command is the stream of instructions going up (do this maneuver, load this software, switch to this mode), and tracking is the determination of where each satellite is (feeding the ephemeris that Module 2 and Module 7 depend on). TT&C is how operators know their satellites are healthy and tell them what to do, and it is the most safety-critical link in the system — a satellite that loses its data links is a commercial problem, but a satellite that loses TT&C is a satellite the operator can no longer save.

The link budget for TT&C is easy — it is a low-rate link (kilobits per second for commands, kilobits to megabits for telemetry, against the feeder's gigabits) that closes comfortably even on a satellite's omnidirectional antenna, which matters because TT&C must work when the satellite is tumbling, in safe mode, or otherwise unable to point. The hard part of TT&C is not the link; it is the scale. A constellation of 1,584 satellites, each traditionally needing several contacts a day, generates thousands of contacts daily — and serving them the old way, with a human-tended pass for each satellite, would demand on the order of 117 dedicated ground antennas, an absurd ground network. This lesson shows why the traditional per-satellite TT&C model breaks at constellation scale, and why the answer is autonomy: satellites that manage themselves and require contact only for exceptions, cutting the ground network from 117 antennas to ~15. The spine gains the TT&C contact-demand model.

1. What TT&C is

TT&C — telemetry, tracking, and command — is the satellite's control link, and it has three functions:

Telemetry is the health-and-status data the satellite sends down: bus voltages and currents, component temperatures, attitude and rotation rates, subsystem states, error logs, propellant remaining. It is how the operator knows the satellite is alive and functioning — the satellite's vital signs, streamed to the ground. Telemetry rates are modest (kilobits to a few megabits per second) because health data is small compared to user traffic.

Command is the instruction stream the operator sends up: execute this maneuver, load this software update, reconfigure this subsystem, enter safe mode, point here. Commands are tiny (kilobits) but critical — a wrong command can disable a satellite, so command links carry authentication and verification, and commands are often confirmed by telemetry before they are trusted.

Tracking is determining the satellite's position and velocity — historically by ranging (measuring the round-trip time of a TT&C signal) and Doppler, now largely by the satellite's own onboard GNSS (Module 2). Tracking feeds the ephemeris that everything depends on: the orbit propagator (Module 2), the mesh topology (m7-3), the antenna pointing (m6-5), the collision avoidance (Module 2's debris environment). A constellation that loses tracking loses the deterministic-ephemeris foundation its routing (m7-4) and its optical ISL point-ahead (m7-2) were built on.

TT&C is separate from the data path by design. The user and feeder links carry customer traffic; TT&C carries the operator's control of the spacecraft, and it must work even when the data links do not — when the satellite is newly separated from the launcher, in safe mode after a fault, or tumbling out of control. It is the link of last resort, and its reliability requirement is absolute.

2. The TT&C link budget: easy by design

Unlike the feeder link (m8-1), the TT&C link is deliberately undemanding. It runs at low rate — commands at kilobits per second, telemetry at kilobits to a few megabits — and often in a lower band (S-band, ~2 GHz, is traditional) where the link is robust. The critical design requirement is that it close on the satellite's omnidirectional (or low-gain) antenna, not the high-gain data antennas, because TT&C must work when the satellite cannot point: at separation from the launcher (before attitude is established), in safe mode (when the satellite has shut down to protect itself), or during a tumble (when pointing is impossible). An omni antenna radiates in all directions at low gain (~0 dBi), so the link budget must close with a near-omni satellite antenna at one end.

It does, easily. A ground station with a large TT&C dish (a 10 m S-band antenna has ~45 dBi of gain) receiving a few watts from a satellite's omni antenna over the ~1,800 km slant range at low elevation closes the telemetry link with large margin at the low rates TT&C needs — the received power is comfortably above threshold, because the low data rate demands little signal-to-noise ratio and the big ground dish supplies plenty of gain. The command uplink is even easier (the ground station transmits high power through its big dish to the satellite's omni). The TT&C link, in short, is a solved problem at the link-budget level — which is exactly why this lesson is about scale, not budget. The physics of talking to one satellite is trivial; the logistics of talking to fifteen hundred is not.

3. The contact-demand problem

Here is where TT&C at scale diverges from TT&C for a single satellite. A traditional satellite is contacted several times a day — to dump telemetry, upload commands, update its clock and ephemeris, and let operators check its health. Call it four contacts per day per satellite. For a single satellite, that is four short passes a day over a ground station, trivially served. For a constellation of 1,584 satellites:

1,584 satellites × 4 contacts/day = 6,336 contacts/day = 264/hour = 4.4/minute

That is a contact starting every fourteen seconds, around the clock. And each contact ties up a ground antenna for the duration of the pass — a LEO satellite is in view of a ground station for only ~8 minutes per pass, and one antenna can serve only one satellite at a time. A single TT&C antenna, accounting for the fraction of time a satellite is actually in view and schedulable, manages perhaps ~54 contacts per day. To serve 6,336 daily contacts:

6,336 contacts/day ÷ 54 per antenna = ~117 TT&C antennas

One hundred and seventeen dedicated tracking antennas, distributed globally so that satellites are always in view of one, is an enormous and absurd ground network — larger than the data-gateway network (m8-3) and built entirely for control, not revenue. The traditional per-satellite TT&C model, which worked fine for fleets of ones and tens, collapses at fleets of thousands. Something has to give, and it is the assumption that every satellite needs frequent human-tended contact.

4. Autonomy: the answer to scale

The escape from the 117-antenna problem is autonomy: satellites that manage themselves and require ground contact only for exceptions, not routine. The insight is that most of a satellite's routine operations — maintaining attitude, managing power, running its payload, keeping its clock, even executing planned maneuvers — do not actually need a human in the loop; they need only a healthy satellite executing its programming. If the satellite handles its own routine housekeeping and contacts the ground only when something is wrong (an anomaly, m8-6) or when a scheduled update is due, the contact demand collapses.

Dropping from four contacts per day to something like one contact every two days per satellite — routine health-and-status plus exceptions — cuts the demand by a factor of eight:

1,584 satellites × 0.5 contacts/day = 792 contacts/day ÷ 54 per antenna = ~15 antennas

Fifteen antennas instead of 117 — a ground network an order of magnitude smaller, achievable and affordable. Autonomy is not a luxury or an optimisation for a mega-constellation; it is a necessity, because the alternative ground network does not scale. This is the operational counterpart to the routing lesson of m7-4: just as reactive routing could not keep up with orbital churn and had to be replaced by proactive scheduling, human-tended per-satellite operations cannot keep up with fleet scale and must be replaced by satellite autonomy. The constellation flies itself, and the ground contacts it only when it cannot fly itself — the exceptions, which m8-6 will handle.

5. Three ways to reach a satellite

TT&C contact can be delivered three ways, each with a role:

Dedicated TT&C ground stations. Separate S-band (or similar) antennas whose only job is TT&C. These are the safe, always-works option — they reach a satellite in any state, including safe mode and tumbling, because they use the robust omni link (§2). They are the ground network the 117/15-antenna arithmetic sizes, and they are indispensable for the states where nothing else works. Every constellation needs some, even if autonomy minimises how many.

In-band via the feeder link. A healthy, pointing satellite can carry its telemetry and receive commands over its high-rate feeder link (m8-1), riding the data path rather than a dedicated TT&C link. This is efficient (no separate antenna time) and high-rate (megabits of telemetry), but it only works when the satellite is healthy enough to point and run its data links — exactly the condition that does not hold in the emergencies TT&C exists for. In-band TT&C handles the routine; dedicated TT&C handles the crises.

Via the ISL mesh. A satellite that is not over any ground station can still be reached by relaying its TT&C through the mesh (Module 7): a command travels to a satellite that is over a ground station, then hops across the ISLs to the target satellite. This decouples TT&C contact from ground-station geography — a satellite anywhere in the constellation can be reached as long as it is meshed — and it is a major reason the mesh's ISLs earn their cost beyond user traffic. The mesh is not just a data network; it is a control network for the fleet.

The three combine: routine TT&C rides in-band or over the mesh (cheap, high-rate, no dedicated antenna), and dedicated S-band ground stations stand by for the states where only the robust omni link works. Autonomy minimises how often any of them is needed.

6. TT&C as the fleet's nervous system

TT&C is the constellation's nervous system: telemetry is the sensation (what is every satellite feeling?), command is the motor control (make them act), and tracking is the proprioception (where is every part of the body?). At fleet scale, this nervous system cannot be run by a human watching each satellite — there are too many, moving too fast, generating too much telemetry. It must be largely autonomic: the satellites regulate themselves, the ground monitors the aggregate, and human attention is reserved for the exceptions the autonomy escalates.

This reframes what "operating a constellation" means. It is not flying 1,584 satellites; it is supervising a fleet that flies itself, watching for the small fraction that need intervention, and maintaining the TT&C network — dedicated, in-band, and mesh-relayed — that can reach any satellite when it does. The contact-demand model this lesson built is the sizing tool for that network, and the autonomy it demands is the theme of the rest of Part II: early-orbit operations that commission satellites at industrial scale (m8-5), and the anomaly-handling and fleet automation that catch the exceptions (m8-6). TT&C is where fleet operations begins, because it is the link through which everything else is done.

8. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

Stage 4 of the spine is the TT&C contact-demand model: given a fleet size, a per-satellite contact rate, and a per-antenna contact capacity, it sizes the dedicated TT&C ground network — and shows how autonomy (reducing the contact rate) collapses it from ~117 to ~15 antennas. It models the three contact paths (dedicated, in-band, mesh-relay) and the link budget that lets TT&C close on an omni antenna. The ground segment now includes not just the data gateways (Part I) but the control network that flies the fleet, sized by the contact demand and minimised by autonomy.

What the contact-demand model assumes is that the satellites can be made autonomous — that routine operations need no human, and only exceptions need contact. That assumption has two parts the rest of Part II must justify. First, the satellites must be brought into service autonomously and at industrial scale — a mega-constellation launches hundreds at a time, and commissioning them one-by-one by hand does not scale any better than contacting them does (m8-5, early-orbit operations). Second, the exceptions the autonomy escalates must be handled — detected, diagnosed, and resolved across a fleet of thousands without a human per satellite (m8-6, anomalies and fleet automation). TT&C is the link; m8-5 and m8-6 are what flows through it at scale.

Exercise 1 — The TT&C link budget

Show it closes on an omni antenna.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x) if x > 0 else -999.0
Re, h = 6371.0, 550.0

def slant_km(elev_deg, alt=h):
    e = math.radians(elev_deg)
    return Re*(math.sqrt(((Re+alt)/Re)**2 - math.cos(e)**2) - math.sin(e))

def fspl_db(range_km, f_ghz):
    return 92.45 + 20*math.log10(range_km) + 20*math.log10(f_ghz)

def dish_gain_db(D_m, f_ghz, eta=0.6):
    return db(eta*(math.pi*D_m/(0.3/f_ghz))**2)

def ttc_telemetry_margin(rate_kbps, sat_eirp_dbw=7, gs_dish_m=10,
                         f_ghz=2.2, elev=10, tsys_k=150):
    # C/N0 = EIRP - FSPL + G/T + 228.6 ; Eb/N0 = C/N0 - 10log10(rate)
    d = slant_km(elev)
    gt = dish_gain_db(gs_dish_m, f_ghz) - db(tsys_k)
    cn0 = sat_eirp_dbw - fspl_db(d, f_ghz) + gt + 228.6
    ebn0 = cn0 - 10*math.log10(rate_kbps*1e3)
    return ebn0 - 10          # required Eb/N0 ~10 dB

print("TT&C telemetry margin (satellite omni, 10 m ground dish):")
for rate in [1, 10, 100, 1000]:
    m = ttc_telemetry_margin(rate)
    print(f"  {rate:>4} kbps: margin {m:>6.1f} dB "
          f"{'(closes)' if m > 0 else '(FAILS)'}")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 4: the TT&C model

The control network, sized by contact demand.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x) if x > 0 else -999.0
Re, h = 6371.0, 550.0

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M8 SPINE PROJECT — GROUND SEGMENT
# Stage 4: TT&C contact-demand model.
#   Part I (m8-1..3) built the DATA ground network.
#   Stage 4: the CONTROL network — the link that flies the
#   fleet, sized by contact demand, minimised by autonomy.
#   Next: m8-5 commissions the fleet, m8-6 handles exceptions.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class TTCModel:
    PASS_MIN = 8
    DUTY = 0.30
    def __init__(self, n_sats):
        self.n = n_sats
        self.per_antenna = (24*60/self.PASS_MIN)*self.DUTY

    def contact_demand(self, contacts_per_sat_day):
        return self.n * contacts_per_sat_day

    def antennas_needed(self, contacts_per_sat_day):
        return math.ceil(self.contact_demand(contacts_per_sat_day)
                         / self.per_antenna)

    def link_closes_omni(self, rate_kbps, sat_eirp_dbm=37,
                         gs_dish_m=10, f_ghz=2.2):
        d = Re*(math.sqrt(((Re+h)/Re)**2 - math.cos(math.radians(10))**2)
                - math.sin(math.radians(10)))
        gs_gain = db(0.6*(math.pi*gs_dish_m/(0.3/f_ghz))**2)
        fspl = 92.45 + 20*math.log10(d) + 20*math.log10(f_ghz)
        gt = gs_gain - db(150)                        # gateway G/T
        cn0 = sat_eirp_dbm - fspl + gt + 228.6        # sat_eirp in dBW here
        ebn0 = cn0 - 10*math.log10(rate_kbps*1e3)
        return (ebn0 - 10) > 0

    def report(self):
        print(f"  fleet: {self.n} satellites")
        print(f"  per-antenna capacity: {self.per_antenna:.0f} contacts/day")
        print(f"  omni telemetry link (10 kbps) closes: "
              f"{self.link_closes_omni(10, sat_eirp_dbm=7)}")
        print(f"\n  {'model':<18}{'contacts/day':>14}{'antennas':>10}")
        for label, cps in [("traditional (4/d)", 4),
                          ("reduced (1/d)", 1),
                          ("autonomous (0.5/d)", 0.5)]:
            print(f"  {label:<18}{self.contact_demand(cps):>14.0f}"
                  f"{self.antennas_needed(cps):>10}")

ttc = TTCModel(1584)
ttc.report()

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 1

Section 4 made autonomy a necessity by the contact-demand arithmetic, and section 6 reframed operations as supervising a fleet that flies itself. But autonomy has a dark side: a satellite that manages itself can also mis-manage itself, and an autonomous fault response that is wrong propagates without a human catching it. Weigh the trade: autonomy is required for scale, but it moves the human further from the loop precisely as the fleet gets larger and the consequences of a systematic error (a bad autonomous behaviour replicated across thousands of identical satellites) grow. Determine where the human must stay in the loop even in an autonomous fleet, and design the boundary between what a satellite may decide alone and what it must escalate — connecting this to m8-6's anomaly handling.

PROMPT 2

Section 5's three contact paths include mesh relay, which decouples TT&C from ground-station geography and lets the mesh serve as a control network. But this creates a dependency: TT&C-via-mesh works only if the mesh works, and the mesh is one of the things TT&C exists to keep healthy. Trace the circular dependency: if a routing fault (m7-4) partitions the mesh, the satellites on the wrong side of the partition may be unreachable by mesh relay exactly when they need commanding to fix the fault. Decide how much a constellation should rely on mesh-relay TT&C versus dedicated ground stations, and specify the minimum dedicated-TT&C capability that must exist independent of the mesh so that a mesh failure is always recoverable.

PROMPT 3

Section 2 noted TT&C traditionally uses S-band, a robust low-frequency band, while the data path uses Ka/Q/V. This means a gateway site (m8-3) optimised for the data feeder is not automatically a TT&C site — different antennas, different band, possibly different locations. Interrogate the ground-network architecture: should TT&C and data gateways share sites (saving land and operations but needing dual-band antennas) or be separate networks (simpler antennas but more sites)? Work the trade for the ~15 TT&C antennas against the ~150 data sites, and decide whether the control network and the data network should be one ground segment or two.

PROMPT 4

This lesson's headline — 117 antennas collapsing to 15 through autonomy — rests on a contact-rate assumption (4/day traditional, 0.5/day autonomous) that is illustrative, and on a per-antenna capacity (~54 contacts/day) built from an 8-minute pass and a 30% duty cycle. The conclusion (autonomy is necessary) is robust across any plausible numbers, but the specific antenna count that sizes the control-network cost is not. Apply the load-bearing-assumption discipline: which input (fleet size, contact rate, pass duration, duty cycle) most strongly drives the antenna count, and what would an operator measure from its actual fleet to replace the assumption? State whether the 15-antenna figure is a design target or an order-of-magnitude illustration, and what the real number depends on.
