Walker Patterns & Coverage Geometry

MODULE 2
CONSTELLATION DESIGN

The previous lessons built the geometric tools for a single satellite. This lesson uses them to answer the first constellation design question: how many satellites, in how many orbital planes, at what inclination, provide continuous coverage of a target region at a given minimum elevation angle? The answer is structured around the Walker constellation — a family of regular multi-plane arrangements described by three integers — and a coverage criterion that guarantees at least one satellite above the minimum elevation at every point in the region at every moment. The spine project propagator reaches its first end-to-end milestone here: seeded with Walker initial conditions, it evaluates coverage fraction over a latitude band.

1. The Coverage Problem Stated Precisely

Continuous coverage of a region requires that at every point P in the region and at every time t, at least one satellite in the constellation is above the minimum elevation angle ε_min as seen from P. This is a geometric condition on the positions of all satellites relative to all surface points, and it must hold for all times — not just at the moment of a favourable pass.

The condition is hardest to satisfy at the points farthest from any orbital plane — typically the equatorial gaps between adjacent planes' ascending nodes, or the high-latitude regions beyond the inclination of a non-polar constellation. Those worst-case points determine the required constellation size: if the geometry works at the hardest point, it works everywhere in the coverage region.

Three parameters capture most of the problem: the orbital altitude h (which sets the coverage radius per satellite), the inclination i (which sets which latitudes the constellation can reach), and the minimum elevation angle ε_min (which shrinks the effective coverage radius). Given these three, the question becomes how many satellites N in how many planes P with what in-plane spacing are needed.

2. Walker Constellation Notation

Walker's 1977 parametrisation of symmetric multi-plane constellations has become the industry standard. A Walker constellation is described by three integers:

T / P / F

T is the total number of satellites, P is the number of orbital planes, and F (0 to P−1) is the phasing parameter that sets the relative mean anomaly offset between adjacent planes. All planes share the same altitude and inclination. The T/P satellites per plane are equally spaced in mean anomaly (360°/S where S = T/P). Adjacent planes are separated by 360°/P in RAAN.

A Walker delta is any Walker constellation at inclination i < 90°; a Walker star has planes arranged near-symmetrically around the pole (i near 90°, with alternating ascending and descending nodes). Walker delta constellations have coverage gaps above the inclination latitude but no polar seam; Walker star constellations cover polar regions but create a high-relative-velocity node crossing near the poles where conjunction risk is elevated.

3. The Single-Satellite Coverage Cap

A satellite at altitude h is visible above ε_min from a spherical cap of the Earth's surface with Earth-central half-angle:

ρ_cap = arccos(R⊕ · cos ε_min / r) − ε_min

The fractional area of Earth's surface within this cap is (1 − cos ρ_cap) / 2. At 550 km with ε_min = 0°, ρ_cap ≈ 59.0° and the cap covers about 21.5% of the surface. With ε_min = 35°, ρ_cap ≈ 33.4° and the cap covers 8.3%. The ratio (21.5/8.3 ≈ 2.6) means a 35° minimum elevation constraint requires roughly 2.6× as many satellites as a 0° constraint for the same coverage fraction — before accounting for geometry and overlap.

The theoretical minimum satellite count is roughly 1/A_cap. At 550 km with ε_min = 35° this gives about 12. Real constellations need 3–6× this theoretical minimum because fixed orbital planes cannot tile a sphere perfectly, J2 precession continuously evolves the phasing, and link-budget margin requirements raise the effective elevation floor.

4. In-Plane Coverage: Spacing Between Satellites

Within a single orbital plane, adjacent satellites provide continuous in-track coverage when their caps overlap. The condition is that the in-plane spacing 360°/S (where S = T/P) is less than twice the cap half-angle:

2ρ_cap ≥ 360° / S

Rearranging, the minimum number of satellites per plane is:

S_min = ⌈180° / ρ_cap⌉

At 550 km with ε_min = 35°, ρ_cap ≈ 33.4°, so S_min = ⌈180/33.4⌉ = 6. At ε_min = 0°, S_min = ⌈180/59⌉ = 4. A single plane with this many satellites provides a continuous covered corridor, but the corridor sweeps westward pass by pass — multiple planes are still required for area coverage.

5. Multi-Plane Coverage: The Inter-Plane Gap

Adjacent planes are separated by ΔΩ = 360°/P in RAAN. For full coverage of a latitude band ±φ, adjacent planes must each contribute a cap that closes the gap at the worst-case point between them. The approximate condition at the equator is:

ρ_cap ≥ (ΔΩ/2) · cos i

This gives the minimum number of planes:

P_min = ⌈180° / (ρ_cap / cos i)⌉ (approximate, equatorial gap)

For a Walker delta at 53° and 550 km with ε_min = 35°: ρ_cap/cos(53°) ≈ 33.4/0.602 ≈ 55.5°, so P_min = ⌈180/55.5⌉ = 4 planes. Combined with S_min = 6, this gives a theoretical minimum of 24 satellites for continuous mid-latitude coverage. Real constellations need more, but this is the analytic floor.

In a Walker star (near-polar) constellation there is a seam near the poles where adjacent planes cross at high relative velocity. The two planes on either side move in opposite directions; positional errors produce conjunctions at closing speeds that can exceed 15 km/s. This is why Walker star constellations carry elevated debris risk near the poles — and why Starlink chose 53° for its primary shell rather than a polar orbit, despite the coverage tradeoff above 53° latitude.

THE POLAR SEAM In a Walker star the ascending and descending planes cross near the poles at relative velocities up to 15 km/s. A collision at that speed converts two functioning satellites into thousands of debris fragments, each of which poses further conjunction risk. This is not a theoretical concern: it is the main reason near-polar constellations must operate at slightly different altitudes per plane or use manoeuvring protocols near the crossing zones.

6. Inclination Selection

Inclination is the single most important architectural choice after altitude, and the tradeoffs are sharp.

Coverage reach: the ground track reaches ±i. A 53° shell leaves everything above 53° uncovered from that shell; an 87.9° shell covers virtually the whole globe from one shell.

Dwell time distribution: for a Walker delta at inclination i, the satellite spends proportionally more time near ±i latitude (the ground track turns around there). Coverage density is higher at latitudes near i than at lower latitudes — the opposite of most commercial demand distributions. A higher inclination wastes capacity over sparsely populated high-latitude ocean.

Launch Δv: reaching a high inclination costs more Δv from most launch sites. From Cape Canaveral (28.5°N), a 53° inclination costs more than 28.5°; a 97.6° retrograde orbit costs more still. This couples inclination to launch site and vehicle selection.

Inter-plane seam and conjunction risk: Walker star constellations have the polar seam; Walker delta at moderate inclination do not.

Gateway siting: a lower-inclination constellation is best served by gateways in the mid-latitude populated band; a polar one requires gateways at diverse latitudes including high-latitude sites with adverse conditions.

The alternative — a single polar shell — would waste capacity over oceans and polar regions while introducing the polar seam risk at much higher total satellite count. The multi-shell architecture allows each shell to be optimised for its market segment.

7. Coverage Fraction and the Sizing Loop

In practice, constellation sizing is an iterative loop:

  • Choose altitude h and ε_min (driven by link budget and latency requirements).
  • Compute ρ_cap.
  • Choose inclination i (driven by coverage requirement and market geography).
  • Choose number of planes P to close the inter-plane gap at the worst-case latitude.
  • Choose satellites per plane S to close the in-track gap.
  • Set T = P × S and check both conditions simultaneously, accounting for Walker phasing F.
  • Simulate the full constellation with the J2 propagator and evaluate coverage fraction over the target region at a representative surface point grid.

Step 7 is not optional. The analytic conditions are necessary but not sufficient: J2 precession continuously changes the relative phasing between planes, the elevation condition must hold for all times, and the worst-case geometry may not occur at the equator for all inclinations. Simulation validates the analytic estimate and reveals coverage holes the formulas miss. Exercise 3 in this lesson runs this loop.

8. The Walker Phasing Parameter F

The phasing parameter F (0 to P−1) sets the mean anomaly offset between adjacent planes. If plane 1 has a satellite at mean anomaly M, the satellite in plane 2 at the next RAAN is at M + F × (360°/T). F = 0 means all planes start with a satellite at the same mean anomaly; F = 1 offsets adjacent planes by one satellite slot.

F matters for two reasons. First, it affects coverage uniformity: a well-chosen F distributes worst-case gaps more evenly, often reducing the required satellite count for a given coverage level. Second, it affects deployment sequencing: a launch vehicle seeding one plane at a time must plan the constellation's evolution from partial to full configuration, and F determines which service regions are covered at each deployment step. For most real constellations F = 0 or F = 1 are used; the difference in coverage is usually small compared to the choice of T and P.

9. Continuous Coverage Threshold

The minimum constellation size for continuous coverage above ε_min is approximately:

N_min ≈ (1 / A_cap) × k_geometry

where k_geometry is a geometry factor typically between 3 and 6. For a 550 km Walker delta at 53° with ε_min = 35°, k_geometry ≈ 4–5, giving N_min ≈ 50–60 satellites for continuous mid-latitude coverage (up to 53°). For full global coverage at the same parameters from a single shell, N_min rises to several hundred because the caps must tile a full sphere rather than a latitude band.

The industry numbers bear this out. Starlink's 1,584-satellite first shell gives dense, continuous global coverage up to 53° with high minimum elevation angles (often 40°+). OneWeb's 648 satellites at 87.9° give global coverage at a lower minimum elevation. Both numbers are far above the theoretical minimum because real constellations must handle J2 precession, phasing evolution during deployment, satellite outages, and link-budget margin.

THE KEY DESIGN NUMBER N_min ≈ ⌈1/A_cap⌉ × k_geometry, where k_geometry = 3–6. At 550 km, ε_min = 35°: theoretical floor ~12, practical minimum ~50–60 for mid-latitude continuous coverage, several hundred for global. The gap between theory and practice is not waste — it is the cost of regularity, reliability, and margin.

Exercise 1 — Coverage Cap and Satellite Count Estimate

Compute ρ_cap, cap area fraction, and theoretical minimum satellite count across altitude and elevation combinations.

import math

RE = 6378.137   # km

def coverage_cap(altitude_km, min_elev_deg):
    """Coverage cap half-angle (deg) and fractional Earth area for one satellite."""
    r   = RE + altitude_km
    eps = math.radians(min_elev_deg)
    arg = max(-1.0, min(1.0, RE * math.cos(eps) / r))
    rho = math.acos(arg) - eps
    area_frac = (1 - math.cos(rho)) / 2
    return math.degrees(rho), area_frac

print(f"{'Alt':>6}{'ε_min':>7}{'ρ_cap':>8}{'Area%':>7}{'N_min':>7}{'S_min':>7}")
print("-" * 46)
for alt in [550, 700, 1200]:
    for eps in [0, 10, 25, 35]:
        rho, af = coverage_cap(alt, eps)
        n_min   = math.ceil(1 / af)
        s_min   = math.ceil(180 / rho)
        print(f"{alt:>6}{eps:>6}°{rho:>7.1f}°{af*100:>6.1f}%{n_min:>7}{s_min:>7}")
    print()

Exercise 2 — Walker Initial Conditions Generator

Generate the RAAN and mean anomaly for every satellite in a Walker T/P/F constellation.

def walker_elements(T, P, F, altitude_km, inclination_deg,
                    raan0_deg=0.0, m0_deg=0.0):
    """
    Walker T/P/F initial conditions.
    Returns list of dicts: plane, sat index, RAAN (deg), M (deg).
    """
    S    = T // P
    dR   = 360.0 / P     # RAAN spacing between planes
    dM   = 360.0 / S     # mean anomaly spacing within a plane
    dF   = F * 360.0 / T # phasing offset per plane

    sats = []
    for p in range(P):
        raan = (raan0_deg + p * dR) % 360.0
        for s in range(S):
            M = (m0_deg + s * dM + p * dF) % 360.0
            sats.append({"plane": p, "sat": s, "RAAN": raan,
                          "M": M, "alt": altitude_km,
                          "inc": inclination_deg})
    return sats

# Small Starlink-like example
sats = walker_elements(36, 6, 1, 550, 53.0)
print(f"Walker 36/6/1  550 km  53°  ({len(sats)} satellites)\n")
print(f"{'Plane':>6}{'Sat':>5}{'RAAN':>9}{'M':>9}")
print("-" * 32)
for s in sats[:12]:
    print(f"{s['plane']:>6}{s['sat']:>5}{s['RAAN']:>8.1f}°{s['M']:>8.1f}°")
print(f"  ... ({len(sats)-12} more)")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 4: Coverage Fraction Over a Latitude Band

Seed the J2 propagator with Walker initial conditions and evaluate the fraction of time at least one satellite is above ε_min at test points across the coverage region.

import math

MU          = 398600.4418
RE          = 6378.137
J2          = 1.08263e-3
EARTH_ROT_S = 7.2921150e-5

def sat_elev(alt, inc_deg, raan0_deg, m0_deg, obs_lat, obs_lon, t):
    """Elevation angle from one satellite to an observer at time t."""
    r  = RE + alt
    n  = math.sqrt(MU / r**3)
    i  = math.radians(inc_deg)
    nr = -(3/2)*n*J2*(RE/r)**2*math.cos(i)
    raan  = math.radians(raan0_deg) + nr*t
    theta = math.radians(m0_deg)   + n*t
    xo = r*math.cos(theta); yo = r*math.sin(theta)
    xi = xo*math.cos(raan) - yo*math.cos(i)*math.sin(raan)
    yi = xo*math.sin(raan) + yo*math.cos(i)*math.cos(raan)
    zi = yo*math.sin(i)
    gst = EARTH_ROT_S*t
    xe = xi*math.cos(gst)+yi*math.sin(gst)
    ye = -xi*math.sin(gst)+yi*math.cos(gst)
    ze = zi
    phi_o = math.radians(obs_lat); lam_o = math.radians(obs_lon)
    phi_s = math.asin(ze/r)
    lam_s = math.atan2(ye, xe)
    cos_r = max(-1.0, min(1.0,
        math.sin(phi_o)*math.sin(phi_s)
        + math.cos(phi_o)*math.cos(phi_s)*math.cos(lam_s-lam_o)))
    rho = math.acos(cos_r); sr = math.sin(rho)
    if sr < 1e-9: return 90.0
    return math.degrees(math.atan((math.cos(rho)-RE/r)/sr))

def walker_list(T, P, F, alt, inc):
    S = T//P; dR = 360/P; dM = 360/S; dF = F*360/T
    return [(p*dR, (s*dM+p*dF)%360)
            for p in range(P) for s in range(S)]

# Walker 24/6/1 at 550 km, 53°
T, P, F, alt, inc = 24, 6, 1, 550, 53.0
min_elev = 35.0
sats = walker_list(T, P, F, alt, inc)   # list of (RAAN, M)

test_pts  = [(-50,0),(-30,0),(0,0),(30,0),(50,0),(60,0)]
dur_s     = 3 * 3600
step_s    = 60
times     = range(0, dur_s+1, step_s)

print(f"Walker {T}/{P}/{F}  {alt}km  {inc}°  ε_min={min_elev}°  {len(sats)} sats\n")
print(f"{'Lat':>6}  {'Coverage %':>11}  {'Note'}")
print("-" * 38)
for lat, lon in test_pts:
    hits = sum(
        1 for t in times
        if max(sat_elev(alt, inc, r, m, lat, lon, t)
               for r, m in sats) >= min_elev
    )
    pct  = hits / len(times) * 100
    note = "CONTINUOUS" if pct >= 99.9 else ("GOOD" if pct >= 90 else "GAP")
    print(f"{lat:>+5}°  {pct:>10.1f}%  {note}")
