Introduction

The satellite constellation is only half the system. Every beam reaching the ground must terminate in a device that a person, an aircraft, a ship, or a cell tower can actually use. User terminals — also called ground terminals, subscriber units, or dishes — are where the link budget meets reality, and their evolution over the past decade has been as dramatic as the spacecraft above them.

For most of SATCOM's history, the user terminal was a large, heavy, mechanically-steered parabolic dish: a GEO VSAT that pointed at a fixed spot in the sky and stayed there. LEO constellations broke this model entirely. A LEO satellite moves at ~7 km/s relative to the ground, crossing the entire visible sky in minutes. No mechanical gimbal is fast enough — or cheap enough — to track that motion at consumer scale.

The answer was the electronically-steered flat-panel antenna, sometimes called an AESA (Active Electronically Scanned Array) or phased array. By adjusting the phase of hundreds or thousands of small antenna elements electrically — not mechanically — these terminals can steer their beam from horizon to horizon in milliseconds, with no moving parts. The cost, mass, and power consumption of this technology have dropped precipitously since 2018, enabling terminals that sit on a rooftop, a car roof, or a boat deck and look unremarkable to a casual observer.

Key Framing
The user terminal defines the commercial viability of a LEO constellation. A $50,000 terminal that requires professional installation serves only enterprise customers. A $499 self-install terminal opens consumer, maritime, and aero markets. Terminal economics are often the binding constraint on a constellation's business case.

This lesson covers the physics and engineering behind modern LEO user terminals, compares the major technology approaches in use today, walks through the link budget from the terminal's perspective, and introduces the mobility and handover challenges unique to LEO tracking.

Phased Array Fundamentals

A phased array is a collection of individual antenna elements whose signals are combined with controlled phase offsets. When the phase differences are chosen correctly, the individual wavefronts add constructively in the desired look direction and cancel destructively elsewhere — forming a beam.

``` ELEMENT PHASE STEERING — SIMPLIFIED VIEW Element 1 ──[φ₀]──┐ Element 2 ──[φ₁]──┤ Element 3 ──[φ₂]──┤─── Σ combiner ──► Beam toward θ Element 4 ──[φ₃]──┤ … │ Element N ──[φₙ]──┘ Phase shift: φₙ = (2π/λ) · d · n · sin(θ) where d = element spacing, θ = steering angle from boresight // Typical spacing: d = λ/2 (avoids grating lobes) // Ku-band λ ≈ 25 mm → element pitch ≈ 12.5 mm
```

The key advantage for LEO tracking is beam agility. Phase shifts are applied by phase shifters — analog or digital circuits — that switch in nanoseconds. The beam can jump from one satellite to another with no inertia, no motor, no settling time. In practice, modern terminals interleave tracking across multiple simultaneous satellites, enabling seamless handover.

Gain is proportional to the number of elements: doubling the array doubles the aperture area and increases gain by ~3 dB (in a simplified model). A flat panel with 1,000 elements at Ku-band can achieve ~34–38 dBi of gain, competitive with a 60 cm GEO dish.

Scan Loss
Phased arrays lose gain as the beam steers away from boresight (normal to the panel surface). At 60° off-boresight, gain drops by roughly 3–6 dB depending on element pattern. This limits the practical operational scan range and means terminal placement (tilt, orientation) matters for link margin, especially at low satellite elevations.

Phase shifters can be implemented in analog RF (traditional approach, lower cost per element), digital (full digital beamforming — highest flexibility, high power/cost), or hybrid architectures that split the array into subarrays, each with analog phase shifting feeding a digital combiner. Most commercial LEO terminals in 2024 use hybrid beamforming as the cost-performance sweet spot.

Starlink Dish Architecture

The pivotal engineering decision in Starlink's terminal is vertical integration. SpaceX designed its own beamformer ASIC (the "BFM" chip), which integrates phase shifters, amplifiers, and ADC/DAC functions for many elements onto a single die. This integration drove down the per-element cost by roughly two orders of magnitude compared to discrete component approaches, enabling the sub-$500 retail price point.

No Moving Parts
Gen 1 Starlink included a small motor to tilt the dish toward the satellite-dense portion of the sky on startup. Gen 2 eliminated it entirely — the electronic scan range is sufficient to acquire satellites from any flat mounting without adjustment. This reduced failure modes and simplified manufacturing.

The terminal's obstruction detection software, activated during first use, creates a sky map identifying trees, eaves, and other blockage sources. It uses this map to predict outage windows and reports them to the user app. This "self-survey" approach is unique to the LEO context — a GEO dish needs obstruction assessment only once at installation.

OneWeb / Eutelsat Terminal

OneWeb (now merged with Eutelsat to form Eutelsat OneWeb) targets a different market segment from Starlink: government, enterprise, and wholesale rather than direct-to-consumer. This distinction shapes the terminal design philosophy significantly.

OneWeb operates in Ku-band for user links. Its user terminals are supplied by third-party manufacturers (including Hughes, Satixfy, and others) under certification rather than by the operator itself. This partner model differs from Starlink's captive terminal approach and creates a more heterogeneous terminal ecosystem.

Feature OneWeb Partner Terminal Starlink Standard
Market segment Enterprise / Gov / B2B2C Consumer / SMB / mobility
Terminal supply model Third-party certified Operator-captive
Typical aperture 32–50 cm ~29 cm (standard)
SLA / QoS support Yes — enterprise-grade Limited (Priority tier)
Regulatory acceptance Broader — ITU-aligned, easier to certify in regulated markets FCC / Ofcom / limited, expanding
Retail price $1,500–$5,000+ $299–$2,500

Satixfy's SX-3000 chipset, used in several OneWeb-compatible terminals, supports digital beamforming across 1,024+ elements with dual-beam capability and on-chip modem integration. It represents a different philosophy from SpaceX's approach: rather than one company owning the full stack, Satixfy provides silicon IP that terminal manufacturers integrate.

Airbus Link
For the HBC+ airborne connectivity program, terminal selection and certification is a critical architectural decision. Airbus DS evaluates terminals not just on RF performance but on DO-160 environmental qualification, DO-254/178 software assurance, EASA STC compatibility, and MSP integration. These constraints significantly narrow the eligible terminal pool compared to the consumer market.
06 / 09

Flat Panel & AESA Design Considerations

The term "flat panel antenna" encompasses several distinct implementations. Understanding the differences is essential for evaluating terminal claims and comparing competitor products.

Phase shifting is performed in the RF domain before digitization. Each element (or subarray) has an analog phase shifter, typically implemented as a switched-delay line or varactor-based circuit. A single data stream is formed. Advantage: low cost, low power per element. Limitation: single simultaneous beam per aperture; limited ability to null interference.

Each element is connected to its own ADC/DAC and DSP chain. Beams are formed digitally in software. Advantage: unlimited simultaneous beams, adaptive nulling, full spatial multiplexing. Limitation: high power consumption and cost — digitizing thousands of elements requires enormous baseband processing. Used in high-end military and SIGINT applications; increasingly viable for commercial terminals as silicon prices fall.

The dominant commercial LEO terminal architecture. The aperture is divided into subarrays (e.g., 64 elements each). Each subarray uses analog phase shifting internally to form a subarray beam. The subarray outputs are then digitally combined. Result: a limited number of simultaneous beams (typically 2–8) with moderate cost and power. Starlink's BFM chip is a highly integrated hybrid beamformer.

An alternative to RFIC-based phased arrays. Liquid crystal (LC) material fills the radiating layer; varying voltage across LC pixels changes the local refractive index, steering the beam. Advantage: extremely low power (LC is a passive material), potentially very low cost in high volume, thin form factor. Limitation: slow steering speed (milliseconds vs. nanoseconds for RFIC), temperature sensitivity, limited scan range. Developed by companies including Kymeta and Isotropic Systems. Better suited for slow tracking GEO/MEO than fast LEO tracking in most current implementations.

The thermal management of flat panel terminals is a non-trivial challenge. A 1,500-element Ku-band array dissipating 75 W in a panel ~30 cm square generates significant surface heat. SpaceX uses snow-melt as a feature: the Gen 2 standard dish melts snow automatically, reducing outages in northern climates. The thermal design that enables this is also the thermal design that keeps the electronics within specification.

Link Budget at the Terminal

In any satellite link, the terminal's contribution to the link budget comes down to two figures: its receive G/T (gain-to-noise temperature ratio, dB/K) on the downlink, and its transmit EIRP (Effective Isotropic Radiated Power, dBW) on the uplink.

``` TERMINAL DOWNLINK (RX) BUDGET — SIMPLIFIED Satellite TX EIRP (dBW) − Free Space Path Loss (dB) // FSPL = 20·log(4πd/λ) − Atmospheric & Rain Attenuation (dB) + Terminal Receive Gain (dBi) // = array gain − scan loss − Terminal Noise Figure contribution ──────────────────────────────────── = Received C/N₀ (dBHz) TERMINAL UPLINK (TX) BUDGET Terminal TX EIRP (dBW) // = Tx Power (dBW) + Antenna Gain (dBi) − FSPL − Atmo loss + Satellite G/T ──────────────────────────────────── = Uplink C/N₀ at satellite (dBHz) Regulatory constraint on UL: EIRP density must stay below ITU/FCC PFD limits // Prevents interference with adjacent GEO satellites
```

For a LEO terminal, the free-space path loss (FSPL) is dramatically lower than GEO. At 550 km altitude (Starlink shell), Ku-band FSPL is approximately 166 dB. A GEO at 35,786 km gives ~205 dB — a 39 dB difference. This means a LEO terminal can close the link with a much smaller aperture and lower TX power than a GEO VSAT.

Scan Loss in the Budget
When the terminal steers to low elevation angles (e.g., 25°), scan loss of 4–6 dB directly degrades the link margin. Constellation architects set minimum service elevation angles partly to keep terminals operating in their high-gain scan regime. Starlink's minimum service elevation is approximately 25°; below this, service degrades. This is why dense constellations (fewer coverage gaps) matter operationally, not just for latency.

The G/T figure for a well-designed LEO terminal at boresight is approximately +12 to +16 dB/K for a standard residential unit. For comparison, a 90 cm GEO dish at Ku-band achieves roughly +18 to +22 dB/K — the larger aperture compensates for GEO's higher FSPL. When GEO's higher path loss is accounted for, the LEO system can still close the link with a smaller terminal — the math works in LEO's favour at equivalent throughput targets.

Mobility & Handover

The defining operational challenge of a LEO user terminal is satellite handover: as one satellite passes below the minimum service elevation, the terminal must switch to a new satellite — without dropping packets, increasing latency visibly, or requiring user action.

Modern flat panel terminals handle handover through make-before-break dual beam operation. While the primary beam tracks the serving satellite to the horizon, a second beam acquires the next satellite and establishes sync. The modem layer performs a soft handover: traffic is shifted to the new beam once the link quality is confirmed, and the old beam is released. The visible effect on the user connection is typically a latency spike of 10–30 ms and no packet loss in well-implemented systems.

Handover Scenario Typical Duration User Impact
Routine satellite transition ~2–5 min between events Invisible with make-before-break
Obstruction passage 1–30 sec Outage if no adjacent satellite visible
Beam edge crossing (in-beam handover) Milliseconds Handled within constellation, transparent to terminal
Gateway handover (ground network) ~50–200 ms Latency spike; depends on ISL availability
Polar / high-latitude gap (low-density coverage) Seconds to minutes Service outage until next visible satellite

For aero and maritime terminals, the mobility challenge compounds. The terminal itself is moving — potentially at 900 km/h for commercial aviation — adding a large Doppler contribution on top of the satellite's own orbital velocity. LEO terminals must compensate for relative Doppler shifts of up to ±100 kHz at Ku-band, which is handled in the waveform's frequency tracking loops rather than mechanically.

Airborne Certification Complexity
Aero LEO terminals must hold an STC (Supplemental Type Certificate) covering not just RF performance but vibration, temperature, EMI, and depressurization. The fuselage-mounted flat panels used on commercial aircraft (e.g., the Starlink Aviation kit or Intelsat / SES aero units) are engineered products distinct from the residential dish — different mechanical design, higher environmental ratings, and lower profile to reduce drag.

Form Factors & Market Segments

User terminals are not one product — they are a family differentiated by form factor, environmental rating, and target market. Understanding this segmentation matters for both commercial analysis and system architecture.

Segment Key Requirements Representative Products
Residential (fixed) Self-install, low cost, 25–150 Mbps DL Starlink Standard, Starlink Mini
Enterprise (fixed) Higher throughput, SLA, managed service Starlink Business, OneWeb partner terminals
Maritime (vessel) IP67+, stabilized or electronically scanned, 24/7 ops Starlink Maritime, Intellian H60L, Kymeta u8
Aviation (commercial) DO-160, low profile, Doppler resilience, STC Starlink Aviation, Honeywell GX, Collins HBB
Government / Tactical SIGINT isolation, INFOSEC, anti-jam, manpack L3Harris, ViaSat Link-16, MORPHEUS
IoT / Backhaul Low power, small aperture, asymmetric traffic Starlink Mini, Swarm SpaceBEE (DTE), custom
Cell Backhaul (NTN) High-gain, high-throughput, tower mount AST SpaceMobile (direct-to-cell, no terminal), Starlink Fixed Wireless

The Starlink Mini, launched in 2024, deserves mention as a form-factor inflection point: a terminal roughly the size of a laptop that can be carried in a backpack and powered from a USB-C power bank. At 110×220 mm and under 1.2 kg, it represents the minimum viable LEO terminal for portable use — not yet a handheld phone, but a significant step toward making satellite connectivity truly portable without dedicated professional equipment.

The longer-term trajectory of LEO terminals points toward direct-to-device (D2D) service — where the "terminal" is the user's existing smartphone, using its existing antenna. AST SpaceMobile is the most prominent attempt at this model, using very large satellite apertures (up to 64 m²) to compensate for the smartphone's negligible antenna gain. This would effectively eliminate the user terminal entirely as a distinct product category for basic broadband access.

Architectural Implication
From a constellation architecture perspective, terminal capability is an independent variable that shapes the space segment design. A constellation designed around high-gain enterprise terminals can use less satellite TX power per user. A constellation targeting smartphones must compensate in space — larger apertures, higher satellite EIRP, more sophisticated interference management. Terminal and spacecraft designs are co-optimised, not independent.
