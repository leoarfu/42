User Terminals — Technology Overview

QUARTER 1
FOUNDATIONS

The constellation is only half the system. Every beam reaching the ground terminates in a device a person, an aircraft, a ship, or a cell tower can actually use — and over the past decade the user terminal evolved as dramatically as the spacecraft above it. The GEO instinct is a fixed parabolic dish pointed once and forgotten. LEO broke that model: a satellite crossing the sky at ~7 km/s cannot be tracked by any gimbal cheap or fast enough at consumer scale. The answer was the electronically steered flat panel. This lesson covers the physics behind it, contrasts the major terminal approaches in the field today, walks the link budget from the terminal's side, and treats the mobility and handover problems unique to LEO — closing on why terminal capability is not downstream of the constellation but co-designed with it.

1. The Terminal as the Binding Constraint

For most of satellite communications history the user terminal was a large, heavy, mechanically steered parabolic dish — a GEO VSAT that pointed at a fixed spot in the sky and stayed there. LEO discarded that model entirely. A LEO satellite moves at roughly 7 km/s relative to the ground, crossing the visible sky in minutes. No mechanical gimbal is fast enough, or cheap enough, to track that motion at the volume a consumer constellation requires.

The replacement is the electronically steered flat-panel antenna — an AESA (Active Electronically Scanned Array), or phased array. By adjusting the phase of hundreds or thousands of small elements electrically rather than mechanically, it steers its beam horizon-to-horizon in milliseconds with no moving parts. Cost, mass, and power for this technology fell steeply after 2018, producing terminals that sit on a rooftop, a vehicle, or a deck and look unremarkable.

KEY FRAMING The user terminal defines the commercial viability of a LEO constellation. A $50,000 professionally installed terminal serves only enterprise. A $499 self-install terminal opens consumer, maritime, and aero. Terminal economics are frequently the binding constraint on the business case — which is exactly why the 1990s LEO ventures (Lesson 1) failed on the terminal, not the spacecraft.

2. Phased Array Fundamentals

A phased array is a set of individual antenna elements whose signals are combined with controlled phase offsets. When the phase differences are chosen correctly, the wavefronts add constructively in the desired look direction and cancel elsewhere — forming a steerable beam without moving the panel.

The key property for LEO is beam agility. Phase shifts are applied by analog or digital circuits that switch in nanoseconds; the beam jumps from one satellite to another with no inertia, no motor, no settling time. Modern terminals interleave tracking across more than one satellite simultaneously, which is what makes seamless handover possible.

Gain scales with element count: doubling the array roughly doubles the aperture area and adds about 3 dB. A Ku-band panel of ~1,000 elements can reach roughly 34–38 dBi, competitive with a 60 cm GEO dish.

SCAN LOSS A phased array loses gain as the beam steers away from boresight (normal to the panel). At 60° off-boresight, gain drops on the order of 3–6 dB depending on the element pattern. This bounds the usable scan range and makes panel tilt and orientation matter for link margin, especially at low satellite elevations. It is the single most important non-obvious property of these terminals — hold onto it for Section 7.

Phase shifting can be analog (lower cost per element), fully digital (maximum flexibility, high power and cost), or hybrid — subarrays with analog phase shifting feeding a digital combiner. Most commercial LEO terminals settle on hybrid beamforming as the cost-performance sweet spot; Section 6 treats the distinctions.

4. Starlink Dish Architecture

SpaceX's terminal — "Dishy McFlatface" internally during development — is the most widely deployed LEO user terminal to date, with millions of units in the field. It has moved through several hardware generations since 2020, each smaller, cheaper, and more capable. Approximate figures for a current standard residential unit:

~29 cmStandard aperture (HP larger)
KuUser band; Ka feeder
~1,500Elements (custom RFIC)
~25/75 WIdle / peak power
±75°Electronic scan from zenith
OFDM/TDDProprietary air interface

The pivotal engineering decision is vertical integration. SpaceX designed its own beamformer ASIC — the BFM chip — integrating phase shifters, amplifiers, and data converters for many elements onto a single die. That integration drove per-element cost down by roughly two orders of magnitude versus discrete-component approaches, which is what made a sub-$500 retail terminal possible. No competitor without captive silicon has matched the price.

NO MOVING PARTS Gen 1 included a small motor to tilt the dish toward the satellite-dense sky on startup. Later generations dropped it: the electronic scan range is sufficient to acquire from any flat mounting without mechanical adjustment. Removing the motor cut a failure mode and simplified manufacturing — the same restraint-over-decoration logic that runs through good LEO terminal design.

The obstruction-detection software, run at first use, builds a sky map of trees, eaves, and other blockage, predicts outage windows, and reports them in the app. This self-survey is a LEO-specific need: a GEO dish assesses obstruction once at install because its target never moves. A LEO terminal must reason about an entire arc of sky.

5. OneWeb / Eutelsat and the Partner-Terminal Model

Eutelsat OneWeb targets a different segment from Starlink — government, enterprise, and wholesale rather than direct-to-consumer — and that shapes the terminal strategy. OneWeb runs Ku-band user links, and its terminals are supplied by third-party manufacturers under certification rather than built by the operator. This partner model produces a more heterogeneous terminal ecosystem than Starlink's captive approach.

Silicon vendors such as Satixfy supply beamforming chipsets with on-chip modem integration that terminal manufacturers build around — the opposite philosophy to SpaceX owning the full stack. The trade is the recurring one in this industry: captive integration buys cost and iteration speed at the price of a single point of supply; the partner model buys vendor diversity and broader certification reach at the price of margin shared with intermediaries.

DEEPER CONTEXT — TERMINAL SELECTION FOR AIRBORNE CONNECTIVITY

For an HBC+-class airborne connectivity programme this is the architectural decision, not a procurement footnote. The terminal is evaluated not only on RF performance but on DO-160 environmental qualification, software assurance for any safety-relevant function, EASA/FAA STC compatibility for the specific airframe, and integration with the aircraft's modem/server platform.

These constraints collapse the eligible terminal pool to a fraction of the consumer market. A residential flat panel — however good its G/T — is not a candidate: it has no path to certification. The aero terminal is a distinct engineered product, and selecting it early constrains the link budget, the frequency plan, and the service architecture downstream. Treat terminal choice as a gating item in any aviation LEO design, covered in depth in Q3 and Q8.

6. Flat Panel & AESA Design Considerations

"Flat panel antenna" covers several distinct implementations. Telling them apart is essential for evaluating vendor claims and comparing products that look identical from the outside.

Analog beamforming

Phase shifting happens in the RF domain before digitisation; each element or subarray has an analog phase shifter, and a single data stream is formed. Low cost and low power per element, but one simultaneous beam per aperture and limited ability to null interference.

Digital beamforming (DBF)

Each element has its own data converter and DSP chain; beams are formed in software. Unlimited simultaneous beams, adaptive nulling, full spatial multiplexing — at the cost of high power and price, since digitising thousands of elements is enormous baseband processing. Historically military and SIGINT; increasingly viable commercially as silicon prices fall.

Hybrid beamforming

The dominant commercial LEO architecture. The aperture is split into subarrays (e.g. 64 elements), each forming a beam with internal analog phase shifting; the subarray outputs are combined digitally. The result is a limited number of simultaneous beams — typically 2–8 — at moderate cost and power. Starlink's BFM is a highly integrated hybrid beamformer.

Liquid crystal antenna (LCA)

An alternative to RFIC arrays: liquid crystal fills the radiating layer, and varying voltage across LC pixels changes local refractive index to steer the beam. Extremely low power (the LC is passive), potentially very cheap at volume, very thin. But steering is slow — milliseconds versus nanoseconds for RFIC — with temperature sensitivity and limited scan range, which makes it better suited to slow GEO/MEO tracking than fast LEO tracking in most current implementations.

THERMAL MANAGEMENT IS NOT A SIDE ISSUE A 1,500-element Ku-band array dissipating ~75 W in a ~30 cm panel generates real surface heat. SpaceX turns this into a feature — the standard dish melts snow automatically — but the thermal design that enables snow-melt is the same design that keeps the electronics in spec. For aero and maritime, where ambient and airflow differ sharply from a residential roof, thermal qualification is a first-order constraint.

7. Link Budget at the Terminal

The terminal's contribution to any satellite link reduces to two figures: receive G/T (gain-to-noise-temperature, dB/K) on the downlink, and transmit EIRP (effective isotropic radiated power, dBW) on the uplink. Everything else about the panel — element count, aperture, noise figure, TX power — feeds into one or the other.

DirectionBudget chain (simplified)
Downlink (RX)Sat EIRP − FSPL − atmo/rain + terminal RX gain (array gain − scan loss) − noise contribution = received C/N₀
Uplink (TX)Terminal EIRP (TX power + antenna gain) − FSPL − atmo + satellite G/T = uplink C/N₀ at the satellite

The uplink carries a hard regulatory ceiling: terminal EIRP density must stay below ITU/FCC PFD limits to protect adjacent GEO systems. That constraint — not just the power amplifier — caps how hard a terminal may transmit, and it is treated properly in Lesson 14 and Q7.

The defining advantage for LEO is free-space path loss. At a 550 km shell, Ku-band FSPL is on the order of 165–170 dB depending on elevation; a GEO at 35,786 km is around 205 dB. A difference of roughly 35–40 dB means a LEO terminal closes the link with a far smaller aperture and lower TX power than a GEO VSAT — the core reason a flat panel a third the size of a GEO dish is viable.

~165–170 dBKu FSPL, 550 km LEO
~205 dBKu FSPL, GEO
~35–40 dBLEO advantage
~25°Typical min service elevation
SCAN LOSS LANDS IN THE BUDGET When the terminal steers to low elevation (say 25°), the scan loss of Section 2 — 4–6 dB — comes straight off the link margin. Constellation architects set a minimum service elevation partly to keep terminals in their high-gain scan regime. This is a concrete reason dense constellations matter operationally: more satellites in view means the terminal can favour high-elevation passes and avoid the scan-loss penalty, independent of any latency argument.

For scale: a well-designed standard LEO terminal sits around +12 to +16 dB/K G/T at boresight, against roughly +18 to +22 dB/K for a 90 cm Ku GEO dish. The GEO dish wins on raw G/T because of its larger aperture — but once its ~35–40 dB higher path loss is paid back, the LEO system still closes the link with the smaller terminal at equivalent throughput targets. The aperture math favours LEO.

8. Mobility & Handover

The defining operational challenge of a LEO terminal is satellite handover: as a satellite drops below the minimum service elevation, the terminal must switch to a new one without dropping packets, spiking latency visibly, or requiring user action.

Modern flat panels handle this through make-before-break dual-beam operation. While the primary beam tracks the serving satellite to the horizon, a second beam acquires the next satellite and establishes sync. The modem performs a soft handover — traffic shifts to the new beam once link quality is confirmed, then the old beam releases. Well implemented, the visible effect is a latency blip of 10–30 ms and no packet loss. This is the same make-before-break logic the gateway antenna farm uses on the feeder side (Lesson 11); the terminal applies it on the user side.

Handover scenarioTypical durationUser impact
Routine satellite transition~2–5 min between eventsInvisible with make-before-break
Obstruction passage1–30 sOutage if no adjacent satellite visible
Beam-edge crossing (in-beam)MillisecondsHandled in constellation, transparent
Gateway handover (ground)~50–200 msLatency spike; depends on ISL availability
Polar / high-latitude gapSeconds to minutesOutage until next visible satellite

For aero and maritime, mobility compounds: the terminal itself moves — up to ~900 km/h for commercial aviation — adding a large Doppler contribution on top of the satellite's own orbital velocity. LEO terminals compensate relative Doppler shifts of up to roughly ±100 kHz at Ku-band in the waveform's frequency-tracking loops, not mechanically.

AIRBORNE CERTIFICATION COMPLEXITY Aero LEO terminals need an STC covering not just RF but vibration, temperature, EMI, and depressurisation. The fuselage-mounted panels on commercial aircraft are engineered products distinct from the residential dish — different mechanical design, higher environmental ratings, lower profile to cut drag. The certification path, not the RF, is usually the schedule driver.

9. Form Factors & Market Segments

User terminals are not one product but a family differentiated by form factor, environmental rating, and target market. The segmentation matters for commercial analysis and for system architecture, because the terminal a constellation is designed around dictates how much it must spend on the space segment per user.

The Starlink Mini is a form-factor inflection point: a terminal roughly the size of a laptop, carryable in a backpack and powered from a USB-C bank. Not a handheld phone, but a real step toward portable satellite connectivity without dedicated professional equipment.

The longer arc points toward direct-to-device (D2D) — where the "terminal" is the user's existing smartphone using its existing antenna. AST SpaceMobile is the most prominent attempt, using very large satellite apertures to compensate for the phone's negligible antenna gain. If it succeeds at scale for basic broadband, it removes the user terminal as a distinct product category for that tier of service entirely.

ARCHITECTURAL IMPLICATION — TERMINAL AND SPACECRAFT ARE CO-DESIGNED Terminal capability is an independent variable that shapes the space segment. A constellation built around high-gain enterprise terminals can use less satellite TX power per user. A constellation targeting bare smartphones must compensate in space — larger apertures, higher satellite EIRP, far more aggressive interference management. The two are co-optimised, not designed in sequence. An architect who sizes the spacecraft before deciding the terminal has the dependency backwards.

PYTHON EXERCISES

Three exercises of escalating depth. The first computes the path-loss advantage that justifies the small aperture. The second models scan loss against elevation. The third estimates handover cadence from orbital geometry. Python 3.8+ standard library only, except where an extension calls for matplotlib.

Exercise 1 — Free-Space Path Loss Calculator

Compute slant range and FSPL for LEO, MEO, and GEO at a fixed elevation and frequency, and print the LEO-vs-GEO delta — the number that explains why a flat panel a third the size of a GEO dish can close the link.

import math

R_E = 6371.0     # mean Earth radius, km
C   = 3e8        # speed of light, m/s

def slant_range_km(altitude_km, elevation_deg):
    """Ground-station to satellite slant range, spherical Earth."""
    el = math.radians(elevation_deg)
    return (-R_E * math.sin(el)
            + math.sqrt((R_E * math.sin(el))**2 + 2*R_E*altitude_km + altitude_km**2))

def fspl_db(slant_km, freq_ghz):
    """Free-space path loss in dB."""
    d_m  = slant_km * 1e3
    f_hz = freq_ghz * 1e9
    return 20 * math.log10(4 * math.pi * d_m * f_hz / C)

scenarios = {
    "LEO 550 km (Starlink)": 550,
    "MEO 8000 km (O3b)":     8000,
    "GEO 35786 km":          35786,
}
freq_ghz  = 12.0   # Ku-band downlink
elevation = 45.0   # degrees above horizon

fspl = {}
for name, alt in scenarios.items():
    s = slant_range_km(alt, elevation)
    fspl[name] = fspl_db(s, freq_ghz)
    print(f"{name:24s} slant={s:8.0f} km  FSPL={fspl[name]:6.1f} dB")

delta = fspl["GEO 35786 km"] - fspl["LEO 550 km (Starlink)"]
print(f"\nLEO advantage over GEO: {delta:.1f} dB")

# Questions:
# 1. Re-run at 25 deg and 90 deg elevation. How much does the LEO FSPL
#    move? Why does GEO barely change with elevation but LEO moves more?
# 2. The delta is the budget headroom that lets a LEO terminal use a
#    smaller aperture. Roughly how much aperture-area reduction does
#    35-40 dB buy, all else equal? (Gain ~ area; 3 dB per doubling.)

Expected order of magnitude: LEO FSPL ~170 dB, GEO ~205 dB, delta ~35 dB at 45°. The exact figure shifts with elevation and frequency — that is the point of question 1, not a defect.

Exercise 2 — Phased-Array Scan Loss Model

Model effective array gain against scan angle with a cosine scan-loss approximation, then read off the elevation at which the terminal has dropped 3 dB from its boresight gain — the practical edge of its high-gain regime.

import math

def array_gain_dbi(n_elements, element_gain_dbi, scan_angle_deg, scan_loss_exp=1.5):
    """
    Approximate array gain with a cos^n scan-loss term.
    scan_angle_deg : angle from boresight (0 = pointing at zenith)
    scan_loss_exp  : 1.0-2.0 typical
    """
    theta = math.radians(scan_angle_deg)
    array_linear = (10 ** (element_gain_dbi / 10)) * n_elements
    scan_factor  = max(math.cos(theta), 0.0) ** scan_loss_exp
    if scan_factor == 0:
        return float("-inf")
    return 10 * math.log10(array_linear * scan_factor)

n, eg = 1500, 5.0          # ~Starlink-class: 1500 elements, ~5 dBi/element
boresight = array_gain_dbi(n, eg, 0.0)

print(f"{'Elev (deg)':>10} {'Scan from boresight':>20} {'Gain (dBi)':>12} {'dB below peak':>14}")
print("-" * 60)
for elev in range(10, 91, 10):
    scan = 90 - elev               # boresight is zenith
    g = array_gain_dbi(n, eg, scan)
    print(f"{elev:>10} {scan:>20} {g:>12.1f} {g - boresight:>14.1f}")

Exercise 3 — Handover Cadence Estimator

Estimate how often a fixed terminal hands over, from orbital geometry alone. Compute the orbital period, the fraction of an overhead pass spent above the minimum service elevation, and the implied handover rate for a single-satellite model.

import math

MU  = 3.986e14   # Earth gravitational parameter, m^3/s^2
R_E = 6.371e6    # Earth radius, m

def orbital_period_s(altitude_m):
    r = R_E + altitude_m
    return 2 * math.pi * math.sqrt(r**3 / MU)

def visible_arc_fraction(altitude_m, min_elev_deg):
    """Fraction of an overhead pass above min elevation (spherical Earth)."""
    el  = math.radians(min_elev_deg)
    r   = R_E + altitude_m
    rho = math.acos((R_E / r) * math.cos(el)) - el
    return (2 * rho) / (2 * math.pi)

altitude_m   = 550e3   # Starlink shell
min_elev_deg = 25.0    # minimum service elevation

T  = orbital_period_s(altitude_m)
fv = visible_arc_fraction(altitude_m, min_elev_deg)
t_visible = T * fv
handovers_per_hour = 3600 / t_visible

print(f"Orbital period:        {T/60:.1f} min")
print(f"Visible arc fraction:  {fv*100:.1f}%")
print(f"Max pass duration:     {t_visible/60:.1f} min")
print(f"Handovers / hour:      {handovers_per_hour:.1f}  (single-satellite model)")

# Questions:
# 1. This is a per-satellite overhead-pass upper bound. Real terminals hand
#    over more often -- why? (Hint: most passes are not overhead, and the
#    terminal switches before a satellite reaches the horizon.)
# 2. Re-run at OneWeb's 1200 km. Does higher altitude raise or lower the
#    handover rate, and why?
# 3. Each handover costs a 10-30 ms blip. At your computed rate, what is the
#    aggregate latency overhead per hour? Is it perceptible?
EXTENSION Replace the overhead-pass assumption with a distribution of maximum-elevation passes across the visibility cone, and estimate the mean pass duration rather than the maximum. Compare the resulting handover rate to the overhead bound — the gap is why real dwell times are shorter than this exercise's figure.
