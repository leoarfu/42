PHASE 0: MISSION BRIEFING (Start Here)

00_WHY_LEO EASY

The Problem Statement

50% of humanity has no internet. Fiber can't reach oceans, deserts, or remote regions economically.

  • Maritime: Ships pay $10-50/MB via GEO satellites.
  • Aviation: In-flight WiFi is slow and expensive.
  • Rural: Last-mile fiber costs $10,000+ per home.
  • IoT: Smart agriculture, tracking need global coverage.
üí° The Insight: LEO constellations can provide global coverage with low latency at scale.
Use Cases & Users
  • Consumer Broadband: Rural homes, RVs, disaster relief.
  • Maritime: Commercial shipping, cruise ships.
  • Aviation: Commercial and private jets.
  • Government/Military: Secure comms, remote operations.
  • IoT/M2M: Asset tracking, smart sensors.
  • Enterprise: Remote mining, oil rigs, construction.

00_THE_PLAYERS EASY

Current Constellation Operators
  • Starlink (SpaceX): 4,000+ satellites, ~550km, first-mover advantage.
  • Kuiper (Amazon): 3,236 planned, leverages AWS/Prime integration.
  • OneWeb (Eutelsat): 600+ satellites, B2B focus, higher altitude (~1,200km).
  • Telesat Lightspeed: ~300 satellites, premium enterprise market.
  • China's SatNet: 13,000 planned, geopolitical competitor.
Market Size & Timeline

Total Addressable Market: $1 trillion+ by 2030 (satellite broadband + IoT).

  • 2015-2020: Starlink prototype launches.
  • 2021-2023: Commercial service begins, OneWeb recovers from bankruptcy.
  • 2024-2025: Kuiper launches, competition intensifies.
  • 2026+: Consolidation, profitability targets.

00_THE_AHA_MOMENT EASY

LEO vs GEO vs Fiber: The Latency Showdown
Scenario: Video call from San Francisco to London
  • Fiber: ~80ms (if available) - BEST
  • LEO Satellite: ~30-50ms - GOOD
  • GEO Satellite: ~600ms - UNUSABLE

Why the difference? Speed of light is constant. Distance matters.

  • GEO: 35,786km altitude = 71,572km round trip
  • LEO: 550km altitude = 1,100km round trip
  • That's a 65x distance reduction!

03_HISTORICAL_CONTEXT EASY

The Graveyard: Learning from Failures

LEO constellations aren't new. They failed before. Here's why:

  • Iridium (1998): Launched 66 sats, bankrupt in 1999. Too expensive ($3-8/min). Lesson: User terminal cost kills adoption.
  • Teledesic (1990s): Bill Gates backed, never launched. Tech wasn't ready. Lesson: Timing matters.
  • Globalstar: Survived but struggled. Limited coverage. Lesson: Full global coverage is table stakes.
‚ö†Ô∏è What Changed? SpaceX's reusable rockets slashed launch costs from $10,000/kg to $1,000/kg.
Why Now? The Perfect Storm
  • Launch Costs: Falcon 9 reusability revolution
  • Electronics: Moore's Law made satellites smaller/cheaper
  • Phased Arrays: Electronically steerable antennas
  • Demand: Remote work, streaming, IoT explosion

  • T: Total satellites
  • P: Number of orbital planes

  • Footprint Radius: Ground area covered by one satellite
  • Revisit Time: How often a location sees a satellite

05_RF_SYSTEMS HARD

Antenna Technologies
  • Phased Arrays: Electronically steer beams without moving parts. Starlink's pizza box uses this.
  • Parabolic Dish: Traditional, mechanically steered. Cheaper but bulkier.
  • Horn Antennas: For gateway/ground stations.
  • Helical Antennas: Compact, circular polarization.

06_SPACECRAFT_SUBSYSTEMS MEDIUM

Power Systems
  • Solar Panels: Typical LEO sat: 500W-2kW
  • Batteries: Lithium-ion for eclipse periods (~30 min per orbit)
  • Power Budget: Payload vs housekeeping allocation
  • Degradation: ~2-3% per year due to radiation
Thermal Management

Challenge: Satellites face -270¬∞C (shadow) to +150¬∞C (direct sun)

  • Passive: Multi-layer insulation (MLI), radiators
  • Active: Heaters, louvers, heat pipes
  • Electronics: Must operate -40¬∞C to +85¬∞C
Attitude Control (ADCS)
  • Reaction Wheels: Spin to change orientation
  • Magnetorquers: Interact with Earth's magnetic field
  • Thrusters: Chemical or electric propulsion
  • Star Trackers: Determine orientation using star patterns
  • Requirement: Point antennas at Earth within 0.1¬∞ accuracy

07_NETWORK_ARCHITECTURE HARD

Bent-pipe vs On-board Processing
Bent-pipe (Traditional):
  • Satellite is "dumb relay"
  • All data goes: User ‚Üí Sat ‚Üí Gateway ‚Üí Internet
  • ‚úÖ Simple, reliable
  • ‚ùå Requires ground stations everywhere
On-board Processing (Modern):
  • Satellite routes traffic intelligently
  • Can connect: User ‚Üí Sat ‚Üí Sat ‚Üí Sat ‚Üí Gateway
  • ‚úÖ Fewer ground stations needed
  • ‚ùå Complex, more expensive
OISL: Optical Inter-Satellite Links

Game Changer: Satellites communicate via LASERS at 100+ Gbps

  • Advantage: No spectrum licensing, massive bandwidth
  • Range: Up to 5,000km between satellites
  • Challenge: Pointing accuracy (microradians), tracking fast-moving targets
  • Status: Starlink Gen2 has OISL, Kuiper planning it

08_GROUND_INFRASTRUCTURE MEDIUM

Gateway Stations
  • Purpose: Connect satellite network to terrestrial internet
  • Requirements: Large parabolic antennas (3-13m), high-bandwidth fiber
  • Locations: Must be geographically distributed for redundancy
  • Starlink: 100+ gateway sites globally
User Terminals

The Make-or-Break Component:

  • Target Cost: $200-500 (currently ~$500-600 for Starlink)
  • Technology: Phased array with 1,000+ antenna elements
  • Power: 50-100W typical
  • Challenge: Must manufacture millions at low cost
