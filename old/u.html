The Mission in One Paragraph

UNIVITY is building a VLEO (Very Low Earth Orbit) constellation — branded uniSky — that uses 5G NR (New Radio) mmWave spectrum to let terrestrial telecom operators extend their 5G networks from space. The proposition is B2B: UNIVITY doesn't sell to end users, it sells capacity to MNOs (Mobile Network Operators) who use it to extend 5G coverage where terrestrial cells don't exist. The in-orbit demonstration programme is called uniShape, backed by CNES under France 2030. The strategic partners are TDF (operating three gateway stations — two in metropolitan France, one overseas) and ESA. Charles Delfieux is President; Véronique Bonnet is Programme Director.

WHY THIS MATTERS IN THE INTERVIEW Every answer you give should reference this B2B model. You're not operating a consumer service — you're operating infrastructure that feeds into MNO networks. Think in terms of SLAs to operators, not end-user experience. The CNES/France 2030 backing also means French sovereign connectivity and national security angles are relevant — say the words "souveraineté numérique" if they come up.

What Makes UNIVITY Technically Different

  • Regenerative payload. Unlike bent-pipe satellites that simply relay signals, UNIVITY's payload processes the 5G baseband onboard. The satellite contains a full 5G gNB (base station) — it understands 5G NR protocols natively. This is the "regenerative" part of the June 2025 launch.
  • mmWave spectrum. UNIVITY uses 5G millimetre-wave frequencies (26 GHz range) — the same spectrum bands that terrestrial 5G mmWave uses. This is the architectural bet: operators already own mmWave spectrum licences; UNIVITY delivers the space segment using those same bands, so there is no new spectrum allocation from the operator's perspective.
  • VLEO orbit. Very Low Earth Orbit (~300–450 km) means lower path loss, lower latency, and passive drag deorbit for debris compliance — the same logic described in Starlink's Gen2 VLEO shell.
  • B2B wholesale model. UNIVITY is not a consumer ISP. It sells capacity to MNOs. The gateways connect the space segment to existing terrestrial 5G core networks. TDF operates the ground gateway infrastructure.

Technical Concepts to Own in the Interview

```

5G NTN — What It Actually Is

Non-Terrestrial Networks (NTN) is 3GPP's formal integration of satellite and HAPS into the 5G NR standard. Key Release 17 additions:

  • Extended Timing Advance (TA) for satellite propagation delays
  • Doppler pre-compensation at terminal or satellite
  • Service link (gNB↔UE) and feeder link (gNB↔5G core) defined
  • Transparent mode (bent-pipe) vs regenerative mode (gNB on satellite)

UNIVITY uses regenerative mode — the satellite IS the gNB. The 5G NR stack runs onboard. This is architecturally more complex but removes latency added by ground-based baseband processing.

mmWave in NTN — The UNIVITY Bet

UNIVITY uses 5G mmWave bands (FR2, ~26 GHz — n258/n257/n260 range). This is unusual — most NTN systems use sub-6 GHz for wide coverage. The mmWave choice means:

  • Very high bandwidth available (400–800 MHz channels)
  • Operators already hold mmWave spectrum licences — no new allocation
  • Narrow beams required (high gain to compensate path loss)
  • Rain attenuation is significant at 26 GHz — link margin is a design challenge
  • Small cell coverage areas — concentrated capacity over populated zones

VLEO — Why the Orbit Matters Here

UNIVITY's satellites operate at Very Low Earth Orbit (~300–450 km). Key implications for your ops role:

  • Path loss ~4–6 dB lower than 550 km — critical for closing mmWave link budgets
  • RTT approximately 4–10 ms one-way — enables 5G latency targets (<10 ms for URLLC)
  • Atmospheric drag is significant — daily/weekly orbit maintenance manoeuvres are part of nominal ops
  • Short orbital lifetime if propulsion fails — passive deorbit compliance
  • Higher radiation flux than ISS orbit but below Van Allen — radiation hardening is still required

Regenerative Payload vs Bent-Pipe

This is UNIVITY's core technical differentiator — make sure you can explain it fluently.

  • Bent-pipe: satellite receives signal, frequency-converts, amplifies, retransmits. No onboard processing. Simple, proven. Latency = 2× propagation delay + processing on ground.
  • Regenerative: satellite demodulates the signal, processes the baseband (in UNIVITY's case: a full 5G NR gNB stack), re-modulates and retransmits. More complex, higher power, but enables onboard routing, scheduling, and direct 5G protocol compliance.
  • Operationally: you're monitoring a flying base station, not just a transponder. Telecommands affect 5G RRC/MAC layer configurations, not just RF parameters.

Mission Phases — LEOP to EOL

The JD explicitly mentions all four phases. Know what each means:

  • LEOP (Launch and Early Orbit Phase): first days after separation, safe mode, initial acquisition, first TM/TC contact, attitude determination, solar array deployment, initial RF checks. Most critical and highest-risk phase.
  • Commissioning: functional checkout of all subsystems, payload activation, link budget validation, beam calibration, interface with 5G core. UNIVITY-specific: 5G gNB bring-up and operator integration testing.
  • Nominal Operations: routine health monitoring, orbit maintenance, beam scheduling, performance reporting, anomaly response.
  • EOL (End of Life): de-orbit manoeuvres, passivation (battery/propellant venting), controlled reentry or graveyard orbit.

Ground Segment — Key Components

For a small constellation like UNIVITY's IOD:

  • TT&C station: Tracking, Telemetry & Commanding — the safety-critical link. Usually S-band. Used in LEOP and for housekeeping.
  • Mission (payload) gateway: Ka/mm-wave feeder link carrying 5G NR payload traffic. TDF operates three of these for UNIVITY.
  • MCS (Mission Control System): operator console for TM/TC, orbit planning, event scheduling. Common platforms: SCOS-2000, GMV's HIFLY, or custom.
  • 5G Core (5GC): the terrestrial network side — AMF, SMF, UPF. UNIVITY interfaces to MNO 5G cores via the gateway.
  • Network slice management: each MNO customer gets a logical slice of the 5G NR capacity.

Link Budget Basics — 5G NTN Flavour

You'll be asked about end-to-end link performance. Key parameters for the UNIVITY scenario:

  • FSPL at 26 GHz from 400 km: ≈ 185–190 dB (very high — narrow beams required)
  • Satellite EIRP must be very high: phased array with high gain essential
  • UE antenna: 5G mmWave phones have phased array modules (~20 dBi) but need clear sky view
  • Rain fade at 26 GHz: 5–15 dB/km in heavy rain — link margin ≥ 15 dB needed for tropical zones
  • Doppler at VLEO: up to ±80 kHz at 26 GHz — pre-compensation required per 3GPP NTN spec

Anomaly Resolution — What They'll Probe

The JD asks for anomaly resolution and mission review board participation. Expect scenario questions:

  • Degraded telemetry: is it the spacecraft, the TT&C link, or MCS processing? Isolation approach.
  • Payload performance drop: RF chain fault vs beam management vs 5G gNB software issue?
  • Orbit drift: propulsion anomaly vs atmospheric density prediction error?
  • Gateway link degradation: rain fade vs equipment fault vs link budget margin exhausted?

Structure all answers using an isolation methodology: parameter → symptom → likely cause tree.
```

HARD Explain the difference between a transparent and a regenerative satellite payload, and why UNIVITY chose regenerative for 5G NTN. ▼
MODEL ANSWER FRAMEWORK — TECHNICAL DIFFERENTIATION

Transparent: The satellite acts as a frequency converter and amplifier — it doesn't understand the signal content. Simple, low-risk, proven in most VSAT and GEO systems including the systems you worked with at iDirect. Ground processes all the baseband.

Regenerative: The satellite demodulates the uplink, processes the baseband (in UNIVITY's case, runs a full 5G NR gNB stack), and re-modulates for the downlink. More complex but fundamental advantages:

  • Latency: no round-trip to the ground gateway for baseband processing. Critical for 5G latency targets.
  • The satellite can make scheduling decisions independently — beam management, resource allocation — without ground intervention every slot.
  • Operators see a standard 5G gNB interface; the satellite's space location is abstracted away.
  • The June 2025 launch proved UNIVITY can fly this architecture — it's not theoretical.

Why it matters operationally for your role: "As the Mission Ops Engineer, I'm not just monitoring RF parameters — I'm monitoring a flying base station. If there's a 5G RRC issue, I need to be able to distinguish it from a platform anomaly. That's a more complex diagnostic space, which is part of what makes this programme interesting."

HARD What specific challenges does VLEO orbit pose for mission operations compared to a standard LEO constellation? ▼
MODEL ANSWER FRAMEWORK — SHOW VLEO DEPTH
  • Atmospheric drag: At 350–400 km, the residual atmosphere causes measurable orbital decay. Nominal ops includes regular drag-compensation manoeuvres — you need accurate atmospheric density modelling (NRLMSISE-00 or similar) and a propulsion budget that accounts for solar activity cycles (solar flux raises atmospheric density at VLEO).
  • Contact windows: Each satellite pass is short — approximately 4–6 minutes at VLEO for a typical ground station. LEOP TM/TC windows are extremely tight; you need multiple ground stations or a relay strategy.
  • Collision avoidance frequency: VLEO has a higher debris flux per square kilometre than 550 km. Conjunction analysis and avoidance manoeuvres are more frequent.
  • Radiation: Below the inner Van Allen belt, radiation is lower than MEO — but energetic particle events (SPEs) can still cause single-event upsets in the payload processor.
  • Link geometry: High angular rate means beam steering must be very fast — beam handover between satellites happens every few minutes. Your monitoring of beam performance needs to account for the geometry changing continuously.
STRONG How would you approach writing the commissioning procedure for the 5G payload on the first UNIVITY satellite? ▼
MODEL ANSWER FRAMEWORK — PROCEDURE DESIGN

This maps to your Airbus DS procedure/interface work. Structure your answer around the commissioning sequence logic:

  • Platform first, payload second. Confirm ADCS is stable, power subsystem is nominal, thermal is within range, propulsion is ready. Only then activate the payload.
  • RF calibration before 5G stack bring-up. Verify feeder link margins (gateway to satellite), check EIRP and G/T against link budget predictions. A 2 dB shortfall here needs to be understood before you activate the gNB stack.
  • 5G gNB bring-up in stages. Physical layer first (check 5G NR synchronisation signals are transmitted correctly), then RRC, then data plane. Each stage has pass/fail criteria defined against the payload spec.
  • End-to-end test with MNO test UE. A real 5G device camps on the satellite gNB and runs a data session through to the 5G core. This validates the full chain including TDF gateway infrastructure.
  • Documentation of delta from predictions. Every parameter that deviates from the pre-launch budget goes into the commissioning report — this feeds back into the operations manual for nominal ops.

Bridge from iDirect experience: "At iDirect, satellite commissioning followed a similar staged logic — RF first, then modem configuration, then IP connectivity. The principle is identical; the 5G NR layer adds complexity but the commissioning discipline is the same."

PARTIAL What do you know about 5G NTN as defined in 3GPP, and how does it differ from previous satellite communication standards? ▼
MODEL ANSWER FRAMEWORK — SHOW LEARNING, NOT BLUFFING

Be honest that your primary background is in SATCOM/IP networking, not 3GPP standards, and then demonstrate you've done the work:

"My deep background is in satellite RF systems and IP networking — 3GPP NTN is the area I've been building on specifically for this role, and I can give you my current understanding. 3GPP Release 17 introduced formal NTN support in 5G NR, solving the main challenges that make satellite use hard in a cellular standard:"

  • Timing Advance (TA): In terrestrial 5G, TA is up to ~0.67 ms. From VLEO at 400 km, one-way propagation is ~1.3 ms — so R17 extended TA to handle satellite delays. Terminals must pre-compensate based on their GNSS position and the satellite ephemeris.
  • Doppler pre-compensation: A VLEO satellite at 400 km moves at ~7.7 km/s — at 26 GHz this generates Doppler shifts of tens of kHz. R17 requires terminals or the satellite to pre-compensate this so the gNB's receiver sees a stable frequency.
  • HARQ (Hybrid ARQ) timing: Standard 5G NR HARQ assumes short round-trips. For NTN, HARQ processes are either disabled or extended to account for propagation delays.
  • Before NTN, satellite communications used proprietary standards (DVB-S2, DVB-RCS2, iDirect's SCPC). NTN integrates satellite into the same 3GPP ecosystem as terrestrial 5G — same protocol stack, same device chipsets eventually.

PARTIAL We need to assess end-to-end 5G NTN link performance. Walk me through how you'd approach this. ▼
MODEL ANSWER FRAMEWORK — LINK BUDGET APPLIED TO 5G NTN

This bridges your RF link budget experience with the 5G NTN context. Structure as a chain:

  • Define the path segments: UE (user terminal) → service link uplink → satellite → feeder link downlink → TDF gateway → 5G core → internet.
  • Service link: Compute C/N₀ at the satellite receiver. Inputs: UE EIRP (mmWave phone array, ~20–25 dBm EIRP), FSPL at 26 GHz from 400 km (~187 dB), satellite G/T. Required C/N₀ for target throughput (e.g., 100 Mbps per beam needs ~30 dB·Hz).
  • Feeder link: Same calculation from TDF gateway (~5 m Ka-band dish, high EIRP) to satellite. Typically the easier link to close because the gateway is a high-power, large-aperture fixed installation.
  • Rain/atmospheric margin: At 26 GHz, budget 10–15 dB for rain fade in continental Europe. This is the dominant impairment at mmWave.
  • End-to-end metrics: Throughput, latency (should be <10 ms one-way at VLEO for URLLC), packet loss. Report against SLA thresholds agreed with MNO partners.
  • Optimisation levers: Beam dwell time allocation, AMC (Adaptive Modulation and Coding) thresholds, resource block allocation per operator slice.
