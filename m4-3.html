Frequency Bands:
Ka, Ku, V & E — Tradeoffs

MODULE 4
RF & LINK BUDGETS

Frequency is the most fundamental parameter in satellite link design. It determines path loss, antenna size, available bandwidth, atmospheric susceptibility, and regulatory regime — all simultaneously. The choice of band locks in the physics of every link budget the satellite will ever close. This lesson maps the progression from L-band to E-band, establishes why Ka dominates LEO broadband, and delivers Stage 3 of the spine project: band-aware link budget comparison.

1. The Band Ladder: L through E

The ITU designates satellite frequency bands by letter, each with its own regulatory history, atmospheric behaviour, and technology base. The satellite communications industry has used a progression of these bands over sixty years, driven by congestion at lower frequencies pushing operators higher, and improving component technology making higher bands usable.

  • Ka-band: downlink 17.7–20.2 GHz; uplink 27.5–30.0 GHz
  • V-band: 37.5–42.5 GHz (downlink), 47.2–51.4 GHz (uplink) — less congested, more bandwidth available

2. Why Ka-Band Dominates LEO Broadband

The progression from Ku to Ka was driven by two linked factors: bandwidth availability and antenna physics.

Bandwidth

Ka-band offers roughly 2.5 GHz of available downlink bandwidth (17.7–20.2 GHz), compared to about 2 GHz at Ku-band. More critically, Ka-band spectrum is less congested. Ku-band has been the GEO commercial satellite workhorse since the 1980s; virtually all useful orbital positions above populated regions are occupied and spectrum coordination is exhaustive. Ka-band, as a newer commercial allocation, has less incumbent congestion — particularly for NGSO systems.

Antenna size

Antenna gain scales as (πD/λ)². For a fixed physical aperture D, gain increases with frequency squared. For a fixed gain requirement, the required diameter scales as λ ∝ 1/f. Moving from Ku (12 GHz) to Ka (20 GHz) reduces the required antenna diameter for a given gain by factor 20/12 ≈ 1.67. A Ku-band dish achieving a given gain at 90 cm achieves the same gain at 54 cm at Ka-band. Smaller antennas mean smaller satellites and cheaper user terminals — the economics that make LEO broadband viable.

The tradeoff: rain fade

The cost of Ka-band is atmospheric attenuation. Rain attenuation scales strongly with frequency above 10 GHz, reaching 10–30 dB/km at Ka-band versus 2–10 dB/km at Ku-band. This is Ka-band's primary disadvantage and the primary driver for rain margin in Ka-band link budgets. At LEO altitudes, the shorter atmospheric path at high elevation partially mitigates this relative to GEO Ka-band systems.

THE f² CANCELLATION The additional FSPL at higher frequency is exactly compensated by increased antenna gain for a fixed physical aperture — both scale as f². Moving from Ku to Ka delivers no free link-budget improvement. The real benefits are bandwidth and smaller aperture for a given gain, not a physics shortcut.

Moving from Ku to Ka adds 2.6 dB of path loss. Moving from Ka to V-band adds 7.4 dB. These losses must be recovered by antenna gain or transmit power. However, for a fixed physical aperture, FSPL increase and gain increase cancel exactly — the net link budget effect is zero. Higher frequency's advantages are bandwidth and smaller aperture for a given gain, not a free RF improvement.

4. Ku-Band: The Legacy Broadband Workhorse

Ku-band (12–18 GHz) has dominated commercial satellite communications since the 1980s for several reasons.

Technology maturity: Ku-band amplifiers, LNAs, feeds, and dishes have been manufactured at scale for decades. The technology is cheap, reliable, and available from many suppliers.

Regulatory clarity: Ku-band allocations are well-defined and heavily filed at the ITU. The coordination regime is complex but understood.

Weather resilience: Ku-band rain attenuation is significantly lower than Ka-band — typically 3–8 dB/km versus 10–30 dB/km at Ka-band. Ku-band systems maintain service in rain events that would knock out Ka-band links.

The disadvantages: limited bandwidth (spectrum congestion at prime GEO positions), larger antennas than Ka for equivalent gain, and larger user terminals. For a LEO broadband constellation requiring small user terminals and high throughput capacity, Ka-band is almost always preferred.

5. Ka-Band: The LEO Broadband Standard

Ka-band (satellite allocations primarily 17.7–30 GHz) has become the standard for LEO broadband: smaller antennas for equivalent gain, more available bandwidth, and a less congested spectrum environment than Ku.

The key Ka-band sub-bands used in LEO broadband systems:

  • Ka downlink (user link): 17.7–20.2 GHz — satellite transmits to user terminal
  • Ka uplink (user link): 27.5–30.0 GHz — user terminal transmits to satellite
  • Ka gateway/feeder uplink: 27.5–30.0 GHz or 28–30 GHz
  • Ka gateway/feeder downlink: 17.7–20.2 GHz or 18–20 GHz

The distinction between user links and gateway feeder links matters for interference management. If user and feeder links share the same Ka-band spectrum, their beams must be coordinated to avoid co-channel interference. Some systems use Ka-band for user links and V-band for feeder links to separate the two traffic types entirely.

6. V-Band: The Emerging Feeder Link Standard

V-band (satellite allocations at 37.5–51.4 GHz) offers approximately 10 GHz of available spectrum — four times more than Ka-band. This makes it attractive for high-capacity feeder links where bandwidth hunger is greatest.

Advantages: enormous bandwidth; less crowded regulatory environment than Ka-band; higher antenna gain for a fixed aperture, enabling smaller gateway dishes for equivalent gain.

Disadvantages: severe rain attenuation (significantly worse than Ka-band); atmospheric oxygen absorption peak near 60 GHz requires avoiding that region — the useful V-band satellite allocations are in the windows at 37.5–42.5 GHz and 47.2–51.4 GHz around the O₂ absorption peak; demanding component technology (GaN amplifiers at V-band are less mature than Ka-band).

THE 60 GHz O₂ ABSORPTION PEAK At 60 GHz, atmospheric oxygen absorption reaches 10–15 dB/km at sea level. A link through 5 km of atmosphere at this frequency suffers 50–75 dB of absorption — completely impractical for satellite use. V-band satellite allocations are intentionally placed in the spectral windows on either side of this peak. The 60 GHz band is usable for space-to-space ISLs where there is no atmosphere.

Starlink's FCC filings include V-band user link allocations (37.5–40 GHz downlink, 47.2–50.2 GHz uplink) for dense urban deployment. OneWeb and Telesat Lightspeed use V-band for gateway feeder links.

7. E-Band: Future Directions

E-band (60–90 GHz) represents the frontier of commercial satellite frequency use. The satellite industry's interest is in the windows at 71–76 GHz and 81–86 GHz, which avoid the 60 GHz absorption peak and offer tens of GHz of bandwidth.

The 60 GHz O₂ absorption peak makes satellite links at that frequency impractical through the atmosphere, but it is being studied for satellite-to-satellite ISL links in space where there is no atmosphere. In practice, optical ISLs (laser, ~1,550 nm) already achieve higher performance than E-band RF ISLs for the same aperture size, with no ITU frequency coordination required — making E-band RF ISLs a niche interest rather than a primary development direction.

8. System Implications for LEO Broadband

Band selection drives cascading design decisions across the entire system.

Ka-band user link + Ka-band feeder: simplest architecture, single RF system on the satellite. Requires careful frequency planning to separate user and gateway beams. Used by OneWeb gen 1.

Ka-band user link + V-band feeder: separates user and feeder frequency regimes, reducing interference risk and increasing gateway bandwidth. More complex satellite RF design. Used by Telesat Lightspeed and in Starlink's evolution.

Ka-band user link + optical ISL: Starlink's current configuration. ISLs at ~1,550 nm are entirely separate from RF — no coordination needed, no spectrum consumed. The feeder-link question shifts: with ISLs routing traffic across the constellation, how many gateways are required globally?

The terminal side implications are direct:

  • Ka-band user terminal: 25–75 cm flat-panel phased array; EIRP typically 35–45 dBW; G/T typically 8–15 dB/K
  • Ku-band user terminal: 45–90 cm dish; similar EIRP but larger aperture for equivalent gain
  • V-band terminal (future): 10–20 cm aperture for equivalent gain; very small physically but requires sophisticated GaN amplifiers not yet available at consumer price points

Exercise 2 — Band Comparison Function

Evaluate a link scenario across multiple frequency bands, holding physical apertures fixed.

import math

T0 = 290.0; K_DB = -228.6

def fspl(d, f):         return 92.4 + 20*math.log10(d) + 20*math.log10(f)
def gain_dbi(D, f, eta=0.65):
    lam = 0.3/f
    return 10*math.log10(eta*(math.pi*D/lam)**2)

def band_link(name, freq_ghz, d_km, D_tx_m, p_tx_w,
              D_rx_m, t_sys_k, atm_db, req_cn0):
    eirp = 10*math.log10(p_tx_w) + gain_dbi(D_tx_m, freq_ghz)
    loss = fspl(d_km, freq_ghz) + atm_db
    gt   = gain_dbi(D_rx_m, freq_ghz) - 10*math.log10(t_sys_k)
    cn0  = eirp - loss + gt - K_DB
    return cn0, cn0 - req_cn0

print("Band comparison — fixed physical apertures, varying frequency")
print("Tx: D=1m, P=20W   Rx: D=0.6m, T_sys=300K   Range=1000km\n")
print(f"{'Band':>7}{'Freq':>8}{'EIRP':>8}{'FSPL':>8}{'G/T':>8}{'C/N₀':>8}{'Margin':>8}")
print("-" * 57)
for name, freq, atm in [("Ku",14.0,0.3),("Ka",20.0,0.5),
                          ("Ka_up",30.0,0.8),("V",47.0,3.0)]:
    cn0, m = band_link(name, freq, 1000, 1.0, 20, 0.6, 300, atm, 93.4)  # 16-APSK 3/4 @ 208.3 Msym/s
    eirp = 10*math.log10(20) + gain_dbi(1.0, freq)
    gt   = gain_dbi(0.6, freq) - 10*math.log10(300)
    print(f"{name:>7}{freq:>7.1f}G{eirp:>8.1f}{fspl(1000,freq):>8.1f}"
          f"{gt:>8.1f}{cn0:>8.1f}{m:>+8.1f}")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 3: Band-Aware Link Budget

Extend the LinkBudget class with frequency-aware transmitter and receiver setup, and add a band_sweep() method.

import math

K_DB = -228.6; T0 = 290.0

def fspl(d, f): return 92.4 + 20*math.log10(d) + 20*math.log10(f)
def gain_dbi(D, f, eta=0.65):
    lam = 0.3/f
    return 10*math.log10(eta*(math.pi*D/lam)**2)
def nf_te(nf): return T0*(10**(nf/10)-1)
def loss_te(l, t=T0): return t*(10**(l/10)-1)
def dbl(x): return 10**(x/10)

class LinkBudget:
    """Stage 3: frequency-aware, supports band sweep."""
    def __init__(self, name):
        self.name=name; self.losses=[]; self._chain=[]
        self._eirp=self._fspl=self._grx=self._tant=self._req=None
        self._freq=None

    def set_transmitter(self, p, g): self._eirp=10*math.log10(p)+g
    def set_transmitter_aperture(self, p_w, D_m, freq_ghz, eta=0.65):
        self._eirp = 10*math.log10(p_w) + gain_dbi(D_m, freq_ghz, eta)
        self._freq = freq_ghz
    def set_path(self, d_km, freq_ghz=None):
        f = freq_ghz or self._freq
        self._fspl = fspl(d_km, f); self._freq = f
    def add_loss(self, lbl, v): self.losses.append((lbl,v))
    def set_antenna(self, g, tant): self._grx=g; self._tant=tant
    def set_antenna_aperture(self, D_m, freq_ghz, t_sys_k, eta=0.65):
        self._grx  = gain_dbi(D_m, freq_ghz, eta)
        self._tant = t_sys_k
        self._freq = freq_ghz
    def add_stage(self, lbl, te, g=None): self._chain.append((lbl,te,g))
    def set_required(self, r): self._req=r

    def _tsys(self):
        t=self._tant; cg=1.0
        for _,te,g in self._chain:
            t+=te/cg
            if g: cg*=dbl(g)
        return t

    def compute(self):
        t = self._tsys()
        total = self._fspl + sum(v for _,v in self.losses)
        cn0 = self._eirp - total + self._grx - 10*math.log10(t) - K_DB
        return cn0, cn0-self._req if self._req else None, t

    def band_sweep(self, d_km, D_tx_m, p_w, D_rx_m, t_sys_k, bands, req_cn0):
        print(f"\nBand sweep: {d_km}km, Tx D={D_tx_m}m {p_w}W, "
              f"Rx D={D_rx_m}m T={t_sys_k}K")
        print(f"{'Band':>8}{'Freq':>8}{'EIRP':>8}{'FSPL':>8}"
              f"{'G/T':>8}{'C/N₀':>8}{'Margin':>8}")
        print("-" * 58)
        for name, freq, atm in bands:
            eirp = 10*math.log10(p_w) + gain_dbi(D_tx_m, freq)
            loss = fspl(d_km, freq) + atm
            gt   = gain_dbi(D_rx_m, freq) - 10*math.log10(t_sys_k)
            cn0  = eirp - loss + gt - K_DB
            m    = cn0 - req_cn0
            print(f"{name:>8}{freq:>7.1f}G{eirp:>8.1f}{loss-atm:>8.1f}"
                  f"{gt:>8.1f}{cn0:>8.1f}{m:>+8.1f}")

lb = LinkBudget("Band sweep demo")
lb.band_sweep(
    d_km=1000, D_tx_m=1.0, p_w=20, D_rx_m=0.6, t_sys_k=300,
    bands=[
        ("Ku",    14.0, 0.3),
        ("Ka_dn", 20.0, 0.5),
        ("Ka_up", 30.0, 0.8),
        ("V",     47.0, 2.0),
    ],
    req_cn0=93.4   # 16-APSK 3/4 @ 208.3 Msym/s
)
