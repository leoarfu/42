Ground Tracks & Doppler

MODULE 2
ORBITAL MECHANICS

The previous two lessons described what a satellite does in space. This lesson describes what it looks like from the ground. Two questions govern the view from the surface: where does the satellite's ground track fall as the Earth rotates beneath it, and how does the satellite's motion shift the frequency of its signals? Both follow from the same geometry — satellite position, rotating Earth, observer location. The ground track determines coverage, revisit time, and gateway contact windows. The Doppler shift is a direct consequence of orbital speed and geometry, and it drives the frequency-management problem every LEO air interface must solve. The spine project propagator gains its first Earth-fixed output here: elevation angle, slant range, and Doppler shift to a fixed observer.

1. The Sub-Satellite Point and the Ground Track

The sub-satellite point (SSP) is the point on the Earth's surface directly below the satellite — the foot of the perpendicular from the satellite to the Earth's centre. As the satellite orbits and the Earth rotates, the SSP traces a path called the ground track. Coverage maps and revisit-time analyses are built from that path.

For a circular orbit in a fixed inertial plane, the SSP traces a sinusoidal path across the globe: latitude swings between +i and −i (where i is the inclination), and longitude shifts westward with each pass because the Earth has rotated eastward during the orbital period. The net westward longitude shift per orbit is:

ΔΩ_pass = (T_orbit / T_day) × 360° + dΩ/dt × T_orbit

The first term is Earth's rotation during the period; the second is J2 node regression. At 550 km and 53° inclination — T ≈ 95.6 min, Earth rotates ≈ 23.9° eastward per orbit, J2 shifts the node ≈ 1.04° westward — successive ascending passes fall about 24.9° west of the previous one. With 15.1 orbits per day the equatorial pass spacing is roughly 23.9°, covering the whole equatorial belt in a day.

The maximum latitude reached equals the inclination for prograde orbits (i ≤ 90°), or 180° − i for retrograde. A 53° shell reaches ±53° latitude; a 97.6° sun-synchronous orbit reaches ±82.4°; polar orbits cover all latitudes. Coverage gaps above the inclination are why high-inclination and polar constellations are chosen for global or near-global service.

2. Elevation Angle and the Geometry of a Pass

From a ground observer's perspective the satellite rises, passes overhead or at some maximum elevation, then sets. The elevation angle ε — the angle between the local horizontal and the line of sight to the satellite — captures this geometry. The central relation uses the Earth-central angle ρ between observer and SSP:

cos ρ = sin φ_obs · sin φ_ssp + cos φ_obs · cos φ_ssp · cos(λ_ssp − λ_obs)

The elevation angle then follows from:

tan ε = (cos ρ − R⊕/r) / sin ρ

where r is the orbital radius. The satellite is above the horizon when ε > 0°, which requires ρ < ρ_max where cos ρ_max = R⊕/r. At 550 km, ρ_max ≈ 59.0°, giving a maximum ground range of about 6,540 km from SSP to horizon — the coverage circle of one satellite.

In practice a minimum elevation constraint of 25°–40° is applied at Ka-band, because low-elevation signals traverse more atmosphere and suffer greater path loss and rain fade. At 550 km with ε_min = 35°, the coverage radius shrinks to about 2,700 km — significantly smaller, substantially increasing the number of satellites needed for continuous coverage.

3. Pass Duration and the Contact Window

The duration of a satellite pass over a fixed ground point depends on how close the ground track comes to the observer (the maximum elevation of the pass), the orbital altitude, and the minimum elevation constraint. A near-overhead pass gives the longest window and best geometry; a low-maximum-elevation pass is short and the link is poor.

For a circular orbit, the satellite is above ε_min for the arc of orbit subtending 2ρ_min at the Earth's centre, where ρ_min is the Earth-central angle corresponding to ε_min. It traverses that arc in time:

t_pass ≈ (T / π) × arcsin(sin ρ_min / sin ρ_max_track)

where ρ_max_track is the minimum ground range of the pass (the closest the ground track comes to the observer). Earth's rotation during the pass is a small correction for typical 5–10 minute passes and is omitted here.

Pass duration governs two distinct operational concerns. For a user terminal, a short pass means either a gap in service (if the next satellite is not yet above the threshold) or the coverage requirement that forces higher satellite density. For a ground station handling telemetry, command, or software uploads, the contact window is the total available time per day. Gateway siting and the ground segment network design — Module 8 — are both built on visibility window calculations that start from this geometry.

CONTINUOUS COVERAGE CONDITION A constellation provides continuous coverage when, at every point in the coverage region and at every moment in time, at least one satellite is above the minimum elevation angle. This requires the pass duration to exceed the gap to the next satellite's rise — which is determined by the satellite spacing in orbit and the number of orbital planes. The Walker lesson (m2-4) quantifies this exactly.

4. The Doppler Effect in LEO

A satellite moving at 7–8 km/s relative to a ground observer produces a large and rapidly varying Doppler shift on every signal it transmits and receives. The magnitude and sign depend on the range rate ṙ — the component of the satellite's velocity along the line of sight — and the Doppler shift in frequency is:

Δf = −(ṙ / c) × f₀

where c is the speed of light and f₀ the nominal carrier frequency. Positive ṙ (receding) gives a negative Δf; negative ṙ (approaching) gives a positive Δf — the signal shifts up when the satellite is approaching and down when it is receding.

For a direct-overhead pass at 550 km the Doppler shift starts strongly positive (satellite approaching), passes through zero at closest approach when the range rate is zero, and goes strongly negative as the satellite recedes. The total sweep takes the duration of the pass — 5–10 minutes — and the shift at the extremes reaches ±355 kHz at Ku-band (14 GHz) and ±672 kHz at Ka-band (26.5 GHz). These are large relative to typical modem subcarrier spacings and must be compensated.

5. Range Rate Calculation

The range rate ṙ is the time derivative of the slant range — the straight-line distance from satellite to observer. Given satellite position r_sat and observer position r_obs in the Earth-centred Earth-fixed (ECEF) frame, the slant range vector is ρ_vec = r_sat − r_obs, with magnitude ρ = |ρ_vec|. The range rate is the projection of the satellite velocity onto the unit range vector:

ṙ = (ρ_vec · v_sat) / ρ

The satellite velocity must be in ECEF, not ECI. The transformation from ECI to ECEF requires rotating by the Greenwich Sidereal Time angle, and in ECEF the velocity picks up an additional term from Earth's rotation: v_ECEF = v_ECI rotated − ω_Earth × r_ECEF. For a ground-fixed observer the observer's own ECEF velocity (due to Earth rotation) is up to ≈ 0.46 km/s at the equator, introducing a Doppler contribution of ≈ ±40 kHz at Ka-band — not negligible for a precision modem but manageable as a known systematic.

For a circular orbit in ECI the satellite speed is v = √(μ/r) and the velocity vector is perpendicular to the position vector in the orbital plane. Rotating to ECEF and projecting onto the unit range vector gives ṙ exactly. This is what the spine project Exercise 3 implements.

6. Doppler Compensation in the Air Interface

The Doppler shifts derived above drive fundamental choices in the satellite air interface that propagate into Modules 4 and 5.

Doppler pre-compensation is the standard approach for the uplink. The user terminal measures the satellite's instantaneous Doppler from its downlink signal, predicts the range rate using the satellite ephemeris, and offsets its transmit frequency so that the satellite receives the nominal centre frequency. This requires the terminal to update its frequency offset continuously — typically every fraction of a second — and to have access to the satellite's orbital state. Without pre-compensation, users across a beam would all arrive at the satellite on different frequencies, making coherent detection across the cell impractical.

Inter-beam Doppler gradient arises because a phased-array satellite generates many simultaneous spot beams, each pointing at a different look angle with a different range rate. If users in different beams have different Doppler offsets, the downlink must either allocate separate frequencies per beam or the air interface must be robust to a range of Doppler offsets within a single carrier. This constraint drives beam size and subcarrier spacing in OFDM-based systems — the subject of Module 5.

Doppler rate near closest approach reaches up to ~1.5 kHz/s. A modem's carrier-tracking loop must follow this without losing lock, which imposes a minimum loop bandwidth. The tradeoff with phase noise sensitivity means LEO modem tracking loops are significantly more aggressive than those designed for GEO, where the Doppler shift is almost static.

DOPPLER AS AN AIR-INTERFACE CONSTRAINT The ±672 kHz Ka-band sweep at 550 km is not a nuisance to filter away — it is a fundamental input to air-interface design. Subcarrier spacing, FFT size, guard intervals, and frequency-reference requirements all trace back to this number. Module 5 quantifies these tradeoffs.

7. The Geometry Unified

Elevation angle, slant range, and Doppler shift are not three separate quantities — they are all functions of the same geometric state: satellite position vector, observer position vector, and satellite velocity vector. Computing one gives the others.

The slant range ρ follows from the geometry triangle formed by Earth's centre, the satellite, and the observer: ρ = √(r² + R⊕² − 2rR⊕ cos ρ). The elevation angle follows from tan ε = (cos ρ − R⊕/r) / sin ρ. The range rate is the projection of the ECEF velocity onto the unit range vector. The Doppler shift is Δf = −ṙ/c × f₀. From one consistent state vector, all three follow.

This unification is operationally important. A link budget computed for a given elevation angle automatically implies a specific slant range (and therefore free-space path loss) and a specific Doppler offset. Treating them as independently chosen is an internal inconsistency. The link budget module (M4) builds on this: every row conditioned on elevation also has an implied range and Doppler state.

8. Visibility Windows and the Ground Station Problem

A ground station — gateway, TT&C facility, or tracking site — needs to know when each satellite is visible above its minimum elevation angle. For a single satellite, daily contact time is roughly the sum of all pass durations. For a large constellation the question inverts: there are so many satellites that at least one is usually above threshold, and the question becomes how many simultaneous contacts the station must manage and the maximum gap between contacts, if any.

Visibility windows are computed by propagating all satellite state vectors and evaluating elevation to the station at each time step. The interval of ε > ε_min is the contact window. For a high-inclination or polar constellation with several hundred planes, a well-placed gateway will have near-continuous visibility of multiple satellites. For a lower-inclination shell, gaps appear at latitudes above the inclination — a key architectural difference between Starlink's 53° shells (gaps at high latitudes) and OneWeb's 87.9° shell (near-global coverage from a single shell).

The full ground station network design — gateway siting, TT&C coverage, feeder link capacity — is Module 8. Here the point is that the elevation geometry computed in this lesson is the direct input to that design.

Exercise 1 — Elevation Angle and Pass Geometry

Compute elevation angle and slant range given satellite SSP and observer coordinates.

import math

RE = 6378.137   # km

def earth_central_angle(lat_obs, lon_obs, lat_ssp, lon_ssp):
    """Earth-central angle in degrees between observer and SSP."""
    phi_o = math.radians(lat_obs); lam_o = math.radians(lon_obs)
    phi_s = math.radians(lat_ssp); lam_s = math.radians(lon_ssp)
    cos_rho = (math.sin(phi_o)*math.sin(phi_s)
               + math.cos(phi_o)*math.cos(phi_s)*math.cos(lam_s - lam_o))
    cos_rho = max(-1.0, min(1.0, cos_rho))
    return math.degrees(math.acos(cos_rho))

def elevation_angle(rho_deg, orbital_radius_km):
    """Elevation angle in degrees given Earth-central angle and orbital radius."""
    rho = math.radians(rho_deg); r = orbital_radius_km
    tan_eps = (math.cos(rho) - RE/r) / math.sin(rho)
    return math.degrees(math.atan(tan_eps))

def slant_range(rho_deg, orbital_radius_km):
    """Slant range in km."""
    rho = math.radians(rho_deg); r = orbital_radius_km
    return math.sqrt(r**2 + RE**2 - 2*r*RE*math.cos(rho))

obs = (43.6, 1.4)   # Toulouse, France
alt = 550; r = RE + alt

print(f"Observer: {obs[0]}N {obs[1]}E   Altitude: {alt} km\n")
print(f"{'SSP lat':>8}{'SSP lon':>9}{'rho':>8}{'elev':>9}{'range (km)':>12}{'vis@35°':>9}")
print("-" * 58)
for lat_s, lon_s in [(43.6,1.4),(43.6,10.0),(43.6,20.0),
                      (53.0,1.4),(20.0,1.4),(0.0,1.4)]:
    rho  = earth_central_angle(*obs, lat_s, lon_s)
    elev = elevation_angle(rho, r)
    sr   = slant_range(rho, r)
    vis  = "YES" if elev >= 35 else "no"
    print(f"{lat_s:>8.1f}{lon_s:>9.1f}{rho:>7.1f}°{elev:>8.1f}°{sr:>11.0f} km{vis:>8}")

Exercise 2 — Doppler Shift Through a Pass

Compute the Doppler shift as a satellite sweeps from horizon to horizon over an observer directly below the ground track.

import math

MU = 398600.4418; RE = 6378.137; C = 299792.458   # km/s

def pass_doppler(altitude_km, freq_ghz=26.5, steps=200):
    """
    Doppler through a direct-overhead pass (max elevation = 90 deg).
    Returns list of (t_s, elev_deg, range_rate_km_s, doppler_khz).
    """
    r  = RE + altitude_km
    v  = math.sqrt(MU / r)       # orbital speed km/s
    n  = math.sqrt(MU / r**3)    # mean motion rad/s
    f0 = freq_ghz * 1e9          # Hz
    rho_max = math.acos(RE / r)  # horizon Earth-central angle, rad
    results = []
    for k in range(steps + 1):
        theta = -rho_max + 2*rho_max*k/steps  # rad, -rho_max to +rho_max
        t     = theta / n                      # s from overhead
        sr    = math.sqrt(r**2 + RE**2 - 2*r*RE*math.cos(theta))
        if abs(theta) < 1e-9:
            elev = 90.0
        else:
            sin_t = math.sin(abs(theta))
            tan_e = (math.cos(theta) - RE/r) / sin_t
            elev  = math.degrees(math.atan(tan_e))
        rr  = r*RE*math.sin(theta)*n / sr    # range rate km/s (+ve = receding)
        dop = -(rr / C) * f0                  # Hz
        results.append((t, elev, rr, dop/1000))
    return results

data = pass_doppler(550, freq_ghz=26.5)
print("Ka-band (26.5 GHz) — 550 km — direct overhead pass\n")
print(f"{'t (s)':>8}{'elev':>9}{'rr (km/s)':>12}{'Doppler (kHz)':>15}")
print("-" * 46)
indices = [0, len(data)//4, len(data)//2, 3*len(data)//4, -1]
for idx in indices:
    t, e, rr, dk = data[idx]
    print(f"{t:>8.0f}{e:>8.1f}°{rr:>12.4f}{dk:>14.1f}")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 3: Visibility and Doppler from the J2 Propagator

Integrate elevation angle and Doppler shift into the J2 propagator, outputting contact windows with full link geometry at each step.

import math

MU          = 398600.4418
RE          = 6378.137
J2          = 1.08263e-3
EARTH_ROT_S = 7.2921150e-5   # rad/s
C           = 299792.458      # km/s

def propagate_with_visibility(altitude_km, inc_deg,
                               obs_lat, obs_lon,
                               duration_min, step_s=30,
                               min_elev_deg=10.0, freq_ghz=26.5):
    """
    J2 propagator with elevation, slant range, and Doppler to a fixed observer.
    Returns list of contact-step dicts where satellite is above min_elev_deg.
    """
    r  = RE + altitude_km
    n  = math.sqrt(MU / r**3)
    i  = math.radians(inc_deg)
    nr = -(3/2)*n*J2*(RE/r)**2*math.cos(i)   # node rate rad/s
    v  = math.sqrt(MU / r)                    # orbital speed km/s
    f0 = freq_ghz * 1e9

    phi_o = math.radians(obs_lat); lam_o = math.radians(obs_lon)
    obs_e = (RE*math.cos(phi_o)*math.cos(lam_o),
             RE*math.cos(phi_o)*math.sin(lam_o),
             RE*math.sin(phi_o))

    contacts = []; t = 0.0
    while t <= duration_min * 60:
        raan = nr * t; theta = n * t
        xo = r*math.cos(theta); yo = r*math.sin(theta)
        xi = xo*math.cos(raan) - yo*math.cos(i)*math.sin(raan)
        yi = xo*math.sin(raan) + yo*math.cos(i)*math.cos(raan)
        zi = yo*math.sin(i)
        vxo = -v*math.sin(theta); vyo = v*math.cos(theta)
        vxi = vxo*math.cos(raan) - vyo*math.cos(i)*math.sin(raan)
        vyi = vxo*math.sin(raan) + vyo*math.cos(i)*math.cos(raan)
        vzi = vyo*math.sin(i)
        gst = EARTH_ROT_S * t
        cg = math.cos(gst); sg = math.sin(gst)
        xe = xi*cg + yi*sg;  ye = -xi*sg + yi*cg;  ze = zi
        vxe = vxi*cg + vyi*sg - EARTH_ROT_S*ye
        vye = -vxi*sg + vyi*cg + EARTH_ROT_S*xe
        vze = vzi
        lat_s = math.degrees(math.asin(ze/r))
        lon_s = math.degrees(math.atan2(ye, xe))
        phi_s = math.radians(lat_s); lam_s = math.radians(lon_s)
        cos_r = max(-1.0, min(1.0,
            math.sin(phi_o)*math.sin(phi_s)
            + math.cos(phi_o)*math.cos(phi_s)*math.cos(lam_s - lam_o)))
        rho = math.acos(cos_r); sin_r = math.sin(rho)
        if sin_r < 1e-9:
            elev = 90.0
        else:
            elev = math.degrees(math.atan((math.cos(rho) - RE/r) / sin_r))
        if elev >= min_elev_deg:
            dx = xe-obs_e[0]; dy = ye-obs_e[1]; dz = ze-obs_e[2]
            sr = math.sqrt(dx**2+dy**2+dz**2)
            rr = (dx*vxe + dy*vye + dz*vze) / sr
            dop = -(rr/C)*f0
            contacts.append({"t_min": t/60, "elev": elev,
                              "range_km": sr, "doppler_khz": dop/1000})
        t += step_s
    return contacts

# Toulouse observer, 6-hour window, 10-deg min elevation
ctcts = propagate_with_visibility(550, 53, 43.6, 1.4,
                                   360, step_s=30,
                                   min_elev_deg=10.0, freq_ghz=26.5)
print(f"Steps above 10° elev in 6 hr: {len(ctcts)}")
if ctcts:
    best = max(ctcts, key=lambda x: x["elev"])
    print(f"Peak elev {best['elev']:.1f}° at t={best['t_min']:.1f} min  "
          f"range={best['range_km']:.0f} km  "
          f"Doppler={best['doppler_khz']:.1f} kHz")
    print(f"\n{'t (min)':>9}{'elev':>8}{'range(km)':>11}{'Doppler(kHz)':>14}")
    print("-" * 44)
    stride = max(1, len(ctcts)//8)
    for c in ctcts[::stride]:
        print(f"{c['t_min']:>9.1f}{c['elev']:>8.1f}°"
              f"{c['range_km']:>10.0f} {c['doppler_khz']:>13.1f}")
