Orbital Regimes Compared

MODULE 1
FOUNDATIONS

Altitude is the first and most consequential decision an architect makes, because almost everything else follows from it — how many satellites you need, how fast the link is, how strong the signal is, how hard the radiation hits, how long the satellite lives, and how much it costs to get there. This lesson compares the orbital regimes along every axis that matters. It is a comparison, not a derivation: the orbital mechanics behind these behaviours are the next module, the latency physics the next lesson, and link budgets the RF module. Here the goal is the map and the tradeoffs it forces.

1. The Altitude Map

Orbits are grouped into regimes by altitude, and the boundaries are not arbitrary — each is set by physics. From the ground up:

Very Low Earth Orbit (VLEO) sits roughly below 450 km, with the most aggressive proposals under 300 km. Its lower edge is set by atmospheric drag: below a few hundred kilometres the residual atmosphere is thick enough that a satellite decays in weeks to months without propulsion.

Low Earth Orbit (LEO) spans roughly 200 to 2,000 km. This is where the broadband mega-constellations live — Starlink's main shell at 550 km, OneWeb at 1,200 km, Kuiper near 600 km — along with Earth-observation satellites and the ISS at about 400 km. Its upper edge butts against the inner radiation belt.

Medium Earth Orbit (MEO) spans 2,000 km up to just below geostationary altitude. It is shaped internally by the radiation belts: the navigation constellations sit around 19,000–23,000 km, while O3b's data constellation deliberately sits near 8,000 km in the relatively benign gap between the belts.

Geostationary Orbit (GEO) is a single special altitude, 35,786 km above the equator, where the orbital period equals one sidereal day so the satellite appears fixed in the sky. This is the home of broadcast, weather, and traditional fixed satellite services.

Highly Elliptical Orbit (HEO) is the exception to the altitude-band logic: a deliberately eccentric orbit, the classic example being Molniya, with a low perigee and an apogee near 40,000 km, used to dwell over high latitudes that GEO cannot reach.

2. The Master Tradeoff

Every regime comparison reduces to one tension: altitude buys coverage per satellite, and costs you almost everything else.

Go higher and a single satellite sees a larger slice of the Earth, so you need fewer of them, and at GEO the satellite stops moving across the sky so the user terminal can be a fixed dish. But the same distance that widens the footprint also lengthens the round trip to hundreds of milliseconds, weakens the signal, and — at MEO — drops you into the radiation belts.

Go lower and the round trip collapses to tens of milliseconds, the signal is far stronger so the terminal can be small, and below the inner belt the radiation environment is benign. But the footprint shrinks, so you need hundreds or thousands of satellites for continuous coverage, the terminal must track fast-moving targets, and atmospheric drag starts fighting you.

3. Coverage Geometry

How much of the Earth one satellite can serve depends on its altitude and the minimum elevation angle at which a terminal can still close the link. Higher means a wider footprint and fewer satellites; lower means a narrow footprint and many.

A single GEO satellite is geometrically visible from roughly a third of the Earth's surface, up to about 81° latitude in theory and closer to 70° in practice once a usable elevation angle is required. Three GEO satellites spaced 120° apart give near-global coverage — with one critical exception: the poles. Because GEO sits over the equator, it cannot see high latitudes at all, which is why polar regions have always been a GEO blind spot.

MEO footprints are smaller but still large. The GNSS constellations use on the order of 24 to 30 satellites so that at least four are visible from anywhere for positioning. O3b reaches near-global non-polar data coverage with roughly twenty satellites.

LEO is where the satellite count explodes. A single LEO satellite's footprint is small and it crosses the sky in minutes, so continuous coverage demands dozens for a single-coverage service — Iridium used 66 at 780 km for global voice — and thousands when the goal is broadband capacity rather than mere coverage, which is why Starlink is counted in the thousands. The count rises steeply as altitude drops, because footprint area falls roughly with the square of how close you are.

4. Latency by Regime

Latency is set by distance and the finite speed of light, so it tracks altitude directly. The values below are for comparison; the derivation and the full end-to-end budget are the subject of the next lesson.

A GEO round trip — up and back down, and again for the return path — runs to roughly 500–600 ms once ground handling is included, enough to be felt in any interactive use. MEO at 8,000 km brings the round trip down to roughly 100–150 ms. LEO at 550 km collapses it to roughly 20–50 ms, comparable to terrestrial broadband, which is the entire commercial point of LEO. VLEO shaves a little more off LEO but the marginal latency gain is small; VLEO is chosen for other reasons.

~100–150 msMEO (~8,000 km)

5. Link Strength & Terminal Implications

Signal strength falls with the square of distance, so a closer satellite delivers a far stronger signal for the same transmit power. The practical consequence is the user terminal. A GEO link, spanning 35,786 km, needs a high-gain dish to close; a LEO link a tenth as far needs far less aperture, which is part of why a flat panel can serve LEO. The full link-budget mathematics belongs to the RF module; the regime-level point is that lower orbits permit smaller, cheaper terminals, and higher orbits demand larger ones.

6. The Radiation Environment

Radiation is the axis most often underestimated, and it is the main reason MEO is a harder place to operate than either LEO or GEO. The Earth is wrapped in the Van Allen belts, regions where the magnetic field traps energetic charged particles, and where a satellite sits relative to them drives how much shielding and radiation-hardening it needs — which drives its cost.

The inner belt, dominated by high-energy protons, extends from roughly 1,000 km out to about 6,000 km. Above it lies a relatively calm slot region from about 6,000 to 13,000 km. The outer belt, dominated by energetic electrons, runs from roughly 13,000 km to 60,000 km, most intense around 16,000–20,000 km.

This geography explains the regimes. LEO below about 1,000 km sits beneath the inner belt and enjoys a benign environment, which is exactly what lets mega-constellations use cheaper commercial-grade electronics. MEO is the hard case: the navigation constellations at 19,000–23,000 km sit squarely in the outer belt and must be heavily hardened, whereas O3b's choice of roughly 8,000 km is no accident — it threads the calm slot region between the belts. GEO sits beyond the most intense belts but still in an energetic-electron environment that causes spacecraft surface charging, a different hazard requiring its own mitigation.

One LEO caveat completes the picture: the South Atlantic Anomaly, a region where the inner belt dips down to around 200 km over the South Atlantic, exposing otherwise-benign LEO satellites to elevated radiation each time they pass through it.

WHY MEO IS THE HARD CASE It is the one comms regime that sits inside a radiation belt by necessity. O3b's 8,000 km slot choice and the navigation constellations' heavy hardening are two different answers to the same belt problem.

7. Atmospheric Drag, Orbital Lifetime & Debris

The lower atmosphere does not stop cleanly at a fixed altitude; thin residual gas extends well into LEO and exerts drag that slowly lowers an orbit. How strongly depends sharply on altitude, and it creates a tradeoff with a sustainability dimension that has become central to the industry.

At the ISS altitude of about 400 km, drag is strong enough that the station would re-enter within a year or two without periodic reboosts. At 550 km, a satellite naturally decays over roughly five years once it stops station-keeping — which is why Starlink's main shell is considered relatively self-cleaning. At 1,200 km, the same satellite would take centuries to come down, which is why OneWeb at that altitude must actively deorbit its satellites rather than rely on decay. VLEO is the extreme: below 300 km a satellite needs continuous propulsion just to stay up, and decays in months if it stops.

This produces a genuine architectural tension. Lower orbits demand propulsion and accept shorter lifetimes, but they self-clean: failed satellites and debris re-enter on their own, keeping the environment manageable. Higher LEO buys long satellite life and no drag penalty, but anything left there — a dead satellite, a fragment — persists for centuries, accumulating as a collision hazard. The full treatment of debris, conjunction, and disposal rules is in the resilience module; the regime-level point is that altitude is also a debris decision.

8. Eclipse, Thermal Cycling & Power

A satellite is powered by sunlight on its solar panels and by batteries when the Earth eclipses the Sun. How often that happens depends on the orbit, and it shapes the satellite's power and thermal design.

A LEO satellite orbits in about 90 to 100 minutes, so it makes 15 to 16 orbits a day and is eclipsed on most of them — thousands of charge-and-discharge cycles a year. Battery wear from that cycling is a real life-limiter for LEO satellites and a driver of their sizing. The same rapid passage in and out of sunlight imposes thermal cycling on the structure and electronics.

GEO is the opposite. A geostationary satellite is in continuous sunlight for most of the year and is eclipsed only during two roughly 45-day seasons around the equinoxes, for up to about 70 minutes a day — on the order of 90 eclipse cycles a year rather than thousands. Its batteries live far easier lives, though each eclipse is longer.

9. Doppler & Dynamics

Because a LEO satellite moves fast relative to the ground, its signal carries a large Doppler shift that the system must track and compensate, and it crosses the sky quickly enough to force continuous handover between satellites. A GEO satellite, fixed relative to the ground, has effectively no Doppler and never hands over. MEO sits between. The mechanics of this motion are the next module's subject and the handover and Doppler-compensation machinery is covered in the networking and air-interface modules; the regime-level point is simply that lower orbits trade a fixed, quiet link for a fast, dynamic one.

10. Getting There: Launch Cost by Regime

Altitude also sets how hard the satellite is to launch, because reaching a higher orbit takes more energy. LEO is the cheapest destination. GEO is among the most expensive: a launch must first reach a transfer orbit and then circularise at altitude, requiring substantially more delta-v, and the satellite often carries its own propulsion to finish the job. MEO falls between. This feeds straight into the economics — a GEO satellite is not only more expensive to build and harden, it is more expensive to place — and is part of why the cost-per-delivered-capacity comparison between regimes is not as simple as comparing satellite counts.

11. Regime Profiles & Use Cases

GEO — One satellite covers a third of the planet and never moves in the sky, making it ideal for one-to-many broadcast, weather imaging, and fixed services where latency does not matter. Its weaknesses are latency, the polar blind spot, and the cost of building, hardening, and launching a large satellite.

MEO — A middle ground used where its specific geometry pays off. The navigation constellations live here because the altitude gives good global geometry with a manageable satellite count; O3b uses the benign slot region for lower-latency data than GEO without LEO's satellite count.

LEO — Low latency, strong signal, small terminals, benign radiation, self-cleaning at the lower end — at the price of needing hundreds to thousands of satellites and fighting drag. This is the regime of the broadband mega-constellations, most Earth observation, and crewed spaceflight.

VLEO — Pushes LEO's advantages further: even lower latency, even stronger signal, sharper Earth-observation imagery, and rapid self-cleaning. The price is constant propulsion against drag and short lifetimes. It is mostly the domain of high-resolution imaging and experimental systems, with growing interest for communications.

HEO — The specialist. By dwelling for hours near a high apogee, a Molniya-type orbit covers high-latitude regions that GEO cannot, historically used for high-latitude communications and early-warning missions.

Three exercises grounding the regime comparison — radiation geography, coverage scaling, and the drag-versus-debris tradeoff. Python 3.8+ standard library only.

Exercise 1 — Altitude classifier with radiation flag

Classify an altitude into its regime and flag its radiation exposure, encoding the belt geography from this lesson.

def classify(altitude_km):
    if altitude_km < 450:      regime = "VLEO"
    elif altitude_km < 2000:   regime = "LEO"
    elif altitude_km < 35786:  regime = "MEO"
    elif abs(altitude_km - 35786) < 100: regime = "GEO"
    else:                      regime = "above GEO"

    if 1000 <= altitude_km <= 6000:
        rad = "INNER BELT — proton-hardening required"
    elif 6000 < altitude_km < 13000:
        rad = "slot region — relatively benign"
    elif 13000 <= altitude_km <= 60000 and regime != "GEO":
        rad = "OUTER BELT — electron-hardening required"
    elif regime == "GEO":
        rad = "energetic electrons — surface charging risk"
    else:
        rad = "benign (watch the South Atlantic Anomaly in LEO)"
    return regime, rad

for alt in [350, 550, 1200, 8000, 20200, 35786]:
    regime, rad = classify(alt)
    print(f"{alt:>6} km -> {regime:<10} | {rad}")

# 1. Which two altitudes share the harshest radiation verdict, and why?
# 2. The classifier treats LEO as benign. What real exception did the lesson name?

Exercise 2 — Footprint and satellites for a coverage ring

Estimate how the satellite count for continuous coverage scales as altitude drops, using simple geometry. (Real constellation design is the next module; this is only the floor.)

import math
R_E = 6371.0  # km

def central_half_angle(altitude_km, min_elev_deg):
    el = math.radians(min_elev_deg)
    return math.acos(R_E * math.cos(el) / (R_E + altitude_km)) - el

def sats_floor(altitude_km, min_elev_deg=25):
    rho = central_half_angle(altitude_km, min_elev_deg)
    cap = 2 * math.pi * (1 - math.cos(rho))   # steradians
    return (4 * math.pi) / cap

print(f"{'Altitude':>10}{'Half-angle':>14}{'~Sats (floor)':>16}")
print("-" * 40)
for alt in [350, 550, 1200, 8000, 20200, 35786]:
    rho = math.degrees(central_half_angle(alt, 25))
    print(f"{alt:>8} km {rho:>11.1f} deg {sats_floor(alt):>14.0f}")

# 1. By what factor does the count grow from GEO down to 550 km?
# 2. Why is the real Starlink count far higher than this floor?
