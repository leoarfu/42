Routing
Under Churn

MODULE 7
NETWORKING, ISL & ROUTING
CORE

The mesh of m7-3 is a graph that never holds still. This lesson routes on it. The routing problem itself is elementary — a 1,584-node grid with four edges per node yields a shortest path in microseconds by Dijkstra, a textbook computation — and that is exactly what makes the lesson's real point sharp: the difficulty is not computing a path, it is that the graph the path lives on has already changed by the time a conventional protocol would have agreed on it. Terrestrial routing protocols discover topology by probing and converge on paths by flooding and negotiation, a process that takes seconds for OSPF and tens of seconds to minutes for BGP. A LEO mesh changes some link's state on the order of once a second across the whole constellation, and any individual inter-plane link hands off every few minutes. A protocol that needs thirty seconds to converge on a network that reconfigures every second is not slow — it is broken, permanently chasing a topology that has moved on.

This lesson establishes the routing metrics, computes representative paths (a transcontinental route is ~3 intra-plane hops and ~20 ms), shows precisely why the terrestrial protocols fail — not from inadequate engineering but from a category mismatch between reactive convergence and continuous deterministic change — and introduces the constellation-specific answer: stop reacting, start scheduling. Because the churn is deterministic (m7-3 §6), the topology's entire future is known, and routing becomes a problem of precomputing paths for a known sequence of graphs rather than discovering paths on an unknown one. The spine gains its router core here; m7-5 builds the control architecture that distributes the precomputed result.

1. The routing problem, stated

A packet enters the mesh at one satellite (above its source terminal) and must leave at another (above its destination terminal or a gateway). Between them lies the +grid, and the router must choose a sequence of ISL hops. Formally it is single-source shortest path on a weighted graph — Dijkstra's algorithm, or any of its relatives — and the weight on each edge is where the engineering lives:

  • Hop count — minimise the number of satellites traversed. Simple, but ignores that a long intra-plane hop (1,970 km) and a short inter-plane hop (600 km) count equally while costing very different propagation.
  • Latency — minimise total propagation plus per-hop processing. The physically honest metric for a latency-selling constellation (m7-1), and the one the spine defaults to.
  • Congestion / load — weight edges by their current traffic, steering packets around hot links. Essential under load, but it makes the weights depend on traffic, which changes far faster than the topology.

The metrics conflict: the minimum-hop path and the minimum-latency path and the least-congested path are generally three different routes, and a real router blends them. But all three are cheap to compute on this graph — the entire difficulty is temporal, not algorithmic, and §3 is where that bites.

2. What a path looks like

A representative long-haul route makes the numbers concrete. London to New York is 5,570 km on the ground; across the mesh it is roughly three intra-plane hops (1,970 km each carries the packet along-track) plus the inter-plane hops needed to shift longitude, and the propagation is dominated by the near-straight-line distance at vacuum c:

~18.6 ms propagation + ~3 hops × 0.5 ms processing ≈ 20 ms one way

Two features generalise. First, processing delay is small but not negligible: each regenerative hop demodulates, reads the header, and re-transmits, costing a fraction of a millisecond, and on a many-hop path these accumulate into a real term that trades against propagation (a path with fewer, longer hops beats one with more, shorter hops even at equal distance). Second, the path is a grid route, not a great circle: the +grid only offers along-track and cross-track moves (m7-3), so a packet travels an L1 / "Manhattan" distance through the lattice, slightly longer than the great circle, and the router's choice of when to hop sideways versus stay in-plane shapes both the length and the congestion. The mesh is a Manhattan grid wrapped on a sphere, and routing it is city-block navigation with the blocks in motion.

3. Why terrestrial routing fails

Now the lesson's core. The internet's routing protocols were built for a network whose topology is mostly stable and changes unpredictably — a fibre gets cut, a router reboots, and the protocol must discover the change and re-converge. Two dominate:

OSPF (interior routing) floods link-state advertisements when topology changes, and every router recomputes shortest paths. Convergence takes seconds — acceptable when changes are rare. On a mesh where links change state continuously, OSPF would flood advertisements without pause and recompute perpetually, its convergence never catching up to the change rate. The flooding alone would consume the very ISL capacity it is trying to route.

BGP (inter-domain routing) is worse: it explores paths through iterative announcement and withdrawal, damped by timers (the MRAI, ~30 seconds) specifically designed to slow reaction and prevent instability. Convergence takes tens of seconds to minutes. Against orbital churn this is hopeless — BGP would still be exploring the consequences of one handoff when the next several had already occurred.

The failure is quantitative and decisive. Across the constellation, link states change on the order of once a second; any individual inter-plane link hands off every few minutes; and a conventional protocol needs seconds to tens of seconds to converge on a single change. The convergence time exceeds the inter-change time by one to two orders of magnitude. This is not a tuning problem — no timer setting rescues a reactive protocol whose fundamental loop is slower than the change it reacts to. The category is wrong: reactive convergence assumes changes are events to be discovered and absorbed; constellation topology change is a continuous process with a known trajectory. You cannot react your way through a river; you must know its course.

4. The escape: determinism

The property m7-3 established is the way out. Constellation churn is deterministic — every link's state at every future instant follows from the ephemeris, computable minutes or hours ahead. This changes the problem's nature entirely. A terrestrial router faces an unknown topology it must discover and an unpredictable future it must react to. A constellation router faces a known topology and a known future — it does not need to discover anything, because it can compute the entire sequence of graphs in advance and precompute a path for each.

The reframing: routing a constellation is not "find the path on this graph" (discovered, reactive) but "given the known sequence of graphs G(t₀), G(t₁), G(t₂), …, precompute the path table for each and switch tables on schedule" (computed, proactive). The churn that destroys reactive protocols is harmless to a proactive one, because the proactive router was never surprised — it knew the handoff was coming, computed the post-handoff paths before it happened, and switched to them at the scheduled instant. The seam, the polar shutdown, the inter-plane breathing — all of it is on the schedule. This is topology-scheduled or snapshot routing, and it is the constellation-specific answer the rest of the module builds on.

5. Snapshot routing in practice

The proactive approach discretises time into intervals within which the topology is treated as fixed. For each snapshot interval:

1. Predict the topology from the ephemeris — which links exist, at what range and capacity (m7-2, m7-3). 2. Compute the routing tables for that fixed graph — ordinary shortest-path, cheap. 3. Distribute the tables to the satellites ahead of the interval (m7-5's control plane). 4. Switch all satellites to the new tables at the interval boundary, synchronously.

The snapshot interval is the key parameter. Too long, and the topology drifts significantly within an interval — the "fixed" graph is a lie by the interval's end, and paths degrade. Too short, and the control plane drowns in table updates. The interval is chosen so that intra-interval topology change is tolerable — seconds to tens of seconds, matched to the churn rate — and the deterministic ephemeris makes each interval's table computable well ahead of need. The handoffs between snapshots are exactly the make-before-break events m6-5 handled for terminals, now between satellites: the new path is ready before the old topology expires, and the switch is seamless.

The residual problem snapshot routing does not solve is congestion, because traffic — unlike topology — is not deterministic. The precomputed tables give good paths for the geometry; when a link congests from a traffic surge, the schedule did not predict it. Real systems layer a fast, local, reactive congestion response (reroute around a hot link) on top of the slow, global, proactive topology schedule — the two timescales handled by two mechanisms. Geometry is scheduled; traffic is reacted to. This division is the practical shape of constellation routing.

6. Failure and the reactive floor

Determinism has a limit: it assumes links behave as geometry predicts, and sometimes they do not. A satellite maneuvers for collision avoidance (Module 2's debris environment) and leaves its predicted position; an ISL terminal fails and takes a scheduled link off the map; a satellite goes briefly attitude-unstable and drops its beams. These are unscheduled changes — the very kind reactive protocols exist to handle — and they mean a constellation cannot be purely proactive. It needs a reactive floor: a fast local mechanism that detects a link's unexpected loss and reroutes around it until the next snapshot's tables (recomputed to account for the failure) take over.

The architecture that results is layered by timescale and predictability: proactive scheduling for the deterministic geometric churn (the common case, handled ahead of time), and reactive rerouting for the unpredictable failures (the rare case, handled locally and fast). This is the opposite of the terrestrial internet, which is reactive-first because its changes are unpredictable-first. The constellation inverts the priority because its dominant change source — orbital motion — is knowable, relegating reaction to a fallback for the exceptions. Getting the balance right, and sizing the reactive floor for the real failure rate, is the routing system's central engineering judgement.

8. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

Stage 4 of the spine is the router core: given a topology snapshot from Stage 3, it computes shortest paths under a chosen metric (latency by default, hop count or congestion optionally), returns the path and its latency for any satellite pair, and — crucially — accepts a sequence of snapshots and produces a sequence of path tables with the handoff instants between them. It also carries the reactive hook: mark a link failed, and it reroutes on the current snapshot without waiting for the next. The router now works on the churning graph, by refusing to be surprised by the churn.

What Stage 4 computes locally, m7-5 distributes globally. A path table is only useful on the satellite that holds it, and getting the right tables to 1,584 moving satellites ahead of each snapshot boundary — synchronised, verified, and updated as the schedule advances — is a control-plane problem at constellation scale. The deterministic topology that made routing tractable also makes the control plane tractable, through the same mechanism: precompute from the ephemeris. That is SDN in space, and it is m7-5.

Exercise 1 — Route the grid

Shortest path on a snapshot, three metrics.

import heapq, math

def dijkstra(graph, src, dst):
    """graph: {node: [(neighbor, weight), ...]}. Returns (cost, path)."""
    pq = [(0, src, [src])]
    seen = set()
    while pq:
        cost, u, path = heapq.heappop(pq)
        if u == dst: return cost, path
        if u in seen: continue
        seen.add(u)
        for v, w in graph.get(u, []):
            if v not in seen:
                heapq.heappush(pq, (cost+w, v, path+[v]))
    return math.inf, []

# Tiny +grid: 4 planes × 4 sats, node = (plane, slot)
INTRA_KM, INTER_KM, C = 1970, 600, 299792.458
PROC_MS = 0.5

def build(metric="latency"):
    g = {}
    P = S = 4
    for p in range(P):
        for s in range(S):
            u = (p, s); g[u] = []
            for v in [(p, (s+1)%S), (p, (s-1)%S)]:      # intra
                w = 1 if metric=="hops" else INTRA_KM/C*1000 + PROC_MS
                g[u].append((v, w))
            for v in [((p+1)%P, s), ((p-1)%P, s)]:      # inter
                w = 1 if metric=="hops" else INTER_KM/C*1000 + PROC_MS
                g[u].append((v, w))
    return g

for metric in ["hops", "latency"]:
    cost, path = dijkstra(build(metric), (0,0), (2,3))
    print(f"{metric:>8}: cost {cost:.2f}, {len(path)-1} hops, path {path}")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 4: the router core

Snapshot routing with a reactive floor.

import heapq, math

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M7 SPINE PROJECT — CONSTELLATION ROUTER
# Stage 4: the router core.
#   Stage 3 (m7-3) produced time-varying topology snapshots.
#   Stage 4: shortest-path routing per snapshot + a sequence
#            of path tables with handoff instants + a reactive
#            reroute for unscheduled link failures.
#   Next: m7-5 distributes these tables to the satellites (SDN);
#         m7-6 turns paths into end-to-end latency for transport.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class Router:
    C = 299792.458
    PROC_MS = 0.5
    def __init__(self, metric="latency"):
        self.metric = metric

    def _weight(self, range_km):
        if self.metric == "hops": return 1.0
        return range_km/self.C*1000 + self.PROC_MS   # latency ms

    def shortest_path(self, graph, src, dst, failed=frozenset()):
        """graph: {node:[(nbr, range_km)]}. failed: set of (u,v) edges down."""
        pq = [(0.0, src, [src])]; seen = set()
        while pq:
            cost, u, path = heapq.heappop(pq)
            if u == dst: return cost, path
            if u in seen: continue
            seen.add(u)
            for v, rng in graph.get(u, []):
                if v in seen or (u, v) in failed or (v, u) in failed:
                    continue
                heapq.heappush(pq, (cost + self._weight(rng), v, path+[v]))
        return math.inf, []

    def route_sequence(self, snapshots, src, dst):
        """snapshots: list of (t, graph). Returns table per snapshot +
        the handoff instants where the path changes."""
        tables, last_path = [], None
        for t, graph in snapshots:
            cost, path = self.shortest_path(graph, src, dst)
            handoff = (path != last_path)
            tables.append(dict(t=t, cost=round(cost,2), path=path,
                               handoff=handoff))
            last_path = path
        return tables

    def reactive_reroute(self, graph, src, dst, failed_edge):
        """A link failed mid-snapshot: reroute NOW without waiting."""
        return self.shortest_path(graph, src, dst, failed={failed_edge})

# tiny grid factory (range-weighted)
def grid(P=4, S=4, intra=1970, inter=600):
    g = {}
    for p in range(P):
        for s in range(S):
            u=(p,s)
            g[u]=[((p,(s+1)%S),intra),((p,(s-1)%S),intra),
                  (((p+1)%P,s),inter),(((p-1)%P,s),inter)]
    return g

r = Router("latency")
G = grid()
cost, path = r.shortest_path(G, (0,0), (2,3))
print(f"nominal: {cost:.2f} ms, path {path}")

# simulate a 3-snapshot sequence (topology nudged each step)
snaps = [(0, grid()), (10, grid(inter=800)), (20, grid(inter=1200))]
for row in r.route_sequence(snaps, (0,0), (2,3)):
    tag = " <- HANDOFF" if row['handoff'] else ""
    print(f"  t={row['t']:>2}s: {row['cost']:>6.2f} ms  {row['path']}{tag}")

# reactive: fail the first hop's edge
cost2, path2 = r.reactive_reroute(G, (0,0), (2,3), ((0,0),(0,1)))
print(f"after edge (0,0)-(0,1) fails: {cost2:.2f} ms, path {path2}")

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 01

Section 5 split the routing system into scheduled geometry and reacted-to traffic, on two timescales. But the boundary is not clean: a scheduled topology handoff causes a traffic shift (packets that took the old path now take the new one), which can cause congestion the schedule did not anticipate. Trace this coupling: does the deterministic topology schedule make congestion more predictable (because traffic follows known paths) or less (because handoffs cause synchronised traffic shifts across the whole constellation)? Decide whether the two timescales are truly separable or whether the schedule must anticipate the congestion its own handoffs create.

PROMPT 02

Section 3 argued no timer tuning rescues a reactive protocol whose loop is slower than the change rate. But a hybrid is conceivable: run a reactive protocol only on the stable part of the topology (intra-plane links, which never change) and schedule only the volatile part (inter-plane links). Evaluate this: does separating the graph into a stable sub-graph and a volatile sub-graph let a terrestrial protocol survive on the stable part, and is the resulting hybrid simpler or more complex than pure snapshot routing? Identify what fraction of the routing problem the stable sub-graph actually covers.

PROMPT 03

Section 6's reactive floor must cover unscheduled failures, and its size depends on the failure rate. But there is a feedback: a larger constellation has more links (more to fail) but also more alternate paths (more resilience). Work the scaling: as a constellation grows from hundreds to thousands of satellites, does the reactive floor need to grow, shrink, or hold constant as a fraction of the routing system? Connect the answer to m7-3's observation that the mesh is denser (more redundant) at some latitudes than others, and decide whether the reactive floor should be uniform or latitude-dependent.

PROMPT 04

This lesson's central quantitative claim — reactive convergence is 10–100× too slow — rests on a churn estimate (≈1 link-change/second) built from an order-of-magnitude handoff rate (6 per satellite per orbit), explicitly flagged as an estimate. The conclusion is robust (exercise 2 question 3 shows it holds from 2 to 20 handoffs/orbit), but the specific interval that sets the snapshot period is not. Apply the load-bearing-assumption discipline: which downstream design decision (snapshot interval, control-plane update bandwidth in m7-5, transport RTT-variation in m7-6) is most sensitive to the exact churn rate, and specify the Module 2 + Module 7 computation that would replace the order-estimate with a real number before that decision is locked.
