01 Why LEO Now — The Enabling Conditions → LESSON ›
The Four Enablers
  • Smartphone supply chain — MEMS, Li-ion, CMOS cameras, commodity RF chips drove unit cost from $500k → $500k → sub-$1M → $1k range
  • Reusable launch — Falcon 9 booster reuse cut cost/kg to LEO by ~10×; Starship targets another 10×
  • Flat-panel phased-array terminal — electronically steered, no moving parts, mass-producible; replaced 60cm dish + motor
  • Cheap capital + software ops — autonomous satellite operations; machine-learning scheduling; low headcount per satellite
Why the 1990s Failed
  • Iridium / Teledesic — same concept, $5B–$9B capex; no smartphone components; no reusable launch; dish terminals
  • Unit satellite cost was 100–1000× higher
  • Terminal cost was $3,000+ with bulky mechanical tracking
  • Operations required armies of staff per satellite
  • Incumbency as handicap — telcos couldn't cannibalise GEO revenue; startups lacked capital
Three Demand Pillars
  • Enterprise/gov remote — mining, oil & gas, utilities; pays premium; low elasticity
  • Aero/maritime — inflight Wi-Fi, ship crew; duty-of-care driver; existing Ku-band base
  • Consumer rural/suburban — "no alternative" broadband; large addressable market; price-sensitive
GEO Could Not Answer
  • Latency ~600 ms RTT — kills real-time applications
  • Rain fade at Ka/Ku band degrades availability
  • Small, high-cost geostationary slots — ITU coordination years
  • HTS GEO partially closed the capacity gap but not the latency gap

Master Tradeoff
  • Lower altitude → lower latency, lower path loss, more drag, shorter lifetime, more satellites needed
  • Higher altitude → fewer satellites, longer lifetime, higher latency, more path loss, bigger terminal
  • LEO broadband sweet spot: 540–600 km — all five constraints satisfied simultaneously

  • Doppler: ±30 kHz (L), ±355 kHz (Ku), ±672 kHz (Ka) at 550 km

Latency Budget Terms
  • Propagation delay — physical distance / speed; irreducible floor
  • Queuing delay — packet wait time at congested nodes; variable
  • Processing delay — modem, IP stack, encryption
  • Jitter — variation in delay; worse than mean latency for real-time (VoIP, gaming)
  • Hop count — every bent-pipe relay adds a full propagation leg
Application Sensitivity
  • Interactive voice / gaming — RTT < 150 ms needed; GEO excluded
  • Video streaming — latency-insensitive; buffering; GEO acceptable
  • Financial trading HFT — microseconds; LEO still not fast enough vs. dark fibre
  • TCP throughput — degrades on high-RTT links; bandwidth-delay product limits window
04 Multi-Orbit Hybrid Architectures → LESSON ›
Why Hybrid?
  • Complementarity — GEO: broadcast/wide coverage; MEO: balanced; LEO: latency + capacity
  • Resilience — diversity of orbital regimes; no single point of failure
  • Traffic steering — route latency-sensitive traffic to LEO; broadcast to GEO; polar to MEO
Multi-orbit only pays when the traffic mix genuinely benefits from regime diversity. A pure consumer broadband case has no reason to add GEO latency.
Regime Division of Labour
GEOBroadcast, weather, wide-area backup
MEO (O3b)Mid-lat enterprise; balanced latency/coverage
LEOBroadband, interactive, aero/maritime
HEO (Molniya)High-latitude coverage; niche
Key Distinctions
  • Multi-orbit — different orbital regimes (LEO + GEO)
  • Multi-shell — same regime, different inclinations (Starlink 53° + 70° + 97.6°)
  • Managed service — operator controls steering; user sees unified service
  • Multi-orbit terminal — single dish steering across regimes; adds cost/complexity
When Hybrid Breaks Down
  • Terminal cost of multi-orbit capability may not be justified if one regime serves 95% of traffic
  • Spectrum coordination between regimes adds regulatory complexity
  • Interference between co-frequency LEO and GEO requires EPFD limits

Two-Tier Regulatory System
  • ITU — International Telecommunication Union; global coordination; Radio Regulations; WRC every 4 yrs
  • National regulator (FCC, Ofcom, ARCEP…) — licences operators within ITU framework
  • Filing priority — first to file at ITU gets coordination priority; drives "paper satellite" filings
  • Landing rights — national gate for market access; separate from ITU slot
Critical Regulatory Concepts
  • NGSO — Non-Geostationary Orbit; Starlink, OneWeb, Kuiper all NGSO
  • EPFD — Equivalent Power Flux Density; limits NGSO interference into GEO beams
  • Bringing into use (BIU) — must launch ≥1 satellite in the filed band within 7 years or filing lapses
  • WRC-19 milestone — NGSO must deploy ≥10% of constellation in 2 yr, ≥50% in 5 yr, 100% in 7 yr
  • Spectrum moat — first operator to coordinate and deploy locks out later entrants from same frequencies
Band Choice → Architecture
  • Ka-band → small terminals possible; rain fade ↑; higher capacity
  • Ku-band → legacy compatibility; larger terminals; lower rain fade risk
  • Higher frequency → more bandwidth available; smaller antennas; more rain fade
  • Frequency reuse across spot beams is the capacity multiplier — covered in M3-5
07 The Architect's Mental Model (Synthesis) → LESSON ›
The Six Threads
1. Why now — four enablers; timing matters
2. Regime physics — altitude fixes latency, coverage, lifetime
3. Latency floor — propagation is irreducible; application determines need
4. Multi-orbit — complementarity only; not always needed
5. Commercial delta — GEO limitations opened the market
6. Spectrum & regulation — ITU priority determines competitive moat
The Causal Chain
  • Enablers → cost reduction → viable business case
  • GEO latency gap → LEO demand → constellation sizing
  • Altitude → latency + lifetime + drag
  • Inclination → coverage reach + conjunction risk
  • Spectrum filing → competitive moat
Invariant Tradeoff Axes
  • Altitude — latency vs satellites needed vs lifetime
  • Inclination — coverage reach vs polar seam risk vs market fit
  • Frequency — capacity vs rain fade vs terminal size
  • Design life — unit cost vs replacement cadence
Common Reasoning Errors
  • Confusing multi-orbit with multi-shell
  • Treating GEO latency as a solvable engineering problem (it is not)
  • Ignoring regulatory timing as a first-mover advantage
  • Assuming lower altitude is always better
  • Ignoring the four enablers — cost advantage is not permanent

Kepler's Three Laws
  • 1st law — orbits are ellipses; Earth at one focus

Concepts
  • Sub-satellite point (SSP) — ground point directly below satellite
  • Ground track — path of SSP; sinusoidal between ±inclination
  • Elevation angle ε — angle between horizon and line of sight; zero at horizon
  • Doppler pre-compensation — terminal offsets uplink frequency to cancel Doppler at satellite
  • Doppler rate — highest near closest approach; ~1.5 kHz/s at LEO
Unified Rule
Elevation angle, slant range, and Doppler shift all derive from the same satellite+observer state vector. A link budget must treat them consistently — you cannot assume one elevation angle and a different Doppler.
04 Walker Patterns & Coverage Geometry → LESSON ›
Walker T/P/F Notation
  • T — total satellites
  • P — orbital planes; RAAN spacing = 360°/P
  • F — phasing; mean anomaly offset between adjacent planes = F × 360°/T
  • Walker delta — i < 90°; mid-latitude; no polar seam
  • Walker star — i ≈ 90°; global coverage; polar seam (high-velocity crossings)
  • Starlink v1: 1,584/72/1 at 53° | OneWeb: 648/18/0 at 87.9°

550 km, ε=35°ρ_cap ≈ 33.4°A_cap ≈ 8.3%

Inclination Tradeoffs
  • Coverage reach — max lat = inclination (prograde)
  • Polar seam — Walker star: ascending/descending planes cross at ≤15 km/s relative velocity; debris risk
  • Dwell time — more time near ±i; wastes capacity over polar ocean for commercial service
  • Why 53° — Starlink: market in ±53°; no seam; Falcon 9 direct from Cape Canaveral
  • Why 87.9° — OneWeb: global enterprise/maritime customer; one shell = global coverage
k_geometry
Starlink first shell (1,584 sats) vs theoretical minimum at ε=35° (24 sats) → k_geometry ≈ 66. Not waste: higher effective elevation target (40–45°), J2 phasing margin, outage tolerance, commercial density. k_geo = 3–6 for a basic design; 60+ for a commercial product.

Rocket Equation
  • m_prop = m_dry × (exp(ΔV / g₀·Isp) − 1)g₀ = 9.807 m/s²
  • Hall HET: Isp ≈ 1,600 s, F ≈ 50 mN~2 kg propellant for 100 m/s, 300 kg sat
  • Hydrazine: Isp ≈ 230 s, F ≈ 5 N~14 kg propellant for same Δv
  • CAM time = m·Δv / F50 mN HET: 0.2 m/s on 300 kg → 20 min
Station-Keeping Axes
  • Along-track — differential drag; corrected by orbit boosts; cheap (2–10 m/s/yr)
  • Cross-track (inclination) — luni-solar drift ~0.5°/yr; ΔV ≈ v·Δi ≈ 66 m/s/yr; most operators skip
  • Radial — managed as along-track; small for near-circular LEO
  • Deadband — tolerance before correction triggered; ±0.05° ≈ ±6 km at 550 km
Deorbit Rules
  • IADC 25-yr guideline — international standard; natural or active deorbit
  • FCC 5-yr rule — US-licensed post-2022 constellations must deorbit within 5 yr of EOM
  • 600 km solar-min natural lifetime: 30–50 yr → deorbit burn mandatory
  • 550 km solar-min: 15–25 yr → marginal; active deorbit needed for FCC compliance

03 Payload Architectures: Transparent vs Regenerative → LESSON ›
Architecture Spectrum
Transparent (bent-pipe)No switching, no ISL, no noise decoupling, high gateway dependency, low flexibility
Digital channeliserPartial frequency switching, no ISL, high gateway dependency, medium flexibility
Regenerative (full)Full switching, ISL-compatible, noise decoupled, low gateway dependency, high flexibility
Core Distinction
  • Transparent — amplifies + frequency-converts only; satellite sees RF power, not bits
  • Regenerative — demodulates uplink, processes bits, remodulates downlink; satellite sees bits
Why It's the Highest-Leverage Decision
Every kg of bus mass and every watt of array power exists in service of the payload. The transparent/regenerative choice has the largest downstream consequence for system capacity, latency, flexibility, and cost of any single design decision in the satellite.

Capacity & Beam-Hopping Formulas

  • C/I ≈ G_main − G_sidelobe(θ_sep)co-channel interference
  • Tₖ = Dₖ / Σ Dₖbeam-hopping dwell time fraction

Full Reuse + Hopping = Power-Limited Optimum
For a power-limited (not bandwidth-limited) satellite, full frequency reuse combined with beam-hopping is the capacity optimum: every Hz of spectrum is reused at every beam, and every watt of transmit power is concentrated where traffic demand actually is at any instant.

The f² Cancellation Principle
Higher frequency adds path loss but adds equal antenna gain for a fixed physical aperture — they cancel exactly. The real advantage of going higher is bandwidth and smaller aperture for a given gain, not a free link-budget improvement. Ka dominates LEO broadband for antenna-size economics; V-band is the emerging feeder-link standard with 4× the spectrum.

Sensitivity Ranking & the Structural Asymmetry
EIRP and G/T are 1:1 levers on margin (each dB buys a dB). ACM has the highest leverage: 5–20dB of effective margin at the cost of proportional throughput. LNA NF below 1.0dB gives diminishing returns once T_sys is dominated by T_ant/feed loss. The forward/return asymmetry is structural, not incidental — satellite T_sys (warm Earth) vs ground T_sys (cold sky), plus higher uplink-frequency rain attenuation, always favour the forward link.
