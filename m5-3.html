Multiple Access
& Scheduling

MODULE 5
AIR INTERFACE & ACCESS
CORE

Lessons m5-1 and m5-2 produced a number for one carrier: the information rate a given C/N₀ supports. But a beam does not serve one user; it serves hundreds to thousands of terminals whose traffic arrives in bursts, at different priorities, from different locations within the beam. The mechanism that divides the carrier among them — the multiple access scheme and the scheduler that operates it — determines how much of the information rate survives as capacity a user can actually be allocated, and how quickly a terminal that suddenly needs capacity can get it.

Two structurally different problems hide under one name. On the forward link (gateway → users), one transmitter serves everyone: sharing is a scheduling decision made by a single entity that knows the whole queue, and the access cost is small. On the return link (users → satellite), thousands of uncoordinated transmitters share the uplink: sharing requires either dividing the resource in advance, coordinating requests and grants across the propagation delay, or accepting collisions. Almost all of this lesson's difficulty lives on the return link — the same side that m4-7 showed was power-starved.

A boundary declaration, to prevent double counting across the module: this lesson charges the costs of multi-user sharing — guard times, burst preambles, contention losses, request-grant latency, scheduling quantisation. Lesson m5-4 charges the costs of per-carrier physical-layer framing — frame headers, pilots, and signalling that exist even with a single user. The spine engine keeps the two factors separate and multiplies them.

1. The access families

Five schemes cover the field. Each divides a different resource, and each interacts differently with the amplifier physics of m5-1.

FDMA — frequency division. Each terminal gets its own carrier. Simple, no time coordination needed. Costs: guard bands between carriers, and — decisive on the space segment — multiple carriers through one high-power amplifier generate intermodulation products, forcing several dB of output back-off (the m5-1 §5 problem in its worst form). Classical FDMA is now mostly a component inside hybrid schemes.

TDMA — time division. One carrier, divided into time slots; each terminal transmits bursts at full carrier power. The amplifier sees a single carrier and can run near saturation — the HPA-friendly choice. Costs: every burst needs a preamble (carrier and timing acquisition at the receiver) and a guard time (uncertainty in burst arrival), and all terminals must be time-synchronised to the frame — non-trivial when the range to a LEO satellite changes by kilometres per second (section 7).

MF-TDMA — multi-frequency TDMA. The return-link workhorse, standardised in DVB-RCS2: a grid of moderate-rate carriers × time slots. A terminal hops between carriers and slots as the scheduler assigns them. MF-TDMA keeps per-carrier symbol rates low enough for small terminal amplifiers while retaining TDMA's scheduling flexibility; the price is the burst overhead of TDMA multiplied across every carrier, plus the frequency-hopping agility required of the terminal.

CDMA — code division. All terminals transmit simultaneously across the full band, separated by spreading codes. Graceful degradation with load and no hard slot structure, but spectral efficiency is poor for broadband, and the near-far problem demands tight closed-loop power control. In satellite practice CDMA survives where its robustness matters more than efficiency — GNSS signals, some mobile-satellite services — not in broadband.

OFDMA — orthogonal frequency division. The channel is split into thousands of narrow subcarriers, scheduled in time-frequency blocks. This is the access scheme of 5G NR and therefore of 3GPP NTN (m5-5). Fine-grained two-dimensional scheduling is its strength; its weakness is PAPR — the sum of many subcarriers has a large envelope variation, which is why the NR uplink offers a single-carrier variant (DFT-s-OFDM) for power-limited transmitters. The m5-1 amplifier logic, again.

2. The forward link: one TDM carrier and a scheduler

The DVB-S2 forward link is a single wideband TDM carrier. Frames are transmitted back-to-back, each frame addressed to one or more terminals, each frame potentially at its own MODCOD (this is how ACM physically works — the m5-2 selection is applied per frame, per destination terminal). There is no contention and almost no access overhead; user separation costs only the addressing and signalling inside the framing, which m5-4 owns.

What the forward link does have is a scheduler: the entity that decides, every frame, which terminal's queue is served next. Its efficiency cost is small — quantisation (a frame addressed to one user cannot be partially reassigned) and scheduling signalling, typically a few percent. Its policy consequences are large, and section 6 returns to them. The structural point: forward-link sharing is cheap because coordination is free when one node owns the transmitter.

3. Return-link disciplines: contention, requests, and grants

The return link must get uncoordinated terminals onto a shared uplink. Three disciplines, in increasing order of coordination:

Random access. A terminal simply transmits and hopes. The classical analysis: with Poisson-distributed attempts at normalised load G, pure ALOHA delivers throughput S = G·e^(−2G), peaking at 18.4%; slotted ALOHA (transmissions aligned to slot boundaries) delivers S = G·e^(−G), peaking at 36.8% at G = 1. Beyond the peak, throughput falls as load rises — the signature instability of contention. Modern satellite systems use contention-resolution variants: CRDSA (DVB-RCS2) transmits each burst twice in random slots and uses successive interference cancellation to unpick collisions, raising usable throughput to roughly 50–80% of channel capacity depending on configuration. Random access is used where its latency advantage matters and its efficiency penalty is affordable: initial logon, small control messages, the first packet of a flow.

Demand assignment (DAMA). The terminal sends a small capacity request (itself via random access or a reserved slot); the scheduler grants dedicated slots; the terminal transmits without contention. Efficiency is high — the payload slots carry no collision risk — but every fresh burst of demand pays the request-grant round trip (section 5). DAMA in several flavours (rate-based, volume-based) carries the bulk of return traffic in managed satellite networks.

Dedicated assignment. Slots permanently allocated to a terminal (constant rate assignment). Zero access latency, zero contention — and zero statistical multiplexing: capacity is burned whether or not the terminal has traffic. Reserved for constant-rate services (voice trunks, telemetry) and as the guaranteed floor under QoS contracts.

Real return links blend all three: a contention pool for logon and requests, DAMA for the traffic bulk, dedicated slots for guaranteed-rate services. Choosing the blend — how much capacity to reserve for contention, how aggressive to make the grant cycle — is a live operational tuning problem, not a design-time constant.

4. The overhead budget: burst-length economics

Every MF-TDMA burst pays a fixed tax: a preamble for the demodulator to acquire carrier phase and symbol timing (order 100–300 symbols), and a guard time absorbing residual timing uncertainty (order 50–200 symbols). The tax is fixed per burst, so its relative cost depends entirely on burst length:

The table encodes a genuine design tension. Long slots on fast carriers are efficient but coarse: the minimum allocation quantum is large, so a terminal with a trickle of traffic wastes most of its grant (allocation quantisation loss — a second, hidden overhead). Short slots on slow carriers allocate finely but drown in preamble. The scheduler's job includes matching burst sizes to traffic — and the 70% corner of the table is what a badly configured return link looks like.

5. The request-grant latency budget

DAMA's efficiency is paid in latency. The cycle: terminal detects queued traffic → sends request → scheduler receives, decides, and signals the grant → terminal transmits in the granted slot. The floor under this cycle is propagation: for a LEO bent-pipe system with ~1,000 km slant paths, four legs (request up, request down to gateway, grant up, grant down) cost about 13 ms, plus the scheduler's own decision period (commonly 10–50 ms), plus waiting for the granted slot. Total: roughly 25–65 ms of allocation delay before the first payload symbol of a fresh burst.

The GEO comparison explains an entire generation of user experience: the same four legs at 35,786 km cost 477 ms of propagation alone — half a second of allocation delay on top of the half-second data RTT. It also explains the discipline blend: random access exists precisely so that small, latency-sensitive bursts can skip the grant cycle, and schedulers pre-grant capacity speculatively to active terminals to hide the cycle from ongoing flows. The residual access latency of a well-tuned LEO return link is a defining input to M7's end-to-end latency budget — this number is exported, not just computed.

6. Scheduling policies and the ACM interaction

Given demand exceeding supply, the scheduler must choose. Three canonical policies:

Round robin — equal turns. Simple, predictable, indifferent to both channel state and traffic value.

Max-throughput — always serve the terminal whose current MODCOD (per m5-2 ACM) is highest. Maximises beam capacity and starves anyone in a fade — the users who arguably need service continuity most.

Proportional fair — serve the terminal with the best ratio of current achievable rate to its recent average rate. The compromise that dominates practice: it exploits good channel states (a terminal at a MODCOD peak gets served preferentially) without starving faded users (whose falling average raises their priority).

ACM makes scheduling and the physical layer inseparable: the cost of serving a user depends on their channel. A frame sent to a terminal at QPSK 1/2 delivers one-third the bits of the same frame at 16-APSK 3/4 (m5-2). A beam-wide capacity figure is therefore not a constant but a traffic-weighted average over the users' MODCOD distribution — and a scheduler policy choice shifts that average. On top sit QoS classes: guaranteed-rate tiers, weighted shares, priority queues, all implemented as constraints on the same slot-assignment decision. The m5-2 reflection on fade-time fairness is exactly a scheduler-policy question; here it becomes concrete: the policy is a few lines of arithmetic in the grant loop, and it silently encodes the operator's commercial promises.

7. LEO-specific mechanics: the access layer in motion

Everything above holds for GEO. LEO adds motion, and the access layer feels it twice:

Timing. TDMA and MF-TDMA require bursts from all terminals to arrive at the satellite aligned to the slot grid. The range to a LEO satellite at 550 km changes at up to ~7.0 km/s above 5° elevation — a one-way delay drifting at 23 µs per second. A 10 Msym/s carrier's symbol lasts 0.1 µs: uncorrected, timing error grows by hundreds of symbols per second. The options are a per-terminal timing advance continuously updated from ephemeris and position (the terminal knows where the satellite is and pre-compensates — the standard LEO answer, and the mechanism 3GPP NTN formalises in m5-5), closed-loop timing correction signalled by the hub, or guard times fat enough to absorb the drift — which the section-4 table prices, and the price is unacceptable. Ephemeris-driven pre-compensation is what makes tight guard times, and therefore efficient bursts, possible at LEO.

Frequency. The same ~7 km/s produces up to ~700 kHz of Doppler at a 30 GHz uplink (the m2-4 geometry, landing here as an access-layer problem). Uncompensated, a terminal's burst arrives outside its assigned MF-TDMA carrier slot's tolerance. As with timing, the fix is pre-compensation from ephemeris, and the residual (from position error and oscillator drift) is what the preamble and the carrier-spacing guard must absorb.

Both compensations assume the terminal knows the satellite ephemeris and its own position — and has the protocol machinery to act on them. A broadband terminal satisfies both trivially. A smartphone has GNSS but, unless it implements the Release-17 NTN features (m5-5), lacks the machinery — so direct-to-device systems serving unmodified phones must move the compensation to the network side, which is one of the quiet shaping forces of m5-6.

9. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

The m5-2 information rate is a carrier property. This lesson converts it toward a user property: the forward link loses a few percent to scheduling quantisation, the return link loses burst overhead, contention-pool reservation, and allocation quantisation — and gains an allocation-delay figure that M7's latency budget will consume. The scheduler policy, not the physics, decides how the surviving capacity is divided among users, and the policy encodes commercial commitments.

Spine Stage 3 attaches an access model to the throughput engine: a forward TDM efficiency and weighted per-user allocation, and a return MF-TDMA model with burst-length-dependent efficiency and a contention-pool reservation. What the engine still assumes: framing is free. m5-4 charges that bill — physical-layer framing, pilots, and signalling — and examines how the standardised air interface (DVB-S2X) and the proprietary ones package everything from m5-1 to m5-3 differently.

Exercise 2 — Burst-length economics

Quantify the fixed-tax structure of MF-TDMA bursts and the quantisation loss.

def burst_efficiency(rs_syms, slot_ms, overhead_sym=300):
    n = rs_syms * slot_ms / 1e3
    return (n - overhead_sym) / n, n

print(f"{'Rs (Msym/s)':>12}{'slot (ms)':>10}{'sym/slot':>10}{'eff':>8}")
for rs in [10e6, 5e6, 2e6, 1e6]:
    for slot in [4.0, 2.0, 1.0, 0.5]:
        eff, n = burst_efficiency(rs, slot)
        print(f"{rs/1e6:>12.0f}{slot:>10.1f}{n:>10.0f}{eff*100:>7.1f}%")

# Allocation quantisation: a terminal needs `demand` bits in a scheduler
# period but grants come in whole slots.
def quantisation_loss(demand_bits, slot_payload_bits):
    import math as m
    slots = m.ceil(demand_bits / slot_payload_bits)
    return slots * slot_payload_bits - demand_bits

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 3: the access model

The throughput engine gains its sharing layer: forward TDM allocation and a return MF-TDMA model.

import math

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M5 SPINE PROJECT — THROUGHPUT ENGINE
# Stage 3: multiple access & scheduling.
#   Stage 1 (m5-1): bandwidth, roll-off -> symbol rate
#   Stage 2 (m5-2): C/N0 -> MODCOD -> information rate
#   Stage 3 (here): information rate -> access-layer capacity
#                   -> per-user allocation; return burst model
# Next: m5-4 framing overhead, m5-5 NTN branch, m5-6 D2D,
#       m5-7 full assembly.
# Boundary: this stage charges SHARING costs only. Framing,
# pilots, and PHY headers are m5-4's bill.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

MODCOD_TABLE = [  # (name, eta bit/sym, EsN0 threshold dB) — EN 302 307-1 Tbl 13
    ("QPSK 1/4", 0.490243, -2.35), ("QPSK 1/2", 0.988858, 1.00),
    ("QPSK 3/4", 1.487473, 4.03),  ("8PSK 3/4", 2.228124, 7.91),
    ("16APSK 2/3", 2.637201, 8.97),("16APSK 3/4", 2.966728, 10.21),
    ("32APSK 3/4", 3.703295, 12.73),("32APSK 9/10", 4.453027, 16.05),
]

def select_modcod(esn0_db, margin_db=1.5):
    best = None
    for name, eta, thr in MODCOD_TABLE:
        if esn0_db >= thr + margin_db and (best is None or eta > best[1]):
            best = (name, eta, thr)
    return best

class ForwardAccess:
    """Single TDM carrier + weighted scheduler. Sharing cost:
    scheduler efficiency (quantisation + signalling)."""
    def __init__(self, bw_hz, alpha, cn0_dbhz, sched_eff=0.97):
        self.rs = bw_hz / (1 + alpha)                     # Stage 1
        esn0 = cn0_dbhz - 10*math.log10(self.rs)
        self.modcod = select_modcod(esn0)                 # Stage 2
        self.info_rate = self.modcod[1] * self.rs if self.modcod else 0.0
        self.sched_eff = sched_eff

    @property
    def access_capacity(self):
        return self.info_rate * self.sched_eff            # Stage 3

    def allocate(self, weights):
        total = sum(weights.values())
        return {u: self.access_capacity * w / total for u, w in weights.items()}

class ReturnAccess:
    """MF-TDMA grid: n_carriers x slots. Sharing costs: burst
    overhead, contention-pool reservation, quantisation (coarse)."""
    def __init__(self, bw_hz, n_carriers, alpha, esn0_db,
                 slot_ms=2.0, overhead_sym=300, contention_frac=0.05):
        carrier_bw = bw_hz / n_carriers                   # incl. spacing
        self.rs_carrier = carrier_bw / (1 + alpha)
        self.n_carriers = n_carriers
        self.modcod = select_modcod(esn0_db)
        syms = self.rs_carrier * slot_ms / 1e3
        self.burst_eff = (syms - overhead_sym) / syms
        self.contention_frac = contention_frac
        self.slot_ms = slot_ms

    @property
    def access_capacity(self):
        if not self.modcod: return 0.0
        info = self.modcod[1] * self.rs_carrier * self.n_carriers
        return info * self.burst_eff * (1 - self.contention_frac)

    def grant_delay_ms(self, slant_km=1000, sched_period_ms=20):
        prop = 4 * slant_km * 1e3 / 3e8 * 1e3             # request+grant legs
        return prop + sched_period_ms + self.slot_ms / 2

# ── Demonstration: one Ka user beam, both directions ────
fwd = ForwardAccess(bw_hz=250e6, alpha=0.10, cn0_dbhz=96.0)
print(f"FORWARD  {fwd.modcod[0]:>12}: info {fwd.info_rate/1e6:7.1f} Mbps"
      f" -> access {fwd.access_capacity/1e6:7.1f} Mbps")

alloc = fwd.allocate({"premium": 3, "standard": 1, "standard2": 1})
for u, r in alloc.items():
    print(f"    {u:<10} {r/1e6:7.1f} Mbps")

ret = ReturnAccess(bw_hz=125e6, n_carriers=25, alpha=0.20, esn0_db=4.5)
print(f"\nRETURN   {ret.modcod[0]:>12}: {ret.n_carriers} carriers x "
      f"{ret.rs_carrier/1e6:.1f} Msym/s, burst eff {ret.burst_eff*100:.1f}%")
print(f"    access capacity {ret.access_capacity/1e6:7.1f} Mbps")
print(f"    DAMA grant delay ~{ret.grant_delay_ms():.0f} ms  (exported to M7)")

# ── Burst-length sensitivity on the same return grid ────
print(f"\n{'slot (ms)':>10}{'burst eff':>11}{'capacity (Mbps)':>17}")
for slot in [4.0, 2.0, 1.0, 0.5]:
    r = ReturnAccess(125e6, 25, 0.20, 4.5, slot_ms=slot)
    print(f"{slot:>10.1f}{r.burst_eff*100:>10.1f}%{r.access_capacity/1e6:>16.1f}")

PROMPT 04
