Site Diversity & Feeder-Link Availability

MODULE 8
GROUND SEGMENT & OPERATIONS
CORE

m8-1 built a feeder link with tens of gigabits of clear-sky capacity and flagged the problem this lesson solves: that capacity is worthless if the link is down every time it rains. A feeder link concentrates a whole satellite's traffic (m8-1 §5), so it must be highly available — but it runs at Ka or Q/V-band, where rain attenuation is brutal, and the availability a concentration point demands cannot be bought with link margin at those frequencies. This lesson quantifies the rain problem — a single Ka gateway needs some 23 dB of margin for 99.99% availability and effectively unaffordable margin for 99.999% — and then presents the solution that the gateway's siting freedom (m8-1 §2) makes possible: site diversity. Build several gateways far enough apart that rain never soaks all of them at once, and route the feeder link to whichever gateway is currently clear. The rain problem, unsolvable per-site with margin, becomes solvable geographically.

The lesson's verified core is the multiplication that makes diversity work. Rain outage at separated sites is largely independent — a rain cell over one gateway rarely extends to another 50–100 km away — so if each gateway alone achieves a modest 99% availability, three independent gateways achieve 99.9999% jointly, because all three must be rained-on simultaneously for the feeder to fail, and that near-never happens. Site diversity converts a link-margin problem the physics forbids solving into a geographic problem the operator can solve by building more gateways. The spine gains the diversity availability model — the tool that turns a target feeder availability into a required number and separation of gateway sites.

1. The rain problem at feeder bands

Module 4 established rain attenuation; at feeder bands it is severe. Applying the rain model at 30° elevation for the rain rates that correspond to different availability targets:

Availability Rain rate Ka (20 GHz) fade Q/V (40 GHz) fade
99.9% (three-nines)~12 mm/h5.8 dB18.3 dB
99.99% (four-nines)~42 mm/h23.2 dB59.4 dB
99.999% (five-nines)~100 mm/h~60 dB~134 dB

The numbers climb catastrophically with both availability and frequency. At Ka-band, reaching four-nines requires surviving a 23 dB fade — more margin than the entire feeder link budget can spare — and five-nines is physically hopeless with margin alone. At Q/V-band it is worse by another factor: an 18 dB fade just for three-nines, and utterly impossible fades beyond. This is the trap m8-1 flagged: the wide bandwidth that makes the feeder capable (Q/V-band's gigahertz) comes with rain attenuation that makes single-site high availability unreachable. You cannot buy feeder availability with link margin at these frequencies. The feeder link needs a different kind of answer.

2. Why margin fails and geography works

The reason margin fails is that rain fade is a heavy-tailed problem: the fade you must survive grows steeply as you chase the last fractions of a percent of availability, because the rare, intense downpours that cause the worst fades are precisely the ones that define five-nines. To survive them with margin, the feeder link would need to run, in clear sky, tens of decibels below its capability — throwing away most of the capacity m8-1 built — just to have headroom for a storm that occurs 0.001% of the time over that one gateway. It is a colossal waste to size a link for the worst weather at a single point.

Geography sidesteps the tail entirely. The insight: the worst rain over one gateway is a local event — a storm cell tens of kilometres across — and it is very unlikely that an equally bad storm sits over a second gateway 100 km away at the same moment. So instead of one gateway hardened against its own worst weather, build two or three gateways in different weather, and use whichever is clear. The feeder link fails only if all the diversity sites are rained-on at once, which — for well-separated sites — is far rarer than any one being rained-on. The problem the tail made unsolvable per-site becomes easy across sites, because the tails do not coincide.

3. The diversity multiplication

The arithmetic is the lesson's centre. If a single gateway achieves availability a (outage probability p = 1 − a), and N gateways have independent rain outages, then the feeder fails only when all N are simultaneously out, with joint outage p^N and joint availability 1 − p^N:

Sites Single-site availability Joint availability
199%99%
299%99.99%
399%99.9999%

Each independent site multiplies the outage probability. Three gateways, each achieving only a modest 99% availability on its own (a 1% outage, easily met with normal margin), together achieve 99.9999% — six-nines — because all three must be rained-on at once for the feeder to fail, a coincidence of probability 0.01³ = one in a million. This is why site diversity is transformative: it reaches availability targets that are physically impossible per-site, using gateways that are individually unremarkable. The operator trades hardware (more gateways) for availability (the multiplication), and the trade is enormously favourable because the exponent works in their favour.

The catch is the word independent, and it is m8-2's central caveat (§4). The multiplication assumes the sites' rain outages do not coincide, which is true only if they are far enough apart to be in genuinely different weather. Correlated sites do not multiply — two gateways in the same storm's path fail together, and their joint availability is barely better than one. The whole diversity gain rides on decorrelating the sites, which means separating them.

4. How far apart: decorrelating the sites

Rain is spatially correlated over the scale of weather systems. Convective storm cells — the intense downpours that cause the worst fades — are typically a few kilometres to tens of kilometres across, while the broader frontal systems that bring them are larger. For two gateways' rain outages to be usefully independent, they must be separated by more than the correlation scale of the heavy rain — as a rule of thumb, 50 to 100 kilometres or more, and ideally in different meteorological settings (different sides of a mountain range, different distances from a coast, different microclimates). Sites much closer than this share weather and do not diversify; sites far apart in different climates diversify strongly.

This is a real constraint on gateway placement (m8-3). Diversity sites cannot be co-located for operational convenience — they must be deliberately spread, which means more land, more backhaul (each diversity site needs its own fibre to the point of presence), and more antennas. The separation also cannot be so large that no single satellite can see both sites (the m7-1 co-visibility geometry) if the diversity is to be seamless — the satellite must be able to switch its feeder link from one diversity gateway to another without the traffic touching the ground in between, which requires both to be in its footprint. So the diversity separation is bounded below by rain decorrelation (~50–100 km) and above by satellite co-visibility (the footprint, ~2,000+ km) — a comfortable window, but a real one.

5. Gateway switching: using the diversity

Diversity is only useful if the system can switch to the clear gateway when one is raining. The feeder link is redirected — the satellite retargets its feeder beam from the rained-on gateway to a clear diversity site, and the traffic that was descending at gateway A now descends at gateway B — and this switch must happen fast enough that the rain fade does not cause an outage before the switch completes. Because rain fades build over seconds to minutes (not milliseconds), the switch has time to be orderly: the system monitors each diversity site's fade (from the feeder link's own signal quality or a co-located rain sensor), predicts an impending outage, and hands the feeder over to a clear site before the fade deepens — the m6-5 make-before-break pattern once more, now switching gateways instead of satellites.

The switch has a cost: the clear diversity gateway must have spare capacity to absorb the traffic redirected to it, which means the diversity sites cannot all run at full capacity simultaneously — there must be headroom to carry a neighbour's redirected load. This is the hidden cost of diversity: not just extra gateways, but extra capacity at each, so that a clear site can take over a rained-on site's traffic. A diversity scheme sized so that any one site can fail over to the others needs each site provisioned above its own average load — the availability is bought with both redundant sites and redundant capacity.

6. The availability-weighted feeder capacity

Site diversity resolves m8-1's deferred question (reflection 4): what capacity does the feeder link actually deliver, accounting for rain? With diversity, the feeder is almost always running on a clear gateway at full clear-sky capacity, so the availability-weighted average capacity is close to the clear-sky number — the diversity scheme's whole purpose is to keep the link out of the rain. Without diversity, the feeder would spend meaningful time in fade, backing off its MODCOD (m5-2) to survive, and its average deliverable capacity would be materially below clear-sky. Diversity therefore does double duty: it delivers the availability a concentration point demands, and by keeping the link on clear gateways it preserves the capacity m8-1 computed. The clear-sky feeder capacity is a trustworthy sizing number because diversity keeps the link in clear sky — the two lessons resolve together.

This is the ground segment's first principle: availability is bought with geography, not margin. A user terminal, stuck at the user's location, had only margin and ACM to fight rain (Module 4, m5-2). A gateway, free to be sited and duplicated, fights rain with diversity — a fundamentally more powerful tool that only the ground segment's siting freedom unlocks. It is the single most important difference between operating a feeder link and operating a user link, and it shapes everything about how gateways are placed (m8-3) and how many there must be.

8. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

Stage 2 of the spine is the diversity availability model: given a target feeder availability, a single-site availability (from the rain climate and the link margin), and a site-independence assumption, it computes the number of diversity gateways required and checks the separation for decorrelation. It also computes the availability-weighted feeder capacity — confirming that with diversity, the clear-sky capacity of m8-1 is close to the deliverable capacity. The feeder link is now not just a budget but an available budget: a capacity the operator can actually promise, backed by enough gateways to keep it out of the rain.

What remains is to place those gateways in the real world. Diversity says how many gateways and how far apart; it does not say where — and placement is a joint problem of rain climate, backhaul availability, regulatory access, and the mesh's egress needs (where the traffic wants to come down). That is m8-3, and it turns the diversity requirement into a concrete map of gateway sites, connected to the terrestrial internet through a point-of-presence topology. The number of gateways diversity demands is one of the biggest cost drivers in the ground segment, and m8-3 is where that cost gets located.

Exercise 1 — The rain problem at feeder bands

Quantify why margin fails.

import math

def specific_atten(f_ghz, R):
    tbl = {12:(0.0188,1.217), 20:(0.0751,1.099), 30:(0.187,1.021),
           40:(0.350,0.939), 50:(0.536,0.873)}
    k, a = tbl[min(tbl, key=lambda x: abs(x-f_ghz))]
    return k*R**a

def rain_fade_db(f_ghz, R, elev_deg, hR=3.0, hs=0.1):
    gamma = specific_atten(f_ghz, R)
    Ls = (hR-hs)/math.sin(math.radians(elev_deg))
    r = 1/(1 + Ls*math.cos(math.radians(elev_deg))/35)
    return gamma*Ls*r

# rain rate for each availability target (temperate zone, approx)
targets = {"99.9%": 12, "99.99%": 42, "99.999%": 100}
print(f"{'availability':>12}{'Ka fade':>10}{'Q/V fade':>10}")
for label, R in targets.items():
    print(f"{label:>12}{rain_fade_db(20,R,30):>9.1f}{rain_fade_db(40,R,30):>10.1f}")

Exercise 2 — The diversity multiplication

Turn a target into a site count.

import math

def joint_availability(single_avail, n_sites):
    p = 1 - single_avail
    return 1 - p**n_sites

def sites_needed(target_avail, single_avail):
    p, pt = 1-single_avail, 1-target_avail
    n = 1
    while (p**n) > pt:
        n += 1
    return n

print("joint availability by site count (99% single):")
for n in [1, 2, 3, 4]:
    print(f"  {n} sites: {joint_availability(0.99, n)*100:.4f}%")

print("\nsites needed for targets (99% single-site):")
for target in [0.9999, 0.99999, 0.999999]:
    print(f"  {target*100:.4f}% -> {sites_needed(target, 0.99)} sites")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 2: the diversity model

Feeder availability, solved geographically.

import math

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M8 SPINE PROJECT — GROUND SEGMENT
# Stage 2: site-diversity availability model.
#   Stage 1 (m8-1) gave a feeder link with clear-sky capacity
#   but no availability. Stage 2: turn a target availability
#   into a required number & separation of diversity gateways,
#   and confirm the availability-weighted capacity ≈ clear-sky.
#   Next: m8-3 places these gateways in the real world.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class DiversityModel:
    MIN_SEPARATION_KM = 75      # rain decorrelation (§4)
    MAX_SEPARATION_KM = 2000    # satellite co-visibility (§4)

    def __init__(self, single_site_avail=0.99, correlated_floor=0.0):
        self.p = 1 - single_site_avail
        self.floor = correlated_floor    # residual correlated outage

    def joint_availability(self, n_sites):
        indep = self.p**n_sites
        return 1 - max(indep, self.floor)

    def sites_needed(self, target_avail):
        n = 1
        while self.joint_availability(n) < target_avail:
            n += 1
            if n > 20: return None    # floor prevents reaching target
        return n

    def separation_ok(self, sep_km):
        return self.MIN_SEPARATION_KM <= sep_km <= self.MAX_SEPARATION_KM

    def weighted_capacity_gbps(self, clear_sky_gbps, n_sites):
        # with diversity, link is clear almost always -> ~clear-sky
        avail = self.joint_availability(n_sites)
        return clear_sky_gbps * avail    # tiny derate

    def report(self, target_avail, clear_sky_gbps):
        n = self.sites_needed(target_avail)
        print(f"  target availability: {target_avail*100:.4f}%")
        print(f"  single-site: {(1-self.p)*100:.1f}%")
        print(f"  diversity gateways needed: {n}")
        if n:
            print(f"  joint availability: {self.joint_availability(n)*100:.4f}%")
            print(f"  weighted capacity: "
                  f"{self.weighted_capacity_gbps(clear_sky_gbps, n):.1f} Gbps "
                  f"(clear-sky {clear_sky_gbps})")

print("── DIVERSITY (99% single-site, dry climate) ──")
DiversityModel(0.99).report(0.99999, clear_sky_gbps=48)

print("\n── DIVERSITY (97% single-site, wet climate) ──")
DiversityModel(0.97).report(0.99999, clear_sky_gbps=48)

print("\n── with a correlated floor (imperfect independence) ──")
DiversityModel(0.99, correlated_floor=1e-4).report(0.99999, 48)

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 1

Section 3's multiplication (p^N) is the lesson's engine, and section 4's independence caveat is its limit. Real rain is neither fully independent nor fully correlated — nearby sites share frontal systems but not convective cells, and the correlation decays with distance in a way that depends on the climate. Construct the honest model: how should an operator estimate the effective independence of two candidate sites, and what data would they need (rain radar climatology, historical concurrent-outage records)? Decide whether the clean p^N model is a useful design tool or a dangerous oversimplification, and what safety factor an operator should apply to the site count to account for residual correlation.

PROMPT 2

Section 5 revealed diversity's hidden cost: spare capacity at each site to absorb redirected load. This means a 3-site diversity scheme is not 3× a single gateway's cost but more, because each site must be over-provisioned. Work the true cost multiplier: if any one site must be able to absorb the full load of a rained-on neighbour, how much spare capacity does each site need, and does the answer change with the number of sites (more sites = smaller share each must absorb)? Determine whether diversity gets cheaper per-nine as you add sites, or whether the over-provisioning cost grows, and where the economic optimum sits.

PROMPT 3

Section 6 claimed diversity resolves m8-1's deferred question by keeping the feeder near clear-sky capacity. But this assumes the switch always finds a clear site — which fails if a large frontal system covers all the diversity sites at once (the correlated case §4 warned about). Consider the worst case: a weather system large enough to rain on every gateway in a diversity cluster. What does the feeder do — ride the fade with MODCOD backoff (m5-2), reroute the traffic through the mesh to a distant clear gateway (m7-4), or accept the outage? Evaluate each option's cost and decide which a well-designed constellation should use, connecting the mesh's routing freedom (Module 7) to the ground segment's availability.

PROMPT 4

This lesson established that availability is bought with geography, not margin — a principle that inverts the user link's approach. But it rests on the gateway's siting freedom being real, which assumes the operator can actually acquire land, backhaul, and regulatory access wherever rain decorrelation demands. Interrogate that assumption for the hard cases: a constellation needing diversity gateways in a region with few dry sites, restrictive land access, or a hostile regulator. Where the siting freedom is constrained, does the ground-segment first principle break down — and what does the operator fall back on (Q/V→Ka retreat, accepting lower availability, mesh rerouting)? Specify the conditions under which geography is not available as a tool, and what replaces it.
