Bus Architecture: Power, Structure & Thermal

MODULE 3
SPACE SEGMENT

The satellite bus is the platform on which the communications payload operates. It provides electrical power, a stable mechanical structure, and a controlled thermal environment. A bus that fails to deliver power at the right voltage, that allows the payload to overheat, or that loses attitude control is a loss of service regardless of payload capability. This lesson covers the three subsystems that most directly determine whether the four objectives of mass, cost, performance, and reliability can be simultaneously met: power generation and storage, structural design, and thermal control.

1. The Power Subsystem

Every function on the satellite — payload, computers, heaters, thrusters, radio — runs on electrical power. The power subsystem generates it, stores it, regulates it, and distributes it. Getting the power budget right is one of the first and most fundamental acts of satellite design: undersize it and the payload is power-limited; oversize it and the satellite is too heavy and too expensive.

Solar array generation

The primary power source for LEO satellites is photovoltaic solar arrays. Each square metre of panel exposed to the Sun at 1 AU receives approximately 1,361 W (the solar constant). Commercial silicon cells convert this at roughly 18–22% efficiency; high-efficiency triple-junction gallium arsenide (GaAs/Ge) cells reach 28–32%. At the system level — accounting for panel angle, interconnect losses, temperature derating (~0.4–0.5%/°C), and end-of-life radiation degradation — useful power at the panel is typically 140–200 W/m² for triple-junction cells.

For a LEO broadband satellite requiring 3–7 kW of payload power plus 1–2 kW of bus loads, total power is roughly 4–9 kW. At 150 W/m², this requires 25–60 m² of deployed panel area. Starlink v1 reportedly carried approximately 10 m² per satellite, generating ~1.5–2 kW — consistent with a relatively modest early payload. Later generations deploy significantly more area.

Eclipse and battery storage

At 550 km the orbital period is 95.6 minutes and the maximum eclipse duration is approximately 37 minutes — roughly 39% of each orbit. During eclipse the arrays produce nothing and the satellite runs on battery.

The required stored energy is:

E_storage = P_eclipse × t_eclipse

For 5 kW over 37 minutes: E = 5,000 × (37/60) = 3.1 kWh. At 120 Wh/kg (Li-ion module level), this requires approximately 26 kg of battery — a significant fraction of a 300 kg satellite's dry mass. The depth of discharge (DoD) is a key design parameter: shallower DoD extends battery cycle life but increases required battery capacity and mass.

At LEO, the satellite sees approximately 15 orbits per day — 15 charge-discharge cycles — roughly 5,475 per year. A battery rated to 20,000 cycles at 30% DoD survives approximately 3.7 years; 30,000 cycles covers a 5-year mission with margin.

Power regulation and distribution

A power conditioning unit (PCU) regulates the array voltage (which varies with temperature and illumination), charges the batteries, and distributes regulated power to satellite loads at the required bus voltages (commonly 28 V or 50 V for small satellites). Distribution harnesses can account for several kilograms and percent-level power losses in a large satellite.

2. The Structure Subsystem

The structural subsystem must withstand launch loads (vibration, acoustic, shock, quasi-static acceleration), provide mounting points for all other subsystems, maintain geometric relationships between components, and do all of this at minimum mass and cost.

Launch loads

A Falcon 9 second stage subjects payloads to quasi-static accelerations of approximately 6 g axially and 2 g laterally, sinusoidal vibration up to 1 g across 5–100 Hz, random vibration on the order of 0.02–0.1 g²/Hz, and acoustic levels of approximately 140–145 dB OASPL inside the fairing. For mass-produced LEO broadband satellites stacked flat in the fairing, the structural design is dominated by the stacking load path — the satellite must be stiff enough in the stacking direction to transfer loads between neighbours without interface failure, and deployed-panel hinges must survive the acoustic environment.

Materials and mass fraction

Primary structural materials are aluminium alloys (Al 6061, Al 7075) for machined and extruded members, CFRP panels and booms where stiffness-to-mass ratio matters most, and titanium alloys for high-stress fittings. For mass-produced constellations, aluminium dominates because it machines well and enables high manufacturing rates. CFRP saves mass but requires expensive autoclave tooling incompatible with high-volume production.

The structural mass fraction is typically 15–25% of dry mass — approximately 45–75 kg for a 300 kg satellite. This is a first-order design driver, not a residual after payload sizing.

Fundamental frequency requirement

The satellite's first structural mode must exceed the launch vehicle's frequency exclusion zone — typically above 35–50 Hz axially and 10–15 Hz laterally — to avoid dynamic coupling that would amplify loads and risk structural failure. Meeting this while minimising mass requires finite element modelling (FEM) validated against structural test data.

3. The Thermal Subsystem

A LEO satellite cycles between direct solar illumination (unprotected surfaces can reach +120°C) and eclipse (−150°C) every 95 minutes. Internally, electronics must be kept within operating limits; batteries require −20°C to +40°C; and high-power amplifiers must reject heat efficiently to avoid exceeding their junction temperature limits.

Passive thermal control

The dominant tool is surface optical properties: absorptivity α (fraction of incident solar radiation absorbed) and emissivity ε (efficiency of infrared re-radiation). White paint (α ≈ 0.2, ε ≈ 0.9) absorbs little sunlight and radiates efficiently. Black anodising (α ≈ 0.9, ε ≈ 0.9) absorbs solar radiation and re-radiates nearly as efficiently. Multi-layer insulation (MLI) — alternating aluminised Mylar and netting — minimises radiative heat loss to space with an effective emittance of 0.01–0.03.

The primary heat rejection path is conduction through the bus structure to dedicated radiator panels on the anti-Sun face. Radiator power density follows the Stefan-Boltzmann law:

Q_rad = ε · σ · A_rad · (T_rad⁴ − T_sink⁴)

At T_rad = 60°C (333 K), ε = 0.85, facing deep space: Q_rad ≈ 600 W/m². To reject 3 kW of payload waste heat requires approximately 5 m² of radiator — a significant claim on satellite surface area that competes directly with solar array real estate.

Active thermal control

Electric heaters maintain batteries above their minimum operating temperature during cold eclipse phases. Heat pipes carry heat from high-dissipation components (power amplifiers, digital processors) to radiating surfaces without mechanical pumps. For high-power payloads, the thermal path from amplifier junction to radiating panel is a critical design constraint: junction temperatures of 150°C–200°C must not be exceeded, requiring low-resistance thermal paths through direct-bonded copper substrates and high-conductance thermal interface materials.

Atomic oxygen erosion

Below approximately 600 km, solar UV photodissociates molecular O₂ into atomic oxygen — a highly reactive species that attacks organic materials, carbon composites, and certain coatings on the ram (leading) face of the satellite. Surfaces must use atomic-oxygen-resistant materials (inorganic coatings: SiO₂, Al₂O₃) or be protected against direct exposure. This is a non-trivial materials qualification requirement for operational altitudes below 600 km.

4. Power-Structure-Thermal as an Integrated Design

The three subsystems are not designed independently. Solar array area is simultaneously a structural element (surviving launch loads and deploying reliably) and a thermal element (cell temperature affecting efficiency and degradation). Larger arrays generate more power but add structural mass, deployment complexity, and atomic oxygen exposure area.

The satellite body is simultaneously the structural backbone, the thermal spreader, and the mounting surface for all subsystems. Its material selection — aluminium versus CFRP — affects stiffness (structural), thermal conductivity (thermal), and machinability (manufacturing cost). A highly conductive aluminium structure spreads heat effectively but couples warm electronics to cold panels during eclipse, which can be helpful or harmful depending on the subsystem requirements.

Radiator panels must be sized for worst-case heat dissipation, but placement is constrained by the structural layout (short conductive paths to heat sources) and orbital geometry (avoiding Sun illumination over most of the orbit). For a 53° inclination LEO satellite, the Sun angle varies continuously and the radiator placement must account for the worst-case illumination geometry across all orbital phases.

THE AREA COMPETITION A high-power payload simultaneously requires large solar arrays (power) and large radiators (heat rejection). Both compete for the same satellite surface area. A 5 kW system may need 30–40 m² of solar array and 8 m² of radiator. These geometries must be reconciled with the stacking configuration in the fairing — a concrete three-dimensional constraint that shapes every bus design.

5. Mass-Production Implications

For a constellation of hundreds to thousands of satellites, every kilogram of bus mass multiplied by fleet size is a concrete launch cost. Every hour of manufacturing time multiplied by fleet size is a factory floor cost. These multipliers make mass-production design philosophy fundamentally different from traditional one-off or small-batch spacecraft design.

Traditional spacecraft design produces one or two flight units from a qualification unit, using heritage components with long lead times, extensive unit-level testing, and high cost. LEO broadband constellation design targets hundreds of units per year from a production line, using commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) components, statistical process control rather than 100% unit-level acceptance testing, and design-for-manufacturability principles that sacrifice some performance margin for repeatability and throughput.

Structurally: bolted assemblies rather than bonded joints — faster to make and inspect. Thermally: standardised radiator panels and interface materials across all units rather than custom-routed heat pipes. In power: standardised battery modules and PCU boards that can be inserted and tested in minutes.

The shift from unit-level to fleet-level reliability thinking is the deepest change. A GEO satellite cannot be replenished and must be highly reliable for 15 years. A LEO constellation satellite that fails is replaced by the next launch; the fleet provides system-level redundancy. This allows lower component-level reliability margins, simplified redundancy architectures, and lower cost per satellite — at the price of accepting a non-zero loss rate in normal fleet operations.

FLEET REDUNDANCY VS UNIT RELIABILITY For a 1,584-satellite constellation, a 1% annual failure rate means ~16 satellites lost per year — absorbed by the constellation without significant coverage impact. Designing each satellite to the reliability standard of a single irreplaceable GEO asset would cost orders of magnitude more per unit. The constellation architecture changes what reliability specification is rational.

Exercise 1 — Power Budget and Solar Array Sizing

Size the solar array area and battery for a given total satellite power requirement and eclipse fraction.

import math

SOLAR_CONST   = 1361.0   # W/m^2 at 1 AU
CELL_EFF_EOL  = 0.27     # triple-junction GaAs, 5-yr EOL
PACKING       = 0.90     # cell-to-panel packing efficiency
COSINE_LOSS   = 0.85     # average cosine of Sun angle
TEMP_DERATE   = 0.95     # temperature derating
PERIOD_MIN    = 95.6     # minutes, 550 km orbit
ECLIPSE_MIN   = 37.0     # minutes, max eclipse at 550 km

P_DENSITY = SOLAR_CONST * CELL_EFF_EOL * PACKING * COSINE_LOSS * TEMP_DERATE
SUNLIT_MIN = PERIOD_MIN - ECLIPSE_MIN

def size_power(P_total_w, dod=0.30, batt_kwh_kg=0.12):
    """
    Size solar array (m^2) and battery (kWh, kg) for P_total_w.
    dod: depth of discharge for battery sizing.
    batt_kwh_kg: battery module energy density in kWh/kg.
    """
    # Array must supply load AND recharge battery during sunlit phase
    P_array = P_total_w * PERIOD_MIN / SUNLIT_MIN
    area_m2 = P_array / P_DENSITY
    E_kwh   = P_total_w * ECLIPSE_MIN / 60 / 1000   # kWh
    mass_kg = E_kwh / (batt_kwh_kg * dod)
    return area_m2, E_kwh, mass_kg

print(f"Panel power density: {P_DENSITY:.1f} W/m² (EOL)\n")
print(f"{'P_total (W)':>12}{'Array (m²)':>12}{'Battery (kWh)':>15}{'Batt mass (kg)':>16}")
print("-" * 57)
for p in [1500, 3000, 5000, 8000, 12000]:
    a, e, m = size_power(p)
    print(f"{p:>12}{a:>11.1f} m²{e:>13.2f} kWh{m:>14.1f} kg")

# 2. Reduce eclipse_fraction to 25% (lower altitude). How does
#    battery mass change for a 5,000 W satellite?
# 3. Recalculate with CELL_EFF_EOL = 0.20 (silicon cells).
#    How much larger does the array need to be for 5,000 W?

Exercise 2 — Radiator Sizing

Compute the radiator area required to reject a given heat load for different temperatures and view factors.

import math

SIGMA     = 5.670374419e-8   # W/m^2/K^4
T_SPACE   = 3.0              # K, deep space
T_EARTH   = 255.0            # K, Earth IR equivalent

def radiator_area(Q_w, T_c, emissivity=0.85, earth_view=0.0):
    """
    Radiator area (m^2) to reject Q_w watts at T_c degrees Celsius.
    earth_view: fraction of hemisphere seeing Earth blackbody (0=deep space).
    """
    T_k    = T_c + 273.15
    T_sink = (earth_view * T_EARTH**4
              + (1 - earth_view) * T_SPACE**4) ** 0.25
    q_m2   = emissivity * SIGMA * (T_k**4 - T_sink**4)
    return Q_w / q_m2, q_m2

print("Radiator area to reject 3 kW of waste heat\n")
print(f"{'T_rad (°C)':>11}{'View factor':>13}{'W/m²':>9}{'Area (m²)':>12}")
print("-" * 47)
for t in [20, 40, 60, 80]:
    for fv, lbl in [(0.0, "deep space"), (0.3, "30% Earth")]:
        area, qm2 = radiator_area(3000, t, earth_view=fv)
        print(f"{t:>9}°C  {lbl:>11}{qm2:>8.0f}  {area:>10.2f} m²")
    print()

Exercise 3 — Battery Cycle Life Check

Compute charge-discharge cycles over the satellite lifetime and check against rated battery life.

import math

def cycle_check(life_yr, alt_km=550, dod=0.30, rated_cycles=20000):
    """
    Compute eclipse cycles over mission life and compare to rated cycle count.
    """
    MU = 398600.4418; RE = 6378.137
    r  = (RE + alt_km) * 1e3
    T_min = 2 * math.pi * math.sqrt(r**3 / (MU * 1e9)) / 60
    orbits_per_day = 1440 / T_min
    total_cycles   = orbits_per_day * 365.25 * life_yr   # 1 eclipse per orbit
    margin         = rated_cycles / total_cycles
    ok = margin >= 1.0
    print(f"Alt {alt_km}km  Life {life_yr}yr  DoD {dod*100:.0f}%  "
          f"Rated {rated_cycles:,} cycles")
    print(f"  Period: {T_min:.1f} min  |  Cycles/yr: {orbits_per_day*365.25:.0f}  "
          f"|  Total: {total_cycles:.0f}  |  Margin: {margin:.2f}x  "
          f"|  {'OK' if ok else 'FAIL'}\n")
    return total_cycles, margin

cycle_check(5,  alt_km=550, dod=0.30, rated_cycles=20000)
cycle_check(7,  alt_km=550, dod=0.30, rated_cycles=20000)
cycle_check(5,  alt_km=400, dod=0.30, rated_cycles=20000)
cycle_check(10, alt_km=550, dod=0.30, rated_cycles=30000)
