```
Why LEO Now

1. The Problem GEO Could Not Solve

For the first 40 years of commercial satellite communications, geostationary orbit was the only practical option. Sit a satellite at 35,786 km above the equator and it appears stationary from the ground — one satellite covers a third of the Earth's surface, ground antennas are simple fixed dishes, and the system scales without the complexity of tracking a moving object.

But GEO carries an unavoidable physical penalty: the round-trip signal delay. At the speed of light, a single hop to GEO and back takes approximately 240–280 ms. Add terrestrial network processing and you reach 600 ms or more of round-trip time. For voice calls that means noticeable echo. For TCP-based data protocols it means every packet acknowledgement carries that delay, throttling throughput. For real-time applications — trading, gaming, aviation safety services, video conferencing — GEO latency is simply disqualifying.

The satellite industry tried to paper over this problem with TCP acceleration proxies, spoofing protocols, and pre-fetching tricks. These worked well enough for broadcast and simple web traffic in the 2000s, but the internet was evolving. HTTPS encryption broke transparent proxies. WebRTC and modern video codecs demanded sub-100 ms latency. Cloud-native applications assumed always-on, low-latency connectivity that GEO fundamentally could not deliver.

THE CORE CONSTRAINT GEO latency is not an engineering problem waiting to be solved. It is a consequence of orbital altitude and the speed of light. No amount of signal processing eliminates it. The only fix is to bring the satellite closer to Earth.

The bandwidth illusion

GEO operators responded to latency criticism by pointing to bandwidth. High-throughput satellites (HTS) using spot beams and frequency reuse pushed per-satellite capacity from a few Gbps toward 100–200 Gbps by the mid-2010s. ViaSat-3 was designed for over 1 Tbps. More bandwidth meant cheaper broadband for rural users. The story seemed compelling.

But bandwidth and latency are independent dimensions. A 1 Tbps GEO satellite still delivers 600 ms RTT. For the growing share of internet traffic that was latency-sensitive, more GEO capacity was irrelevant. The satellite industry was solving the wrong problem.

2. What Changed to Make LEO Viable

LEO is not a new idea. Motorola's Iridium constellation launched its first satellite in 1997. The concept of hundreds of small satellites in low orbit had been studied since the 1980s. The reason it failed commercially in the 1990s and why it succeeded two decades later comes down to cost — specifically the cost of building and launching satellites at scale.

$1B+ Cost to build a GEO satellite (2000s)
~$500K Target unit cost per Starlink v1 satellite
$65K/kg Launch cost to LEO, Shuttle era
~$2K/kg Launch cost to LEO, Falcon 9 reuse era

The three enablers

Miniaturisation and commoditisation. The smartphone revolution drove aggressive cost reduction in processors, memory, radios, sensors, and solar cells. Satellite components that previously required bespoke aerospace manufacturing could increasingly be sourced from commercial supply chains. A LEO satellite in 2020 carries more computing power than a GEO satellite from 2000, at a fraction of the mass and cost.

Reusable launch vehicles. SpaceX's development of Falcon 9 with a reusable first stage transformed the economics of launch. Before Falcon 9, getting 1 kg to LEO cost $20,000–65,000 depending on the vehicle. By 2020, Starlink launches on Falcon 9 were achieving effective costs below $2,000/kg — and SpaceX's own Starship aims to reduce this further by an order of magnitude. Without cheap launch, a 4,000-satellite constellation is economically impossible.

Phased-array terminal technology. A GEO terminal is a fixed dish pointing at a stationary target. A LEO terminal must track satellites moving at ~7.5 km/s across the sky, handing off between satellites every few minutes. Mechanical tracking antennas are bulky, fragile, and expensive. Electronically steered phased-array antennas — flat panels with no moving parts — became manufacturable at consumer price points around 2018–2020. This was the final enabling technology that made LEO broadband commercially deployable.

KEY INSIGHT FOR ARCHITECTS The LEO opportunity is fundamentally a manufacturing and launch cost story, not a physics story. The physics of low-Earth orbit have been understood for decades. What changed was the industrial infrastructure needed to exploit those physics economically.

3. The Demand Side: Why Connectivity Became Infrastructure

Even with dramatically lower costs, mega-constellations require enormous capital investment — SpaceX's Starlink program is estimated to have consumed $10–15 billion to reach operational scale. That capital only makes sense if the addressable market is large enough and durable enough to justify it.

The demand argument rests on three pillars.

The unconnected billions

Approximately 3 billion people remained without reliable internet access as of 2020, mostly in rural and remote areas where terrestrial infrastructure is economically unviable. Satellite is the only practical technology for many of these locations. Even capturing a small fraction of this market at modest ARPU represents tens of billions of dollars annually.

The mobility market

Aviation and maritime connectivity were historically served by GEO satellites using Ku-band. The latency and bandwidth limitations of GEO became increasingly visible as passengers expected inflight WiFi to match their home broadband. Airlines were paying hundreds of millions of dollars per year for GEO capacity that delivered an inferior experience. The global connected aircraft market alone represented a multi-billion dollar annual opportunity that LEO could fundamentally reshape.

Government and defence

Governments increasingly viewed ubiquitous, resilient, low-latency connectivity as a strategic asset. The US Department of Defense has contracted with SpaceX for Starlink services. NATO allies have explored LEO for battlefield communications. Ukraine's use of Starlink terminals from early 2022 demonstrated operational military value in ways that reshaped government procurement thinking globally. Government and defence contracts provide stable, high-margin revenue that underpins the economics of commercial LEO constellations.

4. The Competitive Landscape Crystallises

Between 2015 and 2022, the LEO broadband market went from a theoretical concept to a genuine industry with multiple well-funded competitors. Understanding who entered, why, and with what architecture is foundational to everything in Q1.

THE FOUR MAIN ACTORS SpaceX Starlink (US) · Amazon Kuiper (US) · OneWeb / Eutelsat (UK/France) · Telesat Lightspeed (Canada). Each made distinct architectural choices driven by their respective funding structures, launch access, target markets, and technical bets. You will study each in depth in Lessons 7–10.

The competitive dynamic is unusual compared to most industries. SpaceX had a structural advantage: it built the launch vehicle as well as the satellites, giving it launch cost and cadence advantages no competitor could match in the near term. Amazon had cloud infrastructure and distribution advantages. OneWeb had spectrum filings and a completed first-generation constellation before entering bankruptcy. Telesat had MEO engineering heritage and government backing.

For the systems architect, the important lesson is that architecture choices cannot be separated from business context. Starlink's flat-panel consumer terminal was designed for a direct-to-consumer retail model. OneWeb's terminal strategy was B2B-only, targeting telcos and aviation integrators. These are not just marketing decisions — they drive antenna design, frequency plan, gateway topology, and service level architecture.

6. Implications for the Systems Architect

Before moving on, internalise these architectural implications of the LEO shift. They will reappear throughout the programme.

  • Coverage requires constellations, not satellites. A single LEO satellite covers a ground footprint for only minutes. Full global coverage requires hundreds to thousands of satellites. This drives every cost, operational, and regulatory challenge in the programme.
  • Handover is not optional. Unlike GEO where the terminal points at a fixed target indefinitely, every LEO terminal must continuously hand off between satellites. This is a networking problem, a RF problem, and a latency problem simultaneously.
  • The terminal is the bottleneck. Ground segment cost and form factor determine which markets LEO can serve. An expensive, heavy, power-hungry terminal cannot serve aviation or maritime. This is why phased-array terminal technology is so central to the LEO story.
  • Scale changes economics non-linearly. The cost per bit delivered by a LEO constellation improves as satellites are added (up to a point), because more satellites means more capacity over the same gateway and spectrum investment. Understanding constellation scaling economics is an architect-level skill.
  • Regulatory complexity scales with constellation size. A single GEO satellite requires one ITU filing. A 4,000-satellite constellation requires coordination of thousands of orbital slots, frequency assignments, and interference analyses. The regulatory dimension is not optional for an architect.

PYTHON EXERCISE — LATENCY CALCULATOR

Write a Python script that calculates the one-way propagation delay (in milliseconds) and round-trip time for a given orbital altitude. Then compare GEO, MEO, and LEO values side by side. This is the kind of back-of-envelope calculation you will run constantly as an architect.

```

# LEO vs GEO Latency Calculator

# ─────────────────────────────

# Constants

C = 299_792  # speed of light, km/s

def propagation_delay_ms(altitude_km, elevation_deg=90):
“””
Calculate one-way signal delay in ms.
elevation_deg: ground terminal elevation angle to satellite.
At 90° (directly overhead) the path = altitude.
At lower elevations the slant range increases.
“””
import math
earth_radius_km = 6371
# Slant range from ground to satellite using law of cosines
# r = sqrt(Re^2 + (Re+h)^2 - 2*Re*(Re+h)*cos(90+el)) simplified:
elev_rad = math.radians(elevation_deg)
Re = earth_radius_km
h = altitude_km
# Slant range:
slant = math.sqrt(
Re**2 + (Re + h)**2
- 2 * Re * (Re + h) * math.cos(math.radians(90 + elevation_deg))
)
delay_s = slant / C
return delay_s * 1000  # convert to ms

print(“─” * 70)

ow = propagation_delay_ms(alt, elevation_deg=90)
