Station-Keeping & Orbital Decay

MODULE 2
PROPULSION & LIFETIME

The previous lesson sized a constellation geometrically — how many satellites, in how many planes. That sizing assumed satellites stay where they are placed. They do not. Atmospheric drag removes energy continuously; J2 precession shifts each plane; solar activity drives unpredictable density variations; and manufacturing tolerances introduce deviations from the ideal Walker geometry. Station-keeping is the set of manoeuvres that corrects these deviations. Orbital decay is the endpoint when station-keeping ends. This lesson quantifies both: the Δv budget per axis, the propellant mass from the rocket equation, propulsion system tradeoffs, lifetime limits, and the regulatory deorbit landscape. The spine project adds propellant mass to the satellite model here.

1. What Station-Keeping Must Correct

A satellite in a LEO constellation deviates from its nominal slot in three ways that station-keeping must address.

Along-track drift arises from differential perturbations that change the orbital period relative to the nominal. Drag is the primary driver: a satellite with a slightly higher ballistic coefficient decays more slowly than its neighbours, falls behind in mean anomaly, and eventually creates a gap in the constellation pattern. Station-keeping corrects this by periodically boosting the orbit to restore along-track position within a deadband.

Cross-track drift arises from differential inclination perturbations due to luni-solar gravity, accumulating slowly over years. If uncorrected, satellites in the same plane diverge in inclination, widening the plane and creating coverage gaps. Inclination changes require thrust perpendicular to the velocity vector — the most expensive station-keeping axis in Δv terms.

Radial drift arises from differential drag and J2 effects on the semi-major axis. For LEO constellations at nearly identical altitudes, radial separation is small and is usually managed as part of along-track correction rather than as a separate axis.

In practice operators set a station-keeping deadband — a box in along-track and cross-track position within which the satellite is allowed to drift before a correction manoeuvre is triggered. Tighter deadbands require more frequent manoeuvres and more propellant; looser deadbands reduce propellant use but permit larger coverage gaps at constellation boundaries.

DEADBAND AS DESIGN VARIABLE A ±0.05° along-track deadband at 550 km corresponds to roughly ±6 km. That is the box within which along-track position is maintained. Every correction manoeuvre consumes propellant and perturbs the orbit; every widened deadband risks a coverage gap. The deadband is set by the coverage specification and the propellant budget simultaneously.

2. The Δv Budget

The propellant cost of station-keeping is expressed as Δv per year. The total mission Δv budget has three main components:

ΔV_total = ΔV_drag + ΔV_SK_incl + ΔV_deorbit (+ CAM budget)

Drag makeup (ΔV_drag) restores semi-major axis lost to atmospheric drag. At 550 km with a Starlink-class ballistic coefficient (β ≈ 27 kg/m²), drag makeup costs roughly 3–9 m/s/yr depending on the phase of the solar cycle.

Inclination station-keeping (ΔV_SK_incl) corrects slow secular inclination drift due to luni-solar gravity (~0.5°/yr). The Δv cost is:

ΔV_incl ≈ v · Δi (for small Δi, in radians)

At 550 km, v ≈ 7.6 km/s, so correcting 0.5°/yr costs approximately 66 m/s/yr. This is the largest single station-keeping cost — more than drag makeup and deorbit combined for a multi-year mission. Most operators deliberately omit inclination correction, accepting slow drift in exchange for dramatically lower propellant mass.

Deorbit (ΔV_deorbit) lowers the orbit to an altitude with a compliant natural re-entry time. From 550 km, lowering perigee to achieve a 5-year natural re-entry costs approximately 25–30 m/s — a one-time end-of-life expense.

3. The Rocket Equation and Propellant Mass

The Tsiolkovsky rocket equation gives the propellant mass required for a given Δv:

m_prop = m_dry × (exp(ΔV / (g₀ · Isp)) − 1)

where m_dry is the satellite dry mass, g₀ = 9.80665 m/s², and Isp is the specific impulse in seconds. Higher Isp means less propellant for the same Δv — the fundamental advantage of electric propulsion for station-keeping.

The tradeoff between Isp and thrust is critical. Chemical propulsion delivers high thrust — manoeuvres complete in minutes — but carries heavy propellant. Electric propulsion (Hall thrusters, gridded ion) delivers very high Isp — propellant mass is small — but thrust is low, so manoeuvres take hours to days. For Starlink, SpaceX uses krypton-propellant Hall thrusters at Isp ≈ 1,500–1,700 s, requiring only a few kilograms of propellant per satellite for a five-year mission.

4. Orbital Lifetime and the Decay Curve

The natural orbital lifetime of a satellite that has exhausted its propellant is determined by integrating the drag-decay equation from the current altitude to re-entry (~80 km). Lifetime is extremely sensitive to altitude near the boundary of IADC compliance.

At 550 km with solar-mean activity, a Starlink-class satellite (β ≈ 27 kg/m²) has a natural lifetime of roughly 7–12 years. At 600 km the same satellite takes 15–25 years — close to the IADC 25-year limit. At 650 km it exceeds 25 years under solar-minimum conditions, violating the guideline. This nonlinearity is why operators cluster at 540–600 km: small altitude differences produce large lifetime changes.

Solar activity introduces a factor of 2–3× uncertainty in lifetime at any given altitude. Satellites are designed to ensure that even at solar minimum, the deorbit manoeuvre is completed before the 25-year limit — or that natural re-entry occurs within the limit without a burn. This is more conservative than designing to the solar-mean case and is the correct engineering approach.

A satellite that loses propulsion at 600 km without a deorbit burn becomes debris for decades. The operational response is to target 540–570 km and execute a controlled deorbit burn to ~350 km at end of life, ensuring re-entry within weeks regardless of solar activity.

5. Collision Avoidance and the Conjunction Problem

Station-keeping is not only about maintaining the designed slot — it is also about avoiding collisions. When a conjunction is predicted with a probability of collision above the operator's threshold (typically 1 in 10,000 to 1 in 1,000), the satellite performs a collision avoidance manoeuvre (CAM) — a small Δv (typically 0.1–1 m/s) that moves it out of the predicted conjunction corridor. For a large constellation, CAMs happen at high frequency: Starlink reportedly executes thousands per year across its fleet.

The Δv cost of CAMs is small per event but non-trivial in aggregate, and each CAM perturbs the along-track position, requiring subsequent routine station-keeping to restore the nominal slot. Operators budget for CAM propellant separately from drag makeup and inclination station-keeping.

As more constellations are deployed, the number of tracked objects increases, the number of conjunction predictions grows, and the operational burden of CAM screening escalates. This is one of the motivations for inter-operator coordination and the ITU coordination framework discussed in Modules 9 and 10.

6. Propellant as a Mission Lifetime Constraint

The total propellant mass on the satellite is fixed at launch. It determines the maximum achievable ΔV_total and, through the Δv budget, the maximum mission lifetime. This creates a hard coupling between design life and propellant allocation that drives the constellation business case.

A five-year design life requires a certain ΔV_total (drag + CAM + deorbit + margin). This Δv sets the required propellant mass via the rocket equation. The propellant mass adds to the launch mass, increasing the per-satellite launch cost. The lifetime sets the replacement rate — a five-year satellite requires fleet replacement every five years, driving manufacturing cadence and recurring cost. Longer design lives reduce replacement costs but require heavier satellites with more propellant.

Starlink targets approximately five years of operational life per satellite, minimising satellite complexity while keeping propellant mass modest. The combination of high-Isp electric propulsion and a modest design life is the dominant cost architecture for first-generation LEO broadband constellations.

7. End-of-Life and the Deorbit Imperative

Every satellite in a LEO constellation must have a credible deorbit plan before launch. The regulatory landscape is tightening: the FCC now requires US-licensed LEO operators to deorbit within 5 years of end of mission for constellations approved after 2022 — a major tightening from the IADC's 25-year guideline. The ESA Zero Debris Charter sets a goal of zero debris added to the orbital environment from new missions.

Achieving five-year deorbit compliance from 550 km requires either a controlled deorbit burn or an initial altitude low enough for natural re-entry within five years. At 550 km natural re-entry takes 7–25 years depending on solar activity — too variable to rely on without a burn — making a controlled deorbit manoeuvre effectively mandatory for five-year compliance.

The design implications are three. First, enough propellant must remain at end of life to execute the deorbit burn; this is a reserved portion of the total budget. Second, the deorbit burn must be reliable, which argues for redundant propulsion or propellant margin. Third, satellites that lose propulsion early must still re-enter within the compliance period — which at 550 km they will do under solar-mean and solar-max conditions, but not necessarily at solar minimum, a residual risk operators mitigate by targeting operational altitudes at 540–560 km rather than 590–600 km.

FCC 5-YEAR DEORBIT RULE (2022) For new US-licensed LEO systems authorised after September 2022, the FCC requires disposal within 5 years of end of mission. This applies to the largest constellation operators and effectively makes a controlled deorbit burn mandatory for most architectures above 500 km. Non-compliant satellites cannot be relicensed.

Exercise 1 — Rocket Equation and Propulsion Comparison

Compute propellant mass for typical mission Δv budgets across several propulsion systems.

import math

G0 = 9.80665   # m/s^2

def prop_mass(dry_kg, dv_m_s, isp_s):
    """Propellant mass from Tsiolkovsky rocket equation."""
    return dry_kg * (math.exp(dv_m_s / (G0 * isp_s)) - 1)

def mass_frac(dry_kg, dv_m_s, isp_s):
    """Propellant mass fraction."""
    mp = prop_mass(dry_kg, dv_m_s, isp_s)
    return mp / (dry_kg + mp)

dry = 300.0   # kg

systems = [
    ("Cold gas (N2)",        65),
    ("Monoprop hydrazine",  230),
    ("Bipropellant",        320),
    ("Hall thruster (Kr)", 1600),
    ("Gridded ion",        3000),
]

cases = [
    ("Drag + deorbit (no incl SK)",   90),
    ("Drag + incl SK + deorbit 5yr", 430),
]

for label, dv in cases:
    print(f"\nΔV = {dv} m/s  —  {label}")
    print(f"{'System':>22}{'m_prop (kg)':>13}{'Mass frac':>11}")
    print("-" * 48)
    for name, isp in systems:
        mp = prop_mass(dry, dv, isp)
        mf = mass_frac(dry, dv, isp) * 100
        print(f"{name:>22}{mp:>12.1f} kg{mf:>9.1f}%")

Exercise 2 — Orbital Lifetime Integrator

Estimate natural re-entry time as a function of altitude and solar activity by integrating the drag-decay equation.

import math

MU      = 398600.4418
RE      = 6378.137
RHO_REF = 2.0e-10   # kg/m^3 at 400 km, solar mean
H_SCALE = 70.0      # km scale height

SOLAR = {"min": 0.35, "mean": 1.0, "max": 2.8}

def density(alt_km, factor):
    return RHO_REF * factor * math.exp(-(alt_km - 400) / H_SCALE)

def decay_rate_km_day(alt_km, beta, factor):
    r   = (RE + alt_km) * 1e3
    rho = density(alt_km, factor)
    v   = math.sqrt(MU * 1e9 / r)
    da  = -2 * rho * v * r / beta   # m/s
    return da * 86400 / 1e3         # km/day

def lifetime_yr(start_alt, beta, factor, reentry=80, step=1.0):
    h = start_alt; days = 0.0
    while h > reentry:
        rate = decay_rate_km_day(h, beta, factor)
        if rate >= 0: return float('inf')
        days -= step / rate
        h -= step
    return days / 365.25

beta = 300 / (2.2 * 5)   # Starlink-class, ~27 kg/m^2

print(f"Natural lifetime (beta={beta:.0f} kg/m^2)\n")
print(f"{'Alt':>6}", end="")
for s in SOLAR: print(f"  {s:>10}", end="")
print(f"  {'IADC 25yr?':>12}")
print("-" * 58)

for alt in [400, 500, 550, 575, 600, 650, 700]:
    lifetimes = {s: lifetime_yr(alt, beta, f)
                 for s, f in SOLAR.items()}
    comply = ("YES" if all(v < 25 for v in lifetimes.values())
              else "MARGINAL" if any(v < 25 for v in lifetimes.values())
              else "NO")
    print(f"{alt:>5} km", end="")
    for s in SOLAR:
        lt = lifetimes[s]
        tag = f"{lt:.0f} yr" if lt < 200 else "> 200 yr"
        print(f"  {tag:>10}", end="")
    print(f"  {comply:>12}")

# 2. At 600 km solar min, does the 25-year limit apply?
# 3. At 550 km solar min, does a failed satellite re-enter in 25 years?

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 5: Constellation Mass Budget

Add station-keeping Δv and propellant mass to the Walker constellation model to produce the first fleet-level mass and launch-cost budget.

import math

G0     = 9.80665
MU     = 398600.4418
RE     = 6378.137
RHO0   = 2.0e-10
H_SC   = 70.0
SOLAR  = {"min": 0.35, "mean": 1.0, "max": 2.8}

def drag_dv_yr(alt_km, beta_kg_m2, solar_case="mean"):
    """Approximate annual drag makeup Δv in m/s."""
    r   = (RE + alt_km) * 1e3
    rho = RHO0 * SOLAR[solar_case] * math.exp(-(alt_km - 400) / H_SC)
    v   = math.sqrt(MU * 1e9 / r)
    return rho * v**2 / beta_kg_m2 * 365.25 * 86400   # m/s/yr

def mission_dv(alt, life_yr, beta,
               incl_sk=False, cam_yr=5.0,
               deorbit=28.0, solar="mean"):
    """Total mission ΔV budget."""
    dv_drag  = drag_dv_yr(alt, beta, solar) * life_yr
    dv_incl  = (7600 * math.radians(0.5) * life_yr) if incl_sk else 0
    dv_cam   = cam_yr * life_yr
    return dv_drag + dv_incl + dv_cam + deorbit

def prop_mass(dry, dv, isp):
    return dry * (math.exp(dv / (G0 * isp)) - 1)

LAUNCH_KG = 6000   # $/kg

configs = [
    # name, alt, life_yr, beta, incl_sk, solar, dry_kg, isp, n_sats
    ("Starlink-like",    550, 5, 27, False, "mean", 300, 1600, 1584),
    ("OneWeb-like",      600, 5, 25, False, "mean", 150, 1600,  648),
    ("High-perf 7yr",   550, 7, 30, True,  "max",  250, 1600,  400),
]

print(f"{'Config':>16}{'ΔV (m/s)':>10}{'m_prop':>9}{'wet(kg)':>9}"
      f"{'fleet(t)':>10}{'cost $M':>10}")
print("-" * 66)
for name, alt, life, beta, isk, sol, dry, isp, n in configs:
    dv  = mission_dv(alt, life, beta, isk, solar=sol)
    mp  = prop_mass(dry, dv, isp)
    wet = dry + mp
    ft  = wet * n / 1e3
    cost= wet * n * LAUNCH_KG / 1e6
    print(f"{name:>16}{dv:>9.0f} m/s{mp:>8.1f}kg"
          f"{wet:>8.0f}kg{ft:>8.1f}t  ${cost:>7.0f}M")

print("\nBreakdown — Starlink-like:")
dv_s = mission_dv(550, 5, 27, False, solar="mean")
print(f"  Drag makeup:  {drag_dv_yr(550,27,'mean')*5:.1f} m/s")
print(f"  CAM budget:   {5*5:.1f} m/s")
print(f"  Deorbit:      28.0 m/s")
print(f"  Total:        {dv_s:.1f} m/s")
print(f"  m_prop (HET): {prop_mass(300, dv_s, 1600):.2f} kg")
