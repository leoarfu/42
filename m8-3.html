Gateway Placement & the PoP Topology

MODULE 8
GROUND SEGMENT & OPERATIONS
CORE

m8-2 said how many gateways a target availability demands — several diversity sites per location — but not where they go. This lesson places them. Gateway placement is a joint optimisation over four constraints that routinely conflict: the rain climate (m8-2 wants dry sites, where fewer diversity gateways are needed), the backhaul (gateways need fibre to an internet point of presence, which favours sites near existing network infrastructure), the regulatory access (a gateway needs landing rights and a spectrum licence in its host country), and the mesh egress geography (where the traffic actually wants to come down, which the routing of Module 7 shapes). These pull in different directions — the dry desert with perfect rain statistics is often far from any internet exchange, and the country with the best backhaul may have the most restrictive licensing — and gateway placement is the art of resolving them into a concrete map of sites. This lesson also introduces the alternative to building gateways at all: ground-station-as-a-service, where an operator rents antenna time from a provider rather than constructing sites, and the cloud convergence that co-locates gateways with cloud data centres so traffic enters the cloud directly.

The lesson's verified anchors size the ground network: a constellation delivering 10 Tbps of aggregate traffic needs on the order of 50 gateway locations at ~200 Gbps each (capacity-driven), which the m8-2 diversity requirement multiplies to ~150 physical sites, each needing a fibre backhaul equal to its capacity — some 10 Tbps of terrestrial fibre in total. The ground segment is a large, expensive, geographically-distributed network in its own right, and this lesson maps it. The spine gains the gateway placement model — the tool that turns the diversity requirement and the mesh's egress needs into a set of sites, connected to the internet through a point-of-presence topology.

1. How many gateways, and what drives the count

Three independent forces set the gateway count, and the binding one is whichever is largest:

Capacity. The constellation delivers some aggregate traffic — call it 10 Tbps — and every bit of it must egress through a gateway. If each gateway handles ~200 Gbps (several feeder links, m8-1), the constellation needs at least ~50 gateway locations just to carry the traffic. This is usually the dominant driver for a high-capacity constellation: you need enough gateways to backhaul the total throughput, and more capacity means more gateways.

Coverage / co-visibility. In a bent-pipe constellation, gateways must tile the served area (m7-1's ~2,605 km reach), so coverage drives the count catastrophically upward. In a mesh constellation, this force nearly vanishes — traffic reaches a gateway continent over the ISLs, so gateways cluster on landmasses with backhaul rather than tiling for coverage. The mesh's whole ground-segment advantage (m7-1) is that it converts a coverage-driven gateway count into a capacity-driven one, which is far smaller.

Diversity. Each gateway location is really several diversity sites (m8-2), so the ~50 capacity-driven locations become ~150 physical sites at a 3× diversity factor. Diversity multiplies the site count without adding capacity — the extra sites are for availability, not throughput — and this multiplication is one of the ground segment's largest hidden costs.

The result: ~50 locations, ~150 sites, for a representative high-capacity mesh constellation. The number is large enough to make the ground segment a major system in its own right, and small enough — thanks to the mesh — to be buildable, which is exactly the m7-1 trade playing out in hardware.

2. The four placement constraints

Where each gateway goes is decided by four constraints that must all be satisfied and often conflict:

Rain climate. m8-2 showed availability is bought with geography; a dry-climate site needs fewer diversity gateways (higher single-site availability) than a wet one. So placement favours arid and temperate-dry regions — deserts, high plateaus, rain-shadowed valleys — where a gateway achieves high availability with less diversity. This is a strong pull toward specific, often remote, geographies.

Backhaul. A gateway is useless without a fat fibre connection to an internet point of presence (§3). This pulls placement toward existing network infrastructure — near internet exchanges, near metro fibre, near the terrestrial backbone — which tends to mean populated, connected regions, often the opposite of the dry-remote regions rain climate prefers.

Regulatory access. A gateway needs landing rights (permission to operate a satellite ground station) and a spectrum licence in its host country, plus data-sovereignty compliance for the traffic it carries. Some countries grant these readily; others restrict or forbid them. Regulatory access can rule out otherwise-ideal sites entirely and force gateways into second-choice geographies — a constraint of politics rather than physics.

Mesh egress geography. The mesh routes traffic to a gateway near where it needs to reach the ground (m7-4). Egressing near the traffic's destination minimises the total path (short ISL route, then terrestrial backhaul the rest of the way); egressing near the source wastes the mesh and adds terrestrial backhaul. So gateways want to be near the major traffic destinations — the population and data centres — which again pulls toward connected regions.

These four rarely align. The dry desert (perfect rain) is far from the internet exchange (bad backhaul) and may be in a restrictive jurisdiction (bad regulatory) and far from the traffic (bad egress). Gateway placement is the constrained optimisation that resolves the conflict — trading a little availability (a wetter site with more diversity) for much better backhaul, or accepting a longer mesh path to reach a permissive jurisdiction. There is no site that is best on all four axes; placement is the negotiated compromise.

3. The point-of-presence topology

A gateway connects to the internet through a point of presence (PoP) — a facility where the operator's network meets the terrestrial internet, exchanging traffic with other networks. The gateway's fibre backhaul carries its full capacity (~200 Gbps) to the nearest PoP, and at the PoP the operator peers with other networks (settlement-free traffic exchange) and buys transit (paid connectivity to the rest of the internet) — the standard terrestrial-ISP arrangements, now serving satellite-originated traffic. For a 50-gateway constellation each backhauling ~200 Gbps, the total backhaul is ~10 Tbps of fibre, a substantial terrestrial network that the "satellite" operator must build or lease alongside its space segment.

The PoP topology shapes the constellation's terrestrial footprint. A few large PoPs at major internet exchanges concentrate the peering and simplify the network but lengthen the backhaul from remote gateways; many small PoPs shorten the backhaul but fragment the peering. The operator's PoP-and-peering strategy — where to interconnect, whom to peer with, how much transit to buy — is a terrestrial-networking problem indistinguishable from a large ISP's, and it is a reminder that a satellite operator is, on the ground, an ISP: it must interconnect with the internet on the same commercial and technical terms as any other network. The space segment is exotic; the ground segment is a conventional, if large, terrestrial network.

4. Ground-station-as-a-service: rent, don't build

Building ~150 gateway sites — acquiring land, installing antennas, running backhaul, obtaining licences in dozens of countries — is a vast capital undertaking that delays a constellation's revenue for years. Ground-station-as-a-service (GSaaS) offers an alternative: rent antenna time from a provider that operates shared ground stations, paying per-contact or per-hour operating expense instead of building capital sites. The provider has already acquired the land, installed the antennas, obtained the licences, and connected the backhaul; the operator simply schedules time on them.

The trade is the classic build-versus-rent. Building gives full control, dedicated capacity, and lower long-run cost at scale, but demands enormous upfront capital and years of deployment. Renting (GSaaS) gives fast deployment, no capital sink, and geographic reach the operator could not build alone, but at a per-use cost that grows with usage and with less control over a shared resource. Many constellations use both: GSaaS to launch quickly and reach markets where building is impractical, and owned gateways where the traffic justifies the capital and control matters. GSaaS also lowers the barrier to entry — a small operator can access a global ground network without building one — which changes the competitive landscape of who can field a constellation at all.

5. Cloud convergence: the gateway at the data centre

The deepest trend in gateway placement is cloud convergence: co-locating the gateway with a cloud data centre, so that traffic descending from the constellation enters the cloud directly, without traversing the public internet first. A gateway at a cloud region means a user's traffic can reach a cloud-hosted application (the majority of internet traffic) with the shortest possible path — mesh to the gateway, then straight into the cloud — bypassing the terrestrial internet's routing entirely for cloud-destined traffic.

This aligns gateway placement with cloud geography: put gateways where the cloud regions are, because that is where the traffic is destined (§2's egress-near-destination principle, made concrete). It also blurs the line between the satellite operator and the cloud provider — a gateway inside a cloud data centre is a joint facility, and the commercial relationship (does the cloud provider host the gateway, does the operator run edge compute at the gateway) becomes part of the ground-segment strategy. Cloud convergence is where the ground segment stops being merely a backhaul and becomes an on-ramp to the cloud, and it is reshaping where gateways go: toward the data centres, because that is where the internet's traffic actually lives.

6. Placement as the ground segment's defining problem

Gateway placement ties together everything Part I built. The feeder link (m8-1) sets each gateway's capacity; the diversity requirement (m8-2) multiplies the site count; and this lesson resolves the four constraints into a map, connected through a PoP topology, built or rented via GSaaS, converging on the cloud. The output is the concrete ground network: a set of sites, each with a capacity, an availability, a backhaul, and a role in egressing the mesh's traffic.

And placement feeds back into the space segment. Where the gateways are determines where the mesh must route traffic to (m7-4), which shapes the ISL load; a constellation with gateways clustered on a few continents routes differently from one with gateways spread widely. The gateway map and the mesh routing co-evolve — the m7-7 export ("the gateways where the mesh touches down") was always a two-way constraint, and this lesson closes the loop: the mesh delivers traffic to gateways, and the gateways' placement shapes how the mesh delivers. With the ground network placed, Part I is complete: the constellation can reach the internet, at capacity, at availability, in the right places. Part II turns to keeping the constellation itself alive — the operations that fly the fleet.

8. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

Stage 3 of the spine is the gateway placement model: it takes the diversity requirement (m8-2's site count), the mesh's egress geography (m7-7's delivered-traffic map), and the four placement constraints, and produces a set of gateway sites — each with a location, a capacity, an availability, and a backhaul requirement — connected through a PoP topology. It scores candidate sites against the constraints, resolves the conflicts into a placement, and sizes the total backhaul. The ground network is now concrete: not "several diversity gateways" but a specific map of where they are and what they cost.

Part I is complete. The constellation can reach the internet — the feeder link carries the traffic (m8-1), diversity keeps it available (m8-2), and placement puts the gateways in the right places connected to the internet (m8-3). What Part I did not address is keeping the constellation itself flying: the satellites need commanding, their health needs monitoring, their anomalies need handling, and a fleet of thousands needs all of this without a human per satellite. That is operations, and it is Part II — beginning with the control link to every satellite, the TT&C that flies the fleet (m8-4).

Exercise 1 — Sizing the ground network

From constellation capacity to a gateway count.

import math

def gateway_count(total_tbps, per_gateway_gbps, diversity_sites):
    locations = math.ceil(total_tbps*1000 / per_gateway_gbps)
    return locations, locations*diversity_sites

def total_backhaul_tbps(locations, per_gateway_gbps):
    return locations*per_gateway_gbps/1000

for total in [1, 5, 10, 20]:
    loc, sites = gateway_count(total, 200, 3)
    bh = total_backhaul_tbps(loc, 200)
    print(f"{total:>2} Tbps constellation: {loc:>3} locations, "
          f"{sites:>3} sites, {bh:.0f} Tbps backhaul")

Exercise 2 — Placement under conflicting constraints

Score sites against the four axes.

def score_site(rain_avail, backhaul_gbps, regulatory_ok, egress_km):
    """Higher is better. Returns None if regulatory forbids."""
    if not regulatory_ok:
        return None
    rain_score = rain_avail                        # 0..1, dry = high
    backhaul_score = min(1.0, backhaul_gbps/200)   # need ~200 Gbps
    egress_score = max(0, 1 - egress_km/5000)      # near destination
    return round((rain_score + backhaul_score + egress_score)/3, 3)

# candidate sites: (name, rain_avail, backhaul, regulatory, egress_km)

    ("high plateau",          0.99,  150, True,  1500),
    ("restricted jurisdiction",0.99, 300, False, 500),
    ("wet but connected",     0.95,  400, True,  100),
]
print(f"{'site':<26}{'score':>8}")
for name, ra, bh, reg, eg in sites:
    s = score_site(ra, bh, reg, eg)
    print(f"{name:<26}{str(s) if s else 'FORBIDDEN':>8}")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 3: the placement model

Resolve the constraints into a ground network.

import math

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M8 SPINE PROJECT — GROUND SEGMENT
# Stage 3: gateway placement + PoP topology.
#   Stage 1 (m8-1): feeder link & gateway capacity.
#   Stage 2 (m8-2): diversity -> site count for availability.
#   Stage 3: place the sites against the four constraints,
#            size the backhaul, connect to PoPs. Part I complete.
#   Next: Part II operates the fleet (m8-4 TT&C onward).
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class GatewaySite:
    def __init__(self, name, rain_avail, backhaul_gbps, regulatory_ok,
                 egress_km, lat_lon):
        self.name, self.rain_avail = name, rain_avail
        self.backhaul, self.reg = backhaul_gbps, regulatory_ok
        self.egress_km, self.pos = egress_km, lat_lon

    def score(self, w_rain=1, w_bh=1, w_egress=1):
        if not self.reg: return None
        parts = [w_rain*self.rain_avail,
                 w_bh*min(1.0, self.backhaul/200),
                 w_egress*max(0, 1 - self.egress_km/5000)]
        return round(sum(parts)/(w_rain+w_bh+w_egress), 3)

class GroundSegment:
    def __init__(self, total_tbps, per_gateway_gbps=200, diversity=3):
        self.total_tbps = total_tbps
        self.per_gw = per_gateway_gbps
        self.diversity = diversity
        self.locations = math.ceil(total_tbps*1000/per_gateway_gbps)

    def physical_sites(self):
        return self.locations*self.diversity

    def total_backhaul_tbps(self):
        return self.locations*self.per_gw/1000

    def place(self, candidates, weights=(1,1,1)):
        scored = [(c.score(*weights), c) for c in candidates]
        viable = sorted([(s,c) for s,c in scored if s is not None],
                        key=lambda x: x[0], reverse=True)
        return viable[:self.locations]

    def report(self, candidates):
        print(f"  constellation: {self.total_tbps} Tbps")
        print(f"  gateway locations needed: {self.locations}")
        print(f"  physical sites (×{self.diversity} diversity): "
              f"{self.physical_sites()}")
        print(f"  total backhaul: {self.total_backhaul_tbps():.0f} Tbps")
        chosen = self.place(candidates)
        print(f"  top sites chosen:")
        for score, site in chosen:
            print(f"    {site.name:<24} score {score}")

candidates = [
    GatewaySite("dry plateau A",   0.995, 200, True,  1500, (34, -106)),
    GatewaySite("coastal metro B", 0.97,  400, True,  200,  (40, -74)),
    GatewaySite("desert C",        0.995, 100, True,  3000, (24, 54)),
    GatewaySite("restricted D",    0.99,  300, False, 500,  (55, 37)),
    GatewaySite("connected wet E", 0.95,  400, True,  100,  (1, 103)),
]
gs = GroundSegment(total_tbps=0.6)   # small: needs ~3 locations
gs.report(candidates)

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 1

Section 2's four constraints conflict, and section 3 revealed the satellite operator is, on the ground, an ISP. This means gateway placement is partly a networking-business decision (peering, transit, PoP location) and partly a physics decision (rain, co-visibility). Interrogate which dominates: for a real constellation, is the gateway map shaped more by where the good rain and clear sky are, or by where the internet's peering and cloud infrastructure already sit? Take a position on whether the ground segment is fundamentally a satellite problem or fundamentally a terrestrial-networking problem wearing a satellite costume — and what that implies about who an operator should hire to design it.

PROMPT 2

Section 4's build-versus-rent (GSaaS) trade and section 5's cloud convergence point in the same direction: toward the operator not owning its ground infrastructure, but renting antennas and co-locating with clouds. Extrapolate this: if GSaaS and cloud convergence win, the satellite operator becomes a space-segment company that outsources its entire ground segment to specialised providers. Is that the efficient future, or does it cede a strategic asset (control of where and how traffic egresses) to third parties? Decide whether a serious constellation should own its ground segment or rent it, and identify the specific capability that, if outsourced, would leave the operator dangerously dependent.

PROMPT 3

Section 5's cloud convergence optimises for cloud-destined traffic (the majority) by putting gateways at data centres. But this couples the constellation's ground segment to the cloud providers' geography — and the major cloud regions are concentrated in a few wealthy, connected countries. Consider the consequence for the constellation's coverage promise: the mesh serves oceans and poles (m7-1), but if gateways cluster at cloud regions, does traffic from a remote user have to cross the whole mesh to egress at a distant data centre, eroding the latency advantage that justified the mesh? Quantify the tension between cloud-convergent gateway placement (few, concentrated) and the mesh's global-coverage value (traffic egressing far from remote users), and decide whether they are compatible or in conflict.

PROMPT 4

The shape of the conclusions (capacity-driven count, diversity multiplication, backhaul-equals-capacity) is robust, but the specific site count that drives the ground-segment cost is assumption-dependent. Apply the load-bearing-assumption discipline that has run through the program: which single input (per-gateway capacity, diversity factor, total constellation capacity) most strongly determines the ground-segment cost, and what real-world measurement would an operator need to pin it down before committing capital? State whether the ~150-site figure is a design target or merely an order-of-magnitude illustration.
