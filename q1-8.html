OneWeb Deep Dive

QUARTER 1
MODERN CONSTELLATIONS
PART III

OneWeb is the most instructive case study in the modern LEO era — not because it succeeded, but because it failed spectacularly once, was rescued twice, and emerged with a fundamentally different business model than it started with. It began as a mass-market broadband vision, collapsed into Chapter 11 bankruptcy in March 2020 with 74 satellites in orbit and $3.4 billion spent, was acquired by a UK government and Indian conglomerate consortium, relaunched its constellation, and pivoted entirely to B2B and aviation markets where Eutelsat's acquisition in 2023 repositioned it as part of a hybrid LEO+GEO operator. This lesson traces that arc — original architecture and Greg Wyler's founding thesis, the structural reasons for the bankruptcy, the rescue and its terms, current constellation status and technical architecture, and the B2B/aviation commercial strategy that defines OneWeb today.

1. Greg Wyler and the Founding Thesis

```

OneWeb was founded by Greg Wyler in 2012, though its direct antecedent was O3b Networks, which Wyler had founded in 2007 and sold to SES in 2013. Wyler's founding thesis for OneWeb was more ambitious than O3b's: not a medium-Earth-orbit constellation serving underserved enterprise markets, but a true LEO mega-constellation capable of delivering broadband connectivity to every person on Earth. The mission was explicitly humanitarian in framing — Wyler frequently cited the 3+ billion people without meaningful internet access as the target constituency, and positioned OneWeb as infrastructure for closing the global digital divide.

The original business logic rested on unit cost reduction at scale. Wyler's argument was that a purpose-designed small satellite, manufactured in large numbers through a highly automated factory, could achieve per-satellite costs of $500,000 or less — low enough that a 648-satellite constellation could be built and launched for under $3 billion, generating sufficient capacity to serve both underserved populations at subsidised rates and commercial customers at margins that would sustain the business. This cost thesis was the constellation's central financial assumption, and it proved optimistic in ways that contributed directly to the 2020 bankruptcy.

Wyler's background shaped the architecture in a specific way. O3b had been a GEO-adjacent play — medium orbits, large enterprise customers, fibre-quality latency that justified premium pricing. OneWeb was conceptually the opposite: low orbits, mass-market scale, commodity pricing, and global coverage including polar regions that no GEO constellation could serve. Wyler wanted to reach the places no existing infrastructure reached, which drove the constellation's orbital design toward high-inclination orbits with near-polar coverage.

The SoftBank relationship

OneWeb's most consequential early decision was accepting a $1 billion investment from SoftBank's Vision Fund in 2016 — at the time the largest single investment in a satellite company. SoftBank's involvement provided capital for the constellation buildout but also created a dependency that became structurally dangerous when SoftBank's Vision Fund encountered portfolio stress in 2019–2020. The SoftBank relationship brought additional investors into the OneWeb cap table, including Airbus (which became the joint-venture manufacturing partner), Qualcomm, Hughes Network Systems, Grupo Salinas, Intelsat (briefly), and the Virgin Group. This broad investor syndicate reflected confidence in the LEO broadband thesis but also created a complex governance structure that would complicate the bankruptcy proceedings.

The Airbus manufacturing joint venture

OneWeb's satellite manufacturing strategy was to build a dedicated high-volume factory in Toulouse, France, through a joint venture with Airbus called OneWeb Satellites. The factory was designed to produce satellites at a rate of several per day when at full production — an unprecedented manufacturing velocity for spacecraft. The Toulouse factory ultimately built the Gen1 OneWeb satellites. A second factory was planned in Florida (Exploration Park near Cape Canaveral) to serve the US market and provide manufacturing redundancy, but was never brought to full production before the bankruptcy.

2. Original Constellation Architecture

```

OneWeb's original constellation design was a 648-satellite Walker constellation at 1,200 km altitude in 18 orbital planes with 36 satellites per plane, at an inclination of 87.9° — effectively polar orbits, providing global coverage including the poles. This architectural choice was deliberate and distinguishing: a near-polar 87.9° inclination provides coverage at all latitudes including the Arctic and Antarctic, which GEO satellites cannot serve and which even Starlink's initial 53° inclination shells covered poorly. OneWeb's polar coverage capability was from day one a core competitive differentiator, particularly for maritime routes (transpolar shipping, Arctic oil and gas) and high-latitude government and defence customers.

Altitude selection: 1,200 km

The choice of 1,200 km altitude placed OneWeb in the lower portion of the Van Allen inner radiation belt — a zone of elevated energetic particle flux that requires radiation-hardened components and reduces satellite lifetime compared to lower orbits. This was a significant design compromise. The motivation for 1,200 km over a lower orbit like Starlink's 550 km was coverage geometry: at 1,200 km, each satellite's footprint on the ground is larger, meaning fewer satellites are needed to achieve global coverage. At 550 km, a 648-satellite constellation would leave significant coverage gaps at high latitudes; at 1,200 km, 648 satellites provides continuous global coverage with generous elevation angle margins.

The radiation environment at 1,200 km drove component selection costs upward compared to Starlink's 550 km architecture, contributing to the per-satellite cost overruns that preceded the bankruptcy. The trade-off between altitude (coverage per satellite vs radiation hardening cost) was one of the fundamental architectural decisions where OneWeb's original team and Starlink's team reached opposite conclusions — Starlink accepted more satellites at lower altitude and lower cost per satellite; OneWeb chose fewer satellites at higher altitude but higher cost per satellite due to the radiation environment.

Satellite design: the Gen1 OneWeb satellite

The Gen1 OneWeb satellite was designed for mass production, with a target unit cost of approximately $500,000 at high production volume. Each satellite had a mass of approximately 147 kg — significantly lighter than Starlink v1.0 (260 kg), reflecting the smaller aperture of OneWeb's user-link antennas. The satellite used Ku-band for user links and Ka-band for gateway feeder links, the same frequency allocation as Starlink Gen1. Propulsion was provided by a cold gas thruster system for station-keeping and deorbit — adequate for the 1,200 km operational altitude but not capable of significant orbital manoeuvring.

Critically, the Gen1 OneWeb satellite design had no inter-satellite links. All traffic was relayed to the nearest ground gateway — a bent-pipe architecture identical to Starlink v1.0. This means OneWeb, even after full Gen1 constellation deployment, would depend entirely on the ground gateway network for routing. In regions without nearby gateways, service quality would be constrained by the gateway geometry, exactly as early Starlink was. OneWeb's planned Gen2 satellite design included ISLs, but Gen2 was never built under the original company.

Gateway and ground segment architecture

OneWeb planned a network of approximately 40–50 ground gateways globally, using Ka-band feeder links to connect the constellation to the terrestrial internet. The gateway siting strategy prioritised geographic coverage over density: fewer, larger gateways rather than the denser gateway network Starlink built. This made economic sense for OneWeb's B2B focus — enterprise and government customers willing to pay premium prices required connectivity solutions that could be engineered end-to-end, and a sparser but carefully sited gateway network was sufficient for the initial commercial launch. The user terminal for OneWeb service was a small Ku-band flat-panel terminal approximately 40 cm in diameter, designed for easy installation and suitable for maritime, aviation, and fixed enterprise installations.

DEEPER CONTEXT — THE 1,200 KM ALTITUDE TRADE-OFF

OneWeb's choice of 1,200 km placed its constellation at the inner edge of the Van Allen radiation belt's proton flux region. Energetic protons at this altitude damage semiconductor junctions in solar cells and electronics, degrading performance and shortening satellite lifetime. The original OneWeb design life was 5 years — shorter than the 7-year design life of Starlink Gen1 at 550 km, which sits below the bulk of the proton flux. The radiation hardening required at 1,200 km added approximately 15–25% to electronic component costs compared to a comparable satellite at 550 km. Ironically, the radiation environment that justified choosing 1,200 km for coverage reasons (larger footprint, fewer satellites needed) simultaneously made each satellite more expensive, partially undermining the cost-reduction logic.

Debris mitigation at 1,200 km is also more challenging than at 550 km. At 550 km, atmospheric drag provides meaningful passive orbit decay over 5–7 years. At 1,200 km, orbital decay without active propulsion takes decades to centuries. OneWeb's deorbit strategy relies on its cold gas thrusters actively lowering the orbit to a point where drag-assisted decay brings the satellite down within 25 years — the ITU and FCC standard for post-mission disposal. The adequacy of this strategy at scale (648+ satellites) has been scrutinised by orbital debris researchers.

3. The Road to Bankruptcy: 2020

```

OneWeb's Chapter 11 filing on 27 March 2020 was one of the largest satellite industry bankruptcies in history, exceeded only by Iridium Original (1999) and ICO Global Communications (1999). Understanding why it happened requires tracing three concurrent failures: a cost thesis that proved too optimistic, a market entry that was too slow, and a financing structure that became brittle when a single key investor withdrew.

The cost overrun problem

OneWeb's financial model was built on the assumption that its Toulouse factory, once at full production rate, would manufacture satellites at approximately $500,000 each. This unit cost target required the factory to reach a sustained production rate of several satellites per day and for the supply chain to deliver components at volumes that enabled commodity pricing. Neither condition was fully achieved before the bankruptcy. The factory took longer to reach design production velocity than planned; supply chain negotiations for radiation-hardened components at volume were more complex than anticipated; and first-of-kind integration and test costs were higher than the financial model assumed. Independent estimates placed actual satellite unit costs at $900,000–$1.2 million per satellite in the early production runs — roughly double the target — a gap that materially changed the economics of the full 648-satellite buildout.

Market entry delay

OneWeb's first launch occurred in February 2019 — approximately three years after Starlink's regulatory filing and nine months before Starlink's first batch of 60 operational satellites launched in May 2019. The companies were roughly contemporaneous in first launch, but OneWeb's constellation buildout was slower: by March 2020, it had 74 satellites in orbit (six launches on Soyuz), while Starlink had conducted six Starlink launches totalling 360 satellites and was preparing to begin beta service. The slower OneWeb buildout reflected both manufacturing constraints and a more conservative launch campaign pace. With no commercial service revenue to partially offset costs, OneWeb was entirely dependent on investor capital for every dollar of constellation buildout — a dependency that became fatal when investor sentiment changed.

The SoftBank withdrawal

The proximate cause of the bankruptcy was SoftBank's decision in late February 2020 to decline participating in a planned $2 billion funding round that OneWeb urgently needed to continue operations. SoftBank's Vision Fund was under severe pressure in early 2020 from the WeWork collapse (announced September 2019), portfolio impairments across multiple investments, and the early signals of COVID-19's economic impact. SoftBank had already invested approximately $1 billion in OneWeb and was the single largest shareholder. When it declined to continue funding, other investors — uncertain about the constellation's commercial trajectory and facing their own COVID-related financial pressures — declined to fill the gap. Without the $2 billion, OneWeb had insufficient cash to continue launch operations. It filed for Chapter 11 within weeks of SoftBank's withdrawal, laying off approximately 85% of its workforce and halting the launch campaign.

THE STRUCTURAL FRAGILITY OF SINGLE-INVESTOR DEPENDENCY OneWeb's bankruptcy illustrates a structural risk pattern that recurs across the historical LEO failures: concentration of funding in a small number of investors whose continued participation is essential to project viability. Iridium Original depended on Motorola as guarantor; OneWeb depended on SoftBank as the majority capital provider and implicit lead investor whose participation signalled confidence to the syndicate. When SoftBank withdrew, not only did its direct capital disappear but the withdrawal signal caused the rest of the syndicate to step back. The lesson for constellation financing is that a diverse, government-anchored, or revenue-backed capital structure is more resilient than a VC-style lead investor model — which is directly reflected in the structure of OneWeb's rescue (UK government as anchor) and in the EU's approach to IRIS² (government-anchored from inception).

4. The Rescue: Bharti, UK Government, and the Chapter 11 Exit

```

OneWeb emerged from Chapter 11 bankruptcy in November 2020 — seven months after filing — through an acquisition led by two unlikely partners: Bharti Global, the international investment arm of Indian conglomerate Bharti Enterprises (owner of Airtel, one of India's largest telecom operators), and the UK Government. The acquisition price was approximately $1 billion for the entire OneWeb estate — 74 satellites in orbit, spectrum licences, manufacturing assets, intellectual property, and the Airbus joint venture — against the $3.4 billion that had been invested. Creditors received cents on the dollar; equity holders were largely wiped out.

Why the UK Government?

The UK Government's participation in the OneWeb rescue is best understood as a strategic industrial and sovereign connectivity decision taken in the context of Brexit. In the months before the OneWeb bankruptcy, the UK had been negotiating its continued participation in the EU's Galileo navigation satellite programme — and the EU had determined that post-Brexit, the UK could not participate in Galileo's security-critical PRS (Public Regulated Service) as a non-member state. The UK government was therefore actively evaluating sovereign alternatives to Galileo, and OneWeb's low-Earth-orbit constellation was identified as a potential asset for building UK sovereign satellite communications and, speculatively, a navigation capability.

The UK Government invested approximately £400 million for approximately a 20% equity stake in the restructured OneWeb, alongside Bharti's investment of approximately $500 million for a similar stake. Importantly, the acquisition gave the UK Government board representation and specific rights over OneWeb's UK operations and spectrum licences — providing a mechanism for sovereign control over the asset if needed. The navigation use case was subsequently deprioritised as it became clear that OneWeb's Ku-band constellation architecture was ill-suited for navigation (which requires dedicated L-band signals and a different orbital geometry), but the connectivity rationale remained and was operationally valid.

Bharti's strategic rationale

Bharti Global's participation was strategically coherent: Bharti Airtel serves hundreds of millions of mobile customers across India and sub-Saharan Africa, many of them in rural and underserved areas where satellite backhaul for 4G/5G towers would be a natural application. OneWeb's Ku-band constellation provided satellite connectivity infrastructure that Bharti could deploy as a distribution channel — using its existing enterprise and telecom relationships to sell OneWeb capacity to enterprises, ISPs, and telecom operators in its served markets. The acquisition effectively gave Bharti a satellite wholesale capability without the need to build a constellation from scratch.

Post-acquisition restructuring

The restructured OneWeb retained a small core team of approximately 300 employees — a skeleton crew compared to the 600+ before bankruptcy. The Toulouse factory was preserved and restarted under modified commercial terms with Airbus. The launch campaign was restarted using Soyuz rockets, which OneWeb had been using since its first launch, and which were available through Arianespace at competitive rates. The post-bankruptcy constellation buildout accelerated significantly: from 74 satellites at bankruptcy to 648 satellites (the complete Gen1 constellation) by the end of 2022. The speed of this reconstitution was made possible by the Toulouse factory's ability to return to production quickly once funding was secured.

SPECTRUM AS THE CROWN JEWEL OneWeb's bankruptcy estate was worth far more than its 74 satellites. The most valuable asset was its ITU spectrum coordination filings — an extensive set of Ku-band and Ka-band spectrum rights that had been filed and defended over years of ITU regulatory process. 5. Reconstitution: Relaunch, Soyuz, and the Ukraine Disruption

```

OneWeb's post-bankruptcy launch campaign resumed in December 2020 with a Soyuz launch from the Baikonur Cosmodrome in Kazakhstan — the same launch vehicle and site that had carried all of OneWeb's pre-bankruptcy satellites. The reconstitution plan required approximately 14 additional Soyuz launches to reach the 648-satellite constellation target. Between December 2020 and early 2022, OneWeb conducted 13 successful Soyuz launches, placing 428 additional satellites into orbit and reaching a total of approximately 502 satellites.

The Russia–Ukraine crisis and Soyuz suspension

On 24 February 2022, Russia invaded Ukraine. The geopolitical consequences for OneWeb were immediate and severe. Russia's Roscosmos state space agency announced that it would suspend Soyuz launch services for OneWeb unless OneWeb provided guarantees that its satellites would not be used for military purposes and unless the UK Government divested its stake — conditions that OneWeb could not accept. A launch campaign that had been scheduled for 5 March 2022 at the Baikonur Cosmodrome was halted: Roscosmos demanded the return of already-integrated OneWeb satellites from the launch pad, and after tense negotiations they were eventually returned, though Roscosmos retained 36 satellites that had already been integrated into the Soyuz vehicle. OneWeb lost those 36 satellites — a total loss with no compensation.

The Soyuz suspension left OneWeb's constellation at approximately 502 satellites — short of the 648 needed for complete global coverage — with no alternative launch vehicle immediately available. This was a genuine operational crisis: OneWeb had no redundant launch provider relationship because Soyuz had been its sole launch partner since inception. The suspension exposed a single-point-of-failure in the supply chain that the company had failed to mitigate despite the obvious geopolitical context (the UK Government's £400M investment should have prompted immediate launch vehicle diversification).

Launch vehicle pivot: SpaceX and Arianespace

OneWeb's response was rapid and pragmatic — it contracted with two alternative launch providers almost simultaneously. SpaceX agreed to launch OneWeb satellites on Falcon 9, despite the obvious competitive dynamic (Starlink is SpaceX's own LEO broadband constellation). The first SpaceX launch of OneWeb satellites took place in March 2023. Arianespace contracted to use Ariane 6, Vega-C, and eventually the new Arianegroup launch vehicles. The mix of providers — a European prime (Arianespace) and a US commercial (SpaceX) — also provided geopolitical diversification appropriate to a UK Government–co-owned asset. The per-launch cost of Falcon 9 was higher than Soyuz (which had been among the cheapest orbital launch vehicles available), but the urgency of completing the constellation overrode cost optimisation.

By mid-2023, OneWeb had completed the 648-satellite Gen1 constellation through a combination of the remaining Soyuz-launched satellites and Falcon 9 launches — a turnaround from the crisis that demonstrated operational resilience, albeit at significant unplanned cost.

SINGLE-LAUNCH-PROVIDER DEPENDENCY: A RECURRING RISK PATTERN OneWeb's Soyuz dependency illustrates a supply chain concentration risk that appears in multiple forms across the industry. Iridium Original depended on McDonnell Douglas Delta II vehicles; OneWeb depended on Soyuz; early Globalstar depended on Zenit. In each case, loss of the primary launch provider would have been catastrophic without a backup. Modern constellation operators have learned from these failures: SpaceX uses only its own vehicles (eliminating third-party risk but creating internal dependency); Amazon Kuiper has contracted with ULA, New Glenn, and Arianespace simultaneously; Telesat Lightspeed has multi-vehicle contracts. For any architect evaluating a constellation's risk profile, launch provider concentration is a first-order infrastructure risk question — not a footnote.

6. Current Constellation Status and Technical Architecture

```

As of early 2025, OneWeb operates 618 active Gen1 satellites in its near-polar constellation at 1,200 km altitude — slightly below the full 648-satellite target due to satellite failures and deorbit decisions on early production units. The constellation provides continuous global coverage above approximately 40° latitude with reasonable coverage below that latitude, though with lower satellite density (fewer simultaneous visible satellites) near the equator than near the poles. This coverage profile is the inverse of Starlink's: Starlink's 53° inclination primary shells provide excellent mid-latitude coverage but reduced polar coverage; OneWeb's near-polar constellation provides excellent polar coverage but proportionally less mid-latitude satellite density.

Technical architecture: what Gen1 can and cannot do

The current Gen1 constellation has several important technical limitations that bound OneWeb's commercial offering. There are no inter-satellite links — every packet must travel to the nearest ground gateway, transit the terrestrial internet, and return. This creates a hard dependency on the gateway network and means that transoceanic routing incurs full round-trip latency through two uplinks and the terrestrial path, similar to Starlink pre-ISL. Latency is approximately 30–50 ms to a nearby gateway, but effective end-to-end latency for transoceanic routes can be 60–100 ms depending on gateway proximity and terrestrial routing.

Throughput per satellite on the Gen1 design is significantly lower than Starlink Gen1, reflecting the smaller aperture and power budget: approximately 7–8 Gbps per satellite in optimal conditions. At 618 active satellites, the aggregate constellation capacity is in the range of 4–5 Tbps — substantially less than Starlink's Gen1 capacity of approximately 20–40 Tbps. This capacity gap directly limits OneWeb's ability to serve mass-market residential customers and is the principal reason the B2B strategy — where volumes are lower but per-bit pricing is higher — is the commercially rational path.

Ground terminal: the OneWeb terminal ecosystem

OneWeb's user terminal is a Ku-band flat-panel phased array approximately 40 cm in diameter, purpose-designed for enterprise and mobility applications. Unlike Starlink's consumer-grade terminals with plug-and-play installation, OneWeb's terminal ecosystem targets professional installation and managed service delivery. Terminals are sold through distribution partners (telecom operators, system integrators, aviation connectivity providers) rather than direct-to-consumer. The terminal supports download speeds of 100–200 Mbps with latency of 30–50 ms — competitive for enterprise and aviation use cases, though below Starlink's peak residential performance. OneWeb has also qualified terminals for maritime and aviation installations, including aeronautical terminals certified for in-flight connectivity.

Gen2 planning: the post-Eutelsat question

OneWeb's Gen2 constellation design — which included ISLs, higher per-satellite capacity, and potentially a lower orbital altitude — was planned before the Eutelsat acquisition but has not been formally committed to as a funded programme as of early 2025. The Eutelsat merger (Section 9) has created uncertainty about whether the capital required for Gen2 will be available, and whether a standalone OneWeb Gen2 or a combined Eutelsat-OneWeb GEO+LEO next-generation architecture is the right investment. Gen2 planning continues, but a commercial launch decision with funded manufacturing contracts had not been announced as of early 2025.

7. B2B-Only Strategy: Rationale and Market Segments

```

OneWeb's post-bankruptcy commercial strategy is explicitly and exclusively B2B. The company does not sell directly to individual consumers. Every OneWeb service is sold through a distribution channel — a telecom operator, system integrator, ISP, maritime service provider, or aviation connectivity provider — who purchases capacity wholesale and packages it for end customers. This is a fundamental strategic departure from the original Greg Wyler vision of mass-market consumer broadband and from Starlink's direct-to-consumer model.

Why B2B?

The B2B-only decision reflects an honest assessment of where OneWeb's Gen1 constellation can compete given its technical constraints. With approximately 4–5 Tbps of aggregate capacity and no ISLs, OneWeb cannot win in mass-market residential broadband against Starlink's 20–40 Tbps Gen1 constellation and growing Gen2 capacity. The per-bit economics of mass-market residential service require volume and low customer acquisition costs that OneWeb's smaller constellation and higher per-bit cost cannot sustain. B2B enterprise customers, by contrast, value reliability, managed service quality, SLA guarantees, and specific geographic capabilities — notably polar and high-latitude coverage — over raw throughput. OneWeb's near-polar constellation excels precisely at the high-latitude coverage that Starlink's primary shells underserve.

The B2B model also avoids the terminal subsidy trap that hurt Starlink early on. Starlink subsidised its consumer terminal from $499 (with an estimated manufacturing cost of $1,000–1,500 per unit at launch) to accelerate subscriber growth — a cash cost that totalled hundreds of millions of dollars before terminal costs fell to match retail pricing. OneWeb terminals are sold at professional installation prices through distribution partners, and the cost of terminal provisioning is embedded in the partner's service economics rather than subsidised by OneWeb directly.

Government and defence

OneWeb's most important B2B market segment is government and defence. The UK Government's equity stake creates an implicit preferential relationship with UK government customers — the Ministry of Defence, GCHQ-adjacent programmes, and UK-aligned NATO members have natural procurement reasons to prefer a UK sovereign-adjacent satellite operator over a US-controlled competitor. OneWeb has pursued government connectivity contracts in the UK, EU (for programmes not covered by IRIS²), and allied nations. The near-polar constellation's coverage of Arctic maritime routes and high-latitude military training ranges is specifically relevant to NATO's northern flank priorities — a capability Starlink cannot fully match with its mid-latitude primary shells.

Enterprise backhaul and telecom operators

OneWeb sells backhaul capacity to telecom operators deploying 4G/5G towers in areas where terrestrial fibre backhaul is unavailable or prohibitively expensive. Bharti Airtel's market presence in rural India and sub-Saharan Africa provides a natural distribution channel for this use case: a rural tower site in Rajasthan or Uganda that lacks fibre can receive 4G/5G backhaul via a OneWeb terminal, extending mobile network coverage without the cost of fibre installation. This wholesale backhaul model allows OneWeb to serve millions of indirect users (mobile network customers) through a small number of direct B2B contracts with telecom operators.

Maritime enterprise

OneWeb serves commercial maritime customers — cargo vessels, fishing fleets, offshore energy platforms — through maritime service provider partners. The near-polar constellation provides reliable coverage on transpolar Arctic shipping routes (increasingly economically significant as Arctic ice reduction extends the northern sea route season), oil and gas platforms in the North Sea and Arctic Ocean, and high-latitude fishing operations. These are exactly the customers that Starlink's initial 53° inclination shells underserved and where OneWeb's polar coverage provides a genuine differentiated capability. Maritime pricing is in the range of several hundred to several thousand dollars per month per vessel depending on service tier.

8. Aviation: OneWeb's Primary Growth Market

```

Aviation in-flight connectivity is OneWeb's most strategically important commercial market and the segment in which it has invested most heavily in terms of terminal qualification, airline relationships, and distribution partnerships. The commercial aviation IFC (in-flight connectivity) market is projected to grow from approximately $3 billion in 2023 to $7–9 billion by 2030, driven by passenger expectations for broadband-quality connectivity and airline revenue opportunities from connectivity services. OneWeb is competing for this market directly with Starlink Aviation, Panasonic Avionics (which distributes both GEO-based capacity and LEO solutions), ViaSat (now part of Inmarsat/Viasat), and SES/O3b.

The LEO advantage for aviation

Aviation in-flight connectivity has historically been dominated by GEO satellite systems — Ku-band and Ka-band services from operators like Inmarsat, ViaSat, and Panasonic. GEO connectivity provides adequate capacity for most cabin use cases but suffers from several structural limitations: the 600–700 ms round-trip latency of GEO links makes real-time applications (video calls, certain enterprise applications) frustrating; the GEO satellite's footprint is fixed and cannot be steered to track aircraft, so aircraft flying at high latitudes (polar routes) experience degraded or no GEO service; and GEO capacity per aircraft is limited by the fixed spot-beam architecture of the GEO payload.

LEO constellations address all three limitations: latency drops to 30–60 ms, well within the threshold for real-time applications; the constellation's global coverage follows aircraft on any route including polar; and the capacity-per-aircraft scales with the number of satellites in view. For an airline operating a mix of mid-latitude and transoceanic polar routes, a LEO IFC provider is uniquely able to offer a consistent service level on all routes — the transoceanic business class cabin video call that works over the North Atlantic also works over the pole to Tokyo.

OneWeb's aviation terminal and partners

OneWeb's aviation terminal is qualified for commercial aviation installation under relevant airworthiness standards. The terminal is a flat-panel Ku-band phased array designed for fuselage conformal mounting, optimised for the SWaP (size, weight, and power) constraints of commercial aircraft. The aeronautical terminal supports download speeds of up to 195 Mbps per aircraft — lower than Starlink Aviation's quoted 350 Mbps but adequate for typical cabin connectivity use cases (streaming, email, enterprise VPN) at commercial load factors.

OneWeb has pursued aviation market access primarily through distribution partnerships rather than direct airline sales. The key partnerships include SES (the Luxembourg-based GEO/MEO operator, which distributes OneWeb capacity on certain aviation routes), aviation connectivity integrators such as Satcom Direct and Global Eagle, and regional airlines seeking LEO upgrades from aging GEO systems. OneWeb's distribution-through-partners model means it does not carry the cost of direct airline certification programmes (which require Supplemental Type Certificate approval per aircraft type), leaving that cost with the integrator partner — a capital-efficient approach that trades margin for reach.

The Starlink Aviation competitive threat

Starlink's entry into aviation IFC — with higher throughput (350 Mbps), direct airline relationships, and a growing track record with Alaska Airlines, United Airlines, and others — is the most significant competitive threat to OneWeb's aviation ambitions. Starlink's advantage is structural: more capacity per satellite, lower latency via ISLs on transoceanic routes, and SpaceX's vertical integration enabling aggressive pricing. OneWeb's counter-positioning is on reliability-over-spectacle: enterprise airline customers want proven, managed service with contractual SLAs, not the fastest possible peak throughput. OneWeb's B2B-through-integrators model, combined with its polar coverage superiority on high-latitude routes, provides a defensible niche — but it requires OneWeb to win on service quality and coverage rather than throughput.

POLAR ROUTES: ONEWEB'S STRUCTURAL AVIATION ADVANTAGE The defining aviation use case where OneWeb's near-polar constellation has a structural advantage over Starlink's primary shell architecture is transpolar routing. The polar great circle route from London Heathrow to Tokyo Narita (approximately 9,550 km) passes north of 70° latitude for a significant portion of the flight. On this route, Starlink's 53° inclination primary shells provide no coverage — aircraft on this routing rely on Starlink's smaller polar shell (fewer satellites, lower density) or fall back to GEO service. OneWeb's 87.9° near-polar constellation provides continuous, high-elevation-angle satellite coverage on this route. 9. Eutelsat Merger and the Hybrid LEO+GEO Operator Thesis

```

In November 2022, Eutelsat Communications — one of Europe's largest GEO satellite operators with a fleet of approximately 36 GEO satellites — announced a merger with OneWeb, completing in September 2023. The combined entity, operating under the Eutelsat brand but incorporating OneWeb's LEO constellation, became the world's first major hybrid LEO+GEO satellite operator: a company owning and operating both geostationary high-capacity broadband and media distribution satellites alongside a low-Earth-orbit broadband constellation.

The merger logic

The merger thesis was driven by complementary strengths and existential pressures on both sides. Eutelsat's GEO business faced secular revenue decline: video distribution (the core GEO revenue driver, accounting for ~70% of Eutelsat's revenue) was under structural pressure from the shift of TV viewing to streaming delivered over terrestrial broadband, and GEO broadband was losing ground to LEO competitors. Without access to a LEO constellation, Eutelsat had no credible offering for the enterprise, maritime, and aviation markets that LEO was beginning to win. OneWeb provided that LEO asset.

From OneWeb's perspective, Eutelsat brought capital, a global distribution network through existing GEO customer relationships, and — critically — the financial resources to consider Gen2 constellation development. As a standalone entity, the restructured OneWeb was growing but remained capital-constrained relative to the investment required to maintain competitive constellation capabilities against Starlink's Gen2 deployment. The Eutelsat balance sheet and access to European capital markets provided a route to longer-term financial stability.

The hybrid LEO+GEO product vision

The combined Eutelsat-OneWeb commercial vision is a multi-orbit connectivity offering: LEO for enterprise, aviation, maritime, and any application requiring low latency; GEO for broadcast, media distribution, and high-throughput fixed broadband in served markets where GEO economics remain competitive. The combined satellite fleet — GEO satellites at 36 orbital slots plus 600+ LEO satellites — gives Eutelsat the ability to offer customers a single contract for multi-orbit connectivity, with automatic switching between LEO and GEO links depending on application requirements and coverage geography. This is the architecture that individual operators competing with only one orbit type cannot offer.

The integration of LEO and GEO service delivery is technically and commercially complex: different ground terminals, different latency profiles, different pricing models, and different regulatory regimes must be bridged in a coherent customer offering. As of early 2025, the OneWeb and Eutelsat commercial operations were being progressively integrated but had not yet reached a fully unified multi-orbit product portfolio.

Financial stress and capital concerns

The Eutelsat-OneWeb merger has not been without financial difficulty. Eutelsat's stock price declined significantly following the merger announcement and continued to underperform through 2023–2024, reflecting investor concern about the capital requirements of Gen2 constellation development, the continued decline of Eutelsat's video distribution revenue, and uncertainty about the combined entity's path to profitability. Eutelsat's net debt position following the merger exceeded €2 billion, and the company's ability to fund a full OneWeb Gen2 constellation while servicing existing debt and managing the GEO business transition has been a persistent market concern. Rating agency downgrades followed the merger, increasing the cost of any additional debt financing.

The EU's IRIS² programme (Infrastructure for Resilience, Interconnectivity and Security by Satellite) is a €2.4 billion government-funded LEO constellation designed to provide European sovereign broadband connectivity. import numpy as np

Re = 6_371  # km Earth radius

def visible_sats_walker(lat_deg, alt_km, incl_deg,
                         n_planes, n_per_plane, elev_min_deg=25):
    """
    Estimate mean satellites visible from a ground station at lat_deg.
    Simplified Walker coverage model — analytical approximation.
    """
    lat  = np.radians(abs(lat_deg))
    incl = np.radians(incl_deg)
    elev = np.radians(elev_min_deg)

    # Earth central half-angle for minimum elevation
    sin_rho = Re / (Re + alt_km) * np.cos(elev)
    rho = np.arcsin(sin_rho)
    lam = np.pi/2 - elev - rho   # coverage half-angle from satellite nadir

    # Single-satellite footprint as fraction of Earth surface
    fp_frac = (1 - np.cos(lam)) / 2

    total_sats  = n_planes * n_per_plane
    total_fp    = total_sats * fp_frac   # total footprint fractions (can exceed 1 = overlap)

    # Latitude weighting: for inclined Walker, satellite density is higher
    # near the inclination limit (Walker "bunching" effect)
    # For near-polar (incl ≈ 90°), density is roughly uniform with latitude
    # For inclined (incl = 53°), density peaks near ±53° and drops at poles
    if incl_deg >= 80:
        # Near-polar: roughly uniform density up to incl_deg, slight increase at poles
        if abs(lat_deg) <= incl_deg:
            lat_factor = 1.0 + 0.15 * (lat / incl)
        else:
            lat_factor = 0.05   # very few satellites beyond inclination
    else:
        # Inclined Walker: density enhancement near inclination limit
        if abs(lat_deg) < incl_deg - 5:
            lat_factor = 0.6 + 0.4 * np.sin(lat / incl * np.pi / 2)
        elif abs(lat_deg) <= incl_deg:
            lat_factor = 1.0  # peak density near inclination
        else:
            lat_factor = max(0.05, 1 - (abs(lat_deg) - incl_deg) / 15)

    # Expected visible satellites
    mean_vis = max(0.1, total_fp * lat_factor * 4)
    return min(mean_vis, total_sats * 0.15)  # cap at physically plausible

pcts = np.linspace(0.01, 0.30, 100)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(16, 5))
fig.patch.set_facecolor('#0a0a0a')
for ax in axes:
    ax.set_facecolor('#0d0d0d')
    ax.tick_params(colors='#888')
    for sp in ax.spines.values(): sp.set_color('#333')

# Left: revenue by segment and total vs penetration
ax = axes[0]
total_rev = np.zeros(len(pcts))
for name, seg in SEGMENTS.items():
    rev = [revenue_at_penetration(seg, p) for p in pcts]
    ax.fill_between(pcts*100, total_rev, total_rev + rev,
                    alpha=0.4, color=seg['color'])
    ax.plot(pcts*100, total_rev + rev, color=seg['color'],
            linewidth=1.5, label=name)
    total_rev += np.array(rev)

ax.plot(pcts*100, total_rev, color='white', linewidth=2.5, label='Total revenue')
ax.axhline(y=1000, color='#ffffff33', linewidth=1, linestyle='--')
ax.text(1.5, 1020, '$1B target', fontsize=8, color='#ffffff55')
ax.axvline(x=5, color='#ff004f33', linewidth=1, linestyle=':')
ax.text(5.2, 50, '5% pen.', fontsize=7, color='#ff004f66')
ax.set_xlabel('Average B2B market penetration (%)', color='#aaa')
ax.set_ylabel('Annual revenue ($M)', color='#aaa')
ax.set_title('OneWeb B2B Revenue Model\nby Segment and Penetration', color='#ccc')
ax.legend(facecolor='#111', edgecolor='#333', labelcolor='white', fontsize=7.5)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.07, color='#444')

# Centre: capacity utilisation vs penetration
ax = axes[1]
total_units  = []
cap_used_pct = []
for p in pcts:
    units = sum(total_units_at_penetration(seg, p) for seg in SEGMENTS.values())
    capacity_used_tbps = units * BITS_PER_SUBSCRIBER_MBPS / 1e6   # Mbps → Tbps
    total_units.append(units)
    cap_used_pct.append(capacity_used_tbps / CONSTELLATION_CAPACITY_TBPS * 100)

ax.fill_between(pcts*100, cap_used_pct, alpha=0.2,
                color=[('#ff004f' if c > 80 else '#39ff14') for c in cap_used_pct][0])
ax.plot(pcts*100, cap_used_pct, color='#00d9ff', linewidth=2)
ax.axhline(y=80, color='#ff004f55', linewidth=1, linestyle='--', label='80% cap threshold')
ax.axhline(y=100, color='#ff004f', linewidth=1.5, linestyle='-', label='Capacity limit')

# Find saturation penetration
sat_pct = None
for i, c in enumerate(cap_used_pct):
    if c >= 100:
        sat_pct = pcts[i] * 100
        break
if sat_pct:
    ax.axvline(x=sat_pct, color='#ff004f88', linewidth=1.5, linestyle=':')
    ax.text(sat_pct+0.3, 50, f'Capacity\nfull at\n{sat_pct:.0f}% pen.',
            fontsize=7.5, color='#ff004f88')

ax.set_xlabel('Average B2B market penetration (%)', color='#aaa')
ax.set_ylabel('Gen1 constellation capacity utilised (%)', color='#aaa')
ax.set_title('Capacity Utilisation vs Market Penetration\n(OneWeb Gen1, 4.5 Tbps)', color='#ccc')
ax.legend(facecolor='#111', edgecolor='#333', labelcolor='white', fontsize=8)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.07, color='#444')

# Right: base-case scenario breakdown (at base penetration rates)
ax = axes[2]
seg_names  = list(SEGMENTS.keys())
seg_revs   = [revenue_at_penetration(SEGMENTS[n], SEGMENTS[n]['penetration_base'])
              for n in seg_names]
seg_colors = [SEGMENTS[n]['color'] for n in seg_names]
total_base = sum(seg_revs)

bars = ax.bar(range(len(seg_names)), seg_revs, color=seg_colors, alpha=0.85, edgecolor='#111')
ax.set_xticks(range(len(seg_names)))
ax.set_xticklabels([n.replace(' ', '\n') for n in seg_names], color='#aaa', fontsize=8)
ax.set_ylabel('Annual revenue ($M)', color='#aaa')
ax.set_title(f'Base Case Revenue by Segment\n(Base penetration rates — total ${total_base:.0f}M/yr)',
             color='#ccc')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.07, color='#444', axis='y')
for bar, val, name in zip(bars, seg_revs, seg_names):
    pct = val / total_base * 100
    ax.text(bar.get_x() + bar.get_width()/2, val + 5,
            f'${val:.0f}M\n({pct:.0f}%)', ha='center', va='bottom',
            fontsize=8, color='#ccc')

ax.axhline(y=total_base, color='#ffffff33', linewidth=1, linestyle='--')
ax.text(len(seg_names)-0.5, total_base+5, f'Total: ${total_base:.0f}M', fontsize=8,
        color='#ffffff55', ha='right')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('oneweb_b2b_model.png', dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight')
plt.show()

print(f"\nBase case annual revenue:  ${total_base:.0f}M")
print(f"Constellation capacity:    {CONSTELLATION_CAPACITY_TBPS} Tbps")
print(f"Capacity at base case:     {sum(total_units_at_penetration(SEGMENTS[n], SEGMENTS[n]['penetration_base']) for n in seg_names) * BITS_PER_SUBSCRIBER_MBPS / 1e6:.2f} Tbps")
print(f"Revenue needed for $1B:    {1000/total_base*sum(SEGMENTS[n]['penetration_base'] for n in seg_names)/4*100:.1f}% average penetration")
```
The three panels together reveal OneWeb's financial and capacity reality: at realistic B2B penetration rates (4–8%), revenue is well below $1 billion annually — the level typically cited as needed for constellation financial sustainability. The capacity utilisation panel shows that Gen1's 4.5 Tbps is not the binding constraint at realistic penetration rates; the binding constraint is revenue, not capacity. Gen2's value is therefore not just capacity — it is also the ability to serve higher-throughput enterprise customers that increase per-subscriber ARPU.
