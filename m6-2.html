Phased Arrays:
Elements & the Array Factor

MODULE 6
USER TERMINALS & D2D
CORE

Lesson m6-1 used the array gain law — 10·log₁₀(N) plus an element gain minus an allowance — as a black box. This lesson opens it. The tool that opens it is one of the most economical ideas in antenna engineering, pattern multiplication: the radiation pattern of an array of identical elements is the pattern of one element multiplied by a purely geometric term, the array factor, that depends only on where the elements sit and what phases they are fed. Everything a flat panel does — steering, beamwidth, sidelobes, grating lobes — lives in the array factor; everything it cannot escape — scan roll-off, polarisation degradation — lives in the element. Design cleanly separates into geometry and electromagnetics, and this lesson takes them in that order.

The numbers in this lesson are produced by a pattern engine the spine project gains as Stage 2 — a numerical array-factor calculator with an honest measurement routine (main-lobe nulls located before sidelobes are searched; the difference between −5.6 dB and the correct −13.2 dB is exactly that bookkeeping). Every classical result is reproduced numerically before it is used: the 0.886·λ/(N·d) beamwidth, the −13.2 dB uniform sidelobe, the grating-lobe condition, the taper trade. The lesson's deliverable is the honest version of m6-1's gain law, with every constant accounted for.

One orientation note. This module cares about arrays as terminals, but nothing here is terminal-specific: the identical mathematics sized the Module 3 payload's beams and will reappear whenever this program touches an aperture. Learn it once, here, with runnable code.

1. Pattern multiplication

For N identical elements at positions r_n, fed with complex weights w_n, the far-field pattern factorises:

E_total(θ, φ) = E_element(θ, φ) × AF(θ, φ), AF = Σ w_n · e^(jk·r_n·û)

The factorisation holds exactly when every element has the same pattern — an idealisation that mutual coupling perturbs (section 7) but that organises all first-order design. The division of labour: the array factor is pure geometry plus feeding — it steers, it sets beamwidth and sidelobes, it is what software controls at microsecond timescales. The element pattern is fixed hardware — it bounds what any steering can achieve, and it is what decays as the beam scans away from broadside. m6-1's black-box law is this factorisation evaluated at one angle; this lesson evaluates it everywhere.

2. The array factor, derived

Take the uniform linear array (ULA): N elements spaced d along a line, element n fed with phase n·β. At angle θ from broadside, the path difference between adjacent elements is d·sin θ, so the phase difference per element is ψ = k·d·sin θ + β (k = 2π/λ). The array factor is a geometric series:

AF(ψ) = Σₙ e^(jnψ) → |AF| = |sin(Nψ/2) / sin(ψ/2)|

Its peak, |AF| = N, occurs where ψ = 0 — where the feeding phase exactly cancels the geometric phase. Steering is therefore nothing but choosing β: setting β = −k·d·sin θ₀ moves the peak to θ₀. No metal moves; a table of phase settings rewrites and the beam is elsewhere within the settling time of the phase shifters — the microsecond agility that won the NGSO market in m6-1 §4, now with its mechanism visible. Planar arrays extend the same formula to two dimensions; every result below carries over per axis.

3. Beamwidth: aperture measured in wavelengths

The main lobe's half-power width for a uniform ULA:

θ₃dB ≈ 0.886 · λ / (N·d·cos θ₀)

Verified numerically for N = 32, d = λ/2:

Steer θ₀ Beamwidth (engine) Theory
0°3.16°3.17°
45°4.48°3.17°/cos 45° = 4.49°
60°6.36°3.17°/cos 60° = 6.34°

Two readings. First, beamwidth is set by the aperture's extent in wavelengths — N·d — exactly as the dish's 70·λ/D; the two families obey one law. Second, the cos θ₀ in the denominator is the first appearance of the module's central tax: a scanned array's effective aperture is its physical aperture projected toward the target, and the beam broadens as 1/cos θ₀. Broadening is the benign face of scan loss; the gain consequence is m6-4's subject.

4. Sidelobes and the taper trade

A uniformly weighted array radiates its highest sidelobe at −13.2 dB relative to the main beam — a mathematical constant of the uniform aperture, independent of N. Sidelobes matter twice in a terminal: they receive interference from directions the user never chose (m4-6's C/I has a hardware ancestry), and they transmit toward other operators' satellites — which is precisely what the m6-4 regulatory off-axis mask constrains. A consumer NGSO terminal's sidelobe envelope is a compliance document, not an aesthetic.

Sidelobes are bought down by amplitude taper — feeding edge elements more weakly than central ones. The engine's verified example: a cosine-on-pedestal taper (pedestal 0.3) moves the first sidelobe from −13.2 to −20.3 dB, at the cost of 0.35 dB of gain (taper efficiency 0.923) and a beamwidth of 3.68° versus 3.16°. That is the shape of the whole trade: smoother illumination → lower sidelobes, wider beam, less gain. Classical syntheses (Taylor, Chebyshev) make the trade optimally; the engine's simple taper makes it visibly. This resolves m6-1's flagged item: the "taper loss" inside the 1.5 dB allowance is not a loss at all — it is sidelobe performance, purchased deliberately, and the m6-4 mask decides how much a terminal must buy.

5. Grating lobes and the spacing rule

The array factor is periodic in ψ: whenever k·d·sin θ + β passes through a multiple of 2π, a second full-strength main beam appears — a grating lobe, at

sin θ_g = sin θ₀ − m·λ/d

A grating lobe is not a large sidelobe; it is a complete copy of the main beam (the engine's demonstration: d = 0.7λ steered to 40° puts a 0.0 dB lobe at 51.8° on the other side of broadside). For a terminal it is disqualifying twice over — the receive copy admits interference at full gain, and the transmit copy radiates a second beam across the GEO arc, which no regulatory mask on Earth permits.

Keeping every grating lobe out of visible space while scanning to ±θ_max requires:

d/λ ≤ 1 / (1 + sin θ_max)

For ±60° scan: d ≤ 0.536 λ. This single inequality explains why every wide-scanning panel sits near half-wavelength spacing — and therefore why m6-1's element counts are what they are: the lattice pitch is regulated by grating-lobe physics, the aperture size by the gain requirement, and N is just their quotient squared. The temptation to space wider (fewer elements, less cost — m6-3's economics) is real and is paid in scan range: at d = 0.7λ the grating-lobe-free scan collapses to ±25°, a lattice for a satellite that stays overhead, not for a LEO terminal that must reach toward the horizon.

6. Planar lattices

Real panels are two-dimensional, and the grid choice matters. On a rectangular lattice the spacing rule applies per axis. On a triangular (hexagonal) lattice, the grating lobes of the two axes interleave less efficiently in sine-space, and the classical result (Sharp, 1961) follows: a triangular lattice covers the same grating-lobe-free scan volume with about 13% fewer elements than a rectangular one. Thirteen percent of element count is thirteen percent of the beamformer silicon, the assembly, and the DC power — m6-3 will price it — which is why triangular lattices are common in cost-driven panels. The aperture boundary matters separately: a circular boundary tapers the sidelobes of the two principal planes more evenly than a square one, one reason consumer panels tend toward rounded outlines rather than sharp rectangles.

7. The element itself

What the array factor multiplies. Terminal arrays overwhelmingly use printed patch radiators — planar, cheap, integrable above the beamformer ICs — with an embedded element gain near 5 dBi and a pattern the standard model captures as cos^q θ with q ≈ 1.2–1.5 — and one convention to fix precisely: the exponent's first power is the aperture projection itself. Aperture theory makes an ideal element in an ideal lattice scan as cos θ exactly (the array's directivity is its projected area, shared N ways); the excess above 1 is impedance mismatch and pattern deviation. cos^q is therefore the total scan behaviour, not a term to be multiplied by a separate projection factor. The roll-off, verified at q = 1.3:

Scan angle Element loss
30°−0.8 dB
45°−2.0 dB
60°−3.9 dB
75°−7.6 dB

This table is the main term of m6-4's scan-loss bill — aperture projection included, per the convention just fixed: unlike beamwidth broadening, it is pure lost gain, and no phase setting recovers it. Three second-order realities to file: mutual coupling — elements half a wavelength apart are not electromagnetically independent, so the honest element pattern is the embedded pattern, measured or simulated in the lattice, not in isolation; scan blindness — at particular scan angles the lattice can couple into a surface wave and the active reflection coefficient spikes toward unity, a design check every panel must clear (kept qualitative here); and polarisation — satellite terminals radiate circular polarisation, and a patch's axial ratio degrades off boresight, so the m4-1 polarisation-loss line is scan-dependent for a panel in a way it never was for a dish.

Broadside only, still: the full gain law is G(θ₀) = 10·log₁₀(N) + G_e + 10·q·log₁₀(cos θ₀) − losses, and its scan term — the §7 table, joined by polarisation degradation and the regulatory off-axis mask — is where m6-4 begins. The lattice pitch is fixed by §5, the element count by the gain requirement, the taper by the mask: the panel's geometry is now fully determined by requirements, which is what "design" means.

10. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

The spine gains Stage 2: a pattern engine that computes any lattice-and-taper's array factor, steers it, and measures it honestly — beamwidth from the half-power points inside the null-bounded main lobe, sidelobes searched only beyond the first nulls, grating lobes flagged when the spacing rule is violated. m6-1's gain allowance is now an itemised budget, and the panel's geometry (pitch from the scan requirement, N from the gain requirement, taper from the sidelobe requirement) is a solved design procedure rather than a datasheet mystery.

What the geometry cannot answer: what electronics sit behind each element, what they cost, and what they draw — the difference between a 1,280-element pattern on a screen and a 1,280-element product at a consumer price. That is m6-3. And the scan tax this lesson quantified — the embedded element's cos^q roll-off, projection included — meets polarisation degradation and the regulatory off-axis mask in m6-4, where the panel's low-elevation performance is settled.

Exercise 1 — The pattern engine

Compute, steer, and honestly measure an array factor. This function set is spine Stage 2's core.

import math, cmath
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x) if x > 0 else -999.0

def pattern(N, d_lam, steer_deg, taper=None, step=0.02):
    """|AF|^2 vs angle, normalised to the taper's coherent sum."""
    k = 2*math.pi
    w = taper or [1.0]*N
    psi0 = -k*d_lam*math.sin(math.radians(steer_deg))
    pts, t = [], -90.0
    while t <= 90.0:
        s = sum(w[n]*cmath.exp(1j*n*(k*d_lam*math.sin(math.radians(t)) + psi0))
                for n in range(N))
        pts.append((t, abs(s)**2/sum(w)**2))
        t += step
    return pts

def measure(pts, steer):
    """Peak, 3 dB beamwidth, first sidelobe — nulls located FIRST.
    Searching sidelobes without bounding the main lobe reads the
    main-lobe skirt (~ -5 dB) as a sidelobe. The nulls are the fence."""
    ipk = max(range(len(pts)), key=lambda i: pts[i][1] - abs(pts[i][0]-steer)*1e-9)
    pk_t, pk_v = pts[ipk]
    def first_null(direction):
        i = ipk
        while 0 < i < len(pts)-1:
            i += direction
            if pts[i][1] < pts[i-direction][1] and pts[i][1] < 0.01*pk_v:
                while 0 < i < len(pts)-1 and pts[i+direction][1] < pts[i][1]:
                    i += direction
                return i
        return i
    lo, hi = first_null(-1), first_null(+1)
    half = [t for t, v in pts[lo:hi+1] if v >= 0.5*pk_v]
    bw = max(half) - min(half)
    sll = max([v for _, v in pts[:lo]] + [v for _, v in pts[hi+1:]], default=0)
    return pk_t, bw, db(sll/pk_v)

for steer in [0, 30, 45, 60]:
    pk, bw, sll = measure(pattern(32, 0.5, steer), steer)
    print(f"N=32 d=0.5λ steer {steer:>2}°: peak {pk:6.2f}°, "
          f"BW {bw:5.2f}°, first SLL {sll:6.1f} dB")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 2: the honest gain law

Assemble G(θ₀) from its verified parts and hand it to the aperture model.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x) if x > 0 else -999.0

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M6 SPINE PROJECT — TERMINAL MODEL
# Stage 2: the array's gain law, honestly assembled.
#   Stage 1 (m6-1) used G = 10log10(N) + Ge − L at broadside.
#   Stage 2 itemises L and adds the scan dependence:
#     G(θ0) = 10log10(N) + Ge + 10·q·log10(cos θ0)
#             − taper − network − radome
#   plus the lattice legality check (grating-lobe rule).
# Next: m6-3 puts electronics and euros behind each element;
#       m6-4 adds polarisation-at-scan and the off-axis mask
#       (cos^q above is TOTAL scan loss — projection included).
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class ArrayGainModel:
    def __init__(self, name, N, pitch_lam, scan_req_deg,
                 Ge_dbi=5.0, q=1.3,
                 taper_db=0.35, network_db=0.45, radome_db=0.3):
        self.name = name; self.N = N
        self.pitch = pitch_lam; self.scan_req = scan_req_deg
        self.Ge, self.q = Ge_dbi, q
        self.losses = taper_db + network_db + radome_db
        # lattice legality (rectangular; triangular relaxes ~13%)
        self.pitch_limit = 1.0/(1.0 + math.sin(math.radians(scan_req_deg)))
        self.legal = pitch_lam <= self.pitch_limit + 1e-9

    def gain(self, scan_deg):
        elem = 10*self.q*math.log10(math.cos(math.radians(scan_deg))) \
               if scan_deg < 90 else -999
        return db(self.N) + self.Ge + elem - self.losses

    def report(self):
        print(f"\n{'─'*56}")
        print(f"  ARRAY GAIN MODEL — {self.name}")
        print(f"{'─'*56}")
        print(f"  N = {self.N}, pitch {self.pitch:.3f} λ "
              f"(limit {self.pitch_limit:.3f} λ for ±{self.scan_req}°)"
              f"  {'OK' if self.legal else '** GRATING LOBES **'}")
        print(f"  losses: {self.losses:.2f} dB itemised "
              f"(taper+network+radome)")
        print(f"  {'scan':>6} {'element':>9} {'G (dBi)':>9}")
        for s in [0, 30, 45, 60, 70]:
            e = 10*self.q*math.log10(math.cos(math.radians(s)))
            print(f"  {s:>5}° {e:>8.2f} {self.gain(s):>9.2f}")
        print(f"{'─'*56}")

panel = ArrayGainModel("1,280-el consumer Ka panel", 1280, 0.5, 60)
panel.report()

bad = ArrayGainModel("cost-reduced d=0.7λ variant", 900, 0.7, 60)
bad.report()

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 01

Section 2 showed steering is a phase table, and m6-1 §4 credited make-before-break handover to it. But two simultaneous beams from one aperture require either splitting the array (two half-gain beams) or duplicating electronics per element (m6-3's digital option). Work the handover instant quantitatively: how many dB does a split-aperture terminal give up during the overlap, for how long per m5-3's handover cadence, and does the m5-2 ladder absorb it silently or visibly? Decide whether split-aperture handover is a real design or a datasheet trick.

PROMPT 02

The taper trade was priced at −0.35 dB of gain for 7 dB of sidelobe suppression. A colleague argues the terminal should therefore always run maximum taper "to be safe" against the m6-4 mask. Construct the counter-position from this module's own numbers: what does 0.35–0.9 dB of G/T cost at the m5-7 sensitivity table's two ladder positions, when is uniform illumination plus a smaller mechanical tilt the better compliance strategy, and what does your answer imply about designing the taper per-product versus configuring it per-installation?

PROMPT 03

Section 5 fixed the pitch at ≤ 0.536 λ for ±60° scan, and exercise 2's vendor wanted 0.7 λ. A LEO operator can choose to schedule its terminals only onto satellites above 40° elevation (m2 geometry: more satellites required). Frame the system-level trade honestly: terminal cost saved per unit at the wider pitch versus constellation cost added for the elevation floor — which side of the ledger dominates at one million terminals, and at one hundred thousand? Identify the actor in the value chain who gets to make this decision, and why that matters.

PROMPT 04

The lesson kept scan blindness qualitative: at certain angles the lattice couples into a surface wave and the panel goes briefly deaf. For a consumer terminal this is a design-verification item; for an aviation terminal (m6-6) it intersects certification. Reason about the difference: what test evidence would convince you a blindness angle cannot occur inside the operational scan volume, who bears the consequence if it appears in service in each segment, and what does that asymmetry do to the engineering margin each segment's designer will demand around the same physics?
