Terminal Types
& Aviation Certification

MODULE 6
USER TERMINALS & D2D
ADVANCED

Five lessons built the physics of a terminal; this lesson maps it onto the market. The mapping is not arbitrary: each segment weights m6-1's three currencies — performance, cost, form factor — differently, and the weighting selects an aperture family (m6-1), an architecture (m6-3), a tracking approach (m6-5), and a compliance envelope (m6-4). The segment table is the module's design space evaluated at the points where real money is spent.

One segment then gets a deep treatment, because it is the one where the terminal stops being a communications problem and becomes a certification problem: aviation. An aircraft terminal must survive a qualification regime (DO-160), install against standardised mechanical and electrical provisions (ARINC 791/792), and earn airworthiness approval (the STC path) before it carries a single bit. This lesson treats that regime at the level of the public standards themselves — what DO-160 tests, what ARINC 791 specifies, how an STC is structured — because the standards are public, teachable, and the reason an aero terminal costs and weighs what it does. It deliberately carries no operator- or programme-specific content; every concrete number traces to the spine's own verified figures or to a published standard's structure.

The through-line: the same 1,280-element panel that costs a few hundred euros on a suburban roof becomes, with a DO-160 qualification campaign, an ARINC 791 adapter plate, a radome, and an STC behind it, a six-figure aviation line-replaceable unit — and every increment of that cost is a named requirement, not a markup.

The table restates the module's central finding once more, now priced: stationary, decibel-rational buyers take the dish and its ~10 dB G/T advantage; anything that moves — or that must handover on the NGSO cadence — pays that advantage away for agility, conformality, and reliability. The maritime row is the interesting hybrid: a ship has the deck space and power for a stabilised dish, so it often keeps the decibels and buys a mechanical stabiliser instead of surrendering to a panel — the one moving segment where the dish frequently survives, because its currency (lifetime G/T per euro across a 20-year hull) still favours metal.

2. Segment requirements, beyond the link budget

Each segment adds constraints the link budget never sees:

Consumer — self-install (the m6-5 sky-search dependency), a domestic power and thermal envelope, aesthetic acceptability, and a bill of materials that survives contact with a mass market. The hardest requirement is the one with no decibels: a non-expert must make it work in ten minutes.

Enterprise / cellular backhaul — availability first (the SLA is contractual, so the m5-7 availability curve becomes a penalty clause), managed installation, often frequency and orbit diversity for the five-nines that backhaul demands. Here the dish's decibels buy margin that is worth real contractual money.

Maritime — stabilisation against sea state (m6-5 §6), salt-fog and vibration survival, a radome that is also a structural and safety item, and multi-year unattended reliability at sea. Power and space are relatively abundant; environment and reliability bind.

Land mobile — conformality above all (a vehicle-height antenna is often simply unacceptable), sharp attitude transients, and cost pressure approaching consumer. The ESA is nearly mandatory; the only question is how low-profile.

Aviation — every other segment's constraints at once, plus flight safety, plus certification — and the height/drag currency is measured in fuel over the airframe's life. This is the segment the rest of the lesson dissects.

3. Why aviation is different: the terminal joins the aircraft

A rooftop terminal that fails inconveniences a household. An aircraft terminal that fails — or worse, that fails unsafely, shedding a radome at altitude, radiating into avionics, feeding fire through a penetration — is a flight-safety item on a certificated vehicle. The consequence is categorical: the terminal must prove, by test and analysis, that it cannot compromise the aircraft under any condition of the flight envelope, and it must do so under a regulatory framework (FAA, EASA and equivalents) whose burden of proof is total. Three public standards structure that proof: DO-160 (does the box survive the environment?), ARINC 791/792 (does it install to a common mechanical and electrical interface?), and the STC (is this specific installation on this aircraft type airworthy?). The rest of the lesson takes them in turn.

4. DO-160: environmental qualification

RTCA DO-160 (with its EUROCAE equivalent ED-14) is the environmental qualification standard for airborne equipment — the catalogue of tests a box must pass to be considered airworthy hardware. It is organised as ~26 sections, each a category of environmental stress with defined test procedures and severity categories the equipment is qualified to. The sections most consequential for a satcom antenna and its electronics:

  • Temperature and altitude (§4) — operation and survival across the flight envelope's temperature range and reduced pressure, including rapid decompression; a panel's sun-loaded BFICs (m6-1 §6) meet their thermal worst case here.
  • Temperature variation, humidity (§5–6) — thermal cycling and moisture ingress.
  • Vibration (§8) — the airframe's spectrum, which for a fuselage-top antenna includes aerodynamic buffet; the aperture and every solder joint must survive it for the design life.
  • Explosive atmosphere (§9) — the equipment must not ignite a flammable mixture, a real constraint for anything with power near fuel systems.
  • Waterproofness, fluids susceptibility (§10–11) — rain, spray, and the aircraft's own fluids.
  • Magnetic effect, power input (§15–16) — behaviour on the aircraft's electrical bus, including transients.
  • RF susceptibility and emission (§20–21) — the antenna must neither be disrupted by, nor disrupt, the aircraft's other radios and avionics: the aviation form of the m4-6 / m6-4 interference discipline, now safety-critical.
  • Lightning (§22–23) — induced transients and, for an externally mounted item, direct-strike considerations; a fuselage-top radome is in a lightning zone by definition.

The engineering point for this module: DO-160 does not change the terminal's communications design — the G/T and EIRP are what m6-1 through m6-5 made them — but it dominates the terminal's mass, cost, and schedule. Qualifying a box to DO-160 is a test campaign of months and a mechanical design driven by vibration, lightning, and thermal rather than by RF. The consumer panel and the aero panel can share a beamforming architecture and differ by an order of magnitude in price entirely because of this section.

5. ARINC 791 and 792: the installation interface

Where DO-160 qualifies the box, the ARINC 791 (and the newer ARINC 792 for the second-generation / flat-panel-friendly interface) characteristics standardise how a satcom antenna installs on the aircraft. They define the common mechanical and electrical interface so that antennas from different vendors fit a common provision — the aviation analogue of a standardised bracket, cabling, and connector set. The structure worth knowing:

  • The mounting provision — a defined adapter-plate interface on the fuselage crown, so the airframe is modified once (the structural penetration, the doubler, the wiring) and antennas become comparatively interchangeable above it. This is what lets an airline re-fit a newer antenna without re-opening the airframe structure.
  • Outside/inside split — the standard partitions the system into the outside antenna assembly (under the radome) and inside equipment (modem, power), with a defined interface between, so the pressure-vessel penetration is controlled and standardised.
  • Radome — an aerodynamic fairing that is simultaneously an RF window (its loss, ~0.3–0.5 dB, debited to both G/T and EIRP in §1's table), a structural member (it carries flight loads and must not depart the aircraft), and a lightning-zone component. The radome is where the antenna's electromagnetic, structural, and safety requirements collide, and it is a certification artefact in its own right.

ARINC 791's value is economic: it converts a bespoke integration into a repeatable one, amortising the airframe's structural certification across many antenna generations. For a flat panel the low-profile form factor (m6-3's VICTS and conformal ESAs) pays directly in drag — and drag is fuel, integrated over the airframe's remaining life, which is why aviation weights the form-factor currency in a way no other segment does.

6. The STC: making a specific installation airworthy

DO-160 qualifies the equipment and ARINC 791 standardises the interface, but airworthiness attaches to a specific installation on a specific aircraft type. The instrument is the Supplemental Type Certificate (STC) — an approval, granted by the airworthiness authority, that a defined modification to a type-certificated aircraft remains airworthy. Its public structure:

  • Engineering substantiation — structural analysis of the penetration and doubler, electrical load analysis, a safety assessment showing no hazardous failure mode, and the DO-160 qualification evidence for the equipment.
  • EMC/EMI demonstration — proof, by test on the actual aircraft, that the terminal does not interfere with the type's avionics and radios (the §4 RF sections, now in situ) — the empirical closure of the m6-4 mask discipline against the aircraft's own systems rather than other satellites.
  • Ground and flight test — verification that the installation performs and does no harm through the flight envelope.
  • Continued airworthiness — the maintenance and inspection requirements that keep the modification valid over its life (the m6-1 §7 graceful-degradation property becomes a dispatch-reliability argument here: a panel that loses elements gracefully can fly to a scheduled maintenance action rather than grounding the aircraft).

The STC's cost and duration are why aviation connectivity moves slowly relative to consumer: a new antenna is not a product launch but a certification programme, per aircraft type, and the amortisation is over a fleet and a decade. It is also why ARINC 791's interchangeability matters so much — an STC written against the standardised provision can, in principle, accommodate a qualified successor antenna with far less re-certification than a bespoke installation would demand.

7. What certification does to the module's numbers

Certification changes the terminal's cost, mass, schedule, and reliability requirements — and leaves its communications numbers almost untouched. The aero ESA's ~11 dB/K G/T and ~36 dBW EIRP (§1) are the m6-5 served figures minus a radome's ~0.4 dB; the physics is the physics. What certification adds is everything around the physics: the qualified enclosure, the standardised installation, the airworthiness approval, and the continued-airworthiness tail. The module's spine can therefore carry a single terminal object across segments by attaching a certification profile — a multiplier on cost, mass, and lead time, and a set of environmental and reliability requirements — to the same aperture, architecture, and pass model. That is exactly what Stage 6 does: the physics is computed once; the segment decides what wraps it.

A closing calibration on scope: this lesson has stayed at the level of what the public standards contain and why they drive cost — DO-160's section structure, ARINC 791's interface partition, the STC's substantiation elements. The detailed conduct of a qualification or certification campaign is specialised engineering beyond a self-directed curriculum's remit, and where a learner needs it, the standards themselves (and the authorities' advisory material) are the reference. The curriculum's claim is narrower and sound: you now know why an aviation terminal is a certification artefact, and what that does to the trade space m6-7 will close.

9. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

Stage 6 of the spine attaches a certification profile to any terminal: a segment tag that carries environmental and reliability requirements and multipliers on cost, mass, and lead time, applied to the same physics the earlier stages computed. The parameter table of §1 is now the terminal model's output for any segment, and the deciding currency per row is explicit. The module's design space is fully populated: aperture (m6-1), pattern (m6-2), architecture (m6-3), scan and mask (m6-4), pass and pointing (m6-5), and now segment and certification.

What remains is to use it: to run each segment's terminal through the Module 4 link budget and the Module 5 throughput engine and ask, for each, whether it closes the target service — and where the cost-versus-G/T frontier actually sits. That synthesis, and the degenerate terminal at the bottom of the trade space (the m5-6 handset, revisited from the terminal side), is m6-7.

Exercise 1 — Assemble the segment table

Build §1's parameter table from the module's own verified physics, with radome loss.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x)

def dish_terminal(D_m, f_rx=20, f_tx=30, eta=0.65, pa_w=2.0,
                  tx_loss=0.5, radome_db=0.0, t_sys_clear=122.0):
    lam_r, lam_t = 0.3/f_rx, 0.3/f_tx
    g_rx = db(eta*(math.pi*D_m/lam_r)**2) - radome_db
    g_tx = db(eta*(math.pi*D_m/lam_t)**2) - radome_db
    L = 10**(radome_db/10)
    t_sys = t_sys_clear*L + 290*(L-1)             # radome adds noise too
    return g_rx - db(t_sys), db(pa_w) - tx_loss + g_tx

def esa_terminal(served_gt, eirp0=36.2, radome_db=0.0):
    return served_gt - radome_db, eirp0 - radome_db

rows = [
    ("consumer NGSO", *esa_terminal(10.9)),                 # m6-5 served
    ("enterprise fixed", *dish_terminal(0.75)),
    ("maritime (dish)", *dish_terminal(1.0, radome_db=0.5)),
    ("aviation (ESA)", *esa_terminal(11.5, radome_db=0.4)),
]
print(f"{'segment':<20}{'G/T':>8}{'EIRP':>8}")
for name, gt, eirp in rows:
    print(f"{name:<20}{gt:>7.1f} {eirp:>7.1f}")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 6: the certification profile

The terminal model becomes segment-aware; physics computed once, wrapped per market.

import math

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M6 SPINE PROJECT — TERMINAL MODEL
# Stage 6: segment & certification profile.
#   Stages 1-5 computed the PHYSICS (G/T, EIRP, pass, pointing).
#   Stage 6 attaches a SEGMENT: radome loss, environmental and
#   reliability requirements, and cost/mass/lead multipliers —
#   the same physics wrapped for each market.
# Next: m6-7 runs each wrapped terminal through M4 + M5 and
#       finds the cost-vs-G/T frontier.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class Terminal:
    """Carries physics from Stages 1-5; Stage 6 wraps it per segment."""
    def __init__(self, name, gt_served, eirp, beamwidth_deg):
        self.name = name
        self.gt_phys, self.eirp_phys, self.bw = gt_served, eirp, beamwidth_deg

class SegmentProfile:
    def __init__(self, segment, radome_db, cost_mult, mass_mult,
                 lead_months, env, cert, mtbf_hr):
        self.segment, self.radome = segment, radome_db
        self.cost_mult, self.mass_mult = cost_mult, mass_mult
        self.lead_months, self.env, self.cert = lead_months, env, cert
        self.mtbf_hr = mtbf_hr

    def apply(self, t: Terminal, base_cost=300, base_mass=3.0):
        return dict(
            segment   = self.segment,
            gt        = t.gt_phys - self.radome,      # radome both ways
            eirp      = t.eirp_phys - self.radome,
            cost_eur  = base_cost * self.cost_mult,
            mass_kg   = base_mass * self.mass_mult,
            lead_mo   = self.lead_months,
            env       = self.env, cert = self.cert, mtbf = self.mtbf_hr)

PROFILES = [
    SegmentProfile("consumer",   0.0,  1.0, 1.0,  0, "domestic", "none",          20000),
    SegmentProfile("enterprise", 0.0,  2.0, 1.5,  1, "outdoor",  "type-approval", 50000),
    SegmentProfile("maritime",   0.5,  4.0, 3.0,  3, "salt/vib", "marine-class",  60000),
    SegmentProfile("aviation",   0.4, 15.0, 2.5, 18, "DO-160",   "STC",          100000),
]

panel = Terminal("1,280-el Ka ESA", gt_served=11.3, eirp=36.2, beamwidth_deg=3.2)

print(f"{'segment':<12}{'G/T':>6}{'EIRP':>7}{'cost €':>9}{'mass':>7}{'lead':>6}  {'cert'}")
for p in PROFILES:
    r = p.apply(panel)
    print(f"{r['segment']:<12}{r['gt']:>6.1f}{r['eirp']:>7.1f}"
          f"{r['cost_eur']:>9.0f}{r['mass_kg']:>7.1f}{r['lead_mo']:>5}mo  {r['cert']}")

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 01

Section 1's table shows aviation and consumer terminals with nearly identical communications numbers (~11 dB/K, ~36 dBW) but, by exercise 2's structure, an order-of-magnitude cost gap driven entirely by certification. Argue the strategic consequence for a terminal vendor: does the certification moat protect incumbents (the campaign cost deters entrants) or expose them (a consumer-scale manufacturer who does certify undercuts the traditional aero-only vendor)? Identify which way the module's own economics (m6-3's amortisation axis) tips the answer, and what a rational consumer-LEO operator entering aviation would do first.

PROMPT 02

Section 6 made the STC attach to a specific aircraft type, and ARINC 791 the mechanism that amortises the airframe modification across antenna generations. Frame the tension: the standardised interface lowers the cost of the next antenna but constrains its form factor to the provision defined years earlier. For a technology moving as fast as flat-panel ESAs (m6-3's digital-DBF timeline), is a frozen interface a net accelerant or a net brake? Take a position and defend it with a specific capability from m6-3 or m6-5 that a fixed ARINC 791 provision would or would not accommodate.

PROMPT 03

This lesson kept a hard scope boundary — public standards only, no programme-derived content — and stated it explicitly. Interrogate the boundary as an epistemics question, not a confidentiality one: what can a self-directed learner genuinely know about certification from public standards alone, versus what requires campaign experience that no curriculum can supply? Identify the specific point at which reading DO-160 stops teaching and doing DO-160 begins, and design the honest disclaimer a curriculum should carry at that boundary so a learner does not mistake standards-literacy for qualification competence.

PROMPT 04

Section 7's claim — physics unchanged, everything around it certified — let the spine carry one terminal object across segments with a wrapper. Stress-test the abstraction: name the segment or requirement where certification does reach back and change the physics (hint: consider a DO-160 thermal or vibration limit forcing an element, lattice, or power change from m6-1/m6-2/m6-3). If you can find one, the clean physics/certification separation is a simplification — state where the spine's Stage 6 wrapper would have to become a feedback loop instead, and what that costs the model's tidiness.
