Anomalies, Playbooks & Automated Fleet Operations

MODULE 8
GROUND SEGMENT & OPERATIONS
ADVANCED

m8-4 assumed satellites could be operated autonomously and m8-5 assumed they could be commissioned in a pipeline — both deferring the same question to this lesson: what happens when a satellite deviates? A fleet of thousands of satellites, each with thousands of telemetry channels, generates a continuous stream of anomalies — components drift out of range, subsystems fault, software misbehaves, the space environment strikes — and every one must be detected, diagnosed, and resolved. For a traditional satellite, an anomaly is an event: an alarm sounds, engineers gather, and a team works the problem over hours or days. For a mega-constellation generating some fifty anomalies a day across a fleet monitored by nearly eight million telemetry points per second, the event model is as impossible as everything else at this scale. Anomaly handling must be automated: detection by software watching the telemetry firehose, response by standardised playbooks that resolve known failures without human intervention, and escalation to human engineers only for the small fraction that are genuinely novel. This lesson completes Part II by showing how a fleet that flies itself (m8-4) and commissions itself (m8-5) also heals itself — and where the humans must remain.

The lesson's verified anchors quantify why. A fleet of 1,584 satellites at a modest one anomaly per satellite per month generates ~52 anomalies per day — ten engineers working every day under the traditional model — but if 95% match known playbooks and resolve automatically, the human workload drops below one engineer's daily capacity. Monitoring the fleet means watching ~7.9 million telemetry points per second, far beyond any human's capacity, so detection itself must be automated. And the fleet's identical satellites carry a systematic risk that shadows the whole approach: a design flaw, or a wrong automated response, propagates across all 1,584 at once. The spine gains the fleet-anomaly model — the tool that turns a fleet size and an anomaly rate into an operations workload, and shows how automation and playbooks collapse it.

1. Anomalies at fleet scale

An anomaly is any deviation from expected behaviour: a temperature out of range, a voltage sagging, a subsystem reporting a fault, a maneuver executing imperfectly, an ISL terminal failing to acquire (m7-2), a solar panel underperforming. Some are benign (a sensor glitch), some are serious (a reaction wheel failing), and some are catastrophic (a power system fault that threatens the satellite). For a single satellite, anomalies are rare enough to handle individually — a few a month, each worked by engineers.

At fleet scale the arithmetic changes everything. If each satellite generates even a modest one anomaly per month, a 1,584-satellite fleet generates:

1,584 satellites × 12 anomalies/year = 19,008 anomalies/year = 52 per day = 2.2 per hour

Fifty-two anomalies a day, around the clock, forever. Under the traditional model — an engineer working each anomaly — this would need about ten engineers handling anomalies every single day, doing nothing else, forever. And that is at a modest anomaly rate; a newly deployed constellation, or one hit by a space-weather event, spikes far higher. The event model, where an anomaly summons a team, simply cannot absorb fifty-two events a day. Anomaly handling must become a process — automated detection, automated response, human escalation — the same transformation from event to process that m8-4 and m8-5 forced on contact and commissioning.

2. Detection: watching the firehose

Before an anomaly can be handled it must be detected, and detection at fleet scale is itself beyond human capacity. Each satellite streams thousands of telemetry channels — voltages, temperatures, currents, states, rates — and a fleet of 1,584 satellites at 5,000 channels each, sampled once a second, is:

1,584 × 5,000 × 1 Hz ≈ 7.9 million telemetry points per second

Nearly eight million numbers a second, streaming down from the fleet. No human, and no room full of humans, can watch this. Detection must be automated: software that ingests the telemetry firehose and flags deviations — by simple threshold checks (a temperature exceeds a limit), by statistical methods (a value drifts outside its normal envelope), or by machine-learning models trained on healthy behaviour (a pattern looks anomalous even without crossing a hard threshold). The detection layer is a filter that turns eight million points a second into a manageable stream of flagged anomalies — the ~52 a day that matter — and its quality determines everything downstream: miss a real anomaly and a satellite is lost; flag too many false ones and the human escalation queue drowns. Fleet monitoring is a big-data problem before it is a spacecraft problem.

3. Playbooks: automating the known response

Detection produces a stream of anomalies; most of them are known types with known responses. A reaction wheel saturates — the response is a known desaturation maneuver. A subsystem faults — the response is a known reset sequence. A satellite drifts from its slot — the response is a known station-keeping burn. These are not novel problems requiring engineering judgement; they are recognised failures with established fixes, and the fix can be encoded as a playbook: a standardised, automated response that the system executes when it detects the matching anomaly, without waiting for a human.

Playbooks are the heart of fleet automation. If a large fraction of anomalies — say 95% — match a known playbook, then 95% of the fifty-two daily anomalies resolve automatically, and the human workload drops to the remaining 5%:

52 anomalies/day × 5% = ~2.6 manual anomalies/day → less than one engineer's daily load

The fleet, in effect, heals most of its own wounds: an anomaly is detected, matched to a playbook, and resolved by automated action, with the whole cycle logged for human review but requiring no human decision. The playbook library grows over time — each novel anomaly, once diagnosed by engineers, becomes a new playbook so that the next occurrence resolves automatically — so the automated fraction rises as the constellation matures and the fleet's failure modes become known. This is the mechanism that makes a thousand-satellite fleet operable by a small team: the routine is automated, and the team handles only the genuinely novel.

4. Escalation: where the humans stay

The 5% that no playbook matches — the genuinely novel anomalies — escalate to human engineers, and this is where human judgement remains essential. A novel anomaly is one the fleet has not seen before: an unexpected failure mode, an interaction between subsystems not anticipated, a symptom that matches no known pattern. These require the diagnostic reasoning that automation cannot supply — understanding why a satellite is misbehaving, not just matching a symptom to a fix — and they are exactly the anomalies where a wrong response could make things worse. Human engineers work these, diagnose them, resolve them, and then encode the resolution as a new playbook so the next occurrence is automated.

This escalation model — automate the known, escalate the novel — is the fleet-operations equivalent of m7-4's proactive-with-reactive-floor routing and m8-4's autonomy-with-exceptions contact. The pattern recurs because it is the only way scale works: handle the common case automatically and reserve scarce human attention for the exceptions. The humans are not removed from the loop; they are repositioned — from working every anomaly to working only the novel ones and teaching the system to handle them next time. A fleet operations team is small not because there is little to do, but because most of what there is to do has been automated, leaving the humans to do the part only humans can: reason about the unknown.

5. Graceful degradation and fleet health

A mega-constellation is designed so that individual satellite failures do not break the service — a principle inherited from m6-6's dispatch reliability and m7-4's rerouting, now at fleet scale. At any moment some fraction of the fleet — say 2%, about 32 satellites — is degraded or offline: in an anomaly, in a playbook response, in safe mode awaiting a fix, or simply failed and awaiting deorbit (m8-5). The constellation is engineered to tolerate this: spare capacity absorbs the lost throughput, the mesh reroutes around the degraded satellites (m7-4), and the service degrades gracefully — smoothly and slightly — rather than catastrophically. A user rarely notices that 32 satellites are unhealthy, because the constellation was designed with the redundancy to carry on without them.

Fleet health monitoring is the aggregate view that makes this manageable: rather than tracking 1,584 individual satellites, operators track the fleet's health — how many are nominal, how many degraded, how many failed, and whether the degraded fraction is stable or growing. A stable 2% degraded is normal operation; a rising fraction is a warning that something systematic is wrong (§6). This aggregate view is the human-scale interface to a fleet too large to watch satellite-by-satellite: the operators supervise the statistics of the fleet's health, and the automation handles the individuals. Graceful degradation is what lets them do this — because no single satellite's failure is a crisis, the operators can afford to watch the aggregate and let the automation and the redundancy handle the individuals.

6. The systematic risk of identical satellites

There is a shadow over the whole automated-fleet approach, and it is the flip side of the mass production that makes mega-constellations possible: the satellites are identical. Identical satellites are cheap (Module 3's mass production) and operable (one playbook library serves the whole fleet), but they share every flaw. A design defect — a component that fails after a certain radiation dose, a software bug triggered by a rare condition, a thermal weakness under a particular Sun angle — is present in all 1,584 satellites, and when the triggering condition arrives, it can affect the whole fleet at once. The systematic failure, not the individual anomaly, is the mega-constellation's gravest operational risk: one anomaly is trivial, but the same anomaly on a thousand satellites simultaneously is an existential threat to the service.

And the automation amplifies this risk. A playbook is applied identically across the fleet, so a wrong playbook — an automated response that is subtly incorrect — replicates its error across every satellite that triggers it, potentially damaging the whole fleet through the very mechanism meant to heal it. This is why the escalation to humans (§4) and the fleet-health aggregate view (§5) matter so much: they are the checks against systematic error. A rising degraded-fraction that many satellites share is the signal of a systematic problem, and it must halt the automated response and escalate to humans before the playbook propagates the error further. The operations discipline for a mega-constellation is therefore not just automating the routine but detecting when the routine is wrong at scale — watching for the correlated failure and the mis-applied playbook that identical satellites make catastrophic. Automation handles the fleet; vigilance against systematic error protects it.

Exercise 1 — The anomaly workload

From fleet size to operations team.

import math

def anomaly_rate_per_day(n_sats, per_sat_per_month):
    return n_sats*per_sat_per_month*12/365

def engineers_needed(anomalies_per_day, per_engineer_per_day=5):
    return anomalies_per_day/per_engineer_per_day

def with_playbooks(anomalies_per_day, automated_fraction):
    manual = anomalies_per_day*(1-automated_fraction)
    return manual, engineers_needed(manual)

N = 1584
rate = anomaly_rate_per_day(N, 1)
print(f"fleet anomaly rate: {rate:.0f}/day")
print(f"traditional engineers: {engineers_needed(rate):.0f}")
print(f"\nwith playbook automation:")
for frac in [0.80, 0.90, 0.95, 0.99]:
    manual, eng = with_playbooks(rate, frac)
    print(f"  {frac:.0%} automated: {manual:.1f} manual/day, {eng:.2f} engineers")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 6: the fleet-anomaly model

The fleet heals itself.

import math

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M8 SPINE PROJECT — GROUND SEGMENT
# Stage 6: the fleet-anomaly model.
#   Stages 4-5 gave a fleet that flies and commissions itself.
#   Stage 6: a fleet that HEALS itself — automated detection,
#   playbook response, human escalation of the novel — plus the
#   graceful degradation and systematic-risk watch that scale needs.
#   Next: m8-7 assembles the whole GroundSegment.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class FleetAnomalyModel:
    def __init__(self, n_sats, anomalies_per_sat_month=1,
                 channels_per_sat=5000, sample_hz=1,
                 automated_fraction=0.95, degraded_fraction=0.02):
        self.n = n_sats
        self.rate_month = anomalies_per_sat_month
        self.channels = channels_per_sat
        self.hz = sample_hz
        self.auto = automated_fraction
        self.degraded = degraded_fraction

    def anomalies_per_day(self):
        return self.n*self.rate_month*12/365

    def telemetry_points_per_sec(self):
        return self.n*self.channels*self.hz

    def manual_load(self, per_engineer_day=5):
        manual = self.anomalies_per_day()*(1-self.auto)
        return dict(manual_per_day=round(manual, 1),
                    engineers=round(manual/per_engineer_day, 2))

    def degraded_satellites(self):
        return round(self.n*self.degraded)

    def systematic_risk(self):
        # a wrong playbook or shared defect hits all identical satellites
        return dict(identical=self.n,
                    worst_case="a single flaw/playbook affects the whole fleet")

    def report(self):
        print(f"  fleet: {self.n} satellites")
        print(f"  anomalies: {self.anomalies_per_day():.0f}/day")
        print(f"  telemetry: {self.telemetry_points_per_sec()/1e6:.1f} M points/s "
              f"-> automated detection required")
        print(f"  automation {self.auto:.0%}: {self.manual_load()}")
        print(f"  degraded (tolerated): {self.degraded_satellites()} satellites")
        print(f"  systematic risk: {self.systematic_risk()['worst_case']}")

FleetAnomalyModel(1584).report()

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 1

Section 6 identified the systematic risk of identical satellites and warned that a wrong playbook replicates its error fleet-wide. This creates a fundamental tension with section 3's automation imperative: automation is required for scale, but automation applied to identical satellites is exactly what makes a systematic error catastrophic. Design the safeguard: how should a fleet-operations system apply playbooks so that a subtly-wrong one does not damage the whole fleet before anyone notices? Consider staged rollout (apply a playbook to a few satellites first, watch, then expand), correlation detection (halt if many satellites respond badly to the same playbook), and human checkpoints. Decide where on the automation-versus-caution spectrum a mega-constellation should sit, and what it costs in responsiveness to add the safeguards.

PROMPT 2

Section 4 has humans encode each resolved novel anomaly as a new playbook, so the automated fraction rises over time. But this means the fleet's operational knowledge accumulates in the playbook library, and the human engineers gradually work only the rarest, hardest anomalies — which means they see each type less often and may lose the fluency that comes from routine practice. Interrogate this deskilling risk: as automation handles more, do the humans who must handle the novel anomalies become less capable of doing so, because they no longer practise on the routine ones? Decide whether a fleet-operations organisation should deliberately keep humans in the loop on some routine anomalies to maintain skill, trading efficiency for competence, and how it would strike that balance.

PROMPT 3

Section 5's graceful degradation tolerates ~2% of the fleet being unhealthy, absorbed by spare capacity and rerouting. But "2% tolerated" is a design choice with a cost: the spare capacity that absorbs the degraded satellites is capacity that could have been sold. Work the trade: a constellation designed to tolerate 5% degradation needs more spare capacity (more expensive) but can run its operations more relaxed (fewer urgent anomalies); one designed to tolerate only 1% is cheaper in spare capacity but must fix anomalies faster (more operations pressure). Determine where the optimum sits, connecting the degradation tolerance (a design parameter) to both the spare-capacity cost (Module 3, the space segment) and the operations-team size (this lesson) — and decide whether graceful degradation is bought with hardware or with operations.

PROMPT 4

This lesson's numbers — 52 anomalies/day, 7.9M telemetry points/s, 95% automation — rest on a per-satellite anomaly rate (1/month), a channel count (5,000), and an automation fraction (95%), all representative. The conclusions (detection and response must be automated, systematic risk is the gravest threat) are robust, but the operations-team size that these figures imply is assumption-dependent. Apply the load-bearing-assumption discipline that has run through the program: which input most strongly drives the residual human workload — the anomaly rate, or the automation fraction? Specify what an operator would measure from its actual fleet to pin each down, and state whether the "less than one engineer for anomalies" figure is a design target or an illustration — and what happens to it if the automation fraction is really 80%, not 95%.
