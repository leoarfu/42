1. Launch Vehicle Capacity and Satellites Per Launch

The number of satellites that can be delivered in a single launch is governed by launch vehicle payload mass to the target orbit and fairing volume constraints. The manifest formula is:

N_per_launch = ⌊m_lv / m_sat⌋ (subject to fairing volume)

For a Starlink-class satellite (~300 kg wet) on Falcon 9 (~17,400 kg to LEO), the mass limit suggests ~58 satellites, but the actual manifest is ~53 per launch, constrained by stacking geometry. For a OneWeb-class satellite (~150 kg) on Soyuz-2 (~7,000 kg to LEO), the manifest is approximately 36 per launch — exactly the OneWeb number.

Total launches required:

N_launches = ⌈N_total / N_per_launch⌉

For Starlink's 1,584-satellite first shell at ~53 per Falcon 9: ⌈1584/53⌉ = 30 launches. For OneWeb's 648 satellites at 36 per launch: ⌈648/36⌉ = 18 launches.

2. Inclination and the J2 Drift Technique

A single launch vehicle delivers all its payload to one inclination per launch. Changing inclination after separation costs large Δv, so satellites are delivered to the target inclination directly by the vehicle. This constrains how multiple planes of a Walker constellation can be populated.

Plane-by-plane deployment fills one plane completely before starting the next. It is launch-efficient but produces highly uneven coverage during build-out — dense in some longitude sectors, absent in others.

Distributed deployment using J2 drift exploits differential node regression. Two batches at slightly different altitudes have different J2 rates; their RAANs drift apart at a predictable rate without any plane-change burns. By choosing the altitude difference, an operator uses J2 precession to achieve the correct inter-plane RAAN spacing passively. This is the Starlink approach.

The drift time is:

t_drift = ΔΩ_target / |ΔdΩ/dt|

At 550 km, a 5 km altitude difference gives a node rate difference of ~0.01°/day. To achieve 5° of RAAN separation (needed for a 72-plane constellation) takes 500 days. Starlink uses a much lower staging orbit — higher J2 rate, much larger altitude difference — to reduce this to weeks.

500 DAYS AT 5 km ALTITUDE DIFFERENCE At 550 km, a 5 km altitude difference gives only 0.01°/day differential node rate. Achieving 5° of RAAN separation takes 500 days — impractically long for a commercial deployment. Starlink's solution: use a ~350 km staging orbit where the J2 rate is significantly higher and the differential rate for a given altitude offset is much larger, reducing the drift time to weeks.

3. The Staging Orbit and the Raise Manoeuvre

Most large LEO constellations deploy via a staging orbit — a lower altitude where satellites are commissioned before raising to the operational shell. The reasons: failed satellites at 350 km re-enter within weeks (debris safety); lower altitude allows more payload mass per launch; and operators have time to verify each satellite before committing it to a high-altitude slot.

The raise from staging to operational altitude uses a two-burn Hohmann transfer:

ΔV_raise = ΔV₁ (perigee) + ΔV₂ (apogee circularisation)

For 350 km → 550 km: ΔV_raise ≈ 64 m/s. For 450 km → 550 km: ΔV_raise ≈ 32 m/s. This must be included in the satellite's total Δv budget from m2-5. At Isp = 1,600 s, 64 m/s costs approximately 1.2 kg of propellant for a 300 kg satellite — modest but not negligible at fleet scale.

4. Deployment Cadence and Time to Full Constellation

Time to full constellation:

T_deploy = N_launches / cadence_per_year

Starlink at 30 launches and ~15 dedicated launches per year reached 1,584 satellites in roughly two years. OneWeb at 18 launches on Soyuz-2 with limited cadence took longer and was further disrupted by the 2022 Russia-Ukraine conflict, forcing a pivot to Ariane 6 and other vehicles.

Launch cadence is not only a production rate problem — it is a supply chain problem. A satellite manufacturing rate of ~1,000/year combined with 15 Falcon 9 launches at 53 per launch gives ~800 satellites deployed per year. Imbalance in either direction creates satellite backlogs (factories ahead of rockets) or launch vehicle backlogs (rockets ready before satellites). Real deployment programmes must manage both simultaneously, making cadence planning a critical part of constellation programme management.

5. Minimum Viable Constellation and Partial Coverage

The minimum viable constellation (MVC) is the smallest configuration that generates revenue — not an arbitrary fraction of the total. Coverage from a partially deployed Walker is non-uniform: populated planes provide corridor coverage along their ground track; unpopulated planes leave gaps.

For a Walker delta at 53°, filling the first 12 of 72 planes gives continuous coverage over a longitude strip one-sixth around the Earth — useful for maritime or aviation corridors before global coverage exists. Spreading satellites evenly across all planes gives global but sparse coverage earlier, at lower minimum elevation.

Starlink began beta service ("Better Than Nothing Beta") at approximately 800 satellites — roughly half its first shell — providing continuous coverage over most of the mid-latitude populated band. Both operators structured deployment to prioritise the highest-revenue markets first, illustrating that the MVC is a business decision as much as a technical one.

MVC IS MARKET-DEFINED The MVC depends on what the market will pay for. For maritime shipping, 100 satellites covering major ocean lanes may suffice. For a consumer broadband service requiring continuous global coverage, the MVC is much larger. Designing deployment sequence for the right MVC is the link between constellation engineering and the business plan.

6. Multi-Shell Deployment and Conjunction Risk

Satellites raising from staging orbits to their operational shell pass through altitude bands occupied by existing operational shells. This creates temporary conjunction exposure that must be screened continuously. Operators manage this by constraining raise trajectories to avoid occupied altitude bands during transit, choosing raise epochs that minimise conjunction probability, and maintaining CAM capability throughout the raise.

Multi-shell deployment also complicates J2 drift tracking. If two shells are being populated simultaneously, their RAAN evolution is independent (different altitudes and inclinations give different J2 rates), and both must reach their correct configurations simultaneously. This requires parallel tracking of both drift timelines and can constrain the order in which shells are populated.

7. Launch Cost and the Constellation Economics

Total launch cost:

C_launch_total = N_launches × C_per_launch

Launch cost is typically 30–50% of total constellation deployment cost, with the remainder split between satellite manufacturing, ground segment, and regulatory compliance. For Starlink's first shell at ~30 launches at an estimated internal cost of ~$20M each, total launch cost is approximately $600M — a concentrated cash flow event in a 2–3 year window.

Exercise 1 — Launch Manifest Calculator

Compute satellites per launch, total launches, deployment timeline, and launch cost for given constellation configurations.

import math

def manifest(n_total, sat_wet_kg, lv_cap_kg,
             lv_cost_m, cadence_yr, name=""):
    spl  = math.floor(lv_cap_kg / sat_wet_kg)
    nlch = math.ceil(n_total / spl)
    yrs  = nlch / cadence_yr
    cost = nlch * lv_cost_m
    cpsk = cost * 1e6 / n_total / 1e3   # $k per sat
    return dict(name=name, spl=spl, nlch=nlch,
                yrs=yrs, cost_m=cost, cpsk=cpsk)

# name, n, sat_kg, lv_kg, lv_cost_m, cadence/yr
configs = [
    ("Starlink v1",    1584, 300, 17400, 20, 15),
    ("OneWeb",          648, 150,  7000, 65,  8),
    ("Kuiper",         1156, 500, 45000, 70, 10),
    ("Small demo",       24,  50,  5000, 60,  4),
]

print(f"{'Name':>14}{'Sats/lch':>10}{'Launches':>10}"
      f"{'Yrs':>6}{'Cost $M':>9}{'$/sat $k':>10}")
print("-" * 61)
for c in configs:
    r = manifest(*c)
    print(f"{r['name']:>14}{r['spl']:>10}{r['nlch']:>10}"
          f"{r['yrs']:>6.1f}{r['cost_m']:>8.0f}M{r['cpsk']:>9.0f}k")

Exercise 2 — Hohmann Raise Δv

Compute the two-burn Hohmann transfer Δv from staging orbit to operational altitude, and the propellant cost.

import math

MU = 398600.4418; RE = 6378.137; G0 = 9.80665

def hohmann(h1, h2):
    r1=(RE+h1)*1e3; r2=(RE+h2)*1e3
    v1=math.sqrt(MU*1e9/r1); v2=math.sqrt(MU*1e9/r2)
    vta=math.sqrt(MU*1e9*2*r2/(r1*(r1+r2)))
    vtp=math.sqrt(MU*1e9*2*r1/(r2*(r1+r2)))
    return vta-v1, v2-vtp, (vta-v1)+(v2-vtp)

def pmass(dry, dv, isp=1600):
    return dry * (math.exp(dv / (G0 * isp)) - 1)

print(f"{'Staging':>8}{'Oper.':>7}{'ΔV1':>9}{'ΔV2':>9}"
      f"{'ΔV_tot':>9}{'m_prop':>10}")
print("-" * 55)
for h1, h2 in [(350,550),(400,550),(450,550),(350,600),(500,600)]:
    dv1,dv2,dvt = hohmann(h1,h2)
    mp = pmass(300, dvt)
    print(f"{h1:>6}km{h2:>6}km{dv1:>8.1f}m/s{dv2:>8.1f}m/s"
          f"{dvt:>8.1f}m/s{mp:>9.2f}kg")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 6: Complete Constellation Sizing Table

All six spine project stages integrated into a single model producing a sizing table for real constellation configurations.

import math

MU=398600.4418; RE=6378.137; G0=9.80665
RHO0=2.0e-10; HSC=70.0
SOL={"min":0.35,"mean":1.0,"max":2.8}

def cap_area(alt,eps):
    r=RE+alt; e=math.radians(eps)
    a=max(-1.,min(1.,RE*math.cos(e)/r))
    rho=math.acos(a)-e
    return (1-math.cos(rho))/2

def drag_yr(alt,beta,s="mean"):
    r=(RE+alt)*1e3; rho=RHO0*SOL[s]*math.exp(-(alt-400)/HSC)
    v=math.sqrt(MU*1e9/r)
    return rho*v**2/beta*365.25*86400

def hohm(h1,h2):
    r1=(RE+h1)*1e3;r2=(RE+h2)*1e3
    v1=math.sqrt(MU*1e9/r1);v2=math.sqrt(MU*1e9/r2)
    vta=math.sqrt(MU*1e9*2*r2/(r1*(r1+r2)))
    vtp=math.sqrt(MU*1e9*2*r1/(r2*(r1+r2)))
    return (vta-v1)+(v2-vtp)

def tot_dv(alt,life,beta,stg=350,cam=5,deorbit=28,s="mean"):
    return drag_yr(alt,beta,s)*life+hohm(stg,alt)+cam*life+deorbit

def pm(dry,dv,isp): return dry*(math.exp(dv/(G0*isp))-1)
