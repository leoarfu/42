```
QUARTER 1
HISTORY
PART II — LEARNING FROM FAILURE

Two constellations, two decades apart, two entirely different failure modes — yet both left fingerprints on every major LEO and MEO programme that came after them. Teledesic was the internet broadband satellite of the late 1990s: 840 satellites, Bill Gates and Craig McCaw as backers, a budget that escalated from $9 billion toward $15 billion, and a technical architecture so complex it was eventually dismantled before a single satellite flew. O3b was the opposite: a lean, realistic MEO broadband constellation for the developing world that got the orbit, the technology, and the market exactly right — and still ran out of money in its original corporate form, requiring a full takeover by SES before it could fulfil its potential. Lesson 5 showed you how a market can evaporate under a constellation under construction. This lesson shows you how a design can become too complex to execute, and how a sound technical concept can still fail for purely financial reasons.

PART I — TELEDESIC

1. Teledesic: The Internet-in-the-Sky That Never Was

In 1994, Bill Gates and Craig McCaw jointly announced a satellite broadband project called Teledesic — a system that would deliver fibre-equivalent broadband speeds to any point on Earth via a massive LEO constellation. The announcement landed in the technology press with a force that reflected the identities of its backers: Gates was the richest man in the world and the mind behind the decade's defining technology company; McCaw had built the largest cellular telephone company in the United States and sold it to AT&T for $11.5 billion in 1994. These were not dreamers or academics. They were the most successful operators in both software and wireless communications. When they announced a $9 billion satellite broadband constellation, the industry took it seriously.

The timing made the ambition feel plausible. 1994 was the year the World Wide Web became a consumer technology. Netscape Navigator would ship the following year. The explosive demand for internet access that would drive the dot-com boom was just beginning to be visible. Teledesic was conceived as the infrastructure layer for a world in which every device, everywhere, needed broadband connectivity — and in 1994, before terrestrial broadband infrastructure had been built, satellite was a plausible delivery mechanism for that vision.

The premise Teledesic was built on was not ridiculous. Terrestrial broadband in 1994 reached a small fraction of the global population. DSL was experimental. Cable internet was in its infancy. Fibre to the home was a distant aspiration. If you believed — correctly, as it turned out — that the world would soon demand broadband everywhere, and you did not believe — incorrectly — that terrestrial infrastructure would be built fast enough to serve that demand, then a global LEO satellite broadband constellation was a rational response. The error was not in identifying the demand; it was in underestimating the speed and economics of terrestrial broadband deployment, and in designing a system whose complexity and cost could not be reduced to an executable programme.

The backers and the structure

Teledesic Corporation was founded by McCaw and Gates with initial funding from their personal fortunes. In 1997, Boeing joined as an equity partner and primary contractor, with an announced investment of $100 million and responsibility for satellite manufacturing. In 2000, Motorola — which had by that point just watched its own Iridium investment collapse — acquired a stake in Teledesic in a deal that combined the McCaw/Gates/Boeing consortium with Motorola's satellite experience. Saudi Prince Alwaleed bin Talal invested $100 million. At its peak capitalization, Teledesic had raised approximately $1 billion in equity with commitments to raise substantially more for full constellation deployment.

2. Teledesic's Original 840-Satellite Architecture

The 1994 Teledesic design was extraordinary in its ambition. The proposed constellation comprised 840 satellites (later quoted as 924 including on-orbit spares) in 21 orbital planes at approximately 700 km altitude, with each plane containing 40 satellites. The orbital inclination was 98.2° — a sun-synchronous orbit — chosen to provide consistent illumination geometry for solar power generation and to ensure uniform coverage across all latitudes.

The capacity ambition

The design specification called for user link data rates of 64 Mbps downlink and 2 Mbps uplink per terminal — numbers that were extraordinary by 1994 standards and would remain impressive by early 2000s standards. The aggregate system capacity was designed to support tens of millions of simultaneous users. The user terminals were to be small, affordable, fixed installations comparable in form factor to a residential satellite TV dish — not handheld devices. This user terminal philosophy was directly informed by the Iridium problem: Teledesic explicitly chose to target fixed and portable broadband rather than mobile voice, avoiding the physics constraint of the handheld satellite phone.

The Ka-band user link (28.6–29.1 GHz downlink, 18.8–19.3 GHz uplink) was chosen for its large available bandwidth, which was essential for delivering the multi-megabit per second data rates the specification required. Ka-band also offered smaller antenna sizes for a given gain relative to lower frequency bands, enabling the compact terminal form factor that was part of Teledesic's product vision. The trade-off — as L05 noted for any Ka-band system — was increased rain attenuation, which Teledesic planned to manage through link margin and adaptive coding.

The switching architecture: the source of complexity

The most technically distinctive and ultimately most problematic aspect of Teledesic's original design was its on-board ATM switching architecture. Rather than a simple bent-pipe relay (receive from the ground, amplify, retransmit — the architecture of nearly all GEO satellites and early LEO designs), Teledesic's satellites were designed to be full ATM (Asynchronous Transfer Mode) switches in space. Each satellite would accept ATM cells from user terminals and from adjacent satellites via ISLs, perform routing table lookups, switch cells to the correct output port, and forward them either to the destination user terminal if within the satellite's footprint or to the next satellite in the routing chain via ISL.

This space-based ATM switching architecture was chosen because ATM was the dominant carrier-grade packet-switching technology of the early 1990s — the protocol that telecommunications engineers in 1993–1994 believed would be the foundation of all future broadband networks, terrestrial and satellite alike. ATM's fixed-size 53-byte cells, strict QoS guarantees, and hardware-switching efficiency made it the technology of choice for synchronous voice and video alongside asynchronous data. Building ATM switches into every satellite would enable a pure packet-switched broadband network in space, with guaranteed QoS for different traffic classes and no dependence on a large terrestrial gateway network for routing.

THE ATM BET THAT LOST ATM (Asynchronous Transfer Mode) was the dominant carrier broadband technology from approximately 1990 to 1998, when IP — specifically IP over Ethernet, then IP over SONET/SDH — began displacing it. By 2000, it was clear that the internet would run on IP, not ATM. Teledesic's original architecture was designed around an ATM core at precisely the moment that ATM was being superseded. Redesigning the satellite to use IP switching instead of ATM added further cost, schedule delay, and complexity to a programme that was already strained. The Teledesic case is one of the clearest examples in satellite history of a system designed around a specific network protocol that did not survive long enough to become operational — the satellite architecture was locked in before the protocol wars were resolved.

The ISL grid

Like Iridium, Teledesic planned an extensive ISL network to route traffic through the constellation without depending on a large ground gateway infrastructure. Each satellite was to have four ISL links: two in-plane (to neighbouring satellites in the same orbital plane) and two cross-plane (to satellites in the nearest adjacent planes). At Ka-band, with the switching and routing load added to the ISL communications burden, the onboard processing requirements were far beyond anything that had been flown in 1994. Space-qualified processors of the mid-1990s were several generations behind commercial-grade equivalents and consumed substantially more power per computation. Meeting Teledesic's ATM switching throughput requirements on space-qualified hardware within a reasonable mass and power budget was an unsolved engineering problem at the time of the original design.

3. The Redesign Spiral: From 840 to 288 to Cancellation

Teledesic's history between 1994 and 2002 is a case study in design spiral: a programme in which each attempt to make the system more executable introduces compromises that reduce capability, which in turn undermine the business case, which leads to further redesign, in a cycle that ends either in a viable simplified system or in cancellation. Teledesic's spiral ended in cancellation.

The 1997 redesign: 840 to 288

By 1997, Teledesic's engineers and Boeing's satellite team had concluded that the original 840-satellite design was not manufacturable on a reasonable schedule and within a reasonable budget. The primary driver of the redesign was satellite complexity: the ATM switching payload, Ka-band phased array antennas, and ISL terminals required per satellite represented a level of onboard processing that could not be achieved in a space-qualified form within the power and mass budget of a satellite that could be launched in large quantities at affordable cost. The 1994 design had also been specified before the Internet Protocol had clearly won the packet-switching protocol war — by 1997, the decision to redesign around IP rather than ATM was obvious, but it required substantial rework of the satellite architecture.

The 1997 redesign reduced the constellation to 288 satellites in 12 planes of 24 satellites each, at a slightly higher altitude to compensate for the reduced satellite density. The user link capacity specification was also reduced — 720 kbps download and 200 kbps upload in the revised design, compared to the original 64/2 Mbps. This was still impressive by 1997 standards, but the reduction from the original headline specification eroded the competitive differentiation argument. If Teledesic now offered approximately 720 kbps — a speed that early DSL and cable modem services were beginning to approach — the justification for spending $9–15 billion on a satellite constellation to achieve what a telephone company could provide over copper wire became harder to sustain.

The Motorola merger attempt and subsequent collapse

In 2000, Teledesic and Motorola announced a merger that would combine Teledesic with Motorola's satellite technology business — the same group that had built Iridium. The merged entity would have combined Teledesic's spectrum licences and backing with Motorola's engineering capability. The merger was announced against the backdrop of the dot-com boom's final months; by the time regulatory approval was obtained, the NASDAQ had collapsed by 70% from its March 2000 peak, the dot-com bubble had burst, and the enthusiasm for large capital-intensive technology infrastructure investments had evaporated. The merger did not proceed to completion in a form that produced a viable path to deployment.

THE SPECTRUM LEGACY Teledesic's FCC Ka-band spectrum authorisation did not disappear when the company shut down. Spectrum licences are regulatory assets, and the process of reassigning or maintaining them after a company's collapse is complex. Several of Teledesic's Ka-band spectrum positions were held in reserve or reassigned through regulatory processes that influenced Ka-band satellite planning for years afterward. The lesson — that spectrum filings are durable assets that survive programme cancellation and can be valuable independently of whether the filing company ever launches — is directly relevant to understanding why companies file for large satellite constellations before they have the capital to build them.

4. Why Teledesic Failed: Complexity, Cost, and the Dot-Com Crash

Unlike Iridium, which built and launched a fully operational system before failing commercially, Teledesic failed before it built anything. This makes its failure a different category of cautionary tale: not a market misjudgement but a programme execution failure — a system whose complexity could not be reduced to an executable, fundable programme within the available technology and capital market windows.

The complexity problem

The core technical problem Teledesic could never fully solve was the satellite unit cost and complexity. Building 288 (let alone 840) satellites, each containing Ka-band phased array antennas, Ka-band ISL terminals, IP switching hardware capable of multi-megabit per second routing, and all associated power, attitude control, and propulsion systems, at a per-unit cost that allowed the total programme to be financially viable, required a level of satellite manufacturing industrialisation that the industry had not achieved in the 1990s.

At the time, a typical communications satellite cost $150–300 million to manufacture and launch. Even assuming significant economies of scale from a large production run, achieving a per-satellite cost of $20–30 million for a sophisticated Ka-band broadband satellite was not credibly demonstrated in any engineering analysis that Boeing or Teledesic produced publicly. The total programme cost estimates of $9 billion (original) escalating toward $15 billion (revised) were driven precisely by this unit cost problem: you cannot design a complex system and then assume the cost will drop to whatever number makes the business case work.

This is the "complexity tax" in satellite programme design: every feature added to the satellite payload increases unit cost, reduces manufacturing yield (the fraction of built units that pass qualification), and increases launch mass (which increases launch cost). Teledesic's specification was written as a broadband network design — starting from what the user needed and working backward — without adequate constraint from what was manufacturable at satellite scale and cost in 1997. The gap between what the specification required and what was manufacturable never closed.

The dot-com crash as timing shock

Even if Teledesic's engineering problems had been solved, the capital required to fund full constellation deployment — at minimum $5–10 billion beyond what had been committed — was dependent on investor appetite for large infrastructure bets in technology. The collapse of NASDAQ between March 2000 and October 2002 (peak to trough: approximately 78% decline) destroyed that appetite completely. Teledesic needed to raise multiple billions of dollars in an environment where venture capital and institutional investors had just experienced catastrophic losses on technology infrastructure bets. The timing could not have been worse.

The dot-com crash also fundamentally changed the competitive landscape. By 2001, DSL and cable broadband were being deployed at significant scale in North America and Europe, with monthly costs falling toward $30–50 per household. The addressable market for satellite broadband was narrowing to exactly the segment where terrestrial infrastructure economics would never be positive — very rural and remote areas — which was a smaller and less commercially attractive market than the broad broadband-everywhere vision that had justified the original Teledesic investment.

What Teledesic contributed despite never launching

Teledesic's most durable contribution was probably to Ka-band spectrum planning. Its FCC filings established the principle that LEO broadband systems should use Ka-band for user links, and its engineering work on Ka-band satellite phased arrays informed subsequent programmes including ViaSat's GEO HTS systems and the early design work that eventually became Starlink's Ka-band payload. The programme also produced a generation of engineers with detailed knowledge of the challenges involved in high-throughput LEO broadband satellite design — knowledge that went with those engineers when they moved to subsequent programmes. Institutional knowledge from failed programmes often contributes more to subsequent successes than is acknowledged.

PART II — O3b GEN1

5. O3b Gen1: The Right Idea, the Wrong Balance Sheet

O3b is a fundamentally different kind of case study from both Iridium and Teledesic. Where Iridium built the wrong system for the wrong market, and Teledesic built nothing at all, O3b built a technically correct system for a real and underserved market — and still required rescue. The reasons for O3b's financial difficulties illuminate a category of risk distinct from both market failure and technical failure: the risk of being right too early, at a scale too small to sustain the capital structure needed to get there.

O3b Networks was founded in 2007 by Greg Wyler, a technology entrepreneur whose previous venture had built fibre and wireless broadband infrastructure in Rwanda. Wyler's insight was specific and data-driven: small island nations, remote equatorial communities, and developing-world cities were paying extremely high prices for extremely poor satellite connectivity via GEO — often $1,000–$5,000 per month for 1–2 Mbps of shared C-band or Ku-band capacity. The terrestrial fibre infrastructure that connected these communities to the global internet was thin or nonexistent, and the economics of building it were not positive on any near-term horizon. GEO satellite was the only available option, and GEO's 600 ms latency made real-time applications — VoIP, video conferencing, interactive educational content — unusable.

O3b's name — "Other 3 Billion" — referred explicitly to the approximately three billion people in 2007 who had no access to affordable broadband. It was a mission-driven framing as much as a commercial one, and it attracted an unusual set of initial investors including Google (which invested approximately $50 million), HSBC, Liberty Global, and a group of development-finance-oriented investors including the African Development Bank and KGNM. The combination of mission framing and credible commercial opportunity was effective for early-stage fundraising in a way that a purely commercial pitch might not have been.

6. O3b's MEO Architecture: Why the Orbit Choice Was Correct

Greg Wyler's choice of Medium Earth Orbit at 8,062 km altitude was not the obvious selection for a broadband satellite constellation in 2007. LEO was theoretically preferred for latency; GEO was the established technology. MEO occupied a middle position that required a specific argument for its selection — and the argument was good.

The MEO latency-coverage tradeoff

At 8,062 km, O3b satellites experience a one-way propagation delay of approximately 27 ms at nadir, producing an RTT (round-trip time for the user-to-user path via satellite) of approximately 130–160 ms. This is substantially lower than GEO's 550–700 ms RTT and sufficiently below the G.114 recommended 150 ms threshold to enable VoIP, video conferencing, and interactive applications that GEO cannot support. It is higher than LEO's 25–50 ms RTT but, for O3b's target market — fixed broadband trunk capacity to island nations and remote communities — the difference between 150 ms and 40 ms RTT is far less commercially significant than the difference between 150 ms and 600 ms.

The coverage geometry at 8,062 km gives O3b a footprint diameter of approximately 5,000 km per satellite at the minimum elevation angle O3b's steerable beams can serve. This means that a relatively small constellation — 8 or 12 satellites — can provide continuous coverage of the equatorial band (±45° latitude) where most of O3b's target customers are located. With LEO at 550 km, providing continuous coverage of the same equatorial region would require several hundred satellites. O3b's small initial fleet was directly enabled by its MEO orbit choice: 8 satellites could serve its initial customer base in a way that no LEO constellation could have done at the same scale and cost.

The steerable beam architecture

Each O3b satellite carries 10–12 steerable Ka-band spot beams, each approximately 0.65° in diameter and capable of providing approximately 1.6 Gbps of aggregate forward capacity. The beams can be steered to point at customer sites within the satellite's footprint, allowing a single satellite to simultaneously serve multiple geographically dispersed clients — a Pacific Island nation, a cruise ship corridor, an African coastal city — with dedicated high-capacity beams. This beam agility was the architectural feature that most differentiated O3b from existing MEO and GEO alternatives. A GEO HTS satellite with fixed spot beams could not dynamically redirect capacity from an undersubscribed beam to an oversubscribed one; O3b's steerable beams could, at the cost of beam-switching time (a few tens of milliseconds to repoint a beam, which was operationally acceptable for the traffic types O3b was carrying).

The ground terminal required to access O3b services was a 2.4-metre Ka-band dish — physically larger than a typical VSAT terminal, but no larger than many existing enterprise satellite terminals. The dish needed to be capable of tracking the satellite across the sky as it moved (O3b's satellites are not geostationary — they traverse the sky over approximately 40 minutes from horizon to horizon), which required a motor-driven tracking mount. The tracking requirement added cost and complexity to the terminal relative to a GEO fixed-pointing dish, but for the community gateway application O3b was targeting — one dish serving an entire island or remote town — the terminal cost was a small fraction of the total system cost and was easily amortised over the subscriber base.

DEEPER CONTEXT — WHY NOT LEO IN 2007?

A reasonable question is why O3b chose MEO rather than LEO in 2007, given that LEO's latency advantage is clear and Iridium had already demonstrated LEO ISL routing. The answer comes down to the state of technology and capital in 2007. A LEO constellation providing continuous coverage of the equatorial band at sufficient capacity for O3b's trunk applications would have required at minimum several hundred satellites — implying a programme cost in the billions of dollars before a single customer could be served. O3b raised approximately $1.2 billion total before the SES rescue, barely enough to build and launch 8 MEO satellites. That same capital could not have built a functional LEO coverage layer for O3b's use case. The MEO orbit was not the theoretically optimal choice on latency grounds; it was the practically executable choice given O3b's capital position. This is the architectural lesson: optimal theory must be constrained by available capital and manufacturing capability. A MEO system that actually launches and serves customers is superior to a LEO system that is cancelled.

7. The Business Model: Connecting the Unconnected

O3b's go-to-market model was carefully designed to avoid the Iridium mistake of targeting a market that would be served by improving terrestrial alternatives. O3b specifically targeted locations and applications where terrestrial connectivity was structurally impossible or economically nonviable for the foreseeable future.

The target customer segments

O3b's initial customer base was built around three segments. Telecom operators in developing island nations and coastal regions — the Pacific Islands, the Caribbean, equatorial Africa and coastal West Africa — were the primary segment. These operators were paying extremely high prices for very limited GEO satellite capacity to backhaul their terrestrial mobile and broadband networks. O3b could deliver multi-gigabit capacity to a single gateway at a price per bit substantially lower than available GEO alternatives, with the latency advantage that enabled previously impossible applications. The operator would then distribute this capacity across its territory using its own terrestrial network.

Maritime was the second segment, specifically the cruise industry and high-value shipping. Cruise ships on equatorial and near-equatorial itineraries — Caribbean, Pacific, Indian Ocean — are within O3b's coverage zone and have substantial passenger broadband demand that existing GEO VSAT systems served inadequately. O3b offered the same latency-and-capacity advantage to maritime customers as to telecom operators, with the additional benefit that the satellite's steerable beams could track a moving vessel without requiring the vessel's antenna to repoint its dish (the satellite's beam moves to follow the ship while the ship's fixed dish antenna tracks the satellite across the sky).

Government and enterprise was the third segment: remote mining operations, oil platforms, military forward operating bases, and government installations in regions within O3b's coverage zone. These customers shared the telecom operator's problem — inadequate existing connectivity at high cost — and had the institutional procurement processes and budget authority to sign multi-year contracts at prices that supported O3b's economics.

The price and capacity model

O3b sold capacity on a committed information rate basis — a guaranteed data rate per customer, invoiced monthly, regardless of actual usage. A typical 2014 O3b contract provided 100–500 Mbps of committed capacity to a telecom operator gateway for $100,000–$300,000 per month. For an island operator that had previously been buying 10 Mbps of shared GEO capacity for $50,000 per month, this represented a 10–50× improvement in capacity at a 2–6× increase in cost — a dramatically better price per bit, with the latency bonus on top. The value proposition was genuinely compelling for the addressable market.

The fundamental commercial validation of O3b's model was that its customers did not have improving terrestrial alternatives on any foreseeable horizon. Pacific Island nations that are geographically isolated by thousands of kilometres of ocean are not going to receive submarine fibre cable connections based on market economics alone (though several Pacific nations have received such connections through aid-funded programmes — a risk O3b eventually had to manage). This structural absence of terrestrial alternatives is what O3b's business case required, and it was present in the initial target market in a way that Iridium's business case had assumed — wrongly — for the business traveller market.

8. Financial Collapse and the SES Rescue

O3b Networks launched its first four satellites in June 2013, with commercial service beginning in September 2013. Four more satellites launched in December 2013. The service worked as designed: customers in Pacific Island nations, the Caribbean, and equatorial Africa began receiving multi-gigabit, sub-200 ms satellite connectivity for the first time. By the accounts of its early customers, O3b delivered exactly what it had promised technically.

The financial problem

Despite technical success and genuine customer adoption, O3b Networks was in persistent financial difficulty. The company had raised approximately $1.2 billion by the time of its first launches, through multiple rounds of equity and debt financing. The capital structure included senior secured debt that required debt service payments that O3b's revenue, growing from a standing start in 2013, could not immediately cover. The company had insufficient capital reserves to weather the ramp-up period — the time between launching satellites and building a customer base large enough to cover full operating costs plus debt service.

Several factors made the ramp-up problem worse than a straightforward financial model would have predicted. Customer procurement cycles in developing-world telecom operators were long: signing a contract, installing the ground terminal infrastructure, and beginning to generate meaningful revenue could take 12–24 months from initial contact. The maritime market required type-approval processes for shipboard terminal installation. Government procurement added further delays. O3b's revenue was growing — but not fast enough to stay ahead of its debt service obligations.

Greg Wyler himself departed O3b in late 2012, before the first launches, in circumstances that were publicly described as a disagreement with the board over the company's strategic direction. Wyler went on to found WorldVu Satellites Limited — which became OneWeb, the subject of Lesson 8. His departure removed the founder's institutional knowledge and relationships from O3b at a critical juncture, adding management disruption to financial stress.

The SES acquisition

SES S.A. — the Luxembourg-based GEO satellite operator that is one of the two largest commercial satellite operators in the world — had been an O3b investor since 2010. SES's strategic interest in O3b was clear: O3b's MEO technology complemented SES's GEO fleet, providing latency performance that GEO could not match and coverage of equatorial markets where SES had customers but was constrained by GEO's latency limitations. In 2016, SES acquired majority ownership of O3b Networks in a transaction that effectively rescued O3b from the financial distress that had accumulated since its commercial launch. SES subsequently acquired the remaining O3b equity in 2019, making O3b a wholly owned SES subsidiary.

Under SES ownership, O3b was rebranded as SES Networks and subsequently as the O3b mPOWER programme — a second-generation constellation of 11 (later expanded to more) MEO satellites with dramatically improved capacity, featuring each satellite's 5,000-beam steerable array described in Lesson 4. The mPOWER programme was funded from SES's balance sheet and credit facility, applying the financial strength of a large, established satellite operator to a technically sound system that had been unable to scale under its original capital structure.

THE WYLER CONSTELLATION THREAD Greg Wyler's career traces a remarkable thread through 1990s–2020s satellite development. After O3b he founded WorldVu Satellites in 2012, which was reorganised as OneWeb in 2015 with SoftBank, Airbus, and Qualcomm as investors. OneWeb itself went bankrupt in March 2020 (covered in Lesson 8), was purchased by the UK Government and Bharti Global, and subsequently became part of Eutelsat. Each of Wyler's ventures was technically ahead of its time and commercially financially stressed. The pattern — visionary architect, correct technical concept, insufficient or mispriced capital structure — is consistent across both O3b and OneWeb, and is perhaps the defining characteristic of a certain class of satellite entrepreneur.

9. What Both Cases Teach — A Unified Failure Taxonomy

Iridium, Teledesic, and O3b Gen1 represent three distinct failure modes in satellite constellation history. Placing them in a structured taxonomy clarifies what each case teaches and how the lessons differ.

Failure Mode 1: Market timing (Iridium)

Iridium built the right system for the right market — in the wrong decade. The system was technically operational and served its designed function. Its failure was that the market it was designed to exploit (fragmented mobile communications) was unified by a competing technology (GSM) during its construction. The lesson is about market assumption validation over long timescales: any assumption about the competitive landscape that must hold true for 10+ years to make a programme viable is a high-risk assumption that requires explicit monitoring and built-in response mechanisms.

Failure Mode 2: Technical complexity (Teledesic)

Teledesic could not execute its own design. The architecture was specified top-down from user requirements without adequate bottom-up validation of what was manufacturable at an acceptable unit cost. The redesign spiral — each iteration reducing capability to approach manufacturability, each reduction eroding the business case — ended in cancellation before a single satellite flew. The lesson is about design-to-cost discipline: the satellite count, mass budget, and onboard capability must be constrained from the beginning by achievable manufacturing economics, not specified as requirements to be met and then costed.

Failure Mode 3: Financial structure (O3b Gen1)

O3b built the right system for the right market at the right time — and still required rescue because its capital structure could not survive the revenue ramp-up period. The system was technically validated; the customers were real; the market was exactly as the business plan described. The failure was in the balance between debt obligations (which were fixed and front-loaded) and revenue growth (which was real but slower than required to service the debt). The lesson is about capital structure design: the financing structure must be tolerant of slower-than-planned revenue ramp-up, particularly for systems serving new markets with long customer procurement cycles.

The meta-lesson: resilience requires all three to be correct simultaneously

A satellite constellation programme survives only when all three dimensions are sound simultaneously: the technical architecture must be executable within available manufacturing and launch economics; the market must be sufficiently defensible against competing technologies over the deployment timeline; and the financial structure must be tolerant of the inevitable variance between plan and reality in both cost and revenue. Iridium failed on market. Teledesic failed on technical execution and capital timing. O3b Gen1 failed on financial structure. All three had at least one dimension wrong. Iridium NEXT and O3b mPOWER succeeded because the survivors had corrected the relevant dimension of failure from their predecessor.

Modern LEO broadband constellations — Starlink, Kuiper, OneWeb, Telesat Lightspeed — are all operating in the shadow of these three failures, and their designs reflect explicit responses to each failure mode. Starlink's vertical integration of launch and satellite manufacturing directly addresses the Teledesic cost problem. SpaceX's self-funding model addresses the Iridium and O3b financial structure problem. The targeting of rural residential broadband — a market with no terrestrial alternative — addresses the Iridium market problem. Whether these responses are sufficient to make the modern programmes succeed is the question that the rest of this curriculum explores.
``` 

```
Exercise 2 — O3b MEO vs LEO Coverage Economics

Quantify why O3b chose MEO over LEO: compute the minimum number of satellites for continuous equatorial coverage at both altitudes, and the capital required to build and launch each option, showing the MEO advantage at O3b's constrained capital level.

``` 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

Re = 6_371  # km

def nadir_half_angle_deg(h_km, elev_min_deg=10):
    eps  = np.radians(elev_min_deg)
    rho  = np.arcsin(Re / (Re + h_km) * np.cos(eps))
    lam  = np.pi/2 - eps - rho
    return np.degrees(lam)

def equatorial_coverage_min_sats(h_km, lat_band_deg=45, elev_min=10):
    """
    Minimum satellites for continuous coverage of ±lat_band_deg using
    a Walker constellation at inclination ~ lat_band_deg.
    Simplified: uses footprint area vs band area.
    """
    lam     = nadir_half_angle_deg(h_km, elev_min)
    # footprint circle radius on surface
    fp_rad  = np.radians(lam)
    # area of one footprint
    fp_area = 2 * np.pi * Re**2 * (1 - np.cos(fp_rad))
    # area of the equatorial band ±lat_band_deg (both hemispheres)
    lat_r   = np.radians(lat_band_deg)
    band_area = 4 * np.pi * Re**2 * np.sin(lat_r)
    # apply 1.5× for coverage continuity (overlapping footprints needed)
    return max(4, int(np.ceil(band_area / fp_area * 1.5)))

# Satellite cost assumptions (2007–2010 era)
SAT_COST_MEO = 60e6   # USD — O3b Thales Alenia, radiation-hardened
SAT_COST_LEO = 15e6   # USD — simpler LEO (optimistic for 2007)
LAUNCH_COST_MEO = 60e6  # Soyuz/Arianespace, ~4 sats per launch
LAUNCH_COST_LEO = 25e6  # per satellite (shared launch, e.g. Dnepr multi-sat)

O3B_CAPITAL = 1.2e9   # USD — total available capital

altitudes = {
    "VLEO 350 km":      350,
    "LEO 550 km":       550,
    "LEO 800 km":       800,
    "LEO 1200 km":     1200,
    "O3b MEO 8062 km": 8062,
}

print(f"{'Regime':<22} {'Alt km':>7} {'Min sats':>10} "
      f"{'Sat cost $M':>12} {'Launch $M':>10} {'Total $B':>10} {'Feasible w/ $1.2B?':>20}")
print("-" * 95)

for name, h in altitudes.items():
    if h < 2000:
        n_sats = equatorial_coverage_min_sats(h, lat_band_deg=45, elev_min=10)
        sc, lc = SAT_COST_LEO, LAUNCH_COST_LEO
    else:
        n_sats = equatorial_coverage_min_sats(h, lat_band_deg=45, elev_min=10)
        sc, lc = SAT_COST_MEO, LAUNCH_COST_MEO

    total_cost = n_sats * (sc + lc)
    feasible   = "YES" if total_cost <= O3B_CAPITAL else "NO"
    flag       = " ← O3b choice" if "8062" in name else ""
    print(f"{name:<22} {h:>7,} {n_sats:>10,} "
          f"${sc/1e6:>10.0f}M ${lc/1e6:>8.0f}M ${total_cost/1e9:>9.2f}B "
          f"{feasible:>20}{flag}")

# --- Sweep: total cost vs altitude ---
alts = np.logspace(np.log10(300), np.log10(15000), 300)
costs_leo = []
costs_meo = []
for h in alts:
    n   = equatorial_coverage_min_sats(h, 45, 10)
    if h < 2000:
        costs_leo.append(n * (SAT_COST_LEO + LAUNCH_COST_LEO) / 1e9)
        costs_meo.append(np.nan)
    else:
        costs_leo.append(np.nan)
        costs_meo.append(n * (SAT_COST_MEO + LAUNCH_COST_MEO) / 1e9)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(11, 6))
fig.patch.set_facecolor('#0a0a0a')
ax.set_facecolor('#0d0d0d')
ax.tick_params(colors='#888')
for sp in ax.spines.values(): sp.set_color('#333')

ax.semilogy(alts, costs_leo, color='#39ff14', linewidth=2, label='LEO (simple sat, $15M+$25M/sat)')
ax.semilogy(alts, costs_meo, color='#00d9ff', linewidth=2, label='MEO (hardened sat, $60M+$60M/sat)')

ax.axhline(y=O3B_CAPITAL/1e9, color='#ff004f', linewidth=1.5, linestyle='--',
           label=f"O3b available capital (~${O3B_CAPITAL/1e9:.1f}B)")

# Mark O3b actual
n_o3b = equatorial_coverage_min_sats(8062, 45, 10)
cost_o3b = n_o3b * (SAT_COST_MEO + LAUNCH_COST_MEO) / 1e9
ax.scatter([8062], [cost_o3b], color='#ffaa00', s=120, zorder=7,
           label=f'O3b MEO 8,062 km ({n_o3b} sats, ${cost_o3b:.2f}B)')

ax.set_xlabel('Orbital altitude (km)', color='#aaa')
ax.set_ylabel('Total constellation cost ($ billions, log scale)', color='#aaa')
ax.set_title('Minimum-Coverage Constellation Cost vs Altitude\n(equatorial ±45° band, 2007-era costs)', color='#ccc')
ax.legend(facecolor='#111', edgecolor='#333', labelcolor='white', fontsize=8)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.07, color='#444', which='both')
ax.set_xlim(300, 15000)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('o3b_meo_vs_leo.png', dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight')
plt.show()
print(f"\nO3b MEO minimum satellites needed (±45° band): {n_o3b}")
print(f"Estimated cost at MEO economics: ${cost_o3b:.2f}B")
print(f"Within O3b capital of ${O3B_CAPITAL/1e9:.1f}B: {'YES' if cost_o3b <= O3B_CAPITAL/1e9 else 'NO'}")
