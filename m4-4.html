RF & LINK BUDGETS

The link equation assumes free-space propagation. The signal does not travel through free space. The final tens of kilometres of every satellite link traverse the troposphere — the layer where weather happens. At Ka-band and above, a moderate rain event imposes 10–20 dB of attenuation in seconds. This lesson quantifies each atmospheric contributor, presents the ITU-R P.618 prediction framework, and delivers Stage 4 of the spine engine: rain attenuation computed from climate zone and target availability rather than assumed as a fixed margin.

1. Gaseous Absorption

The atmosphere contains gases that absorb microwave signals at specific resonant frequencies. Two are significant for satellite communications.

Oxygen (O₂) has a cluster of absorption lines near 60 GHz and a weaker line near 119 GHz. At frequencies below 30 GHz, oxygen absorption is small but non-negligible — approximately 0.01–0.05 dB/km at Ka-band, contributing roughly 0.1–0.3 dB of total path attenuation for a vertical path through the troposphere.

Water vapour (H₂O) has absorption lines at 22.2 GHz and 183 GHz, with the 22.2 GHz line falling within the Ka-band uplink region. Water vapour absorption depends strongly on surface precipitable water vapour (PWV), varying by location and season. At Ka-band and moderate humidity, water vapour contributes 0.05–0.3 dB of zenith attenuation, rising to 0.5–1.5 dB in tropical, high-humidity environments.

At elevation angle ε, the slant-path attenuation is:

A_gas(ε) ≈ A_gas_zenith / sin(ε)

At 10° elevation, the slant path through the atmosphere is approximately 5.8× longer than the zenith path. For a Ka-band system at 10° elevation this adds approximately 0.3–0.9 dB of gaseous loss beyond the clear-sky overhead figure.

2. Cloud and Fog Attenuation

Liquid water droplets in clouds and fog scatter and absorb microwave signals, though less severely than rain. The key distinction is drop size: rain drops (0.1–8 mm diameter) are much larger than cloud droplets (typically 0.01–0.1 mm), and rain attenuation scales strongly with drop size.

At Ka-band, cloud attenuation for a standard cloud layer ranges from 0.1–0.5 dB for light cloud to 0.5–2.0 dB for thick stratocumulus. Cumulonimbus associated with heavy rain can add up to 5 dB, but in practice this is dominated by the rain attenuation below the cloud base. Cloud attenuation is included in link budget analysis as part of the ITU-R P.618 statistical model rather than computed separately.

3. Rain Attenuation

Rain attenuation is the dominant atmospheric impairment for Ka-band and above, arising from absorption (converting microwave energy to heat) and scattering (redirecting energy out of the signal beam).

Specific attenuation (ITU-R P.838)

The specific attenuation γ_R (dB/km) through rain of rate R (mm/hr):

γ_R = k_R · R^α

where k_R and α are frequency-dependent empirical coefficients from ITU-R P.838:

Rain at the Ka-band uplink (30 GHz) is approximately 3–4× more severe than at Ku-band (14 GHz) for the same rain rate, and the ratio grows with frequency — the primary rain-fade disadvantage of Ka-band uplinks.

ITU-R P.618 prediction model

The P.618 model predicts rain attenuation exceeded for a given percentage of time at a specified location, frequency, elevation angle, and availability target. It takes: (1) the rain rate R₀.₀₁ exceeded for 0.01% of an average year from ITU-R P.837; (2) the effective rain height h_R from ITU-R P.839 (~3–5 km typically); (3) elevation angle; (4) frequency.

4. Scintillation

Tropospheric scintillation is rapid amplitude and phase variation of the received signal caused by turbulent mixing of air masses with different refractive indices. Unlike rain attenuation (which persists for minutes or hours), scintillation occurs at 0.1–100 Hz and produces short-duration signal fluctuations of ±0.5–3 dB at Ka-band under humid, hot conditions.

Scintillation is most severe at low elevation angles, high frequencies, and in hot, humid environments near the equator or in summer. For a Ka-band LEO link at ε ≥ 20°, scintillation typically contributes 0.5–1.5 dB of additional fade margin requirement. It is included in the link budget as a fixed margin term rather than a separate statistical model.

SCINTILLATION VS RAIN — TIMESCALES MATTER Rain fades evolve over minutes; scintillation fluctuates at sub-second timescales. ACM responds too slowly to track scintillation — it must be included as a fixed margin. Rain fades are slow enough for ACM to respond. The distinction determines whether an impairment is handled by margin or by adaptive mitigation.

5. Combining Atmospheric Impairments

Total atmospheric attenuation:

A_total = A_gas + A_cloud + A_rain + A_scint

In practice: (1) compute rain attenuation A_rain(p%) from ITU-R P.618 for the target availability — this implicitly includes cloud attenuation during rain events; (2) add clear-sky gaseous attenuation A_gas(ε) as a constant loss; (3) add scintillation margin A_scint as a fixed term; (4) sum: A_total = A_gas + A_rain(p%) + A_scint.

The rain attenuation statistic is the dominant variable term. The m4-1 example carries a 15.5 dB clear-sky margin at 16-APSK 3/4 (208.3 Msym/s) and clears the 99.9% bar — but only because its per-beam EIRP is generous. A cost-optimised design with the conventional 3–4 dB of clear-sky margin at its peak MODCOD is insufficient — closing that link requires higher EIRP, better G/T, ACM, or accepting a lower availability target.

6. Fade Mitigation Techniques

Adaptive coding and modulation (ACM): the modulation scheme and coding rate are varied in real time in response to measured link quality. During rain fade the system steps down from e.g. 16-APSK 3/4 to QPSK 1/2, accepting lower data rate in exchange for more robust operation. The link never fails; throughput drops. This is the standard approach in DVB-S2X systems and is built into the air interface design.

Uplink power control (UPC): during an uplink rain event, the terminal increases transmit power to compensate attenuation. Available headroom is typically 3–6 dB — adequate for moderate events but insufficient for the tail of the fade distribution. Unlike ACM, UPC maintains data rate within its headroom range.

Site diversity: two geographically separated gateway sites serve the same satellite simultaneously. Rain cells have limited horizontal extent (typically 5–20 km for convective events), so the probability that both sites experience deep rain simultaneously is much lower than for a single site. Sites separated by 10–20 km achieve diversity gains of 5–10 dB in heavy-rain climates. Primarily applied to gateway links — the cost is prohibitive for user terminals.

Frequency diversity: dropping to a lower frequency band (e.g. Ku-band) during Ka-band rain events. Practical only with dual-band terminal capability — uncommon in consumer equipment.

Higher elevation angle: serving users at higher minimum elevation reduces the slant-path attenuation at the cost of smaller coverage footprint per satellite or shorter service arc per pass.

Exercise 1 — Specific Attenuation Model (ITU-R P.838)

Compute rain-specific attenuation at different frequencies and rain rates using interpolated P.838 coefficients.

import math

P838 = {
    4.0:(0.000650,1.121), 7.0:(0.00301,1.332), 10.0:(0.00887,1.264),
    14.0:(0.0229,1.265),  20.0:(0.0751,1.099), 30.0:(0.187,1.021),
    47.0:(0.546,0.911),   60.0:(0.953,0.826),  80.0:(1.425,0.769),
}

def specific_atten(R_mmhr, freq_ghz):
    freqs = sorted(P838.keys())
    if freq_ghz <= freqs[0]:   k,a = P838[freqs[0]]
    elif freq_ghz >= freqs[-1]: k,a = P838[freqs[-1]]
    else:
        for i,f in enumerate(freqs[:-1]):
            if f <= freq_ghz <= freqs[i+1]:
                t = (math.log10(freq_ghz)-math.log10(f)) / \
                    (math.log10(freqs[i+1])-math.log10(f))
                k = P838[f][0] + t*(P838[freqs[i+1]][0]-P838[f][0])
                a = P838[f][1] + t*(P838[freqs[i+1]][1]-P838[f][1])
                break
    return k * R_mmhr**a

rain_rates = [5, 15, 25, 50, 100, 145]
freqs = [14.0, 20.0, 30.0, 47.0, 80.0]
print("Specific attenuation γ_R (dB/km) — ITU-R P.838\n")
print(f"{'R(mm/hr)':>10}" + "".join(f"{f:>9.0f}GHz" for f in freqs))
print("-" * (10+10*len(freqs)))
for R in rain_rates:
    print(f"{R:>10}" + "".join(f"{specific_atten(R,f):>10.2f}" for f in freqs))

Exercise 2 — Slant-Path Attenuation vs Elevation

Compute total path attenuation as a function of elevation angle and rain rate, and compare bands at 30° elevation.

import math

def specific_atten(R, f):
    P = {14:(.0229,1.265),20:(.0751,1.099),30:(.187,1.021),47:(.546,.911)}
    k,a = min(P.items(),key=lambda x:abs(x[0]-f))[1]
    return k*R**a

def rain_atten_slant(R_mmhr, freq_ghz, elev_deg, h_rain=4.0, h_stn=0.1):
    e  = math.radians(elev_deg)
    Ls = (h_rain-h_stn)/math.sin(e)
    Lg = Ls*math.cos(e)
    L0 = 35.0*math.exp(-0.015*R_mmhr)   # rain-cell size parameter (km)
    return specific_atten(R_mmhr,freq_ghz) * Ls * (1/(1+Lg/L0))

def gas_atten(freq_ghz, elev_deg, zenith=0.07):
    return zenith/math.sin(math.radians(elev_deg))

print("Slant-path rain attenuation vs elevation — Ka 20 GHz\n")
print(f"{'Elev':>7}{'R=5':>9}{'R=25':>9}{'R=50':>9}{'R=100':>9}{'Gas':>8}")
print("-" * 46)
for e in [90,60,45,30,20,15,10]:
    row = [rain_atten_slant(R,20.0,e) for R in [5,25,50,100]]
    gas = gas_atten(20.0,e)
    print(f"{e:>5}°"+"".join(f"{v:>9.2f}" for v in row)+f"{gas:>8.2f}")

print("\nBand comparison at 30° elevation, R=25 mm/hr:")
for f,name in [(14,'Ku'),(20,'Ka_dn'),(30,'Ka_up'),(47,'V')]:
    print(f"  {name} ({f}GHz): {rain_atten_slant(25,f,30):.1f} dB")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 4: Rain-Aware Link Budget

Add ITU-R P.618 rain attenuation to the LinkBudget engine, linking climate zone to link availability, with noise-rain coupling.

import math

K_DB = -228.6; T0 = 290.0

def fspl(d, f): return 92.4 + 20*math.log10(d) + 20*math.log10(f)
def nf_te(nf): return T0*(10**(nf/10)-1)
def loss_te(l, t=T0): return t*(10**(l/10)-1)
def dbl(x): return 10**(x/10)

# ITU-R P.837 rain climatic zones — R0.01 (mm/hr, exceeded 0.01% of an avg year)
RAIN_ZONES = {
    'A':8,'B':12,'C':15,'D':19,'E':22,'F':28,'G':30,'H':32,
    'J':35,'K':42,'L':60,'M':63,'N':95,'P':145,'Q':115
}

def rain_specific(R, f):
    P={14:(.0229,1.265),20:(.0751,1.099),30:(.187,1.021),47:(.546,.911)}
    k,a=min(P.items(),key=lambda x:abs(x[0]-f))[1]
    return k*R**a

def rain_atten_p618(zone, freq, elev, avail=99.9, h_rain=4.0, h_s=0.1):
    # Simplified P.618: build A0.01 from R0.01, then scale the
    # ATTENUATION — never the rain rate — to the exceedance p.
    # Scaling the rate first double-compresses through the sub-linear
    # gamma = k*R^a and badly under-predicts moderate-availability fades.
    # Valid p 0.01-5%, latitude >= 36 deg (beta correction omitted).
    R001=RAIN_ZONES.get(zone.upper(),28); p=max(100-avail, 0.01)
    e=math.radians(elev); Ls=(h_rain-h_s)/math.sin(e); Lg=Ls*math.cos(e)
    L0=35.0*math.exp(-0.015*R001); r=1/(1+Lg/L0)
    A001=rain_specific(R001,freq)*Ls*r
    if p==0.01: return A001
    return A001*(p/0.01)**(-(0.655+0.033*math.log(p)-0.045*math.log(A001)))

class LinkBudget:
    """Stage 4: Rain-aware with P.618 model and noise-rain coupling."""
    def __init__(self, name):
        self.name=name; self.losses=[]; self._chain=[]
        self._eirp=self._fspl=self._grx=self._tant=self._req=None
        self._zone=None; self._freq=None; self._elev=None

    def set_transmitter(self, p, g): self._eirp=10*math.log10(p)+g
    def set_path(self, d, f, elev=30):
        self._fspl=fspl(d,f); self._freq=f; self._elev=elev
    def add_loss(self, lbl, v): self.losses.append((lbl,v))
    def set_antenna(self, g, tant): self._grx=g; self._tant=tant
    def add_stage(self, lbl, te, g=None): self._chain.append((lbl,te,g))
    def set_required(self, r): self._req=r
    def set_rain_zone(self, z): self._zone=z

    def _tsys(self, rain_db=0):
        tant=self._tant+(273*(1-10**(-rain_db/10)) if rain_db else 0)
        t=tant; cg=1.0
        for _,te,g in self._chain:
            t+=te/cg
            if g: cg*=dbl(g)
        return t

    def compute(self, avail=None):
        rain_db=rain_atten_p618(self._zone,self._freq,self._elev,avail) \
            if (avail and self._zone) else 0.0
        t=self._tsys(rain_db)
        total=self._fspl+sum(v for _,v in self.losses)+rain_db
        cn0=self._eirp-total+self._grx-10*math.log10(t)-K_DB
        return cn0,cn0-self._req if self._req else None,t,rain_db

    def print_availability(self, avail_levels):
        print(f"\n  {self.name}  [Rain zone {self._zone}]")
        print(f"  {'Avail%':>10}{'Rain dB':>10}{'T_sys K':>9}{'C/N₀':>8}{'Margin':>9}")
        print("  "+"-"*48)
        for a in avail_levels:
            cn0,m,t,r=self.compute(a)
            print(f"  {a:>10.3f}%{r:>9.1f}{t:>9.0f}{cn0:>8.1f}{m:>+9.1f}dB"
                  f"  {'✓' if m>=0 else '✗'}")

lb = LinkBudget("LEO Ka forward — Europe, 30° elevation")
lb.set_transmitter(20, 34); lb.set_path(1000, 20.0, 30)
lb.add_loss("Gaseous absorption", 0.5); lb.add_loss("Pointing", 0.5)
lb.set_antenna(37.5, 30.0)
lb.add_stage("Feed 0.3dB",    loss_te(0.3),-0.3)
lb.add_stage("LNA NF=1 G=40", nf_te(1.0),  40.0)
lb.add_stage("Downconv NF=8", nf_te(8.0),  20.0)
lb.add_stage("IF NF=10",      nf_te(10.0), None)
lb.set_rain_zone('F'); lb.set_required(93.4)  # 16-APSK 3/4 @ 208.3 Msym/s
lb.print_availability([99.0, 99.5, 99.9, 99.95, 99.99])
