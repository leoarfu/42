```
Reframe Your Story for This Role

This is a consulting role, not an engineering role. The company values your technical depth, but the differentiator they're hiring for is the ability to translate that depth into client value — independently, at a senior level. Your pitch must lead with impact framing, not task framing.

❌ TASK FRAMING

  • "Led architecture for HBC+ connectivity platform"
  • "Coordinated between Airbus Commercial and Airbus DS"
  • "Wrote technical requirements for MSP onboarding"

✓ IMPACT FRAMING

  • "Defined end-to-end architecture for a multi-operator airborne connectivity program — from satellite MSP selection through to terminal integration — across two Airbus divisions"
  • "Sole technical interface for cross-organizational alignment on a €Xm program"
  • "Translated operator SLA requirements into ground segment specs for vendor procurement"
The "Consultant Mindset" Test — What They Actually Want

The posting says "exceptional communication skills, working autonomously, leading high-stakes client meetings." In a consulting context, this means:

  • You walk into a client meeting with no safety net — no manager behind you to correct course. Can you hold your own with a satellite operator CTO?
  • You manage the relationship, not just the deliverable — sensing when the client is nervous, adjusting the pitch, knowing when to push back.
  • You produce structured, polished outputs — reports, presentations, specs that look like they came from McKinsey, not an internal team memo.
Prepare a specific story: a time you led a high-stakes external or cross-organizational meeting solo, managed a difficult stakeholder dynamic, or produced a client-facing deliverable that influenced a major decision.
```

Anticipated Questions & Suggested Answers

```
Q01 Walk me through your background in ground segment architecture.
STRUCTURE: Anchor in iDirect → Airbus DS → synthesize the arc

Open with breadth: "My background spans the full ground segment stack — from RF link budgets and DVB-S2 waveforms at iDirect, where I was doing live fault isolation on customer SATCOM networks, through to system-level architecture at Airbus DS, where I currently lead the ground segment design for the HBC+ airborne connectivity platform."

Then go to depth: "On HBC+, my scope runs from satellite MSP onboarding — defining the interface requirements to the space segment — through to the gateway and teleport architecture, and down to the airborne terminal integration. It's a cross-divisional program involving Airbus Commercial and Airbus DS, so I'm the architectural bridge between two organizations with different operational cultures."

Close with consulting relevance: "What I find is that the most critical skill in architecture work at this scale isn't the technical decisions themselves — it's the ability to translate ambiguous operator requirements into structured specs that procurement and engineering teams can actually execute on. That's exactly where I've been operating."
Q02 What does "end-to-end" mean to you in a ground segment context?
TRAP: Don't just list components. Show systems thinking.

"End-to-end for me means the ability to reason coherently from the customer use case all the way to the physical infrastructure — and to trace the design decisions back to requirements at each layer. Concretely: starting from operator SLAs and service definitions, through network and link architecture, into the TT&C and mission control subsystems, the NOC infrastructure, gateway/teleport selection, and finally the user terminal interface. The end-to-end view matters because failures in ground segment programs almost always originate at the interface between these layers — a requirement that was clear at the service level becomes ambiguous at the procurement spec level, and then costs you six months in integration."
Q03 Tell me about a time you had to manage a complex stakeholder situation independently.
FORMAT: Situation → Your role → What was hard → How you navigated it → Outcome

Use HBC+ cross-divisional dynamic: "On HBC+, I operate as the technical interface between Airbus Commercial Aircraft and Airbus Defence & Space — two organizations with different cultures, priorities, and contract structures. On one occasion, [describe specific conflict or alignment challenge]. My approach was [describe the action — listening first, identifying the actual underlying interest vs stated position, proposing a framing that let both sides move forward]. The outcome was [concrete result — decision reached, program unblocked, spec agreed]. What I learned is that in these situations the technical answer is often the easier part; the harder work is creating a shared language between stakeholders who aren't used to talking to each other."
Q04 You've been in large industry roles. Why consulting, and why now?
KEY: Don't sound like you're escaping Airbus. Sound like you're drawn to the consulting model.

"I've spent my career building and operating within programs — which gives me a strong foundation in what actually works when you're executing at scale. What consulting offers that internal roles don't is range: the ability to apply that experience across multiple programs, operators, and system types in a compressed timeframe, and to shape decisions before they become constraints rather than after. I find that the most valuable engineering work I do at Airbus is often the early architecture framing — when the requirements are still fluid and the right question matters more than the right answer. A consulting model puts me at that stage systematically, rather than once every few years."
Q05 How do you approach translating customer requirements into a technical specification?
SHOW PROCESS: This is a core consulting deliverable — structured thinking matters

"The first step is separating what the customer asks for from what they actually need — those are often different. I spend time understanding the operational context: what failure modes they're most afraid of, what their existing infrastructure constraints are, and what their internal capability to operate the end system looks like. From there I work top-down: service requirements → system-level functional requirements → subsystem specs → interface requirements. I flag assumptions explicitly and validate them before they become embedded in the spec. In my experience, the most expensive errors in procurement happen when an assumption made at the requirements stage is never challenged — so I try to make the dependency chain visible to the customer, not just internally."
Q06 Where do you see yourself in 3–5 years?
ALIGN WITH THEIR MODEL: Show you want to grow within consulting, not through it

"Within a consulting environment, I'd want to have moved from being the technical lead on individual engagements to shaping the portfolio — being the person who defines how we approach a new class of program, whether that's LEO constellation ground segments, government dual-use architectures, or whatever the next inflection point is. I'm also interested in building client relationships over time — the kind where you're called at the problem definition stage, not just the RFP stage. That's the consulting value I find most compelling: being close enough to the client's thinking to influence the question, not just answer it."
```

Questions to Ask Jack During the Call

```
Tier 1 — Must Ask (Understanding the Role)
  • What's the engagement model? — "Are engineers typically embedded with a single client for the duration, or is it more project-to-project with multiple clients running in parallel?" This affects work style significantly.
  • Phase weighting — "What's the typical balance between early-phase architecture definition versus monitoring and advisory during construction and IOT? I want to understand where the role spends most of its energy."
  • Program pipeline — "What types of constellation programs are in the pipeline right now — government, commercial, GEO/LEO, or mixed? And are there specific sectors like defence or broadband that are most active?"
  • Team structure — "Is this a new hire or a backfill, and what does the immediate team look like? Who would I be working alongside day-to-day?"
Tier 2 — Strategic (Calibrating the Opportunity)
  • Salary positioning — "The band is fairly wide — €70k to €110k. Where does a profile like mine typically land within that range, and what factors move someone toward the top?" Ask this early, not at the end.
  • Profit share mechanics — "The posting mentions significant bonus/profit share — can you give me a sense of what that looks like in practice? Is it tied to individual billings, team performance, or firm-wide P&L?"
  • Travel expectations — "The posting mentions international travel for deployment monitoring. What's a realistic average — weeks per year, or something more?"
  • Security clearance pathway — "The EU27 requirement is clear, but is there an existing clearance level expected, or is it more about eligibility for clearance processes down the line?"
Tier 3 — Closing (If You're Interested)
  • "What does the process look like from here — is there a formal interview with the company, and what are they typically evaluating at that stage?"
  • "Is there a preferred timeline on their side for filling this role?"
  • "Is there anything in my profile that you'd expect them to want to probe further, or gaps I should be prepared to address?"
```
