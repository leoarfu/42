Bent-Pipe vs Mesh:
The Architecture Decision

MODULE 7
NETWORKING, ISL & ROUTING
FOUNDATION

Module 6 delivered a solved terminal: a node that earns its bits from whatever satellite the scheduler points it at. This module builds the network those bits travel through — and it opens with the decision that determines whether a constellation is a network at all. A bent-pipe constellation is a collection of independent links: every satellite is a mirror that relays a user's signal straight back down to a ground station, and the network intelligence lives entirely on the ground. A mesh constellation carries traffic between satellites over inter-satellite links (ISLs), routing a packet across the sky before it ever touches the ground — the constellation becomes a router in orbit. The choice is not a detail; it decides the constellation's coverage, its latency, its ground-segment cost, and whether it can serve an ocean.

This lesson establishes the two architectures, derives the geometry that separates them, and quantifies the three consequences that make the decision: coverage (a bent-pipe satellite can only serve a user who shares its footprint with a gateway — about 2,600 km of reach, which leaves most of the ocean dark), latency (a mesh routes at the speed of light in vacuum and beats terrestrial fibre beyond roughly 1,500–2,000 km, because glass is 47% slower than vacuum), and cost (ISLs are expensive optical hardware on every satellite; ground stations are expensive real estate on every continent — the two architectures move the cost to opposite ends of the system). The module's spine, a Python ConstellationRouter, begins here as the topology graph with a switch between the two architectures, and it will grow across the module into a full routing-and-latency engine built on the Module 2 orbital geometry.

1. Two architectures, one picture

A bent-pipe (or "transparent") satellite is an amplify-and-forward relay: it receives the user's uplink, shifts frequency, amplifies, and retransmits on the downlink toward a gateway — the m4-7 forward and return links, back to back, with the satellite adding power but no intelligence. The consequence is absolute: the user and the gateway must be simultaneously visible to the same satellite, because the satellite cannot store the packet or pass it to a neighbour. Every bent-pipe connection is a double hop — up to the satellite, down to a gateway — and the network's routing, switching, and intelligence all live in the terrestrial ground segment.

A mesh (or "regenerative + ISL") satellite demodulates the packet, reads its destination, and can forward it to a neighbouring satellite over an inter-satellite link. Traffic crosses the constellation satellite-to-satellite and descends only where it needs to — near its destination, or at the one gateway that has terrestrial backhaul to the wider internet. The constellation is now a mesh network in motion, and the routing problem (the rest of this module) becomes real: a packet has choices, the topology changes every few seconds, and something must decide the path.

The two architectures are not a quality ranking. A bent-pipe constellation is simpler, cheaper per satellite, and entirely adequate where gateways are plentiful and traffic is local. A mesh constellation is more capable and far more expensive, and it earns that expense exactly where the bent-pipe fails: over oceans, over poles, and on long-haul routes where its latency beats fibre.

2. The bent-pipe coverage constraint

The geometry that bounds a bent-pipe network: a user is served only if some satellite sees both the user (above the user's elevation mask, say 25°) and a gateway (above the gateway's feeder mask, which can be lower — say 10° — since gateways are engineered sites). The satellite's footprint radius at a 25° user mask is 941 km; at a 10° gateway mask, 1,664 km. The maximum user-to-gateway ground separation for a single-satellite relay is the sum — a user on one edge of the footprint, a gateway on the other:

Max bent-pipe reach ≈ 941 + 1,664 ≈ 2,605 km from the nearest gateway.

Beyond that distance from any gateway, a bent-pipe constellation simply has no service to offer — there is no satellite that can see the user and a gateway at once. And here the Earth's geography becomes a systems constraint: 71% of the surface is ocean, gateways need land and terrestrial backhaul, and mid-ocean points sit thousands of kilometres from the nearest coast. A bent-pipe constellation can blanket a continent from a handful of inland gateways, but it cannot serve a ship in the mid-Pacific, an aircraft on a polar route, or any user beyond ~2,600 km of a coastline. This single number is why the constellations that promised global — genuinely global — broadband committed to ISLs.

3. The mesh answer to coverage

A mesh dissolves the constraint. The ocean user's packet goes up to the satellite overhead, hops satellite-to-satellite across the mesh until it reaches a satellite over a continent with a gateway, and descends there to terrestrial backhaul. The user needs no local gateway at all — only a satellite overhead and a connected mesh between it and civilisation. Coverage decouples from ground-station geography entirely: the constellation serves wherever it flies, which for an inclined Walker constellation (Module 2) is essentially everywhere between its latitude extremes, ocean and pole included. The price is an ISL terminal on every satellite (m7-2's optical hardware) and a routing system that can find the path (m7-4, m7-5). The mesh buys global coverage with orbital hardware instead of terrestrial real estate — and it is the only architecture that can.

4. The latency consequence: light in glass versus vacuum

The mesh's second advantage is latency, and it rests on a physical fact terrestrial networks cannot escape: light travels 47% slower in optical fibre than in vacuum (the fibre's refractive index is ~1.468, so signals move at c/1.468 ≈ 204,000 km/s instead of 299,792). A LEO mesh, routing through the near-vacuum of space at almost exactly c, has a structural speed advantage over any fibre route — once the path is long enough to amortise the fixed cost of climbing to orbit and back.

The crossover, verified against real city-pairs (fibre modelled with a realistic ~40% routing detour over great-circle; LEO as up-slant + ISL chord at altitude + down-slant):

Route Distance Fibre (one-way) LEO mesh LEO advantage

Two readings. First, the crossover sits near 1,500–2,000 km: below it, the ~7.5 ms round trip to climb to orbit and back (m7-6's access latency) dominates and fibre wins; above it, the vacuum-speed advantage compounds with distance and the mesh pulls away — 34 ms on a transpacific route, which is a decisive edge in latency-sensitive markets (finance, gaming, interactive applications). Second, this advantage belongs only to the mesh: a bent-pipe constellation cannot even attempt these routes, because no single satellite sees both endpoints. The latency argument and the coverage argument are the same argument — both are consequences of routing in space rather than relaying to the ground.

5. The cost inversion

The architectures move cost to opposite ends of the system. A bent-pipe constellation puts simple, cheap payloads in orbit and pays on the ground: a network of gateways, each an expensive installation with antennas, backhaul, and real estate, tiled densely enough that every served user is within ~2,600 km of one. Coverage scales with ground-station count, and the ground segment becomes the dominant recurring cost and the coverage bottleneck (Module 8's territory). A mesh constellation puts expensive optical ISL terminals on every satellite — added mass, power, cost, and pointing complexity on hundreds or thousands of spacecraft — and needs only a handful of gateways, placed for backhaul convenience rather than coverage. Coverage scales with the constellation itself.

The break-even is a systems-economics question with no universal answer: it turns on the constellation size, the target coverage (regional versus global), the gateway real-estate cost in the target markets, and the ISL hardware cost per satellite. The rule of thumb the geometry supports: regional or continental service with cheap gateway sites favours bent-pipe; global service — especially oceanic, polar, or latency-competitive — requires mesh. The market has split exactly along this line, with some systems ground-relayed and others ISL-meshed, and some deploying ISLs incrementally as the coverage and latency premium justifies the hardware.

6. The hybrid and the transition

The architectures are endpoints of a spectrum, not a binary. A constellation can fly ISLs on some satellites and not others, use ISLs only over oceans while relaying to ground over land, or deploy a bent-pipe network first and retrofit ISLs as the fleet refreshes (the m6-1 graceful-upgrade logic at constellation scale). A regenerative payload without ISLs is already halfway to a mesh — it demodulates and could route, it simply has no neighbour link yet. The decision this lesson frames is therefore continuous: how much of the network's intelligence and connectivity lives in orbit versus on the ground, evaluated per orbit, per coverage region, and per generation of hardware. The spine's topology graph will model both endpoints and the hybrids between them with one switch.

7. What the mesh demands: the rest of the module

Committing to a mesh creates every problem the rest of Module 7 solves. The ISLs must be built — optical links across thousands of kilometres between platforms closing at kilometres per second, with their own link budget and a pointing problem an order of magnitude harder than m6-5's (m7-2). The topology must be understood — which satellites link to which, how the pattern breaks near the poles, how fast it churns (m7-3). The routing must work on a graph that changes every few seconds, defeating the terrestrial protocols built for stable topologies (m7-4). The control plane must scale to a network moving at 7.6 km/s, which the deterministic ephemeris makes tractable through SDN and precomputed paths (m7-5). And the end-to-end latency must be assembled and handed to a transport layer (TCP, QUIC) that was never designed for a path whose RTT changes mid-connection (m7-6). The synthesis (m7-7) assembles all of it into the routing-and-latency model. This lesson's job was to establish why that machinery exists: because a constellation chose to be a network.

9. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

The spine begins as a Topology object built on the Module 2 orbital geometry: satellite positions over time, a visibility test for user-to-satellite and satellite-to-satellite links, and a switch between bent-pipe mode (satellites connect only to gateways) and mesh mode (satellites connect to neighbours). In bent-pipe mode it will answer coverage questions — who can be served, from which gateway; in mesh mode it becomes the graph the router traverses. The architecture decision is now a parameter, and its three consequences — coverage reach, latency crossover, cost location — are quantified and ready to trade.

What the topology graph cannot yet do is carry a packet: it knows which links can exist, not what those links cost to build or how they behave. The ISL itself — its physics, its budget, its brutal pointing problem — is m7-2, and the pattern in which the links assemble into a mesh, and how that pattern churns, is m7-3. Only then can the router of m7-4 traverse a graph that is real.

Exercise 1 — The bent-pipe coverage constraint

Compute the reach, and map what a bent-pipe cannot serve.

import math
Re, h = 6371.0, 550.0

def footprint_radius(emin_deg, alt=h):
    e = math.radians(emin_deg)
    psi = math.acos(Re/(Re+alt)*math.cos(e)) - e
    return Re*psi   # ground arc, km

r_user = footprint_radius(25)
r_gw   = footprint_radius(10)
reach  = r_user + r_gw
print(f"user footprint (25° mask):    {r_user:.0f} km")
print(f"gateway footprint (10° mask): {r_gw:.0f} km")
print(f"bent-pipe max user–gateway reach: {reach:.0f} km")

Exercise 2 — The latency crossover

Find where the mesh beats fibre.

import math
Re, h, c = 6371.0, 550.0, 299792.458
c_fiber = c/1.468

def fiber_oneway_ms(ground_km, detour=1.40):
    return ground_km*detour/c_fiber*1000

def leo_mesh_oneway_ms(ground_km, elev_deg=40, alt=h):
    e = math.radians(elev_deg)
    slant = Re*(math.sqrt(((Re+alt)/Re)**2 - math.cos(e)**2) - math.sin(e))
    isl_chord = (Re+alt)*(ground_km/Re)      # arc at altitude
    return (2*slant + isl_chord)/c*1000

print(f"{'km':>6}{'fiber ms':>10}{'LEO ms':>9}  winner")
for d in [500, 1000, 1500, 2000, 3000, 5570, 10850]:
    f, l = fiber_oneway_ms(d), leo_mesh_oneway_ms(d)
    print(f"{d:>6}{f:>10.1f}{l:>9.1f}  {'LEO' if l<f else 'fiber'}")

Exercise 3 — Spine seed: the Topology graph

The router begins: a switchable bent-pipe / mesh topology on the m2 geometry.

import math
Re, h = 6371.0, 550.0

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M7 SPINE PROJECT — CONSTELLATION ROUTER
# Stage 1 (here): Topology — a visibility graph with a
#                 bent-pipe / mesh switch, on the m2 geometry.
# Growth path:
#   m7-2  ISL link budget & range/rate (what a link COSTS)
#   m7-3  the +grid mesh pattern & its churn (which links EXIST)
#   m7-4  routing under churn (the router core)
#   m7-5  SDN / precomputed path tables
#   m7-6  end-to-end latency + transport
#   m7-7  full router: terminal -> mesh -> ground
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

def footprint_radius(emin_deg, alt=h):
    e = math.radians(emin_deg)
    return Re*(math.acos(Re/(Re+alt)*math.cos(e)) - e)

class Topology:
    """Switchable bent-pipe / mesh graph. Positions are placeholders
    here; m7-3 wires in the real m2 propagator output."""
    def __init__(self, mode="mesh", user_mask=25, gw_mask=10):
        assert mode in ("bent_pipe", "mesh")
        self.mode = mode
        self.r_user = footprint_radius(user_mask)
        self.r_gw   = footprint_radius(gw_mask)
        self.reach  = self.r_user + self.r_gw

    def can_serve(self, user_to_gateway_km, has_local_mesh=True):
        """Can a user this far from the nearest gateway be served?"""
        if self.mode == "bent_pipe":
            return user_to_gateway_km <= self.reach
        # mesh: any user with a satellite overhead reaches a distant
        # gateway via ISL hops — gateway distance is irrelevant.
        return has_local_mesh

    def describe(self):
        print(f"  topology mode: {self.mode}")
        print(f"  bent-pipe reach: {self.reach:.0f} km from a gateway")
        for label, dist in [("suburban (200 km)", 200),
                            ("remote land (2000 km)", 2000),
                            ("mid-ocean (4000 km)", 4000)]:
            ok = self.can_serve(dist)
            print(f"    {label:<22} {'SERVED' if ok else 'NO SERVICE'}")

print("── BENT-PIPE ──")
Topology("bent_pipe").describe()
print("\n── MESH ──")
Topology("mesh").describe()

PROMPT 01

Section 5 called the bent-pipe-versus-mesh choice a cost inversion with no universal break-even. Take a specific operator's seat: a constellation targeting continental broadband over a single large landmass with cheap inland gateway sites. Argue the case against ISLs for this operator using the module's numbers — coverage adequacy, latency indifference, per-satellite cost saved — then identify the single change in their business (a new market, a new competitor, a latency-sensitive customer) that would flip the decision. What does this say about ISLs as an option to be held open rather than a commitment made once?

PROMPT 02

Section 4's latency crossover means the mesh's advantage is real only above ~1,500–2,000 km — yet most internet traffic is regional (well below the crossover), where fibre wins. Reconcile this with the enormous investment in LEO ISLs: if the mesh loses on the majority of traffic by distance, what exactly is the ISL hardware buying, and for whom? Quantify the argument by naming the specific traffic classes (by distance, by geography, by latency sensitivity) where the mesh wins, and judge whether they justify an optical terminal on every satellite or only some.

PROMPT 03

Section 6 framed the architecture as a continuous spectrum and noted constellations can deploy bent-pipe first and retrofit ISLs. But a satellite's design life is years, and ISLs are not a software update — they are optical hardware that must be flown. Construct the fleet-refresh argument: if ISLs are retrofitted only on new satellites, how long until a constellation's mesh is dense enough to route reliably, and what does the routing layer (m7-4) do with a partial mesh where only some satellites have neighbour links? Decide whether incremental ISL deployment is a smooth transition or a discontinuous one requiring a threshold fraction before the mesh is useful at all.

PROMPT 04

This lesson built its coverage argument on a 25°/10° elevation-mask pair and its latency argument on a 40° average-elevation model — both reasonable, both choices. Interrogate the load-bearing assumptions: which of this lesson's headline numbers (the 2,605 km reach, the 1,500–2,000 km crossover, the 34 ms transpacific advantage) is most sensitive to its mask/elevation assumptions, and which is robust? Specify the one number you would most want to re-derive from the real m2 constellation geometry before betting an architecture decision on it, and state what could move it.
