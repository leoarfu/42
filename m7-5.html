SDN & Segment
Routing in Space

MODULE 7
NETWORKING, ISL & ROUTING
CORE

m7-4 established that a constellation should route proactively — precompute a path table for each known future topology and switch on schedule. This lesson builds the machinery that makes that possible at constellation scale: a control plane that computes the tables and gets them onto 1,584 satellites moving at 7.6 km/s, before each is needed. The answer the industry converged on is software-defined networking (SDN) — separate the decision-making (the control plane, which computes paths) from the packet-forwarding (the data plane, which executes them) — and it fits a constellation almost too well, because the one thing SDN needs to be practical is exactly the thing a constellation has for free: a predictable network. On the terrestrial internet, centralised control fights an unpredictable topology; in space, the deterministic ephemeris (m7-3) hands the controller the entire future, and the control problem collapses from "react to what the network is doing" to "compute what the network will do and tell it ahead of time."

The lesson's verified anchors show why this is tractable rather than daunting: a full forwarding table for the whole constellation is a destination-to-next-hop map of ~2.2 KB per satellite (3.4 MB across all 1,584); refreshing them on a 15-second snapshot costs well under 100 kbps per satellite when only the changed entries are pushed; and computing all snapshots for an entire orbit is milliseconds of server time. The control plane is small — not because the network is simple, but because determinism lets it be precomputed on the ground and delivered ahead of need. The lesson also introduces segment routing, which moves the path decision into the packet header itself, letting the source specify the route as a stack of waypoints — a natural fit for a network whose paths are computed centrally and change on a schedule. The spine gains the precomputed path-table sequence and the source-routing option.

1. The SDN idea, and why space wants it

Software-defined networking splits a router's two jobs. The data plane is the fast path: a packet arrives, the router looks up its destination in a forwarding table, and sends it out the right port — simple, fast, done in hardware. The control plane is the slow path: deciding what the forwarding tables should say — running the routing algorithms, responding to topology, setting policy. In a traditional router the two live in the same box, coupled. SDN pulls the control plane out into a separate controller (often centralised) that computes the forwarding tables and installs them into the switches, which become pure data-plane devices executing decisions made elsewhere.

SDN's promise on the terrestrial internet is central visibility and control; its perennial difficulty is that the controller must track a large, changing, partially unpredictable network, and the control loop (controller sees change → recomputes → installs new tables) has latency that fights fast events. A constellation removes the difficulty entirely. The topology is not unpredictable — it is the deterministic +grid of m7-3, computable arbitrarily far ahead. The controller does not need to see a change and react; it computes the change from orbital mechanics before it happens and installs the tables in advance. The control loop is no longer in the critical path, because there is nothing to react to. SDN's one weakness — control-loop latency against unpredictable change — is exactly the weakness a deterministic network deletes. Space is the environment SDN was waiting for.

2. Where the controller lives

A centralised controller for a network moving at 7.6 km/s raises an obvious question: where is it, and how does it reach the satellites in time? Two structures, often combined:

Ground-based control. The controller runs on the ground — in a data centre with the compute to precompute the whole schedule and the orbit-determination feeds (Module 2) that anchor the ephemeris. It pushes tables up through gateways and across the ISL mesh to every satellite. Because the tables are precomputed and delivered ahead of their activation time, the ground-to-satellite control latency (an access RTT of milliseconds, plus mesh delivery) is irrelevant — it is slack absorbed by the lead time, not delay in a reaction loop. The controller computes at hour-scale lead and delivers at minute-scale lead; the second-scale control latency vanishes into the margin.

Distributed / onboard execution. Each satellite holds its precomputed table sequence and a clock, and switches tables autonomously at each snapshot boundary — no per-switch instruction from the ground, just a schedule loaded ahead of time. The ground controller's job is to compute and distribute the schedule; the satellites execute it independently, which means a temporary loss of the control link does not stop routing (the satellite runs its loaded schedule) and only threatens future updates. This decoupling — compute centrally, execute locally, on a shared clock — is the architecture's resilience, and it is only possible because the schedule is deterministic and can be loaded in advance.

The synthesis: a ground controller that precomputes and distributes, satellites that execute a loaded schedule autonomously, and a control loop whose latency is hidden entirely behind the precompute lead time. The 7.6 km/s network is controllable precisely because nobody has to keep up with it in real time.

3. The control plane is small

The intuition that controlling 1,584 satellites must be a heavy data problem is wrong, and the numbers show why. A satellite's forwarding table maps each of the ~1,584 possible destinations to a next-hop neighbour. A satellite ID fits in 11 bits, so a full table is 1,584 × 11 bits ≈ 2.2 KB — and the entire constellation's tables total ~3.4 MB, a rounding error against the terabit-per-second data plane the ISLs carry (m7-2). Refreshing them costs even less: between consecutive snapshots only a few percent of entries change (the topology reconfigures at the edges, not wholesale), so a satellite's own table delta is a few hundred bits per snapshot, and even the aggregate update stream relayed across the mesh to refresh the whole constellation stays in the low kbps per link — a whisper of control traffic riding a mesh built for tens of gigabits per link.

Computing the tables is equally cheap. All-pairs shortest paths on the ~1,584-node grid is 1,584 Dijkstra runs, milliseconds on a server; a full orbit at a 15-second snapshot is ~380 snapshots, computable in seconds and precomputable hours ahead. The control plane is small in every dimension — table size, update bandwidth, compute — and it is small because of determinism: a reactive controller would need to carry state about an evolving network and push updates at the churn rate, but a proactive one computes a compact schedule once and streams tiny deltas. The lesson's quiet result: the hard problem of m7-4 (routing a churning network) produces an easy control plane, because the churn's predictability turns a real-time reaction problem into a batch precomputation problem.

4. Segment routing: the path in the packet

Traditional forwarding is hop-by-hop: each router independently looks up the destination and picks the next hop, and the path emerges from many independent decisions. This works, but it means every satellite must hold a consistent, current table, and a packet's path is only as good as the agreement between all the tables along it. Segment routing offers an alternative that suits a centrally computed network: the source encodes the path into the packet header as an ordered stack of segment identifiers (SIDs) — waypoints the packet must traverse — and each router simply forwards to the next segment in the stack, popping it off as it goes. The path is carried by the packet, not reconstructed hop by hop.

For a constellation this is a natural fit. The controller has already computed the whole path (§3); segment routing lets it express that path directly, pushing a SID stack at the ingress satellite rather than relying on every satellite along the route to independently agree on the next hop. The stack is tiny — a transcontinental path of 8 hops is 8 SIDs at ~2 bytes each, a 16-byte header; even a 15-hop path is 30 bytes — negligible overhead for the benefit of explicit, source-specified, centrally-controlled paths. It also makes traffic engineering trivial: to route a flow around a congested region or along a latency-optimal path, the controller simply pushes a different SID stack at the ingress, without touching any intermediate satellite's table. Segment routing turns the controller's global path knowledge into per-packet instructions, and the deterministic topology makes those instructions valid for the snapshot they were computed for.

5. The control plane meets the data plane

The two planes operate on different clocks, and the interface between them is the snapshot. The control plane, on the ground, computes the schedule at hour-scale lead and streams tables and SID policies to the satellites at minute-scale lead. The data plane, on the satellites, forwards packets at line rate using the currently active table, and switches to the next snapshot's table at the scheduled boundary — synchronously across the constellation, so that no packet is forwarded by a satellite whose table disagrees with its neighbours' about the current topology. Clock synchronisation across the constellation (the same timing infrastructure that serves m5-5's timing advance) is what makes the synchronous switch possible: every satellite changes tables at the same instant, and the mesh transitions cleanly from one snapshot's routing to the next.

This is the make-before-break pattern once more, at the whole-network scale: the next snapshot's tables are loaded and validated before the current snapshot expires, and the switch is instantaneous and coordinated. The reactive floor of m7-4 sits underneath — if a link fails unexpectedly between snapshots, the affected satellites reroute locally on the current table without waiting for the ground — but the common case is a smooth, scheduled, constellation-wide table switch that the users never perceive. The network moves at 7.6 km/s and reconfigures every fifteen seconds, and to a packet crossing it, nothing is happening at all.

6. What SDN does not fix

The same honesty m7-4 applied to snapshot routing applies here. SDN and precomputation handle the deterministic part beautifully and leave the non-deterministic part exactly where it was. Traffic is still unpredictable, so the controller's precomputed paths are geometry-optimal, not traffic-optimal, and congestion still requires the fast local reaction of m7-4 §5 — SDN can help (the controller has global traffic visibility and can push new SID stacks to steer flows) but its control loop, even shortened by precomputation, is slower than a traffic microburst. Failures are still unscheduled, so the reactive floor remains essential. And the controller itself is a dependency: a centralised control plane is a centralised point of failure, mitigated by the onboard schedule execution (§2) that lets satellites run without the controller for a while, but not eliminated. SDN makes the deterministic problem small; it does not make the non-deterministic problems disappear. The constellation's routing system is proactive-where-it-can-be and reactive-where-it-must-be, and SDN is the machinery of the proactive half.

8. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

Stage 5 of the spine is the control plane: it takes m7-4's per-snapshot path tables and produces the deliverables a real constellation needs — compact per-satellite forwarding tables, delta updates between snapshots, and (optionally) segment-routing SID stacks for source-specified paths — plus the schedule that says which table is active when. It models the control-plane budget (table size, update bandwidth, precompute lead) and confirms it is small. The router of m7-4 knew what the paths are; Stage 5 knows how to deliver and activate them across a moving constellation, on a shared clock, ahead of need.

What remains is the packet's experience of all this. The routing and control machinery exists to move a packet from a terminal, across the mesh, to a destination — and the packet's end-to-end latency (access + propagation across the scheduled path + queuing + ground) is what the transport layer sees, and it is where TCP and QUIC, built for stable terrestrial paths, meet a network whose RTT changes on the snapshot schedule. Assembling that end-to-end latency and confronting the transport layer with it is m7-6, the last piece before the synthesis.

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 5: the control plane

Precomputed tables, deltas, and the schedule.

import heapq, math

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M7 SPINE PROJECT — CONSTELLATION ROUTER
# Stage 5: the control plane.
#   Stage 4 (m7-4) computed per-snapshot path tables.
#   Stage 5: turn them into per-satellite forwarding tables,
#            compute deltas between snapshots, size the control
#            budget, and emit segment-routing SID stacks.
#   Next: m7-6 turns a path into end-to-end latency for transport.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

def dijkstra_all_next_hops(graph, src):
    """Return {dest: next_hop_from_src} — src's forwarding table."""
    dist = {src: 0.0}; first_hop = {src: None}
    pq = [(0.0, src, None)]
    while pq:
        d, u, fh = heapq.heappop(pq)
        if d > dist.get(u, math.inf): continue
        for v, w in graph.get(u, []):
            nd = d + w
            if nd < dist.get(v, math.inf):
                dist[v] = nd
                first_hop[v] = fh if fh is not None else v
                heapq.heappush(pq, (nd, v, first_hop[v]))
    return {dst: fh for dst, fh in first_hop.items() if fh is not None}

class ControlPlane:
    def __init__(self, id_bits=11):
        self.id_bits = id_bits

    def forwarding_tables(self, graph):
        """Per-node table: {node: {dest: next_hop}}."""
        return {n: dijkstra_all_next_hops(graph, n) for n in graph}

    def table_bytes(self, table):
        return len(table)*self.id_bits/8

    def delta(self, old_tables, new_tables):
        """Changed (node, dest) entries between two snapshots."""
        changes = 0
        for n in new_tables:
            o, w = old_tables.get(n, {}), new_tables[n]
            for dst, nh in w.items():
                if o.get(dst) != nh: changes += 1
        return changes

    def sid_stack(self, path):
        """Segment-routing header for an explicit path."""
        return path[1:]     # list of SIDs (waypoints after the source)

def grid(P=4, S=4, intra=1970, inter=600, C=299792.458, proc=0.5):
    g = {}
    for p in range(P):
        for s in range(S):
            u=(p,s)
            g[u]=[((p,(s+1)%S), intra/C*1000+proc),
                  ((p,(s-1)%S), intra/C*1000+proc),
                  (((p+1)%P,s), inter/C*1000+proc),
                  (((p-1)%P,s), inter/C*1000+proc)]
    return g

cp = ControlPlane()
# 8x8 grid: large enough that the inter-plane range change reroutes some
# destinations (a 4x4 is too symmetric to show a delta).
g0, g1 = grid(P=8, S=8, inter=600), grid(P=8, S=8, inter=2500)
t0, t1 = cp.forwarding_tables(g0), cp.forwarding_tables(g1)

sample = next(iter(t0))
print(f"table for {sample}: {len(t0[sample])} dests, "
      f"{cp.table_bytes(t0[sample]):.0f} bytes")
print(f"total tables: {sum(cp.table_bytes(t) for t in t0.values())/1e3:.1f} KB")
print(f"delta snapshot0->1: {cp.delta(t0, t1)} changed entries")

# segment-routing stack for an explicit path
import heapq
def sp(graph, s, d):
    pq=[(0,s,[s])]; seen=set()
    while pq:
        c,u,p=heapq.heappop(pq)
        if u==d: return p
        if u in seen: continue
        seen.add(u)
        for v,w in graph[u]:
            if v not in seen: heapq.heappush(pq,(c+w,v,p+[v]))
path = sp(g0, (0,0), (2,3))
print(f"SID stack for {(0,0)}->{(2,3)}: {cp.sid_stack(path)}")

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 01

Section 2 made onboard scheduled execution the architecture's resilience — satellites run a loaded schedule if the controller link is lost. But "a loaded schedule" has a horizon: how far ahead can a satellite's memory and the ephemeris accuracy support precomputed tables? Work the trade: a longer loaded horizon survives longer controller outages but risks the ephemeris drifting from reality (maneuvers, drag) so the old tables route on a stale topology. Determine the right loaded horizon as a function of ephemeris-prediction accuracy and controller-availability, and decide whether a satellite that has run out of valid schedule should keep routing on stale tables or fall back to a reactive protocol — and what that fallback costs.

PROMPT 02

Section 4 showed segment routing lets the controller re-steer a flow by changing one ingress SID stack, and section 6 admitted the controller's loop is still slower than a traffic microburst. These are in tension: SDN's traffic-engineering power (global re-steering) is real but slow, while the fast congestion response is local and myopic. Design the division of labour: which congestion events should the slow global controller handle (with SID re-steering) and which should the fast local reaction handle, and what information must pass between them so they do not fight — the local reaction undoing the controller's engineering or vice versa? Identify the failure mode where the two layers oscillate.

PROMPT 03

Section 6 named the centralised controller as a point of failure, mitigated by onboard execution but not eliminated. Consider the adversarial and the accidental cases together: a controller compromised (pushing malicious SID stacks) versus a controller merely offline. The onboard schedule protects against offline but not against a compromised controller pushing bad-but-valid tables. Propose what a satellite should verify about a control update before applying it — and whether a constellation should have a distributed consensus among satellites as a check on the central controller, trading SDN's simplicity for resilience. Where is the right point on that spectrum for a commercial constellation versus a sovereign one?

PROMPT 04

This lesson's control-plane-is-small result (3.4 MB, sub-100 kbps updates) rests on the forwarding table being destination-to-next-hop — a hop-by-hop structure — while section 4 advocated segment routing, where the path lives in the packet. These are two different data-plane models with different control-plane costs. Interrogate the inconsistency: does a segment-routed constellation need per-satellite forwarding tables at all, or does the ingress SID stack replace them — and if so, what is the real control-plane cost of segment routing (computing and distributing which SID stacks to which ingress points for which flows)? Determine whether segment routing makes the control plane smaller or merely moves its cost from the core to the edge, and which the constellation should prefer.
