1. The Complete Link Equation With All Impairments

Assembling every term from the preceding six lessons:

C/(N₀+I₀) [dBHz] = EIRP [dBW] − FSPL(d, f) [dB] − A_gas(f, ε) [dB] − A_rain(f, ε, zone, p%) [dB] − A_scint [dB] − A_point [dB] − A_misc [dB] + G_R [dBi] − 10·log₁₀(T_sys_Friis(chain, T_ant_rain)) [dBK] + 228.6 [dBW/K/Hz] − interference correction

where T_sys_Friis is computed via the Friis chain with rain-coupling adjustment to T_ant; A_rain and the rain-adjusted T_ant are correlated, both arising from the same rain event; and the interference correction term modifies the effective noise density via the harmonic-sum formula from m4-6. This is the equation the spine project computes numerically — every term has a specific provenance in one of the preceding lessons.

2. The Two-Sided Link: Forward and Return

A complete satellite broadband link has two independent link budgets.

Forward link (satellite → user terminal): the satellite transmits, the terminal receives. Bounded by satellite EIRP and terminal G/T. The terminal G/T is the easier side to optimise — larger aperture, lower noise temperature looking at cold sky.

Return link (user terminal → satellite): the terminal transmits, the satellite receives. Bounded by terminal EIRP and satellite G/T. The satellite G/T is the harder side — warm Earth in the antenna beam, higher T_sys. Return links are typically the tighter budget.

The two links are typically asymmetric in capacity: LEO broadband forward links deliver 50–500 Mbps per beam while return links deliver 10–50 Mbps per beam. This asymmetry drives the engineering tradeoffs discussed in Module 5.

4. Sensitivity Analysis: What Matters Most?

After computing the link budget, the most valuable next step is a sensitivity analysis: how much does the margin change for a unit change in each input parameter? For the Ka-band forward link at 99.9% availability in central Europe, the margin is typically close to zero — just closing or just failing. The parameters with the most leverage:

Satellite EIRP (+1 dBW = +1 dB margin): a 1:1 lever. A 3 dB increase in EIRP buys 3 dB of margin directly.

Terminal G/T (+1 dB/K = +1 dB margin): also a 1:1 lever. Improving G/T by 3 dB — bigger dish, lower noise LNA, or both — buys the same 3 dB of margin.

Rain attenuation: can only be reduced operationally — limiting minimum elevation angle (shorter slant path through rain), applying ACM (reducing required C/N₀ during rain), or site diversity at the gateway.

Required C/N₀ (ACM leverage): a more robust MODCOD during rain reduces the required C/N₀ — the step from 16-APSK 3/4 to QPSK 1/2 saves 9.2 dB, and the full DVB-S2 ladder spans about 18 dB (QPSK 1/4 to 32-APSK 9/10) — buying significant margin at the cost of proportional throughput reduction.

System noise temperature: replacing a 1.5 dB NF LNA with a 1.0 dB NF LNA reduces T_sys by approximately 50 K and improves G/T by approximately 0.5 dB — useful but small.

ACM AND EIRP DOMINATE THE SENSITIVITY RANKING The sensitivity analysis reveals that ACM and EIRP optimisation are the highest-leverage actions, and that chasing low LNA noise figures below 1.0 dB delivers diminishing returns when T_sys is dominated by sky noise and feed loss rather than the LNA itself.

Two features of this table deserve attention. First, the interference correction is largest in clear sky: with C/I = 25 dB over 250 MHz, C/I₀ = 109.0 dBHz sits close to the strong thermal C/N₀ of 111.5 dBHz and costs 4.4 dB — while under rain, the raised noise floor dominates and the same interference costs only 0.5 dB (the m4-6 result). Second, the rain event at 99.9% availability — 7 dB fade plus noise coupling bringing T_sys from 142 K to 385 K — degrades the thermal C/N₀ by 11.4 dB but the combined C/(N₀+I₀) by only 7.5 dB, from 107.1 to 99.6 dBHz, because the clear-sky figure was partly interference-limited. The clear-sky link supports the top of the DVB-S2 ladder at the reference carrier (32-APSK 9/10, required 99.2 dBHz). Under the 99.9% rain condition that rung no longer clears a realistic implementation margin, and ACM steps down to 32-APSK 3/4 (required 95.9 dBHz, margin +3.7 dB). Throughput falls by 17% (spectral efficiency 4.45 → 3.70 bit/symbol); the link never drops. A deeper fade would continue down the ladder — the QPSK 1/2 rung sits 15.0 dB below the clear-sky selection and retains 22% of peak throughput.

Note on model provenance: the hand tables in this lesson use the m4-4 design values (7 dB rain at 99.9% on the 20 GHz downlink, 10.5 dB on the 30 GHz uplink, zone F, per ITU-R P.618). The simplified P.618 implementation in the spine engine computes A₀.₀₁ from R₀.₀₁ and scales the attenuation to the availability target; at this geometry it yields about 6.3 dB (20 GHz) and 12.9 dB (30 GHz) at 99.9% — within 1–2.5 dB of the design values, the residual coming from rain-height assumptions and the simplifications relative to full P.618-13. Where they disagree, full P.618 with local rain data governs.

The return link cannot close a forward-class 208.3 Msym/s carrier at 99.9% availability in central Europe at 30° — it falls 6.0 dB short of even QPSK 1/2. The primary causes: the satellite receive chain T_sys is ~4.3 dB noisier than the ground terminal (Earth vs sky noise), the Ka-band uplink (30 GHz) is significantly more rain-attenuated than the downlink (20 GHz), and the terminal transmit power of 2 W is far below the satellite's per-beam power. In practice return carriers are much narrower, and the deficit converts into capacity rather than outage: at C/(N₀+I₀) = 78.2 dBHz, QPSK 1/2 closes with 2 dB of margin at a symbol rate of about 33 Msym/s — roughly one-sixth of the forward carrier, delivering ~33 Mbps. That is the quantitative form of the structural asymmetry: rain does not kill the return link; it shrinks it.

Possible engineering responses: increase terminal EIRP (more Tx power or larger aperture); reduce minimum elevation angle (shorter slant range, less rain path); accept a lower availability target (e.g. 99.5% instead of 99.9%); use return-link ACM with even more conservative MODCODs (QPSK 1/4, or the DVB-S2X very-low-SNR BPSK modes); narrow the return carrier (lower symbol rate lowers required C/N₀ dB-for-dB, at proportional capacity cost); implement uplink power control (add 3–6 dB headroom).

THE ASYMMETRY IS STRUCTURAL, NOT INCIDENTAL The forward-return capacity asymmetry of satellite broadband is not an engineering oversight to be fixed — it is the structural consequence of the satellite receiving against a warm Earth while the ground terminal receives against a cold sky, combined with the higher uplink frequency suffering more rain attenuation. Every LEO broadband system architecture must account for this asymmetry from the outset.

Exercise 1 — The Complete Link Budget Engine

The fully assembled spine project, run for both forward and return links with an availability table and MODCOD selection.

import math

K_DB = -228.6; T0 = 290.0

def fspl(d, f):     return 92.4 + 20*math.log10(d) + 20*math.log10(f)
def nf_te(nf):      return T0*(10**(nf/10)-1)
def loss_te(l,t=T0):return t*(10**(l/10)-1)
def dbl(x):         return 10**(x/10)
def ldb(x):         return 10*math.log10(x)

# ITU-R P.837 rain climatic zones — R0.01 (mm/hr)
RAIN_ZONES = {'A':8,'B':12,'C':15,'D':19,'E':22,'F':28,'G':30,'H':32,
              'J':35,'K':42,'L':60,'M':63,'N':95,'P':145,'Q':115}

def rain_specific(R, f):
    P={14:(.0229,1.265),20:(.0751,1.099),30:(.187,1.021),47:(.546,.911)}
    k,a=min(P.items(),key=lambda x:abs(x[0]-f))[1]
    return k*R**a

def rain_atten_p618(zone, freq, elev, avail=99.9, h_rain=4.0, h_s=0.1):
    # Simplified P.618: build A0.01 from R0.01, then scale the
    # ATTENUATION — never the rain rate — to the exceedance p (m4-4).
    # Valid p 0.01–5%, latitude >= 36 deg.
    R001=RAIN_ZONES.get(zone.upper(),28); p=max(100-avail, 0.01)
    e=math.radians(elev); Ls=(h_rain-h_s)/math.sin(e); Lg=Ls*math.cos(e)
    L0=35.0*math.exp(-0.015*R001); r=1/(1+Lg/L0)
    A001=rain_specific(R001,freq)*Ls*r
    if p==0.01: return A001
    return A001*(p/0.01)**(-(0.655+0.033*math.log(p)-0.045*math.log(A001)))

def gas_atten(freq_ghz, elev_deg, zenith=0.07):
    return zenith/math.sin(math.radians(elev_deg))

class LinkBudget:
    """Complete LEO satellite link budget engine — Module 4 spine final."""
    def __init__(self, name):
        self.name=name; self.extra_losses=[]; self._chain=[]
        self._eirp=self._fspl=self._grx=self._tant=self._req=None
        self._freq=None; self._elev=None; self._zone=None
        self._scint=0.0; self._ci=None; self._ci_bw=None
        self._gas_zenith=0.07

    def set_tx(self, p_w, g_dbi):           self._eirp = ldb(p_w)+g_dbi
    def set_path(self, d_km, f_ghz, elev=30.0):
        self._fspl=fspl(d_km,f_ghz); self._freq=f_ghz; self._elev=elev
    def add_loss(self, lbl, v):             self.extra_losses.append((lbl,v))
    def set_rx(self, g_dbi, t_ant_k):       self._grx=g_dbi; self._tant=t_ant_k
    def add_chain(self, lbl, te, g=None):   self._chain.append((lbl,te,g))
    def set_required(self, req):            self._req=req
    def set_rain_zone(self, zone):          self._zone=zone
    def set_scint(self, db):                self._scint=db
    def set_ci(self, ci_db, bw_hz):         self._ci=ci_db; self._ci_bw=bw_hz
    def set_gas_zenith(self, db):           self._gas_zenith=db

    def _tsys(self, rain_db=0.0):
        tant = self._tant + (273*(1-10**(-rain_db/10)) if rain_db>0 else 0)
        t=tant; cg=1.0
        for _,te,g in self._chain:
            t+=te/cg
            if g is not None: cg*=dbl(g)
        return t

    def _rain_db(self, avail):
        if self._zone and self._freq and self._elev and avail:
            return rain_atten_p618(self._zone,self._freq,self._elev,avail)
        return 0.0

    def compute(self, avail=None):
        rain = self._rain_db(avail)
        gas  = gas_atten(self._freq, self._elev, self._gas_zenith)
        t    = self._tsys(rain)
        gt   = self._grx - ldb(t)
        extra = sum(v for _,v in self.extra_losses)
        total_loss = self._fspl+gas+rain+self._scint+extra
        c_dbw  = self._eirp - total_loss + self._grx
        n0_dbhz = ldb(t)+K_DB
        cn0_th  = c_dbw - n0_dbhz
        if self._ci is not None and self._ci_bw:
            ci0 = self._ci+ldb(self._ci_bw)
            cn0_eff = -ldb(dbl(-cn0_th)+dbl(-ci0))
        else:
            cn0_eff = cn0_th
        margin = cn0_eff-self._req if self._req else None
        return {'cn0':cn0_eff,'margin':margin,'tsys':t,'gt':gt,
                'rain_db':rain,'gas_db':gas,'eirp':self._eirp}

    def availability_table(self, avail_levels, modcod_table=None):
        print(f"\n  {self.name} — Availability Table")
        print(f"  {'Avail%':>8}{'Rain':>8}{'T_sys':>8}{'C/N₀':>8}"
              f"{'ReqC/N₀':>9}{'Margin':>8}{'MODCOD':>14}")
        print("  "+"─"*68)
        for a in avail_levels:
            r = self.compute(a)
            req=self._req; modcod=""
            if modcod_table:
                for mname,mreq in modcod_table:
                    if r['cn0']>=mreq: modcod=mname; req=mreq; break
                if not modcod: modcod="LINK FAIL"; req=modcod_table[-1][1]
            margin = r['cn0']-req if req else r['margin']
            status='✓' if margin>=0 else '✗'
            print(f"  {a:>7.3f}%{r['rain_db']:>7.1f}{r['tsys']:>8.0f}"
                  f"{r['cn0']:>8.1f}{req or 0:>9.1f}{margin:>+8.1f}  {modcod:<12}{status}")

# Required C/N0 = Es/N0 threshold (EN 302 307-1 Table 13) + 83.2 dBHz
# (10*log10 of the 208.3 Msym/s reference carrier); shifts with Rs — m5-2.
MODCOD_TABLE = [("32-APSK 9/10",99.2),("32-APSK 3/4",95.9),
                 ("16-APSK 3/4",93.4),("16-APSK 2/3",92.2),
                 ("QPSK 3/4",87.2),("QPSK 1/2",84.2),("QPSK 1/4",80.8)]

# FORWARD LINK
fwd = LinkBudget("LEO Ka Forward Link — Central Europe")
fwd.set_tx(20, 34); fwd.set_path(1000, 20.0, 30.0); fwd.add_loss("Pointing", 0.5)
fwd.set_rx(37.5, 30.0)
fwd.add_chain("Feed 0.3dB", loss_te(0.3),-0.3)
fwd.add_chain("LNA NF=1 G=40", nf_te(1.0), 40.0)
fwd.add_chain("Downconv NF=8", nf_te(8.0), 20.0)
fwd.add_chain("IF NF=10", nf_te(10.0), None)

fwd.set_ci(25, 250e6); fwd.set_required(93.4)  # 16-APSK 3/4 @ 208.3 Msym/s
fwd.availability_table([99.99,99.9,99.5,99.0,95.0], MODCOD_TABLE)

# RETURN LINK
ret = LinkBudget("LEO Ka Return Link — Central Europe")

ret.set_rx(34, 255.0)
ret.add_chain("Feed 0.2dB", loss_te(0.2),-0.2)
ret.add_chain("LNA NF=1.5 G=35", nf_te(1.5), 35.0)
ret.add_chain("Downconv NF=8", nf_te(8.0), 20.0)
ret.add_chain("IF NF=10", nf_te(10.0), None)
ret.set_rain_zone('F'); ret.set_scint(0.7); ret.set_gas_zenith(0.1)
ret.set_ci(25, 250e6); ret.set_required(84.2)  # QPSK 1/2 @ 208.3 Msym/s

Prompt 1 — Availability-Capacity Tradeoff, Quantified

Rain at 99.9% availability in central Europe degrades the forward link's combined C/(N₀+I₀) by 7.5 dB (107.1 → 99.6 dBHz), while the underlying thermal C/N₀ falls 11.4 dB — the difference is the interference floor compressing the clear-sky figure. ACM responds with one rung: 32-APSK 9/10 → 32-APSK 3/4 at the 208.3 Msym/s reference carrier, a 3.3 dB threshold saving for a 17% throughput cut. A deeper fade reaching the QPSK 1/2 rung would save 15.0 dB and retain 22% of peak throughput. For a beam delivering 200 Mbps in clear sky, what is the expected throughput averaged over a year in central Europe, accounting for rain statistics across the full exceedance curve (not just the 99.9% point)?

Prompt 2 — Closing the Return Link Cost-Effectively

The return link cannot close a forward-class 208.3 Msym/s carrier at 99.9% availability in central Europe — it falls 6.0 dB short of QPSK 1/2. List five specific engineering changes, each with a quantitative estimate of its effect on the return link margin, and rank them by cost-effectiveness. Which combination would you recommend to close the return link at 99.9% availability while minimising system cost?

Prompt 3 — Modelling Fade Dynamics

The spine engine is a steady-state link budget — it does not model the dynamics of rain fade onset and recovery, which occur over 10 seconds to several minutes. How would you extend the engine to model: (a) the time to detect a fade and step down MODCOD, (b) throughput loss during fade onset before ACM responds, and (c) recovery overshoot when the MODCOD steps back up too aggressively?

Prompt 4 — Integrating Per-Pass Variation Into a Capacity Model

This module computed link budgets at a fixed geometry, but in a real LEO constellation the satellite elevation and slant range change continuously over a 5–8 minute pass — FSPL, gaseous attenuation, the rain slant path, and T_ant all change with elevation. Describe how you would integrate per-pass link budget variation into a system-level capacity model predicting total throughput over a user's location across all passes in a day.
