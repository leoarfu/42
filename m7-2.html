Optical Inter-Satellite Links:
Physics & Pointing

MODULE 7
NETWORKING, ISL & ROUTING
CORE

The mesh of m7-1 needs links between satellites, and the industry answered with lasers. This lesson explains why optical won over radio for inter-satellite links, works the laser link budget — which is the Module 4 budget transported into a regime where the free-space loss is 270 dB and the antenna gain is 106 dBi — and then confronts the problem that makes optical ISLs hard in a way nothing in this program has been: pointing. An optical ISL's beam is roughly 15 microradians wide, it must connect two platforms thousands of kilometres apart and closing at kilometres per second, and — because light takes milliseconds to cross the gap — the transmitter must aim not at where the receiver is but at where it will be, an offset of some 50 microradians, more than three beamwidths of empty space. Acquiring and holding that link is a control problem an order of magnitude beyond m6-5's satellite tracking, and it is the reason optical ISLs took decades to fly reliably.

The lesson's verified spine: a 10 cm optical aperture at 1550 nm delivers 106 dBi of gain and, at 1 W transmit power over a 4,000 km link, closes 10 Gbps with about 1 dB of margin — a budget as tight as any in the program, which is why every term matters and pointing loss is budgeted at 3 dB rather than the 0.3 dB of a pointed dish. The spine project gains the ISL link budget and the range-and-rate geometry that the topology of m7-3 will assemble into a mesh.

1. Why optical, not radio

An inter-satellite link could in principle use RF — and some early and some GEO relay systems do. But for a dense LEO mesh moving vast traffic, optical wins on three counts, each decisive:

Bandwidth. The optical carrier at 1550 nm sits at 193 THz — 6,400 times higher in frequency than a 30 GHz Ka carrier. One percent of that carrier is 1,930 GHz of usable spectrum, against the few hundred megahertz an RF ISL might coordinate. Optical ISLs run 1 to 100 Gbps per link as a matter of course; an RF ISL strains to match a fraction of that. A mesh carrying transcontinental traffic (m7-1) needs the optical pipe.

No spectrum coordination. RF spectrum is a regulated, coordinated, scarce resource (m4-3, and the m6-4 mask). Optical ISLs radiate a pencil beam into space between two satellites, touching no terrestrial band and no other operator's allocation — no ITU filing, no interference budget, no coordination. For a constellation flying thousands of links, escaping spectrum coordination entirely is worth as much as the bandwidth.

Beam confinement. The same short wavelength that gives the bandwidth gives a pencil beam from a tiny aperture: a 10 cm optical aperture produces a 15 µrad beam, where a 10 cm RF aperture at 30 GHz produces a beam ten thousand times wider. The narrow beam is simultaneously the source of the link's gain (§2), its security (a beam microradians wide is nearly impossible to intercept), and its central difficulty (§4's pointing problem). Optical ISLs are the mesh's enabling technology precisely because the physics that makes them powerful also makes them hard.

2. The laser link budget

The Module 4 link equation is unchanged — EIRP, free-space loss, receive gain, sensitivity — but every term moves to a new scale. The optical aperture's gain follows the same (πD/λ)² law as any antenna, and at optical wavelengths it is enormous:

G_optical = (πD/λ)² → for D = 10 cm, λ = 1550 nm: 106 dBi

against which sits a free-space loss as enormous in the other direction. At 4,000 km:

FSPL = 20·log₁₀(4πd/λ) = 270 dB

The budget, assembled (1 W transmit = 30 dBm, two 10 cm apertures, 4,000 km, 3 dB pointing loss, 3 dB optics loss):

Term Value
Transmit power+30 dBm
Transmit aperture gain+106 dBi
Receive aperture gain+106 dBi
Free-space loss−270 dB
Pointing loss−3 dB
Optics/implementation loss−3 dB
Received power−34 dBm
Receiver sensitivity (10 Gbps)~ −35 dBm
Margin~1 dB

Two readings. First, the two 106 dBi apertures — 212 dB of combined gain — are what make a 270 dB loss survivable; optical ISLs live entirely on the extraordinary directivity a pencil beam buys. Second, the margin is thin: 1 dB, against a pointed dish's comfortable rain-margined budgets (m4-1). Every loss term is fought for, which is why the pointing budget is a hard 3 dB and why holding the beam (§5) is a closed-loop control problem, not a one-time alignment. Raising the data rate raises the required sensitivity and eats the margin directly — the link-rate-versus-range trade is the ISL's defining design choice.

3. Range and rate: the geometry of a link in motion

An ISL connects two orbiting platforms, so its range and its pointing direction change continuously. The range is bounded above by the Earth itself: two satellites at 550 km can link only until the Earth's limb blocks the line of sight, which for a grazing altitude of 80 km (above the sensible atmosphere) gives a maximum ISL range of ~5,000 km. Beyond that the beam would have to pass through the atmosphere or the planet. Within that bound, the practical ranges are set by the constellation's geometry (m7-3): intra-plane neighbours a fixed ~1,500–4,000 km apart, inter-plane neighbours whose range varies as the planes converge toward the poles.

The rates are what make it hard. Two satellites in different planes have a relative transverse velocity of kilometres per second, and the pointing direction between them sweeps accordingly. The FSPL at these ranges (262–272 dB across 1,500–5,000 km) means range variation of a few thousand kilometres is ~10 dB of budget swing — the link's margin breathes as the geometry changes, and the ACM-like discipline of m5-2 reappears: an ISL may run a higher rate when its neighbour is close and back off as it recedes. Range, rate, and link capacity are one coupled system, and the spine models them together.

4. The point-ahead problem

Here is what separates optical ISL pointing from everything before it. The beam is 15 µrad wide. Light takes time to cross the link — 13 ms round trip at 4,000 km — and in that time the receiving satellite moves. So the transmitter must aim not at where it sees the receiver (that is where the receiver was, one light-time ago) but at where the receiver will be when the light arrives. This point-ahead angle is the relative transverse velocity divided by the speed of light:

point-ahead ≈ 2·v_transverse / c

For a transverse relative velocity of ~7,600 m/s, the point-ahead angle is ~50 µrad — and the beam is only 15 µrad wide. The transmitter must aim more than three beamwidths off the apparent position of the receiver, into what looks like empty space, and be right. Get the point-ahead wrong by one beamwidth and the link drops. This has no analogue in m6-5's terminal tracking, where the beam was degrees wide and the target's light-time offset was negligible against the beamwidth. At optical ISL scale the light-time offset is the dominant pointing term, and computing it correctly — from the ephemeris of both satellites — is the heart of the acquisition system.

5. Acquisition, tracking, and pointing (ATP)

Establishing an optical ISL is a three-phase control problem, harder at every phase than m6-5's:

Acquisition — finding a 15 µrad target across thousands of kilometres from a cold start. Even with both satellites' ephemerides known (Module 2), residual attitude uncertainty (m6-5 §5) is far larger than the beamwidth, so the transmitter scans a small uncertainty cone with a beacon while the receiver searches for it — a spiral or raster search that must converge in seconds. The tighter the beam, the more precise the pointing but the harder the acquisition: another instance of the confinement paradox.

Tracking — once acquired, a closed-loop fine-pointing system (a fast steering mirror driven by a quadrant detector sensing the incoming beam's position) holds the link against platform vibration, thermal drift, and the changing geometry. The loop bandwidth must exceed the platform's disturbance spectrum — the m6-5 attitude-latency inequality, now at microradian scale.

Pointing — the coarse and fine stages together, with the point-ahead offset (§4) applied continuously from the computed relative velocity. The transmit beam is deliberately aimed off the received-beam direction by the point-ahead angle; the two are not co-boresighted, and the offset changes as the geometry evolves.

The system that does all this — a gimbal or steering assembly, a fine-steering mirror, a quadrant detector, a beacon, and the ephemeris-driven point-ahead computation — is the optical terminal's real complexity and cost, dwarfing the laser itself. It is why optical ISLs, demonstrated in the 2000s, took until the 2020s to fly in quantity: the physics was never in doubt; the pointing was.

6. What the ISL costs the satellite

An optical ISL terminal is mass, power, and cost on every meshed satellite — and a mesh needs several per satellite (typically four: two intra-plane, two inter-plane, to build the +grid of m7-3). Each terminal carries the aperture and its steering, the laser and modem, the ATP electronics, and the thermal and structural provisions to hold optical alignment on a flexing spacecraft. The aggregate — several terminals per satellite across a constellation of hundreds or thousands — is the ISL hardware cost that m7-1's architecture trade weighed against the ground segment. It is also a reliability consideration: an ISL terminal that fails degrades the mesh's connectivity locally, and the routing layer (m7-4) must survive links that come and go — from failures as well as from the geometry.

The design tension the whole lesson circles: the narrow beam is the source of the link's capability (gain, bandwidth, security) and the source of its difficulty (pointing, acquisition, thin margin). A wider beam would be easier to point and acquire but would lose the gain that closes the budget and the confinement that gives the security. The optical ISL sits at a sharp optimum, and the ATP system is what keeps it there. This is the m6-1 confinement principle — gain narrows the beam, narrowing tightens the pointing spec — carried to its physical extreme.

8. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

Stage 2 of the spine: an ISLBudget that closes (or fails to close) a link given aperture, power, range, and data rate; a range-and-rate geometry that computes an ISL's length and its point-ahead angle from two satellites' states; and the ATP feasibility check that decides whether a geometrically possible link is operationally possible. The topology graph of m7-1 gains link weights — a link that closes at 100 Gbps when its neighbour is near and 10 Gbps when far, or not at all beyond 5,000 km — turning the abstract "can-link" edge into a real capacity.

What remains before routing is the pattern: which satellites link to which, how the +grid assembles from intra-plane and inter-plane links, how it breaks near the poles and reverses at the seam, and how fast the whole structure churns as the constellation moves. That is m7-3, and it is what makes the topology graph time-varying — the property that will defeat every terrestrial routing protocol in m7-4.

Exercise 1 — The optical link budget

Close the ISL, and find where it stops closing.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x) if x > 0 else -999.0

LAM = 1550e-9          # 1550 nm
C = 299792458.0

def aperture_gain_db(D_m, lam=LAM):
    return db((math.pi*D_m/lam)**2)

def fspl_db(range_m, lam=LAM):
    return 20*math.log10(4*math.pi*range_m/lam)

def isl_budget(pt_w, D_tx, D_rx, range_km, point_db=3.0, optics_db=3.0):
    pt_dbm = db(pt_w) + 30
    pr = (pt_dbm + aperture_gain_db(D_tx) + aperture_gain_db(D_rx)
          - fspl_db(range_km*1e3) - point_db - optics_db)
    return pr   # dBm received

def sensitivity_dbm(rate_gbps):
    # coherent-ish scaling: ~-35 dBm at 10 Gbps, +10*log10(rate/10) worse
    return -35.0 + 10*math.log10(rate_gbps/10.0)

print(f"{'range km':>9}{'Rx dBm':>9}{'sens@10G':>10}{'margin':>8}")
for d in [1500, 2500, 4000, 5000]:
    pr = isl_budget(1.0, 0.10, 0.10, d)
    m = pr - sensitivity_dbm(10)
    print(f"{d:>9}{pr:>9.1f}{sensitivity_dbm(10):>10.1f}{m:>8.1f}")

Exercise 2 — Point-ahead and the pointing problem

Quantify why optical ISL pointing is a different sport.

import math
Re, h, C = 6371e3, 550e3, 299792458.0
LAM = 1550e-9

def orbital_v(alt=h):
    return math.sqrt(3.986e14/(Re+alt))

def beam_divergence_urad(D_m, lam=LAM):
    return lam/D_m * 1e6

def point_ahead_urad(v_transverse):
    return 2*v_transverse/C * 1e6

v = orbital_v()
print(f"orbital velocity: {v:.0f} m/s")
print(f"beam divergence (10 cm): {beam_divergence_urad(0.10):.1f} µrad")

print(f"\n{'v_trans m/s':>12}{'point-ahead µrad':>18}{'beamwidths':>12}")
for vt in [1000, 3800, 7600, 15000]:
    pa = point_ahead_urad(vt)
    print(f"{vt:>12}{pa:>18.1f}{pa/beam_divergence_urad(0.10):>12.1f}")

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 2: ISLBudget and range/rate

The topology's edges gain real weights.

import math
db = lambda x: 10*math.log10(x) if x > 0 else -999.0
Re, h, C = 6371e3, 550e3, 299792458.0
LAM = 1550e-9

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M7 SPINE PROJECT — CONSTELLATION ROUTER
# Stage 2: ISLBudget + range/rate geometry.
#   Stage 1 (m7-1) gave a can-link topology switch.
#   Stage 2 gives each link a BUDGET (does it close, at what
#   rate?) and a POINT-AHEAD (is it operationally pointable?).
#   Next: m7-3 assembles links into the +grid mesh and its
#   churn; m7-4 routes on the weighted, time-varying graph.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

class ISLBudget:
    MAX_RANGE_KM = 5000     # limb-grazing bound (§3)
    def __init__(self, pt_w=1.0, D=0.10, point_db=3.0, optics_db=3.0):
        self.pt_dbm = db(pt_w) + 30
        self.D, self.point_db, self.optics_db = D, point_db, optics_db
        self.g = db((math.pi*D/LAM)**2)

    def rx_dbm(self, range_km):
        fspl = 20*math.log10(4*math.pi*range_km*1e3/LAM)
        return self.pt_dbm + 2*self.g - fspl - self.point_db - self.optics_db

    def _sens(self, rate_gbps): return -35.0 + 10*math.log10(rate_gbps/10.0)

    def max_rate_gbps(self, range_km, margin_db=1.0):
        if range_km > self.MAX_RANGE_KM: return 0.0
        # rate where rx = sensitivity + margin
        headroom = self.rx_dbm(range_km) - (-35.0) - margin_db
        return 10.0 * 10**(headroom/10.0) if headroom > -50 else 0.0

    def point_ahead_urad(self, v_transverse):
        return 2*v_transverse/C * 1e6

    def link_ok(self, range_km, rate_gbps, v_transverse):
        if range_km > self.MAX_RANGE_KM: return False, "out of range"
        if self.rx_dbm(range_km) - self._sens(rate_gbps) < 1.0:
            return False, "budget fails"
        # point-ahead must be trackable (assume ATP handles <100 µrad)
        if self.point_ahead_urad(v_transverse) > 100:
            return False, "unpointable"
        return True, "OK"

isl = ISLBudget()
print(f"{'range km':>9}{'max rate Gbps':>14}{'point-ahead µrad':>18}")
for d in [1500, 3000, 4500, 5500]:
    pa = isl.point_ahead_urad(7600)
    print(f"{d:>9}{isl.max_rate_gbps(d):>14.1f}{pa if d<=5000 else 0:>18.1f}")

for d, rate, vt in [(2000, 100, 3800), (4000, 10, 7600), (6000, 10, 7600)]:
    ok, why = isl.link_ok(d, rate, vt)
    print(f"link {d} km @ {rate} Gbps, vt {vt}: {why}")

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 01

Section 2's ~1 dB margin makes the optical ISL the tightest link in the program, and section 3 showed the margin breathing ±5 dB with range. A designer could fix this by over-sizing the aperture (more gain, more margin) at the cost of mass, power, and a harder acquisition (§5). Work the trade for a constellation flying four terminals on every one of a thousand satellites: how much aperture over-sizing is worth it, given that margin buys rate-stability but mass and acquisition-difficulty scale with the whole fleet? Identify whether the right answer is a fatter margin per link or a routing layer (m7-4) that simply tolerates links dropping to lower rates — and what that choice says about where robustness should live.

PROMPT 02

Section 4's point-ahead angle is computed from the ephemeris, which the constellation knows precisely (Module 2). But ephemeris has error, and a point-ahead error of one beamwidth drops the link. Quantify the chain: what ephemeris accuracy (position and velocity) does a 15 µrad beam demand, is that achievable with onboard GNSS and orbit determination, and what happens to the mesh if a maneuver or a drag event degrades one satellite's ephemeris? Decide whether optical ISLs make the constellation's orbit-determination system a link-availability dependency — and what that couples together that Module 2 treated as separate.

PROMPT 03

Section 1 credited optical ISLs with freedom from spectrum coordination — no ITU filing, no interference budget. Interrogate the completeness of that freedom: optical beams are unregulated in space, but the ground-to-space optical links some systems propose are not free of the atmosphere, and even space optical links raise questions of orbital debris hazard (a laser is a directed-energy beam) and of eventual coordination if the sky fills with optical constellations. Take a position on whether "no coordination" is a durable advantage or a temporary one that regulation will eventually reach, and name the specific event that would trigger the reach.

PROMPT 04

This lesson carried optical ISL physics at the level of the public link budget and the ATP problem structure, explicitly deferring the detailed ATP control design as specialist engineering — the same scope boundary m6-6 drew for certification. Yet optical ISL and constellation architecture sit close to the professional domain this curriculum excludes. Interrogate the boundary honestly: what can a learner genuinely design from the link budget and geometry in this lesson, versus what requires the ATP control-loop and acquisition-search expertise the lesson deliberately does not teach? Specify the point at which the lesson's ISLBudget spine stops being a design tool and becomes a feasibility estimator, and whether that is the right place to stop.
