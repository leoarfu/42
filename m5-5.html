The 3GPP NTN
Air Interface

MODULE 5
AIR INTERFACE & ACCESS
CORE

Everything in this module so far descended from the satellite industry's own lineage: DVB framing, MF-TDMA returns, MODCOD ladders. This lesson follows the other bloodline. In Release 17 (2022), 3GPP — the body that standardises terrestrial cellular — published Non-Terrestrial Networks (NTN): a set of adaptations that let the 5G NR air interface operate through a satellite. The motivation is not technical superiority; m5-2 established that every modern air interface sits 1–2 dB from Shannon and there is no efficiency prize left to win. The motivation is the ecosystem: three billion NR-capable devices, a silicon industry that amortises modem development across them, and mobile network operators who want satellite coverage as a feature of their existing network rather than a separate system.

NTN comes in two families. NR-NTN adapts 5G broadband — the subject of this lesson. IoT-NTN adapts NB-IoT and eMTC for low-rate sensor traffic and is noted where it differs. Release 17's baseline architecture is transparent (bent-pipe): the gNB stays on the ground, the satellite relays waveforms, and the entire air interface — including its timing — stretches across the space segment. Regenerative architectures (gNB functions on the satellite) are later-release territory and reappear in Module 7's networking context.

The intellectual core of the lesson is a single pattern: NR was engineered around numerical assumptions — cell radii of kilometres, round trips of microseconds to a few milliseconds, Doppler of hundreds of hertz — and the satellite channel violates every one of them by two to four orders of magnitude. Release 17 is the catalogue of fixes, and most of them formalise exactly the mechanism m5-3 §7 sketched: the terminal knows where the satellite is, and pre-compensates.

1. NR in one section: the OFDM resource grid

NR transmits OFDM (m5-3 §1) organised in a time-frequency grid:

  • Subcarrier spacing (SCS): 15 × 2^μ kHz — 15, 30, 60, 120 kHz. Wider spacing means shorter symbols and slots, and more Doppler tolerance.
  • Slot: 14 OFDM symbols, lasting 1 ms / 2^μ — 1 ms at 15 kHz down to 0.125 ms at 120 kHz. The slot is the basic scheduling unit.
  • Resource block (RB): 12 subcarriers. Resource element (RE): one subcarrier × one symbol — the atomic unit; throughput is REs per second times bits per RE.
  • Cyclic prefix (CP): a copied tail prepended to each symbol that makes multipath equalisation trivial. Normal CP costs exactly 6.7% of time (1,024 of every 15,360 samples), at every SCS.
  • Channel utilisation: an NR carrier fills its channel with as many RBs as guard bands allow (TS 38.101):
Channel SCS N_RB Occupied Utilisation
20 MHz15 kHz10619.08 MHz95.4%
50 MHz30 kHz13347.88 MHz95.8%
100 MHz30 kHz27398.28 MHz98.3%
400 MHz120 kHz264380.16 MHz95.0%

Bits per RE are set by the MCS — modulation up to 256-QAM with LDPC code rates to 948/1024, a ceiling of 7.41 bits/RE. (QAM, not APSK: NR assumed terrestrial power amplifiers; the uplink's DFT-s-OFDM option is the concession to power-limited transmitters, per m5-3 §1.) On top of CP and guard, control and reference signals — DMRS for channel estimation, PDCCH for scheduling grants, SSB for synchronisation — consume a traffic-dependent 10–20% of REs.

2. What the satellite channel breaks

The numbers NR was designed around, against what the satellite delivers:

Four mechanisms break specifically:

Random access and timing advance. NR terminals learn their timing correction from the network during random access, and the correction range assumes a small cell. A LEO service link's propagation exceeds the design range outright — and worse, it changes continuously, which terrestrial TA maintenance loops were never built to chase at 23 µs/s.

Doppler. NR's frequency synchronisation tolerates terrestrial Doppler. Tens of kilohertz at S-band — several subcarrier spacings — breaks initial acquisition and steady-state tracking alike.

HARQ. NR's hybrid-ARQ retransmission protocol is stop-and-wait per process, with 16 parallel processes: a transmitter can have at most 16 transport blocks awaiting acknowledgement. The protocol self-throttles when the round trip exceeds 16 slot durations — section 4 quantifies how badly.

Scheduling timelines. NR grants reference slots a few slots ahead ("transmit in slot n+k", k small). When the grant itself takes 13 ms to arrive, the timeline arithmetic breaks.

3. The Release-17 fixes: ephemeris does the work

The fixes share one architecture: push knowledge to the terminal and let it pre-compensate.

Timing: split TA. The network broadcasts satellite ephemeris and a common TA (the delay component shared by the whole beam, referenced to the feeder path). Each terminal computes its UE-specific TA from its own GNSS position and the broadcast ephemeris, and applies the sum — before random access, and continuously updated thereafter, including a signalled TA drift rate. The terminal arrives at the gNB time-aligned despite 23 µs/s of delay drift. This is m5-3 §7's ephemeris-driven timing advance, now a standardised mandatory feature — and it makes GNSS capability a de facto requirement of an NTN terminal, an assumption m5-6 will have to confront for handsets.

Frequency: split Doppler compensation. The network pre-compensates the common (beam-centre) Doppler on the downlink; the terminal computes and pre-compensates its residual from position plus ephemeris on the uplink. Residuals shrink to oscillator-error levels, and the ordinary NR tracking loops handle the remainder.

Scheduling: K_offset. A broadcast slot-offset added to every scheduling timeline, sized to the beam's round trip. Mechanically trivial; it simply makes the grant arithmetic honest about propagation.

HARQ: more processes, or none. Release 17 extends HARQ to 32 processes and adds the option to disable HARQ feedback per process, falling back on RLC-layer retransmission and more conservative MCS selection. Which option matters when is pure arithmetic — next section.

4. The HARQ arithmetic

Stop-and-wait per process means a single terminal's connection can have at most N_proc transport blocks in flight per round trip. With one TB per slot, that terminal's duty factor is:

duty = min(1, N_proc × T_slot / RTT)
Configuration RTT Duty factor
16 processes, 0.5 ms slotsLEO ~25.8 ms31%
32 processes, 0.5 ms slotsLEO ~25.8 ms62%
16 processes, 0.5 ms slotsGEO ~541 ms1%
32 processes, 0.5 ms slotsGEO ~541 ms3%

The table explains the design cleanly. At LEO, doubling the process count doubles a terminal's usable peak rate and gets within reach of full occupancy (wider SCS — shorter slots — makes it worse, not better; more slots fit in the same RTT). At GEO, no plausible process count closes a 541 ms round trip: HARQ feedback is disabled, reliability moves to the RLC layer and to link margin, and the m5-2 selection margin grows to buy the decode probability that retransmission no longer guarantees. One protocol parameter, two entirely different operating philosophies, decided by orbit geometry.

Scope this cap correctly: it binds the per-terminal peak rate, not the beam. Every terminal runs its own HARQ entity, and the scheduler pipelines transport blocks across hundreds of them — the beam's resource grid stays full even while each individual connection is duty-limited. The cap therefore matters exactly where a single user wants the whole carrier: speed tests, single-stream bulk transfers, backhaul-over-NTN. The spine stage encodes both quantities separately.

5. NTN throughput arithmetic: the OFDM ledger

The spine engine's DVB branch multiplied a symbol rate by a Table-13 efficiency and two framing factors. The NR branch counts resource elements:

RE rate = N_RB × 12 × 14 / T_slot, net of CP by construction
throughput = RE rate × (1 − control overhead) × bits-per-RE

Bits-per-RE follows the MCS selection, which — unlike DVB's published Table 13 — has no standardised SNR mapping (CQI reporting is implementation-defined). The spine uses the standard system-study abstraction: an attenuated Shannon curve, η = min(7.41, 0.75 × log₂(1 + SNR)), the ~0.75 factor representing real LDPC/control performance. This is a modelling choice and is labelled as one; it is accurate to within a few tens of percent, which is adequate for architecture comparison and inadequate for equipment specification.

The honest comparison, same 400 MHz of Ka spectrum, same C/N₀ of 96 dBHz, both branches of the engine:

Branch Configuration Net throughput
NR-NTN264 RB @ 120 kHz, SNR 10.2 dB, 14% control~806 Mbps
DVB-S2X381 Msym/s (α = 0.05), 8PSK 3/4, pilots + GSE~799 Mbps

Parity, to within the abstraction's error bars — which is the m5-2 conclusion made concrete: the air-interface decision is not about spectral efficiency, because nobody is far from Shannon. It is about everything else: ecosystem, mobility, control granularity, and the ownership question of m5-4 §7. Note the overhead structures differ even where the totals converge — NR pays CP (6.7%), guard (~5%), and control (10–20%) for scheduling granularity and mobility management that DVB simply does not have; DVB pays almost nothing beyond FEC because it assumes fixed terminals on a broadcast-descended forward link.

6. Cells, bands, and the IoT branch

Earth-moving vs earth-fixed cells. A LEO satellite's beams either sweep the ground with the spacecraft (earth-moving: simple payload, terminals handover every few tens of seconds even standing still) or are steered to hold fixed ground cells during a pass (earth-fixed: the Module-3 phased-array payload doing beam-pointing work so the network layer sees quasi-static cells). Release 17 supports both; the choice trades payload complexity against handover signalling load — a Module 7 topic seeded here.

Bands. Release 17 NTN operates in FR1 satellite bands — n255 (L-band) and n256 (S-band) — the handset-friendly, MSS-allocated spectrum. Release 18 extended NTN to Ka-band (FR2-NTN) for VSAT-class broadband terminals. The physics of m4-3 stands unchanged: L/S-band closes to poor antennas and carries little spectrum; Ka carries the capacity and demands the dish.

IoT-NTN. NB-IoT over satellite: ~180 kHz carriers, kilobit rates, extreme link-margin tolerance, latency indifference. The same ephemeris-based compensation machinery applies, and Release 19 adds store-and-forward operation — a satellite collecting sensor uplinks without a simultaneous feeder link, which quietly reintroduces the regenerative payload through the back door.

8. What this lesson delivers to the design chain

The engine gains its second branch: an NR resource-element ledger alongside the DVB symbol ledger, both consuming the same Module-4 C/N₀ and producing comparable net throughputs — with the comparison demonstrating that the branches differ in structure and ecosystem, not efficiency. The HARQ duty factor enters the engine as a throughput cap that depends on orbit geometry, the first place the module's arithmetic couples to Module 2's, and the RTT values it consumes are exported onward to M7's latency budget alongside m5-3's grant delay.

Strategically: NTN resolves m5-4's decision table into a third option. Not the satellite industry's standard, not a proprietary waveform, but the terrestrial industry's standard — with the handset ecosystem as its dowry. The next lesson takes that dowry to its limit: closing the link to an unmodified phone in someone's hand.

Exercise 1 — The resource-element ledger

Build NR throughput from the grid arithmetic and locate every overhead.

import math

NRB_TABLE = {  # (channel MHz, SCS kHz) -> N_RB, per TS 38.101
    (20, 15): 106, (50, 30): 133, (100, 30): 273, (400, 120): 264,
}

def nr_ledger(bw_mhz, scs_khz, snr_db, ctrl_oh=0.14):
    nrb = NRB_TABLE[(bw_mhz, scs_khz)]
    mu = int(math.log2(scs_khz / 15))
    slot_s = 1e-3 / 2**mu
    re_rate = nrb * 12 * 14 / slot_s          # CP excluded by symbol count
    eta = min(7.406, 0.75 * math.log2(1 + 10**(snr_db/10)))  # link abstraction
    thr = re_rate * (1 - ctrl_oh) * eta
    occ = nrb * 12 * scs_khz * 1e3
    print(f"{bw_mhz} MHz @ {scs_khz} kHz: {nrb} RB, "
          f"util {occ/(bw_mhz*1e6)*100:.1f}%, slot {slot_s*1e3:.3f} ms")
    print(f"  RE rate {re_rate/1e6:.1f} M/s, eta {eta:.2f} b/RE "
          f"-> {thr/1e6:.0f} Mbps net")
    return thr

nr_ledger(20, 15, 2.0)      # S-band handheld class
nr_ledger(100, 30, 10.0)    # Ka VSAT, moderate SNR
nr_ledger(400, 120, 10.2)   # Ka VSAT, wideband

Exercise 3 — Spine Stage 5: the second branch

The engine gains its NR branch and races it against the DVB branch on equal terms.

import math

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# M5 SPINE PROJECT — THROUGHPUT ENGINE
# Stage 5: the NR-NTN branch.
#   DVB branch (stages 1-4): symbol rate x Table-13 eta
#     x scheduler x pilots x GSE
#   NR branch (here): RE rate x (1-ctrl) x abstraction eta,
#     capped by the HARQ duty factor
# Same input currency both sides: C/N0 from the M4 engine.
# Next: m5-6 D2D case, m5-7 full assembly.
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

MODCOD_TABLE = [
    ("QPSK 1/4", 0.490243, -2.35), ("QPSK 1/2", 0.988858, 1.00),
    ("QPSK 3/4", 1.487473, 4.03),  ("8PSK 3/4", 2.228124, 7.91),
    ("16APSK 2/3", 2.637201, 8.97),("16APSK 3/4", 2.966728, 10.21),
    ("32APSK 3/4", 3.703295, 12.73),("32APSK 9/10", 4.453027, 16.05),
]
NRB_TABLE = {(20,15):106, (50,30):133, (100,30):273, (400,120):264}

def dvb_branch(bw_hz, alpha, cn0_dbhz, sched=0.97, pilot=0.9763, gse=0.9934):
    rs = bw_hz / (1 + alpha)
    esn0 = cn0_dbhz - 10*math.log10(rs)
    best = None
    for name, eta, thr in MODCOD_TABLE:
        if esn0 >= thr + 1.5 and (best is None or eta > best[1]):
            best = (name, eta, thr)
    if not best: return 0.0, "no MODCOD"
    return best[1]*rs*sched*pilot*gse, best[0]

def nr_branch(bw_mhz, scs_khz, cn0_dbhz, ctrl=0.14):
    """BEAM-level net throughput: the scheduler pipelines HARQ across
    terminals, so no duty cap applies at beam scope."""
    nrb = NRB_TABLE[(bw_mhz, scs_khz)]
    occ = nrb*12*scs_khz*1e3
    snr = cn0_dbhz - 10*math.log10(occ)
    mu = int(math.log2(scs_khz/15)); slot_ms = 1.0/2**mu
    re_rate = nrb*12*14/(slot_ms*1e-3)
    eta = min(7.406, 0.75*math.log2(1+10**(snr/10)))
    return re_rate*(1-ctrl)*eta, f"SNR {snr:.1f} dB", slot_ms

def ue_peak_duty(n_harq, slot_ms, rtt_ms):
    """PER-TERMINAL peak-rate fraction: the stop-and-wait window."""
    return min(1.0, n_harq*slot_ms/rtt_ms)

# ── Equal-terms race (BEAM level): 400 MHz Ka, C/N0 96 dBHz ──
cn0 = 96.0
d, dn = dvb_branch(400e6, 0.05, cn0)
n, nn, slot_ms = nr_branch(400, 120, cn0)
print(f"DVB-S2X branch: {d/1e6:7.1f} Mbps  ({dn})")
print(f"NR-NTN branch:  {n/1e6:7.1f} Mbps  ({nn})  [beam level]")

# ── The per-terminal HARQ cap, made visible ─────────────
# One terminal wanting the whole carrier gets at most duty x beam rate.
print(f"\n{'n_harq':>7}{'UE peak duty':>13}{'UE peak (Mbps)':>16}")
for np_ in [8, 16, 32, 64]:
    duty = ue_peak_duty(np_, slot_ms, 25.8)
    print(f"{np_:>7}{duty*100:>12.0f}%{n*duty/1e6:>16.1f}")

# ── Orbit sensitivity: beam unaffected, UE peak collapses ──
print(f"\n{'RTT (ms)':>9}{'beam (Mbps)':>13}{'UE peak, 32 proc':>18}")
for rtt in [13, 25.8, 105, 541]:
    duty = ue_peak_duty(32, slot_ms, rtt)
    print(f"{rtt:>9}{n/1e6:>13.1f}{n*duty/1e6:>17.1f}")

REFLECTION

Four prompts. Each forces a decision — write the position down before moving on; the act of committing is the exercise.

PROMPT 01

Section 3's fix catalogue routes everything through terminal self-knowledge: GNSS position plus broadcast ephemeris. Attack the architecture's single point of failure — construct the failure chain for a terminal with a spoofed or jammed GNSS receiver, decide which layer of the NTN stack detects it first, and compare against how the DVB/proprietary world (m5-3 §7, m5-4) handles the same problem. Is the vulnerability intrinsic to the physics or to Release 17's specific design choices?

PROMPT 02

The HARQ table shows numerology and protocol fighting each other: wider SCS improves Doppler tolerance and scheduling granularity while worsening the duty factor. You are configuring a Ka-band LEO NTN carrier — pick an SCS, a process count, and a HARQ mode, and defend the triple against both the section-4 arithmetic and the section-2 Doppler column. State what traffic type would make you reverse the choice.

PROMPT 03

Section 5 declared efficiency parity and moved the decision to ecosystem. Steelman the opposite: find the operating regime in this module's own numbers where the two branches' overhead structures diverge enough to matter — consider low SNR (where control overhead is regressive), small packets (m5-4 §5), and bursty traffic (m5-3 §4) — and characterise the customer for whom the "parity" conclusion is false.

PROMPT 04

Release 17 chose the transparent architecture as baseline, and Release 19's store-and-forward is already reintroducing onboard processing. Extrapolate: list the protocol functions that migrate to the satellite in a regenerative NTN (which pieces of the gNB, in what order, and why), and identify what each migration does to the feeder-link requirements that Module 4 priced and the ISL topics Module 7 will price.
