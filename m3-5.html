Frequency Reuse, Beam-Hopping & Flexible Payloads

MODULE 3
COMMS PAYLOAD

The previous lesson established how a phased array generates a spot beam. This lesson asks what happens when you generate many such beams simultaneously and what you do with the spectrum they share. Frequency reuse multiplies capacity by assigning the same spectrum to angularly separated beams. Beam-hopping redistributes capacity in time to match demand. The flexible payload combines both — making the power, spectrum, and beam-pattern allocation programmable on orbit. All three techniques are in operational use in first-generation LEO broadband systems and are central to the capacity economics of the next generation.

1. The Capacity Equation

The capacity of a single satellite beam is bounded by the Shannon-Hartley theorem:

C = B · log₂(1 + SNR)

Capacity scales linearly with bandwidth B and logarithmically with SNR. For a multibeam satellite with K beams and frequency reuse factor R, the total capacity is approximately:

C_total ≈ (K/R) · B · log₂(1 + SINR)

where SINR is the signal-to-interference-plus-noise ratio — the SNR degraded by co-channel interference from other beams using the same frequency. Tightening reuse (reducing R) increases the bandwidth per beam but increases co-channel interference, degrading SINR. There is an optimal reuse factor that maximises total capacity — it depends on the antenna's sidelobe isolation capability.

2. Frequency Reuse Patterns

The standard commercial satellite multibeam architecture is four-colour reuse: the available spectrum is divided into two halves (A, B) and each half is transmitted in two orthogonal polarisations (RHCP, LHCP), creating four colours — A-RHCP, A-LHCP, B-RHCP, B-LHCP. Adjacent beams are assigned different colours. The minimum geographic separation between same-colour beams is approximately 2–3 beam diameters.

Four-colour reuse gives a capacity multiplication factor of 4, with co-channel interference suppressed by both geographic separation and polarisation isolation (RHCP/LHCP provide 20–30 dB of co-channel rejection).

More aggressive reuse

Four-colour reuse is conservative. More aggressive reuse (3-, 2-, or 1-colour / full reuse) increases the capacity multiplication factor but requires higher antenna sidelobe suppression. Enabling technologies: amplitude-tapered DBF arrays (m3-4), interference cancellation in the digital payload processor, and spatial precoding (multiuser MIMO) placing nulls toward co-channel users.

The sidelobe level achieved by the phased array directly sets how aggressively the spectrum can be reused.

3. Beam-Hopping: Time-Domain Capacity Allocation

Instead of illuminating all K beams simultaneously with 1/K of the power, beam-hopping illuminates each beam in turn with all the available power, spending a fraction T_k of each frame period on beam k:

T_k = D_k / Σ D_k

where D_k is the demand in beam k. A dense urban beam with twice the demand of a rural beam receives twice the dwell time. The frame period is typically 1–10 ms in DVB-S2X Super-frame beam-hopping implementations.

Advantages

Demand-matching: in a fixed-beam system, each beam receives a fixed spectrum and power allocation regardless of how many users are in it. An empty ocean beam wastes capacity that a congested city beam could use. Beam-hopping redistributes dwell time continuously — capacity follows demand, not geography.

Full power per beam: when a beam is being served, it receives the full satellite transmit power for that dwell interval. For a given peak EIRP budget, beam-hopping delivers the maximum possible SNR to each beam, enabling higher modulation orders and greater spectral efficiency.

Limitations

Burst-mode latency: users receive traffic in bursts during their dwell period rather than continuously. For TCP and VoIP, this introduces additional queuing delay — up to one hopping frame period. DVB-S2X Super-frame mode targets frame periods where this delay contribution stays below the link propagation latency floor.

Synchronisation complexity: all ground terminals must synchronise to the hopping schedule and know precisely when their beam will be served. This requires tight timing distribution from the satellite to all terminals.

BEAM-HOPPING vs SIMULTANEOUS BEAMS: THE CORE TRADEOFF Simultaneous multibeam: all beams active, each with fraction of power → moderate SNR per beam, continuous service. Beam-hopping: one beam at a time, full power → maximum SNR per beam, burst-mode service. The right choice depends on whether the system is power-limited (hopping wins) or interference-limited (simultaneous reuse management wins).

4. Beam-Hopping and Full Frequency Reuse

The most powerful combination is full frequency reuse with beam-hopping: at any given moment the satellite illuminates one beam with all the spectrum and all the power. Since only one beam is illuminated at a time, there is no co-channel interference — the beams that would interfere are simply off during each other's dwell periods.

Total system capacity:

C_total = K · T_k_avg · B · log₂(1 + SNR_peak)

This exceeds simultaneous multi-colour reuse when SNR_peak (all power on one beam) >> SNR_shared (power divided across beams) — i.e. when the satellite is power-limited rather than interference-limited. For LEO satellites at 550 km with limited bus power, this condition is typically met. Telesat Lightspeed and the ESA VHTS programmes explicitly combine full frequency reuse with beam-hopping. DVB-S2X Annex E defines the physical layer framing for this mode.

THE CAPACITY OPTIMUM Full reuse + beam-hopping is the theoretical capacity optimum for a power-limited satellite. It eliminates both the spectrum waste of multi-colour reuse and the co-channel interference that makes simultaneous full reuse difficult. The trade is burst-mode delivery and synchronisation complexity at the terminal.

5. The Flexible Payload

A flexible payload allows the power allocated to each beam, the spectrum assigned to each beam, and the beam pointing direction to be changed on orbit — not just at commissioning but continuously in response to market conditions, traffic patterns, and regulatory changes. The hardware enabling this is the active phased array with digital beamforming (m3-4) combined with the digital baseband processor (m3-3).

  • Flexible beam count: DBF weight changes create fewer, larger beams or more, narrower beams from the same aperture
  • Flexible power allocation: DBF amplitude weights redirect transmit power from under-loaded to over-loaded beams
  • Flexible spectrum allocation: the digital channeliser reassigns frequency sub-channels between beams without physical reconfiguration
  • Flexible pointing: phase gradients in the DBF steer each beam to a different ground location — the satellite can serve different geographic regions than originally designed for

Telesat Lightspeed (MDA Aurora platform), Airbus OneSat, and several next-generation GEO and LEO platforms are explicitly designed as software-defined flexible satellites.

FLEXIBILITY IS BOUNDED BY THE HARDWARE ENVELOPE Flexibility means choosing how to allocate fixed hardware resources — it does not create resources not designed in at launch. Total transmit power is fixed by the bus and PA efficiency. Licensed spectrum is fixed by the regulator. Maximum scan angle is fixed by the array geometry. DBF beam count is fixed by processing capacity. Post-launch reconfigurability operates within these bounds.

6. Interference Between LEO Constellation Satellites

Frequency reuse within a single satellite is only part of the interference picture. In a LEO constellation, multiple satellites are simultaneously visible from a single ground terminal.

Co-frequency inter-satellite interference: when two constellation satellites use the same frequency simultaneously, a terminal served by satellite A may receive interference from satellite B's beam. For a well-designed LEO constellation, adjacent satellites are separated enough that sidelobe isolation is sufficient — but this must be verified through system-level simulation, not assumed.

LEO-to-GEO interference (EPFD limits): as discussed in m1-6, NGSO constellations must satisfy ITU equivalent power flux density (EPFD) limits to protect GEO operators sharing the same frequency band. The cumulative downlink power density from all visible LEO satellites at a GEO Earth station must stay below the ITU threshold. This drives LEO downlink EIRP, beam pattern design, and in some cases requires beam blanking — turning off beams that would otherwise illuminate a GEO Earth station — which represents a real but managed capacity loss.

Exercise 1 — System Capacity vs Reuse Factor

Compute total system capacity as a function of reuse factor, accounting for SINR degradation as reuse tightens.

import math

def cap_mbps(bw_mhz, sinr_db):
    return bw_mhz * math.log2(1 + 10**(sinr_db/10))

def system_cap(n_beams, total_bw, R, peak_sinr, degrade_per_step=3.0):
    bw  = total_bw / R
    sinr = peak_sinr - degrade_per_step * (4 - R)
    per  = cap_mbps(bw, sinr)
    return n_beams * per, per, sinr

n_beams = 100; total_bw = 500   # MHz

print(f"System capacity vs reuse factor ({n_beams} beams, {total_bw} MHz)\n")
print(f"{'R':>4}{'Beam BW':>10}{'SINR':>9}{'Cap/beam':>12}{'Total':>12}")
print("-" * 49)
for R in [4, 3, 2, 1]:
    tot, per, sinr = system_cap(n_beams, total_bw, R, 20.0)

          f"{per:>10.1f}Mbps{tot/1000:>10.1f}Gbps")

Exercise 2 — Beam-Hopping Schedule Optimisation

Allocate beam dwell times proportional to traffic demand across a 20-beam constellation.

def hop_schedule(demands, frame_ms=10.0, min_pct=0.0):
    """Demand-proportional beam dwell allocation."""
    total = sum(demands)
    sched = []
    for i, d in enumerate(demands):
        frac  = max(d/total, min_pct/100)
        dwell = frac * frame_ms
        sched.append((i+1, d, dwell, frac*100))
    # Renormalise if floors were applied
    total_dwell = sum(s[2] for s in sched)
    if total_dwell > frame_ms:
        scale = frame_ms / total_dwell
        sched = [(b, d, dw*scale, dw*scale/frame_ms*100)
                 for b, d, dw, _ in sched]
    return sched

demands = [2, 2, 1, 30, 45, 20, 3, 1, 2, 15,
           8,  5, 2,  1,  3, 12, 2,  1, 5, 10]

sched = hop_schedule(demands, 10.0)
print(f"{'Beam':>5}{'Demand':>8}{'Dwell(ms)':>11}{'Time%':>8}  Type")
print("-" * 46)
for b, d, dw, pct in sched:
    t = "URBAN" if d>=15 else ("SUBURBAN" if d>=5 else "RURAL")
    print(f"{b:>5}{d:>8}{dw:>10.2f}ms{pct:>7.1f}%  {t}")

Exercise 3 — EPFD at a GEO Earth Station

Compute the aggregate equivalent power flux density from LEO satellites at a GEO Earth station location and check against the ITU limit.

import math

C = 299792458.0
EPFD_LIMIT = -160.0   # dBW/m^2/Hz, typical ITU downlink limit

def fspl(dist_km, freq_ghz):
    lam = C / (freq_ghz * 1e9)
    return 20 * math.log10(4 * math.pi * dist_km * 1e3 / lam)

def epfd(eirps_dbw, dists_km, freq_ghz, bw_hz):
    total = sum(10**((e - fspl(d, freq_ghz) - 10*math.log10(bw_hz))/10)
                for e, d in zip(eirps_dbw, dists_km))
    return 10 * math.log10(total) if total > 0 else -300

freq = 20.0; bw = 250e6   # Ka-band downlink, 250 MHz ref BW

cases = [
    ("Beam ON GEO station, 3 sats",   [50,45,43], [700,1200,1800]),
    ("Beam NOT on station (−25dB SL)", [25,20,18], [700,1200,1800]),
    ("1 sat overhead, beam on station",[50],        [700]),
]

print(f"EPFD at GEO Earth station  (Ka {freq} GHz, BW={bw/1e6:.0f} MHz)\n")
print(f"{'Scenario':<44}{'EPFD':>16}{'Margin':>10}")
print("-"*72)
for name, eirps, dists in cases:
    e = epfd(eirps, dists, freq, bw)
    m = EPFD_LIMIT - e
    print(f"{name:<44}{e:>14.1f} dBW{m:>+9.1f} dB")
print(f"\nITU limit: {EPFD_LIMIT} dBW/m²/Hz  (positive margin = compliant)")
